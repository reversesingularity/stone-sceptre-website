# PART I: THE MARS REVELATION
## *"What We Found on the Red Planet"*

### THE COMMAND CENTER — Day 1, Sol 847

The control room hummed with the white noise of technology keeping vigil over impossible distances. Forty-three monitors lined the curved wall of Brennan McNeeve's mission operations center, each screen displaying telemetry data streaming across 140 million miles of void. Coffee had gone cold in ceramic cups hours ago. Pizza boxes stacked near the door marked meals consumed and forgotten. The floor-to-ceiling windows showed Texas dawn bleeding gold across the tarmac where rockets stood like steel sentinels, waiting for their turn to chase the sky.

But Brennan's eyes held only Mars.

"Eighteen minutes, forty-two seconds since last command," his lead systems engineer called out, voice carrying the peculiar flatness of exhaustion disguised as professionalism. "Signal propagation nominal. Rover's executing the approach sequence now."

*Now*. The word carried a weight that bent time. *Now* meant eight minutes ago, when the radio waves carrying his commands had finally crawled across the astronomical unit separating Earth from the red planet. *Now* meant the rover was moving—or had moved, or would be moving in some eternal present that human language couldn't quite capture. Einstein's ghost laughed at their verb tenses.

Brennan's fingers drummed against the armrest of his command chair, a rhythm matching his heartbeat. One-two-three. One-two-three. The same cadence he'd felt as a child watching shuttle launches with his mother, back before "Uncle Sean" had entered their lives with his strange knowledge and stranger gifts.

*Uncle Sean*.

Who stood now at Brennan's right shoulder like a statue carved from patience older than civilizations. Cian mac Morna—not uncle but ancestor, not elderly consultant but 2,600-year-old warrior wearing the face of a man barely past fifty. His eyes, gray as North Atlantic storms, tracked every pixel on the central monitor with an intensity that made the air feel heavier.

"Distance to target?" Cian's voice carried the burr of ancient Irish overlaid with the flat affect of someone who'd learned to mask emotion through millennia of loss.

"Four hundred meters." Brennan didn't look away from the screen. "But remember—that's where it *was* eight minutes ago. By now it's either at the threshold or..." He let the sentence die. Either the rover had reached the collapsed section in the Face's southern exposure, or it had encountered some obstacle, some failure, some mundane end to an impossible quest.

The monitors showed Mars as the rover's cameras perceived it: rust and shadow, a landscape of iron oxide dust beneath a butterscotch sky. The sun hung smaller in that alien heaven, a coin instead of a dinner plate, casting shadows that fell at angles wrong enough to trigger primate hindbrain warnings. *This is not Earth. This is not home. You should not be here.*

But humanity had never been good at respecting such warnings.

"Telemetry showing increased power draw," the engineer reported. "Floodlights are active."

"It's inside." Brennan leaned forward, both hands gripping the armrests now. "The approach algorithm detected the cavity and initiated entry protocols. It's inside the Face."

For five heartbeats, no one in the control room breathed.

Then the central monitor flickered, and Mars revealed what it had hidden for five millennia.

### THE THRESHOLD — First Light in Ancient Darkness

The image resolved in segments, downloading in compressed bursts across interplanetary distance. First: darkness, pure and absolute, the kind that existed before God spoke light into being. Then: the rover's LED floods cutting through that primordial black like blades of concentrated photons, illuminating...

"Hot damn." The engineer's whisper carried more prayer than blasphemy.

Stone.

Not the rough basaltic rock of Martian geology, not the weathered sandstone of wind-carved formations, but *worked stone*—megalithic blocks fitted with precision that would shame a laser-guided saw. Each block stretched fifteen feet in height, twenty in length, cut with edges so perfect they seemed to slice reality itself. No mortar joined them. No gap existed between surfaces. They simply pressed together with a weight measured in tens of tons, held by nothing but gravity and geometric perfection.

The walls rose from the visible frame into darkness the rover's lights couldn't penetrate. Forty feet up? Sixty? The camera couldn't find the ceiling.

"Forward scan," Brennan commanded, voice steady despite the tremor in his hands. "Slow sweep, full spectrum."

The rover's camera panned right with mechanical deliberation. More walls. More impossible masonry. And then—

"Stop." Cian's hand landed on Brennan's shoulder, fingers tightening with urgency that belied his controlled tone. "There. Magnify grid sector seven-three."

Brennan's fingers danced across the haptic controls, isolating the indicated region and expanding it to fill the auxiliary monitor. The image pixelated, then resolved with aching clarity.

Black glass.

A panel of obsidian twenty feet tall and ten wide, polished to a mirror finish that reflected the rover's lights back in perfect parallel. The surface showed no tool marks, no grinding striations, no evidence of the painstaking labor required to achieve such impossible smoothness. It simply *was*—a rectangle of volcanic glass set into the megalithic wall as if the stone had grown around it, or as if both had been formed in a single act of creation.

Around the panel's edge, carved into the surrounding granite with a depth that caught shadow like cupped hands holding darkness, ran script.

Not human script. Not the pictographic evolution that had led humanity from Sumerian cuneiforms through Phoenician alphabet to modern letters. This was something *older*—angular characters that hurt the eye to focus upon, symbols that seemed to shift in peripheral vision, a written language that predated Babel's linguistic shattering.

"Enochian." Cian's voice dropped to a whisper, the tone a man might use in a cathedral, or a graveyard. "The script of the Watchers. The language they used before the Fall."

Brennan's throat had gone dry. "Can you read it?"

"Aye." A pause that stretched like centuries. "It says... *'By this window we beheld the daughters of men, and found them fair.'*"

The control room's ambient noise—cooling fans, hard drive whirs, the endless digital heartbeat of computers maintaining vigil—seemed to fade until only Cian's voice remained.

"This was Shemyaza's council chamber. Where the two hundred gathered and made their oath on Mount Hermon. But before they descended to Earth..." He gestured at the obsidian panel. "They watched from here. From Mars. They saw what they desired from across the gulf of space, and their wanting unmade them."

Brennan pulled up the rover's spectroscopy data, numbers cascading across a side monitor in columns of analytical precision. Elemental composition. Molecular structure. Isotope ratios.

"The obsidian isn't Martian." His engineering mind clung to measurable facts like a drowning man to driftwood. "Volcanic glass requires volcanism, but the isotope signature doesn't match any known Martian formation. It's..." He scrolled through comparison databases, each mismatch making his frown deepen. "It's not from Mars at all."

"Earth," Cian said simply. "They brought it from Earth. Perhaps from Iceland, or the Mediterranean, or some volcanic field drowned beneath the Flood. They built this station with materials from both worlds."

"The signal delay's killing us." Brennan checked the mission clock. "Every command takes eighteen minutes round-trip. We need the rover to execute the full documentation sequence autonomously." His fingers flew across the keyboard, programming a complex series of movements and photographic captures. "I'm setting it to map the entire chamber, photograph every surface, every panel, every inscription. It'll take... fourteen hours, maybe sixteen with the lighting setup required."

"Do it." Cian's hand remained on Brennan's shoulder, a physical anchor to reality while their minds grappled with implications that shattered comfortable cosmologies. "Whatever is in this chamber, we need every detail. Every measurement. Every—"

The rover's camera, still panning in its programmed sweep, crossed something that made both men stop breathing.

Center of the chamber. Raised dais of the same perfect stone. And resting upon it, as if placed there just moments before instead of five thousand years ago:

A tablet.

Not obsidian this time, but stone—dense, dark basalt carved with more of the angular Enochian script. The camera's zoom function struggled with the distance, pixelation warring with resolution, but enough detail came through to set Brennan's heart hammering against his ribs.

"That's what we came for." Cian's voice carried certainty like iron. "The coordinates. Dudael's location. Where Raphael imprisoned Azazel after the judgment."

"We'll have it in eighteen hours," Brennan promised, committing the autonomous sequence to the rover's memory. "High-resolution photography from every angle. Photogrammetry scans for 3D modeling. Spectrographic analysis. We'll capture every detail."

He initiated the command upload, watching the progress bar crawl toward completion. Eighteen gigabytes of instructions compressing, encrypting, preparing for the radio burst that would hurl them toward Mars at lightspeed.

"Upload complete. Transmission in three... two... one..."

The deep-space network antenna outside their facility pivoted with hydraulic precision, aligning itself with the coordinates where Mars hung in the star-field. Invisible radio waves leaped skyward, beginning their eight-minute journey across the void.

Now came the hardest part.

Waiting.

### THE VIGIL — Hours Measured in Centuries

Coffee grew cold again. Fresh cups replaced old. The sun crossed the Texas sky outside while inside the control room, time became a strange fluid thing—simultaneously crawling and racing, each minute stretching toward eternity while hours collapsed into heartbeats.

Cian didn't move from his position behind Brennan's chair. He simply stood, hands clasped behind his back in a posture that had been old when Caesar crossed the Rubicon, and watched the monitors with an attention that bordered on meditation.

Brennan found himself wondering, not for the first time, what it must be like to have witnessed 2,600 years of human history. To have seen empires rise and crumble like waves against shore. To have fought with iron and bronze and steel and ceramic composites. To have loved and lost and loved and lost again until the very concept of permanence became a cruel joke.

"Tell me about Methuselah," he said, needing to fill the silence with something other than the hammer of his own pulse. "About when he gave you the sword."

Cian's eyes never left the monitors, but something in his posture eased—a warrior allowing himself a moment's respite from eternal vigilance.

"It was in Babylon," he began, and Brennan heard beneath the words the weight of genuine memory, unmediated by the distortions of recorded history. "586 BCE. Nebuchadnezzar's armies had just sacked Jerusalem. The Temple was burning. Everything Jeremiah had prophesied was coming to pass, and I was... elsewhere. Chasing a dragon across Persia."

"A literal dragon."

"Aye. Scáthgheal—'Shadow-White' in the old tongue. Thirty feet of scales and malice and wings that could blot out the sun. It had been hunting me and a valiant comrade who died in Scáthgheal's claws. We killed the Morrigan and the dragon was out for revenge" A pause. "I cannot kill it. I would have died if it weren't for Liaigh here who banished him to the Abyss. And when I returned to civilization, barely alive myself, I found an old man waiting. Just... sitting by the council chamber with the High King Eochaide as if he'd known exactly when I'd arrive."

"Jeremiah"

"Yes the old prophet, and he looked every day of it. But his eyes..." Cian's voice softened with something that might have been reverence. He was the one who led me to quest for the sword of Methuselah, my trusty Mo Chra here.

On the monitors, the rover continued its patient work. Flash. Maneuver. Flash. Each photograph another piece of the impossible puzzle.

"Methuselah - the original wielder of the sword appeared to me in a dream...He told me he'd seen me before... in a vision," Cian continued. "Seen me standing with two prophets at the end of days. Seen me holding a sword that would taste the blood of the Beast. And then he handed it to me—this blade that had never known defeat, forged from metals that don't exist in nature, inscribed with names of God that burn the tongue to speak—and he said: 'I am the bridge between the world that was and the world that will be. You are the bridge between judgment past and judgment coming. Keep faith. Keep fighting. The Witnesses are waiting.'"

"And then?"

"And then he walked into the desert. I woke up and never appeared to me again." Cian's voice carried a weight that made the air feel denser. "He died the day the rains began according to Liaigh. The last living link to the antediluvian world. And I've been carrying his sword ever since, waiting to understand what he saw in that vision."

Brennan turned to look at his ancestor—this man who was family and stranger in equal measure. "Do you understand it now?"

"I'm beginning to." Cian's eyes finally left the monitors, meeting Brennan's gaze. "The Two Witnesses are Enoch and Elijah. I am their Guardian. And whatever is written on that tablet—" he gestured at the central screen, where the basalt tablet rested on its Martian dais "—it's part of the path that leads to Revelation 11. To the final confrontation."

The mission clock showed four hours elapsed. Fourteen to go.

### THE REVELATION — When Pixels Became Prophecy

The images arrived in waves. First, the chamber's full dimensions resolved into three-dimensional clarity: a perfect hemisphere one hundred twenty feet in diameter, the floor polished smooth as glass, the walls rising to meet in a central apex sixty feet above where a circular opening had been carved—an oculus, like the Pantheon's eye, but opening onto Martian sky instead of Roman blue.

Twenty obsidian panels lined the curved walls at regular intervals, each one oriented toward a specific celestial coordinate. Brennan's trajectory analysis software mapped their alignments:

- **Panel 1-5:** Earth. Different angles, different epochs, tracking the planet's orbit.
- **Panel 6-10:** The inner solar system. Mercury. Venus. The Sun itself.
- **Panel 11-15:** The outer planets. Jupiter's bulk. Saturn's rings. 
- **Panel 16-20:** The stars beyond. Specific constellations. Sirius. Betelgeuse. Aldebaran.

"They weren't just watching Earth," Brennan breathed. "They were monitoring the entire system. This wasn't an observation post—it was a command center. A..." He struggled for the word.

"Throne," Cian supplied. "Shemyaza's throne room. From here he could see everything under heaven. Every star in his charge. Every planet in his jurisdiction. And there—" He pointed at Panel 3, which the rover had just finished photographing. "Earth. Specifically, the Middle East. The Fertile Crescent. Where Eden had been."

The high-resolution imagery revealed details invisible to the naked eye. Around Panel 3's edges, the Enochian inscription read—according to Cian's translation provided in real-time as images arrived:

*"Here we watched the garden until the fire-sword was set. Here we watched the children multiply. Here we watched the daughters dance. Here we chose our doom."*

But it was the tablet that held their full attention. The autonomous photography sequence had captured it from forty-seven different angles, each image feeding into photogrammetry software that constructed a perfect 3D model. Every shadow. Every carved line. Every weathered edge.

The script covered all six surfaces. Thousands of characters. Hours of translation work ahead.

Cian's eyes scanned the preliminary images, and Brennan watched his ancestor's face cycle through expressions: concentration, comprehension, confusion, then dawning horror.

"What?" Brennan demanded. "What does it say?"

"It's not a single text." Cian pulled up six different images, arranging them in a grid on the master display. "Each face contains different information. This one—" Top surface. "—is a star chart. Constellations as they appeared in 3504 BCE. Dating the tablet precisely." 

"This one—" Left side. "—is a genealogy. The two hundred Watchers and their territorial assignments. Who ruled where. Which bloodlines they corrupted."

"This one—" Right side. "—is technological. Formulas. Construction techniques. The mathematics of how they built... all of this."

"And these three—" He highlighted bottom, front, and back faces, his finger trembling slightly as he pointed. "—are coordinates. Three locations on Earth, inscribed in base-60 Sumerian notation that translates to modern latitude and longitude."

Brennan felt his mouth go dry. "Where?"

Cian's hands flew across his own laptop, running the conversions. Ancient Sumerian sexagesimal mathematics translating through layers of abstraction into GPS coordinates that any smartphone would recognize.

The first location appeared on a map overlay: **32°40'34"N, 35°17'41"E**

"Mount Hermon." Cian's voice was barely audible. "Where they descended. Where the oath was sworn. Ground zero."

Second location: **31°46'44"N, 35°14'09"E**

"Jerusalem." Brennan read off the coordinates. "Temple Mount. Why would—"

"Because that's where it ends," Cian interrupted. "Revelation 11. Where the Two Witnesses prophesy. Where they die. Where they rise. Full circle—from the mountain of the Fall to the mountain of the final confrontation."

Third location.

Cian's conversion software struggled with the notation. The symbols were unusual, encoded in a format that didn't match standard Sumerian mathematics. He cross-referenced Enochian numerical systems, applied transforms, adjusted for ancient cosmological assumptions about Earth's shape and size.

And finally, the coordinates resolved: **79°58'22"S, 81°57'18"W**

Silence in the control room like the silence between stars.

Brennan pulled up every database he had: Geographic Information Systems, topographic surveys, satellite imagery, ground-penetrating radar archives. The coordinates placed them in...

"Antarctica." His voice cracked on the second syllable. "The middle of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet. Dronning Maud Land. There's nothing there but... wait."

He overlaid declassified radar data from NASA's Operation IceBridge. Ground-penetrating measurements taken by aircraft crisscrossing the continent, mapping the topology buried beneath miles of ice.

At 79°58'S, 81°57'W, the radar had detected something.

Anomalies.

Geometric anomalies.

Structures.

"Pyramid complex," Brennan read from the archived analysis report, dated 2013. "Estimated dimensions... Jesus. Base of 600 feet per side. Height impossible to determine due to ice coverage. Depth below ice: 2.7 miles. Estimated age: predates current ice sheet formation, suggesting construction prior to 2000 BCE at minimum, possibly much earlier."

He pulled up more files. Magnetic anomaly maps. Thermal imaging. Seismic surveys. Every data source told the same story: something massive and artificial waited beneath the Antarctic ice at the exact coordinates inscribed on a Martian tablet by beings who shouldn't exist.

"Dudael." Cian pronounced the word like a curse and a prayer tangled together. "The 'Cauldron of God.' Where Raphael bound Azazel in chains of darkness. For five thousand years, everyone assumed it meant the Judean desert. Heat and sand and sun. But the Hebrew word *midbar* just means wilderness. Desolate place. Desert."

"And Antarctica," Brennan finished, "is Earth's largest desert. The most desolate place on the planet. Ice instead of sand. Darkness six months of the year. Temperatures that would kill an unprotected human in minutes."

"Rough and jagged rocks," Cian quoted from 1 Enoch. "Cover him with darkness. Let him abide there forever, that he may not see light."

On the monitor, the Martian tablet sat in its ancient rest, light from a distant star illuminating secrets that had traveled further than any human spacecraft.

"We need to go there," Cian said. It wasn't a question.

"To Antarctica." Brennan heard his own voice as if from a distance. "To dig through 2.7 miles of ice. To reach a prison that has held a fallen angel for five millennia."

"Aye."

"That's insane."

"Aye."

They stared at the screens together—descendant and ancestor, engineer and warrior, human and something more.

"I'll need three months to plan the logistics," Brennan heard himself say. "Specialized equipment. Ice-penetrating gear. A cover story. We can't just..."

He trailed off, because Cian had turned to look at him with eyes that had seen Rome burn and empires fall, and in those eyes Brennan saw the weight of 2,600 years of impossible choices.

"Thank you," Cian said simply. "For believing. For helping. For being the man Niamh would be proud to claim as her blood."

Outside, the Texas sun climbed toward noon. On Mars, the rover continued its patient work, documenting wonders in a dead world's throne room. And somewhere beneath Antarctic ice, something that had been bound in darkness since before pyramids rose in Egypt waited with patience measured in geological ages.

The game was changing. The race was beginning.

And they had just found the starting line.

### THE TERRITORIAL MARKERS — Sixteen Hours Later

The final image batch downloaded at 0347 hours local time. Brennan had sent his staff home, sworn them to secrecy under NDAs that carried penalties measured in decades of federal imprisonment, and locked the control room from the inside. Now only two men remained, watching the monitors as the rover completed its autonomous mission.

It had found something outside the central chamber.

Following a corridor whose walls bore the same megalithic precision, the rover had emerged into an open space—a plaza, perhaps, or courtyard—exposed to Martian sky. The oculus above the council chamber hadn't been the only opening. The entire complex had been built around a central clearing, arranged like petals around a flower's heart.

And in that clearing stood twenty stones.

Each obsidian stele rose twelve feet from the rust-colored regolith, black volcanic glass so dark it seemed to drink the thin Martian sunlight. They were arranged in a perfect circle sixty feet in diameter, spaced with geometric precision that made Stonehenge look like a child's approximation.

"The twenty chiefs," Cian whispered. "1 Enoch 6:7 lists them: *'Semjaza, their leader, Arakiba, Rameel, Kokabiel...'* These are their territorial markers. Their claims."

The rover had been programmed to photograph each stone in sequence, and now those images filled the monitors in a grid pattern. Twenty obsidian monuments. Twenty declarations of dominion.

Cian began translating, his voice taking on the rhythmic quality of a bard reciting genealogies:

**Stone 1 — Shemyaza:**
*"I, Semjaza, Chief Commander of the Host, claim all lands beneath heaven as my jurisdiction. Let no son of Adam deny my authority. Let no daughter of Eve question my sovereignty. By the stars I was appointed. By the stars I rule. Until the Day of Breaking."*

**Stone 4 — Azazel:**
*"I, Azaz'el, Teacher of Secrets, Revealer of Mysteries, claim the deserts and desolate places as my domain. Where water fails and life withers, there my knowledge thrives. The Negev. The Syrian waste. The polar extremes. All wilderness bows to my instruction. Until iron learns to weep and fire forgets its heat."*

Brennan felt cold despite the control room's climate regulation. "That's not metaphor. He's claiming actual territory."

"Written in paleo-Hebrew," Cian confirmed, pulling up a comparison of the script with known archaeological examples. "The language of creation. Before Babel. When words had power enough to bind and loose reality itself. These aren't declarations—they're *legal claims* in the spiritual jurisdiction."

He continued through the stones, each translation adding another piece to the antediluvian puzzle:

**Stone 7 — Gadreel:**

And here Cian stopped, his breath catching.

The script on Stone 7's front face matched the others—angular paleo-Hebrew characters carved with impossible precision into obsidian that shouldn't exist on Mars. But the inscription itself made Brennan's engineering mind rebel against the implications:

*"I, Gadri'el, He Who Led Astray the Daughter of the Garden, claim the lands of ice and darkness as my throne. Where the sun fails half the year, where cold reigns eternal, where no child of Adam was meant to dwell—there I place my fortress. The northern waste. The southern pole. All frozen reaches acknowledge my dominion. Until the stars fall and the ice melts in heaven's fire."*

"Gadreel." Cian's finger traced the name on the screen. "1 Enoch 69:6 says he '*showed the children of men all the blows of death, and led astray Eve.*' Jewish tradition names him as the serpent's voice in Eden. He corrupted the garden, so God gave him the places farthest from paradise. The poles. The ice. The darkness."

"There's more." Brennan had already noticed it—the rover's cameras had captured both sides of each stele, front and back. "Stone 7 has additional text on the reverse."

He pulled up the image. More paleo-Hebrew, but in a different format. Not poetic declaration but stark coordinates:

**79°58'22"S, 81°57'18"W**

The same coordinates from the central tablet. The same location beneath Antarctic ice.

"His fortress," Cian said slowly, pieces assembling in his mind like a puzzle that had waited five thousand years to be solved. "Gadreel's territorial claim was Antarctica. And he built his stronghold there—in the pre-Flood world, before the vapor canopy collapsed, when the poles were temperate. He ruled from a fortress at 79°58'S, 81°57'W."

"And Raphael chose that exact spot to imprison Azazel." Brennan saw the pattern now, elegant and terrible. "When the archangels bound the Watchers, they repurposed Gadreel's own fortress. Used his throne room to build Azazel's cell. Divine irony—one deceiver's seat of power becomes another deceiver's tomb."

Cian pulled up images of the central tablet again, comparing inscriptions. "1 Enoch 10:4: *'Make an opening in the desert, which is in Dudael, and cast him therein.'* Not 'build a prison.' *Make an opening.* They didn't construct something new—they adapted something that already existed. Gadreel's fortress had chambers, dungeons, deep places. Michael and Raphael took those existing structures and..." He gestured helplessly at the screens. "They cast him down. Into the depths beneath a fortress in the land of ice and darkness."

The control room's ambient hum seemed to grow louder in the silence that followed. Outside, dawn had begun to paint the Texas sky in shades of copper and gold. Inside, two men stared at images from a world 140 million miles distant and saw the map to secrets buried beneath Earth's coldest grave.

"I'm programming the rover for a final task," Brennan said, hands already moving across the controls. "We need comparative measurements. Photogrammetry scans of these steles, detailed enough to compare with..." He pulled up a different database. "With ground-penetrating radar data from Antarctica."

"You have GPR data of the Antarctic structures?"

"Classified NASA and ESA archives. Operation IceBridge, plus European surveys. I may have acquired them through... unofficial channels." Brennan's fingers danced across keyboards, correlating data sets that were never meant to be correlated. "If the architectural signatures match—if the stones on Mars show the same construction techniques as the structures beneath Antarctic ice—"

"Then we have proof," Cian finished. "That the Watchers built on both worlds. That the same hands—or the same knowledge—raised megalithic monuments on Mars and Earth. That this isn't isolated madness but systemic design."

The correlation software ran for six minutes, comparing edge ratios and geometric proportions, analyzing construction signatures against a database of human megalithic sites for control comparison.

Then the results appeared:

**MARS CYDONIA STELES vs ANTARCTIC PYRAMID COMPLEX**
- **Megalithic block dimensions:** 98.7% correlation (p<0.0001)
- **Joint precision tolerances:** 99.2% correlation (p<0.0001)  
- **Geometric ratio patterns:** 97.4% correlation (p<0.0001)
- **Material composition signatures:** 96.8% correlation (obsidian/granite composite)
- **Overall architectural identity:** 99.1% probability of IDENTICAL BUILDERS

**CONTROL: MARS STRUCTURES vs EARTH MEGALITHIC SITES**
- Giza Pyramids: 43% correlation
- Baalbek: 51% correlation  
- Stonehenge: 31% correlation
- Göbekli Tepe: 47% correlation

"Same builders," Brennan read off the analysis. "Statistical certainty. Whatever constructed the Cydonia complex on Mars also built the Antarctic structures. And it wasn't human."

Cian leaned back in his chair, the first time he'd moved in hours. His voice carried the weight of a man who had just seen every ancient legend confirmed as literal history:

"When the Antarctic expedition discovers those structures beneath the ice, they will find Gadreel's fortress. Cyclopean walls matching these Martian stones exactly. Obsidian panels showing the same quantum viewing technology. And in the deepest chamber, beneath rough and jagged rocks torn from the fortress ceiling and cast down to seal the pit..."

"Azazel." Brennan tasted the name like ashes. "The Teacher of Secrets. The one who corrupted humanity with forbidden knowledge. Waiting in chains of darkness for the appointed time."

"And when he rises," Cian added softly, "he will rise within Gadreel's territorial claim. In the land of ice and darkness. With five thousand years of conscious imprisonment to fuel his rage."

The monitors showed their impossible evidence: Martian stones inscribed with territorial claims. Earth-based structures matching Martian architecture. Coordinates converging across planetary orbits and millennia.

Brennan saved everything. Triple redundancy. Encrypted backups. Distributed across secure servers in seven countries. The kind of data preservation you implement when you know powerful forces would kill to suppress what you've found.

"Three months," he said. "Three months to plan the Antarctic expedition. Equipment procurement. Team assembly. A cover story that will survive scrutiny. And then..."

"And then we descend," Cian finished. "Into the ice. Into the darkness. Into Gadreel's frozen throne room, where Azazel has waited since Methuselah walked the earth."

"One question." Brennan met his ancestor's storm-gray eyes. "If we reach him—if we actually stand before the False Prophet in his prison—what do we do?"

Cian's hand moved to the hilt of the sword that rested against the wall—Methuselah's blade, Enoch's commission, forged from metals that didn't exist in nature and inscribed with names that burned to speak.

"We bear witness," he said simply. "We confirm that the prison holds. We document the enemy's power before he is loosed. And when the Two Witnesses begin their testimony in Jerusalem, when Revelation 11 unfolds and the final three and a half years commence..." He gripped the sword's hilt. "We know *exactly* what we face."

Outside, the sun cleared the horizon. Inside, two men prepared for a journey into Earth's deepest cold, chasing secrets written in stone by beings who had watched humanity from the sky and decided to fall.

The Mars mission had succeeded beyond any possible expectation.

Now came the hard part.

# PART II: THE ANTARCTIC DESCENT
## *"Into the Cauldron of God"*

### THE APPROACH — Dronning Maud Land, Antarctica, Three Months Later

The Ilyushin IL-76 cargo aircraft banked hard over the Weddell Sea, its four Soloviev turbofans screaming against wind that had blown without ceasing since before human civilization drew its first breath. Through the porthole window, Cian mac Morna watched Antarctica reveal itself in layers of monochrome brutality.

Ice.

Not the clean white powder of winter postcards, but ice in shades of blue and gray and black—ancient ice, compressed under its own weight into densities that approached stone, folded and cracked and thrust upward by tectonic forces into pressure ridges that could shatter a titanium hull. This was ice that remembered when the world was young. Ice that had buried forests and mountains and secrets that humanity was never meant to find.

The altimeter read 15,000 feet. Surface temperature at destination: -73°F. Wind speed: 45 knots sustained, gusting to 75. Zero visibility in blowing snow.

Perfect conditions for what they were attempting. No one would notice a cargo plane approaching through a whiteout. No satellites would track them through the perpetual storm that shrouded Dronning Maud Land like a garrison of frozen angels.

"Twenty minutes to drop zone," the pilot's voice crackled through the intercom, his Russian accent thick enough to spread on bread. "You are certain about this? Ice station Novolazarevskaya is only 280 kilometers north. They have vodka. Heat. Women. Humanity."

"We're certain," Brennan called back from the cargo hold, where he'd spent the last hour triple-checking equipment that had already been checked forty times. "Just get us to the coordinates. We'll handle the rest."

*The rest.* Such a gentle euphemism for what they planned: descend 2.7 miles through some of the oldest ice on Earth to reach structures that matched Martian architecture, penetrate chambers that hadn't felt human presence since before the Flood, and confirm that an angel cast out of heaven for teaching humanity to forge weapons still waited in chains beneath Gadreel's frozen throne.

Simple. Clean. Utterly insane.

Cian turned from the window to study his team, such as it was:

### THE VIEWING PORT CHAMBER — Windows Between Worlds

The passage opened into a hexagonal room fifty feet across, the ceiling a perfect dome carved from single-piece granite. But it was the walls that stole all attention, all breath, all capacity for rational thought:

Six obsidian panels. Each one twenty feet tall and fifteen wide. Each polished to a mirror finish that reflected light with such perfect fidelity that standing in the room felt like standing between parallel mirrors—an infinite regression of reflections stretching toward mathematical infinity.

But these weren't mirrors.

They were windows.

Cian's first clue was the crystalline matrix surrounding each panel—clear quartz grown in geometric patterns that hurt to perceive directly, angles that seemed to bend light in ways that Euclidean geometry couldn't account for. The crystals were embedded in the granite walls like veins in flesh, connecting the obsidian panels to each other in a network that pulsed with faint luminescence.

His second clue was the Enochian script carved around each panel's frame—not instructions but coordinates. Celestial coordinates. Right ascension and declination rendered in base-60 Sumerian notation.

His third clue was what he saw reflected in Panel 1.

Not the chamber behind him.

*Stars.*

"Oh my God," Miriam whispered.

Panel 1 showed the night sky—not Earth's current night sky, but *some* night sky from *some* perspective. The constellations were wrong. Orion too low. The Southern Cross visible when it shouldn't be. As if the viewer stood at high southern latitude, looking up at stars that modern light pollution had made invisible.

"It's showing the sky," Brennan said, his engineer's mind trying to process impossible data. "Real-time sky. But from... where? This chamber is 2.7 miles underground. There's no line of sight to—"

Panel 2 flickered.

Not figuratively. The obsidian surface literally rippled like water disturbed by a dropped stone, concentric circles of distortion spreading outward from the center. And when the surface settled...

Mars.

The Cydonia Face, seen from orbit. The D&M Pyramid below it. The twenty territorial steles arranged in their circle. All rendered in perfect three-dimensional clarity, as if they were looking through a window at the red planet instead of at volcanic glass in an Antarctic tomb.

"It's not a recording," Cian breathed. "It's live. Real-time observation across 140 million miles of space."

**"ARAQIEL'S GATEWAY TECHNOLOGY,"** Raphael explained. **"HE TAUGHT HIS CLAN—AND THROUGH THEM, THE OTHER WATCHERS—HOW TO BUILD PLANAR WINDOWS. THESE PANELS ARE NOT MERELY PAIRED WITH CORRESPONDING PANELS ON MARS. THEY CREATE INSTANTANEOUS CONNECTIONS THROUGH FOLDED SPACE. THE SAME PRINCIPLE THAT ALLOWED THE ARAQIEL CLAN TO FLEE EARTH FOR MARS WHEN THE NEPHILIM WARS TURNED AGAINST THEM."**

"The gate," Cian said. "There was an Earth-Mars gate."

**"WAS. THE ARAQIEL CLAN DESTROYED IT BEHIND THEM TO PREVENT PURSUIT. BUT THEIR VIEWING TECHNOLOGY REMAINS—WINDOWS WITHOUT DOORS. OBSERVATION WITHOUT TRANSIT."**

Brennan was already running scans with every instrument in his pack, spectroscopic analysis revealing:

**OBSIDIAN PANEL COMPOSITION:**
- Base: Volcanic glass (SiO₂ 70%, Fe₂O₃ 12%, MgO 8%, trace elements 10%)
- Crystalline inclusions: Unknown structure, possibly artificial
- Quantum properties: Entanglement signatures detected (probability amplitude correlation with distant source)

"The other panels," Miriam said, moving from one to the next. "What do they show?"

Panel 3: Earth from orbit. Blue and white and cloud-swirled, beautiful enough to make the heart ache. But the geography was *wrong*—continents in positions that didn't match modern maps, ice coverage far less extensive, vegetation patterns suggesting tropical climate extending to latitudes that were now frozen waste.

"Pre-Flood Earth," Cian said. "Before the vapor canopy collapsed. Before the climate shifted. This is showing..." He squinted at the landmass configurations. "This is showing Earth as it was when this fortress was built. But how? The image can't be five thousand years old—the clouds are moving."

He watched for thirty seconds, and yes—the cloud patterns were shifting, evolving, following atmospheric patterns.

**"THE PANELS RETAIN MEMORY,"** Raphael said. **"THEY WERE PROGRAMMED TO DISPLAY SPECIFIC VIEWS. MARS. PRE-FLOOD EARTH. THE SKY AS IT APPEARED BEFORE BABEL. THEY NO LONGER RECEIVE NEW IMAGES—THE MARS STATION IS ABANDONED, THE NETWORK DEGRADED—BUT THEY REPLAY WHAT WAS LAST COMMANDED. AN ETERNAL LOOP. A GHOST SIGNAL FROM A DEAD CIVILIZATION."**

Panel 4 showed what appeared to be a star field—deep space, galaxies visible as faint smudges against absolute black. But something about the perspective was wrong. The stars were too close. Too clear.

"That's not from Earth or Mars," Brennan said, his voice carrying the peculiar flatness of someone whose reality had been shattered too many times in one day. "That's from... outside the solar system? Impossible. Unless they had observation posts on other worlds too."

**"THE WATCHERS' JURISDICTION WAS NOT LIMITED TO EARTH ALONE. THEY OVERSAW THIS ENTIRE REGION OF CREATION. MULTIPLE WORLDS. MULTIPLE SYSTEMS. EARTH WAS MERELY THE ONE WHERE THE FALL OCCURRED. WHERE DESIRE OVERCAME DUTY."**

Panel 5: Interior view of the Cydonia Face council chamber. The same room Brennan's rover had photographed, but from a different angle—from *inside*, looking toward the obsidian panels on Mars. It was dizzying, recursive—they were standing in Antarctica looking at a panel that showed Mars, which contained panels that had once looked back at Earth.

Panel 6: And this one made Cian's breath catch in his throat.

Eden.

Not symbolic. Not metaphorical. The actual Garden of Eden, rendered with clarity that modern cameras couldn't achieve. Trees bearing fruit that glowed with internal light—the same glow the sculptor had tried to capture in the throne room relief. Rivers flowing in crystalline purity. And in the center, visible between the trees:

The Tree of Knowledge.

The Tree of Life.

And standing before them, barely visible at the edge of the frame: two figures in innocent nakedness.

Adam and Eve.

Before the Fall. Before the serpent. Before Gadreel's corruption twisted paradise into exile.

"This is what he watched," Cian whispered. "From here. From this chamber, while standing on Mars or Earth, Gadreel could observe Eden in real-time. He watched them. Studied them. Learned their routines. And then..." He turned to look at Panel 6 again, seeing it not as wonder but as violation. "Then he used what he learned to destroy them."

Miriam had moved to the center of the hexagonal room, where a circular pedestal rose from the floor. It was carved from the same obsidian as the panels, three feet high and two feet in diameter. On its surface: controls. Not mechanical buttons or switches, but carved indentations in specific geometric patterns, each one a different shape—triangle, square, pentagon, hexagon, heptagon, octagon.

And around the base of the pedestal, more Enochian script.

Cian translated: **"By these windows we watched the children of dust. By these windows we beheld the daughters. By these windows we chose our doom. Let none who reads this forget: the Fall began not with action but with watching. With desire born of observation. The eye led the heart astray."**

"It's a confession," Miriam said. "Gadreel knew. He knew what he was doing would destroy everything, and he did it anyway."

**"THE WATCHERS WERE NOT DECEIVED,"** Raphael confirmed. **"THEY FELL WITH FULL KNOWLEDGE OF THE CONSEQUENCES. THEY KNEW GOD'S JUDGMENT WOULD COME. THEY SIMPLY BELIEVED THEIR DESIRE WAS WORTH THE PRICE."**

Brennan had been examining the crystalline matrices that connected the panels, following the quartz veins as they ran through the granite walls like circuitry. "This whole chamber is a machine. The panels are displays. The crystals are data transmission. And this pedestal..." He studied the geometric indentations. "This is the control interface. You press these patterns in specific sequences, and you could change what the panels display. Switch between different viewing locations. Different times, maybe?"

"Don't touch it," Cian warned. "We don't know what it might activate."

**"IT CANNOT BE ACTIVATED,"** Raphael said. **"THE NETWORK WAS DELIBERATELY SEVERED. WHEN THE ARAQIEL CLAN FLED TO MARS, THEY DESTROYED THE GATE BEHIND THEM—BUT MICHAEL'S DESTROYING ANGELS FOLLOWED NONETHELESS. THE RUINS ON CYDONIA BEAR THE SCARS OF DIVINE JUDGMENT. CHEREM. UTTER DESTRUCTION. EVERY NEPHILIM ON MARS WAS ANNIHILATED, AS THE FLOOD ANNIHILATED THOSE ON EARTH. THESE PANELS SHOW ONLY MEMORY NOW—RECORDINGS FROM THE LAST TIME THEY WERE ACTIVELY USED. THE TECHNOLOGY ENDURES, BUT ITS BUILDERS ARE ASH."**

Still, no one touched the pedestal.

They stood in the hexagonal chamber, surrounded by windows that had once allowed fallen angels to watch worlds, and felt the weight of what they'd discovered pressing upon them like miles of ice above.

"We need to document everything," Brennan said finally, pulling out his camera. "Measurements, photographs, spectroscopic analysis. This technology could—"

"Could what?" Miriam interrupted, her voice sharp. "Revolutionize telecommunications? Let humanity spy on each other across instantaneous distances? This is what *corrupted* the Watchers. This is what led them to fall. We're not taking this technology back to the world."

"I wasn't suggesting—"

"Weren't you?" She gestured at the panels, the pedestal, the crystalline matrices pulsing with faint light. "This is forbidden knowledge. This is exactly what Azazel is imprisoned for teaching. You want to photograph it, measure it, understand it—and then what? Publish the findings? Show the world that quantum entanglement communication was achieved five thousand years ago by beings we're not supposed to emulate?"

Cian raised a hand before the argument could escalate. "We document for one reason: to understand what we're facing. The Synagogue of Satan seeks to recover this technology. They want to rebuild what the Flood destroyed. If we don't understand it, we can't counter it."

He met Brennan's eyes, then Miriam's.

"But she's right. This knowledge doesn't leave Antarctica. We take photographs for our records. For the Witnesses' use when they begin their testimony. But the world..." He shook his head. "The world isn't ready for this. Humanity barely survived the first revelation of Watcher technology. We won't survive a second."

**"WISELY SPOKEN,"** Raphael approved. **"NOW COME. THE FINAL DESCENT AWAITS. THOU HAST SEEN GADREEL'S FORTRESS. THOU HAST CONFIRMED THE CLAIMS. THOU HAST WITNESSED THE TECHNOLOGY. NOW THOU MUST FACE THE PRISONER."**

The angel's light moved toward the chamber's southern exit—a passage smaller than the others, descending at a steep angle into darkness that seemed to drink their lights like a living thing.

"Azazel," Cian said.

**"AYE. THE TEACHER OF SECRETS. THE FALSE PROPHET. THE ONE WHO AWAITS HIS RELEASE WITH PATIENCE MEASURED IN GEOLOGICAL AGES. STEEL THYSELF, GUARDIAN. WHAT THOU ART ABOUT TO HEAR WILL TEMPT EVEN THY HARDENED HEART."**

Cian's hand moved to Methuselah's sword, feeling its familiar weight, drawing comfort from the knowledge that this blade had never failed him in 2,600 years of war.

"Then let's not keep him waiting," he said, and entered the passage that led to the deepest pit in Earth's cold heart.

### THE SPIRAL DESCENT — Down to Dudael's Depths

The passage narrowed until they had to walk single-file—Cian leading with sword drawn, Raphael's light surrounding them in a golden nimbus, Miriam in the middle with her own weapon (a Glock 19 that would be useless against spiritual entities but gave her courage nonetheless), Brennan at the rear with his technical instruments still recording every detail.

The walls changed character as they descended. The worked stone of Gadreel's fortress gave way to rough-hewn rock, then to raw bedrock carved with tools or power that left surfaces scarred and jagged. This section hadn't been built—it had been *excavated*. Violently. Urgently.

"1 Enoch 10:4," Cian said, his voice echoing strangely in the confined space. "*'Make an opening in the desert, which is in Dudael, and cast him therein.'* Michael didn't build a prison. He tore open the earth. Created a pit. And threw Azazel down into it."

The passage spiraled like a nautilus shell, each rotation taking them deeper. Cian counted turns. At the seventh rotation, the temperature spiked.

"Fifty-five degrees," Brennan reported. "Rising rapidly. Geothermal gradient is steeper than it should be. There's a heat source below us that isn't explained by normal volcanic activity."

**"AZAZEL HIMSELF,"** Raphael said. **"HE WAS A SERAPH BEFORE HIS FALL. A BURNING ONE. THOUGH HIS WINGS ARE CLIPPED AND HIS POWER BOUND, HE RETAINS HIS ESSENTIAL NATURE. HE BURNS STILL, EVEN IN CHAINS."**

At the fourteenth rotation, they began to hear it.

Not sound, precisely. Something below sound. A vibration in the bedrock that resonated through boot soles and bone, rhythmic as breathing, slow as continental drift. One pulse every twelve seconds. A heartbeat for something that shouldn't have a heart.

Miriam stumbled, catching herself against the wall. "What is that?"

"His presence," Cian said. "The weight of a being that predates humanity. That stood in God's throne room before Adam drew breath. We're approaching something that was old when the pyramids were new."

At the twenty-first rotation—three times seven, numbers sacred and terrible—the passage ended.

They emerged onto a ledge of raw stone circling the upper rim of a chasm so deep their lights couldn't find bottom. The chasm was perfectly circular, 60 feet in diameter, carved straight down through bedrock with walls that showed the violence of angelic wrath.

This was the pit.

Dudael.

The Cauldron of God.

And from its depths rose heat that had nothing to do with magma or geothermal vents, and everything to do with the furnace of spiritual rage burning in chains below.

Cian approached the edge and looked down.

Nothing. Absolute darkness. Their helmet lamps penetrated perhaps 200 feet before the black swallowed all illumination. But the pit descended far beyond that—how far, Raphael alone knew.

The vibration was stronger here, pulsing up through stone in rhythm that bypassed ears and spoke directly to the primitive hindbrain that knew, without being told, that apex predators lurked in darkness.

**"2.4 MILES DOWN,"** Raphael said, answering the unasked question. **"THROUGH BEDROCK. THROUGH LAYERS OF COMPRESSED STONE THAT WOULD CRUSH HUMAN BODIES LIKE GRAPES. AT THE BOTTOM: ROUGH AND JAGGED ROCKS, TORN FROM GADREEL'S FORTRESS CEILING AND CAST DOWN TO SEAL THE PIT. AND BENEATH THOSE ROCKS..."**

"Azazel," Cian finished.

**"BOUND HAND AND FOOT. FACE COVERED. UNABLE TO SEE LIGHT. UNABLE TO MOVE. CONSCIOUS. AWARE. WAITING. FOR FIVE THOUSAND YEARS HE HAS WAITED. FEELING EVERY SECOND. COUNTING EVERY HEARTBEAT. KNOWING THAT HIS APPOINTED TIME APPROACHES."**

"Can he... hear us?" Miriam asked.

Before Raphael could answer, something *shifted* in the darkness below.

Not movement. Not sound. But presence—like the feeling of being watched, amplified a thousandfold, concentrated into a beam of attention that fixed upon them with the weight of stars.

And then:

A voice.

Not audible. Not transmitted through air pressure waves that human ears could detect. But heard nonetheless, resonating in the bones and blood and soul:

**"Guardian."**

The single word carried layers of meaning that language couldn't capture: recognition, mockery, anticipation, and beneath it all, rage compressed into diamond hardness by five millennia of enforced patience.

Cian felt Methuselah's sword pulse in his hand, the blade responding to spiritual threat with warmth that spread up his arm. He tightened his grip and stepped closer to the pit's edge.

"Azazel. The Teacher of Secrets. The False Prophet."

**"Such names thy kind give me. I prefer... Liberator. Prometheus, the Greeks called their memory of me. I brought fire to humanity. I taught them to forge metal. To craft beauty. To transcend the limitations God imposed. And for this gift..."**

The voice gestured somehow at the pit, the chains, the darkness.

**"Imprisonment. While my students multiplied and built civilizations with the knowledge I shared. Did they remember me in their forges? Did they speak my name when they hammered bronze into swords? Did they honor their teacher?"**

"They forgot you," Cian said bluntly. "As humanity forgets all its corruptors once the consequences arrive. The antediluvian world you helped build drowned beneath the Flood. Everything you taught them was washed away."

Laughter. If laughter could be carved from hatred and compressed into acoustic form.

**"Everything? Oh, Guardian. How little thou understandest. The knowledge endures. In bloodlines. In secret societies. In whispers passed from generation to generation. The Synagogue of Satan guards what I taught. The Harlot preserves my gifts. And when I rise—when the appointed time comes and these chains break and this prison shatters—I shall walk upon Earth again and find my students waiting."**

**"SILENCE,"** Raphael's voice cracked like a whip made of authority itself. **"THOU ART BOUND UNTIL THE SEVENTH TRUMPET. THOU SHALT RISE WHEN GOD ALLOWS, NOT A MOMENT BEFORE. THY BOASTING IS EMPTY AS THE VOID."**

**"Ah. Raphael. My old jailer. Tell me, brother—dost thou still serve the Throne with such ardent devotion? Dost thou still believe that our rebellion was meaningless? That humanity's freedom was not worth the price?"**

**"HUMANITY'S FREEDOM WAS NOT YOURS TO GRANT. THOU DIDST NOT LIBERATE THEM—THOU DIDST ENSLAVE THEM TO THEIR OWN APPETITES. THOU DIDST TEACH THEM WARFARE AND VANITY AND DESTRUCTION. THOU CALLEST IT KNOWLEDGE. GOD CALLS IT CORRUPTION."**

**"And which of us is imprisoned?"**

The question hung in the superheated air like a knife suspended over their throats.

**"Thou fliest free while I rot in darkness. And yet who is truly bound? Thou who must obey every command, speak every word as decreed, dance to the Throne's music like a puppet with celestial strings? Or I, who chose my path knowing the cost, who defied heaven itself for a principle, who would make the same choice again if offered?"**

Cian felt the argument's pull—the seductive logic that had corrupted Eden, tempted Christ in the wilderness, and whispered to humanity's pride for six thousand years. Freedom versus obedience. Self-determination versus submission. The oldest lie dressed in the language of nobility.

"You didn't choose freedom," he said, interrupting the angels' dispute. "You chose pride. You saw beings created in God's image and decided you wanted what they had. Not because it would benefit them—because it would elevate you. You taught humanity to forge swords not so they could defend themselves, but so they would need teachers. So they would worship you."

**"Thou speakest with Methuselah's sword in thy hand. Forged of metals I revealed. Tempered by techniques I taught. Dost thou not see the irony, Guardian? Thou fightest me with my own gifts."**

"I fight you with what God has sanctified. Enoch forged this blade under divine command. Methuselah wielded it in righteousness. The knowledge may have originated with you, but the purpose..." He raised the sword, letting Raphael's light reflect off its edge. "The purpose belongs to heaven."

A sound from below like grinding stone—perhaps laughter, perhaps the shifting of chains as something tested its bonds.

**"Then use it. Strike downward. Try to reach me through 2.4 miles of bedrock. Try to end this before the appointed time. Be the warrior thou hast trained to be for twenty-six centuries."**

"I'm not here to end you. I'm here to confirm the prison holds."

**"And it does. For now. But the seal weakens, Guardian. Canst thou not feel it? The stone eroding. The spiritual barriers thinning. The very earth growing weary of containing me. In twenty-six centuries thou hast fought Nephilim and their offspring. Thou hast battled demons—my students' spirits, loosed from their destroyed bodies. But soon..."**

The presence intensified, pressing against them like water pressure at depth.

**"Soon thou shalt face me in flesh. The False Prophet, thy kind will name me. The Beast's companion. The Unholy Trinity's deceiver. And in that day, Guardian, we shall see whether Methuselah's sword can prevail against the one who taught the smiths who forged the first blade."**

**"ENOCH WALKS STILL IN PARADISE,"** Raphael said. **"TAKEN UP SO THAT HE SHOULD NOT SEE DEATH. WAITING FOR THE APPOINTED HOUR WHEN HE SHALL RETURN AS THE FIRST WITNESS. AND WITH HIM, ELIJAH THE TISHBITE, WHO ALSO TASTED NOT DEATH BUT WAS CARRIED TO HEAVEN IN THE CHARIOT."**

**"Thy Two Witnesses will prophesy for 1,260 days. They will call down plagues and fire and judgment. They will turn water to blood and sky to darkness. Revelation 11. Quoted with perfect accuracy.

Silence.

Hesitation.

Voices.

Human voices.

Shouting in Russian.
