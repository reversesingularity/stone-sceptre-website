# CHAPTER 2: DEAD RECKONING

✦

*I. THE WOMAN WHO DRILLS*

**Research Vessel *Ashtoreth***

**Ross Sea, Antarctica**

**78°14'S, 179°22'W**

The drilling report arrived at 0347 local time, delivered by a man who had learned three years ago not to knock on the door of the master cabin. He left the tablet on the floor outside, aligned precisely with the threshold, and retreated to the far end of the corridor before the sound of his footsteps could constitute an interruption.

Naamah had been awake for an hour. She was always awake before the reports arrived.

The cabin was cold — not by the standards of the Ross Sea in late spring, where thirty-knot katabatic winds made the surface temperature academic, but by the standards of a woman whose body had not felt the warmth of honest sunlight since the day the sky split open and the fountains of the deep turned the world to water. She kept the thermostat at twelve degrees. Cold enough to think. Not cold enough to remember.

She opened the tablet. Read the report the way she read everything: once for data, once for implication, once for threat.

The acoustics had changed.

She read the relevant section a fourth time.

For three years, the drill site on the Ross Ice Shelf had produced the same acoustic signature: dense sub-glacial rock returning the drill's vibration with the flat, deadened absorption of material that had been compressed beyond geological norms. The sound came back wrong — that had always been the point. The wrongness told her she was in the right place. Something beneath the ice was dense enough and deep enough to eat sound, and the only things that ate sound with that particular appetite were Watcher-era acoustic constructions.

She had the coordinates. She had the drilling schedule. She had three years of acoustic returns confirming that something was down there.

What she did not have was a name.

The coordinates had come from dead reckoning — her own calculations, built from a calibration vector she'd derived when the Peru stele's continental drift displacement gave her a reference point for projecting Gadreel's antediluvian domain onto the modern Antarctic plate. Three hundred kilometres of uncertainty. She'd narrowed it to ninety with seismic mapping, then to twelve with the drill itself. Twelve kilometres. Close.

But the acoustic returns had always been uniform. Consistent. The sound of something very large, very old, and very silent, absorbing what she sent down and giving back nothing but confirmation of its own density.

Until today.

Today the returns had changed character. The absorption frequency had shifted — not by much, barely within the resolution of the acoustic sensors she'd purchased through six layers of shell companies from a Norwegian manufacturer who believed he was supplying an oil exploration platform. But the shift was real. The sound was coming back with a harmonic it hadn't carried before.

A harmonic that meant something beneath the drill site was responding.

Not reflecting. Not absorbing. *Responding.*

Naamah set the tablet down. Pressed her fingertips together. Felt the particular stillness that came over her when four thousand years of patience arrived at the edge of a moment she had been building toward since the day the waters receded and she had crawled onto a shoreline that shouldn't have existed, wearing a body that wasn't entirely what it had been, and made a decision.

She would never be helpless again.

She picked up the intercom.

"Operations."

"Ma'am."

"Drill speed."

"Current: seventy-two percent sustainable maximum. We're managing thermal load on the—"

"Maximum sustainable rate. Now."

A pause. The operations manager knew better than to argue. He also knew better than to comply without the clarification his engineers would require.

"Ma'am, maximum sustainable rate will reduce bit life by approximately forty percent. We'd need to—"

"Replacements are aboard. Increase drill speed. I want a progress report every four hours."

"Yes, ma'am."

She released the intercom.

On the tablet, the acoustic return graph showed the new harmonic as a thin line of colour against the grey of three years' data — a thread of sound that had not been there yesterday, running through the dense silence like a voice heard through a wall.

Naamah did not smile. She had not smiled since Nimrod.

But she knew the sound of a door.

*II. WHAT THE DEAD WOMAN KNEW*

**Austin, Texas**

**McNeeve Industries Safehouse, Secure Suite**

The safe had been built into the wall of the secure suite during the facility's original construction — Cian's specification, his contractor, his combination. But the drive Miriam retrieved from it was her own, placed there eight weeks ago when she'd arrived at the safehouse and asked for somewhere that couldn't be reached by anything with a wireless signal.

The drive was small. Matte black. No label. No markings. The kind of storage device that could hold a woman's life's work in a package that weighed less than a house key.

Miriam held it for a moment before she plugged it in. Sarah, sitting at the adjacent terminal with her tablet open to a blank document, watched the younger woman's hands and said nothing.

The drive mounted. Miriam entered the first decryption key — a thirty-two-character string she had memorised twelve years ago, standing in a cold kitchen in New Mexico at three in the morning, while her mother bled quietly in the next room and spoke in the calm, unhurried voice of a woman who had known this night would come.

*You will not write this down. You will not store it electronically. You will say it back to me three times and then you will leave this house and you will not come back.*

The first layer opened.

"Shell companies," Miriam said. Her voice was steady. Professional. The voice of a GCHQ-trained intelligence operative reading an architecture she understood the way other people understood their native language. "This is the Synagogue's corporate structure. The full map."

Sarah leaned in. "How full?"

"Full." Miriam scrolled. The data populated across three monitors — a web of corporate entities spanning eleven countries, sixty-two jurisdictions, and what appeared to be every major financial centre on the planet. "My mother spent twenty years building this. Every shell company. Every beneficial owner. Every trust structure and nominee directorship and layered holding company the Synagogue used to move money, acquire assets, and maintain operational cover."

Sarah's pen was already moving. "I'm seeing — this is a complete UBO chain? Ultimate beneficial ownership? For all of them?"

"For all of them. Victoria Ashford didn't do things by halves."

"This is..." Sarah trailed off. Her legal mind was running calculations that Miriam could read in the set of her jaw. "This is the kind of map that gets people killed."

"Yes," Miriam said. "It is."

She entered the second decryption key.

The second layer opened.

This one took longer to load. The data was denser — coordinate sets, equipment manifests, shipping records, financial transfers tagged with dates that went back seven years. A procurement chain that Miriam read with the particular fluency of a woman trained in financial intelligence by the same institution that had trained her mother.

"Antarctica," Miriam said. "My mother found a drilling programme under the Ross Ice Shelf. This is everything — coordinates, funding, equipment, logistics. Seven years of active operations."

"Seven years?" Sarah said. "Who's been drilling in Antarctica for seven years without anyone noticing?"

"Someone with a procurement chain routed through the same shell company architecture in Layer One." Miriam's fingers moved across the keyboard with the controlled speed of someone who was reading faster than she was processing. "Same nominees. Same jurisdictions. Same UBO structure. The Antarctic operation and the Synagogue's corporate network are run by the same entity."

Sarah was writing. "Funding source?"

"I'll have that in—" Miriam stopped. Scrolled back. Read a line again. Read it a third time.

"Miriam?"

"The programme started seven years ago." Miriam's voice had changed — not its volume, not its steadiness, but something underneath. The foundation. A fracture line that hadn't been there a moment ago. "My mother's notation dates this discovery to... to her last year. Her last months."

She turned away from the screen. Sarah saw her hands flatten on the desk — the gesture of a woman anchoring herself to something solid because the ground had shifted.

"She found this before she died. Before she—" Miriam stopped. Breathed. "She found the drilling programme. She found the coordinates. She traced the funding chain all the way back to its origin. And then she encrypted it and gave it to me and told me to run."

The room was very still.

"She knew what was under the ice." Miriam's voice was quiet. "She didn't know what it was called. She didn't have the context — the steles, the Watchers, any of it. But she knew someone was drilling toward something in Antarctica that they'd been willing to kill to protect. And she knew it was connected to everything the Synagogue had been doing for — for centuries."

Sarah reached across and placed her hand on Miriam's arm. Not sympathy. Solidarity. The gesture of a woman who had her own dead and understood the particular gravity of inheriting their unfinished work.

"The third layer," Sarah said.

"The third layer." Miriam entered the final key.

The data loaded. And this time, both women went still.

The Paracas supply chain. Elongated cranial specimens routed through three collection sites in Peru, Brazil, and Bolivia. Anomalous DNA markers flagged in Victoria's notes with a single annotation: *not human*. The same procurement architecture. The same beneficial ownership. The same entity, routing biological specimens through the same network that was drilling toward something under the Antarctic ice.

"Genetics," Miriam said. "Someone has been collecting genetic material from Paracas-type specimens — the elongated skulls, the anomalous bone density, the non-standard DNA — and routing it through the same channels as the drilling programme."

"Why?"

"I don't know yet. But my mother—"

Miriam stopped. Pressed her hands flat on the desk again. The fracture line was wider now, visible in the set of her shoulders and the careful regulation of her breathing.

"My mother knew. She connected the drilling to the genetics. She saw the same owner behind both. And she—" Miriam's voice caught. One syllable. One break in the wall she'd spent twelve years building around the night in the kitchen, the thirty-two characters, the instruction to run.

"She died knowing," Miriam said. "She died knowing what they were doing and she couldn't stop them and the only thing she could do was give it to me and hope that someday I'd find someone who could—"

She stopped.

Sarah watched her. Said nothing.

Miriam looked at the data on her screens. Three layers. Twenty years of intelligence. A dead woman's legacy, encrypted with the precision of a woman who had known that the information would be more dangerous than the silence, and had chosen to leave it anyway.

"I need Mac Morna," Miriam said.

She said it like an operational statement. She said it the way she said everything that mattered — clipped, professional, the words precisely chosen. But the name was wrong. Not wrong — *formal*. The formal address. The distance she kept between herself and the man whose first name she could not bring herself to use when the ground was shifting.

Sarah noticed.

She noticed the way Miriam's hand was pressed flat on the desk and the way her voice had gone formal and the way she'd said *Mac Morna* instead of — instead of something else. Something closer. Something that a woman who had spent twelve years building walls would not permit herself to say in a moment when the walls were cracking.

Sarah filed it the way she filed everything. Precisely. Without judgment. In the same mental folder as the corridor observation from an hour ago — a folder that was acquiring data points faster than her legal training had prepared her to classify.

"I'll get him," Sarah said.

She stood. Walked to the intercom. Pressed the button with the steady hand of a woman who knew exactly what she had just seen and would not, under any circumstances, say a word about it.

"Mac Morna. You need to come to the secure room."

A pause. She heard the response in the quality of the silence — the immediate absence of stillness, the sound of a man already moving.

"Now."

*III. DEAD RECKONING*

Cian arrived in under two minutes. Sarah met him at the door and stepped aside. No preamble. No briefing. She simply opened the door and let him see.

Miriam was standing in front of three monitors. Her eyes were red. Her hands were steady.

On the screens: twenty years of a dead woman's intelligence work, arranged in three concentric architectures of corporate shell, Antarctic drilling programme, and genetic procurement chain, all converging on a single point.

"Show me," Cian said.

Miriam walked him through it. Layer by layer. The same briefing she'd given Sarah, but compressed — tighter, faster, the operational efficiency of a woman who had already processed the emotional weight of what she'd found and was now operating in the space beyond it. The space where decisions lived.

Cian listened the way he listened to everything: with the full attention of a man who had been gathering intelligence for twenty-six centuries and understood that the quality of a decision depended entirely on the quality of the data beneath it. He asked three questions. All of them were precise. All of them were the right questions.

Miriam answered them.

When she finished the third layer — the Paracas specimens, the genetic routing, the convergence of all three architectures on a single beneficial owner — Cian turned to Chris's stele map, still displayed on the operations room's main screen next door, visible through the open security door.

"Show me the drilling coordinates."

Miriam pulled them up. A single point on the Antarctic continent, tagged with Victoria's notation: *79°58'22"S, 81°57'18"W. Active drilling site. Seven years.*

Cian looked at the coordinates. Then he walked through the connecting door to the operations room and stood before the stele map. Twenty points of light on a world projection. He reached out and tapped the display interface — zooming, rotating, aligning.

Miriam followed him. Stood behind his right shoulder. Watched.

She had seen Cian in combat — or rather, she had seen the aftermath of Cian in combat, the particular stillness that followed when a man who had been fighting since before Rome was founded stopped moving and the world caught up. She had seen him plan, brief, assess. She had seen him in the Austrian safehouse, in the Scottish Highlands, in the wreckage of an operation that should have killed everyone involved.

She had never seen him like this.

He worked the display with the unhurried precision of a cartographer reading a map that had been written in a language only he spoke. His fingers moved through coordinate systems and projection overlays the way other people moved through their own handwriting — without conscious effort, the product of twenty-six centuries of watching the world be measured and remeasured and measured again. He wasn't calculating. He was *reading*. Reading the earth the way he read a battlefield — not as a collection of data points but as a narrative, a story written in geography and time and the decisions of beings who had been old when the continents were young.

Miriam felt something shift in her chest. Not a thought. Something underneath thought — a recognition, unwilled and unwelcome, of the scale of what she was watching. Not just competence. Not just experience. Something older than expertise. The accumulated weight of a life lived longer than any human frame was designed to carry, applied with the quiet authority of a man who had stopped counting the years because the number had stopped meaning anything.

She caught herself staring and looked away.

"There," Cian said.

He'd overlaid Victoria's drilling coordinates on the stele map. The point landed precisely — exactly, to within the margin of error of two independent data sources separated by five millennia — on a coordinate already marked with a name.

Gadreel.

Victoria Ashford's drilling coordinates and the Titan-5 acoustic data pointed to exactly the same place.

"Your mother found Gadreel's stele," Cian said. His voice was quiet. The quietness of a man processing something that confirmed a fear he'd been carrying for seven weeks. "She didn't know that's what it was. She didn't have the names, the context, the acoustic data. But she found the location. Through financial intelligence. Through following the money."

"The way I would have found it," Miriam said.

It wasn't a boast. It was recognition — the sudden, devastating understanding that her mother's methodology, her mother's tradecraft, her mother's particular genius for reading the architecture of power through the language of money — was the same methodology that had built Miriam's career. That the woman who had bled in a kitchen in New Mexico had been, in the hours before she died, doing exactly what her daughter had been trained to do. Following the same threads. Reading the same patterns. Arriving at the same conclusion.

"She was better at it than I am," Miriam said. Quietly. To no one.

Cian heard her. Said nothing. There were things that silence honoured better than words, and this was one of them.

He turned back to the display. His jaw was set in the particular way it set when operational reality was worse than the planning assumptions.

"Naamah has been drilling toward Gadreel's stele for seven years," he said. "She has coordinates. She has infrastructure. She has a seven-year head start." He looked at the acoustic signature Chris had isolated that morning — the angular, constrained spectrogram with its sub-stele harmonic. "And based on this morning's analysis, something beneath that stele has been vibrating since before the oaths were carved."

"What's beneath it?" Miriam asked.

Cian looked at her. Held her gaze. Chose his words with the care of a man who understood that what he was about to say would change the shape of the next six months of their lives.

"A prison," he said. "The oldest prison on earth. Built by an archangel to hold something that should never have existed."

"What's in it?"

"The conversation about what's in it requires context I don't have time to give you tonight. What I can tell you is that Naamah — the woman behind these shell companies, behind the drilling, behind everything your mother found — has been trying to reach that prison for four thousand years. And she's closer now than she's ever been."

Miriam processed this. Her FININT mind was already running parallel tracks — the financial architecture, the seven-year timeline, the logistics of sustaining a drilling operation in Antarctica under complete secrecy. The cost alone would be staggering. The operational security would require—

"We need intelligence before we can plan an Antarctic operation," she said. "We don't know what we'd be walking into. The site itself, the drilling infrastructure, her security posture, the geological conditions. We need data."

Cian nodded. "There's a stele we can reach first. One that would give us what we need — binding protocols, Watcher domain architecture, the acoustic specifications of the prison itself."

"Where?"

"The Levant. Shemyaza's domain." He paused. "Specifically — beneath a building that's been standing on top of it for seventeen hundred years."

Miriam's eyes narrowed. Financial intelligence operatives didn't need to be told which building in the Levant had been standing for seventeen hundred years.

"The Vatican."

"Vatican Palace. Deep structure. Likely sub-basilica level."

"You want to infiltrate the Vatican."

"I want to scan a five-thousand-year-old acoustic monument that someone built a church on top of." Cian's voice carried the faintest edge of dark humour — the particular comedy of a man who had watched empires rise and fall and build their palaces on the ruins of things they didn't understand. "The Church isn't the obstacle. The architecture is."

Miriam looked at the stele map. At the twenty points of light. At the one in the Levant, labeled *Shemyaza*, sitting precisely where the centre of Christendom had been built.

She looked at the one in Antarctica. The one her mother had found.

"Vatican first," she said.

Not a question.

"Vatican first," Cian confirmed.

The chapter was closing around them — the particular gravity of a decision that, once made, couldn't be unmade. Vatican for intelligence. Antarctica for the race. And somewhere in the Ross Sea, a drill was turning faster than it had turned yesterday, boring through ice toward a door that had been sealed since before the Flood.

Miriam turned back to her mother's data. The funding chain was still on the screen — eleven countries, fourteen shell companies, seven beneficial ownership layers. At the centre: a single name. A name that had appeared in every jurisdiction, controlled every dollar, directed every drill bit toward the ice.

A name Cian had not spoken aloud in four hundred years.

A name that confirmed what the acoustic data implied: the race to Dudael had begun years ago, and they were arriving at the starting line with the finish in sight for the other side.

"I'll build the Vatican operational profile tonight," Miriam said. "Security architecture, access points, cover identities, exfiltration routes. Give me the stele's acoustic specifications and I'll calculate the scan equipment requirements."

She said it looking at the screen, not at him. The professional distance was back — the wall rebuilt, the formal address filed, the brief unguarded moment when she'd said *I need Mac Morna* sealed behind the operational persona she wore the way other people wore armour.

But Sarah, sitting in the corner with her tablet and her notes and her growing folder of observations she hadn't yet labeled, had heard it. Had seen the crack. Had watched the wall go back up with the speed and discipline of a woman who had been practising the reconstruction for twelve years.

Sarah wrote nothing down. Some data was too sensitive even for notes.

Cian nodded. "I'll brief the team at 0600. Vatican planning starts tomorrow."

He turned to leave. At the door, he paused. Looked back — not at the screens, not at the data, but at Miriam. At the woman sitting in the light of her dead mother's intelligence, shoulders straight, hands steady, eyes red and dry, building an operational profile for the most audacious reconnaissance mission of the twenty-first century because it was the only thing she could do with the grief and the rage and the twenty years of silence that had just ended in a room underground in Texas.

He wanted to say something. The impulse arrived from a place he hadn't visited in — how long? Centuries? The impulse to offer comfort. To acknowledge what she had just carried. To speak the kind of words that a man spoke to a woman whose courage he—

He left.

Mo Chrá hummed as he walked the corridor. A tone he hadn't heard from her in a very long time. Warm. Knowing. The sound she made when she perceived something he couldn't see yet — or wouldn't.

"Not now," he muttered.

The sword hummed louder.
