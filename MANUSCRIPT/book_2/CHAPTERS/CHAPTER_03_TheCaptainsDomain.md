# CHAPTER 3: THE CAPTAIN'S DOMAIN

✦

*I. THE OBELISK*

**Vatican City**

**St Peter's Square**

**0214 Hours**

The obelisk had been standing in the wrong place for four hundred and thirty-nine years.

Cian looked up at it from the colonnade's shadow — twenty-five metres of red granite quarried in Heliopolis, shipped to Rome by Caligula, relocated to the centre of St Peter's Square in 1586 by a Pope who thought he was building a monument to God. He wasn't. He was building a marker. A signpost. The kind of navigational aid that a man who had been alive when the stone was first cut could appreciate in its full, unintended irony.

Shemyaza's stele was directly below.

Not below the obelisk — the obelisk's position was forty-three metres northwest of the actual location. The geological surveys Miriam had compiled confirmed what Cian's own cartographic memory had suggested: the stele sat beneath the Basilica's foundations, roughly under the junction of the nave and the north transept, at a depth that put it well below the Vatican Grottoes, below the Scavi excavations, below everything the Church had ever found reason to dig.

Miriam's operational profile had been characteristically thorough. Sixty-seven pages. Cover identity, access routes, security rotations, exfiltration options, and a section titled *Architectural Anomalies in Sub-Basilica Survey Data* that had made Chris swear quietly when he'd read the acoustic analysis.

Something was humming beneath the Vatican. The seismic data said geology. Miriam's analysis said the geology was lying.

Mo Chrá hummed against his spine. Low. Expectant. The hum of a blade who had been listening to Watcher-era frequencies for five thousand years and could hear one through nine hundred metres of Roman concrete the way a conductor hears an oboe tuning in the next room.

*She knows we're here.*

Not Mo Chrá. The stele.

"Yes," Cian murmured. "I know."

**"THE HOUR IS FAVOURABLE,"** Liaigh said. His voice arrived the way it always arrived — as pressure, as warmth, as the sense of standing beside a furnace that burned with something cleaner than fire. **"THE LITURGICAL CALENDAR HATH DELIVERED THEE A MERCY. THE MORNING TAMID IS SEVEN HOURS DISTANT. I AM UNCONSTRAINED UNTIL THEN."**

"Seven hours. Should be enough."

**"IT SHOULD BE. IT MAY NOT BE. THE CAPTAIN'S DOMAIN WAS NOT BUILT FOR EASE OF ACCESS, AND I CANNOT TELL THEE WHAT GUARDETH IT NOW. MY SIGHT REACHETH THE STELE BUT NOT WHAT LIETH BETWEEN THEE AND THE STELE."**

"You can see the stele but not the route."

**"THE STELE IS AN ACOUSTIC BEACON. IT SINGETH TO HEAVEN WHETHER HEAVEN WISHETH TO HEAR IT OR NOT. THE SPACE SURROUNDING IT—"** A pause. The particular pause of a celestial being selecting his next word the way a bomb disposal technician selects his next wire. **"—IS OCCLUDED. SOMETHING BETWEEN THEE AND THE STELE EATETH MY SIGHT."**

"Wards?"

**"ALMOST CERTAINLY. WATCHER-ERA. SHEMYAZA WAS THE CAPTAIN. HIS DOMAIN WOULD HAVE BEEN THE MOST HEAVILY PROTECTED OF THE TWENTY."**

Mo Chrá's hum shifted — a semitone up, sharpening. She had opinions about Watcher wards. Those opinions, based on extensive empirical evidence gathered across twenty-six centuries, could be summarised as: *try me*.

"Noted," Cian said. To both of them.

He moved.

✦

The access point was a maintenance shaft behind the Vatican's electrical substation — a service tunnel that fed power to the Basilica's climate control systems, which Miriam had identified through a 2019 engineering report that someone in the Vatican's infrastructure department had been careless enough to upload to a contractor's shared drive. The contractor had gone bankrupt. The shared drive had been acquired in the liquidation by a data recovery firm in Milan. Miriam had purchased the firm's archive index seventeen hours ago.

"Your mother would be proud of that procurement chain," Cian had told her during the briefing.

Miriam hadn't smiled. But something behind her eyes had softened for precisely one-quarter of a second before the operational persona reassembled itself.

The lock on the maintenance hatch was a Kaba 20 — good enough to discourage a casual intruder, entirely inadequate against a man who had been picking locks since before the concept of a lock had evolved past wooden crossbars. He had it open in fourteen seconds. Mo Chrá could have done it faster — a focused creation frequency applied to the tumbler springs would have aligned them in under two seconds — but stealth meant silence, and Mo Chrá's shortcuts tended to produce harmonics that carried through stone.

The shaft descended at twenty-two degrees. Modern poured concrete for the first thirty metres — smooth, cold, smelling of electrical insulation and the particular mineral staleness of underground Rome. Cian moved through it the way he moved through all confined spaces: weight distributed across three points of contact, breathing controlled to four-count cycles, Mo Chrá's scabbard angled to prevent contact with the walls.

He was six metres into the descent when Mo Chrá's hum changed.

Not louder. *Older*. A shift in the quality of the vibration — the difference between humming a song you know and humming a song you *remember*. The Cydonian ore in her blade was responding to something below. Resonating. The way a tuning fork vibrates when you strike one beside it that shares its frequency.

There was Watcher-era material ahead.

"How far?" he whispered.

Mo Chrá pulsed twice. Her spatial sense, honed over five millennia of proximity to Watcher construction, could estimate distance through acoustic return the way a dolphin read its environment through sonar. Two pulses: between forty and sixty metres. Below and slightly south.

**"THE CONCRETE ENDETH IN APPROXIMATELY TWELVE METRES,"** Liaigh added. **"BENEATH IT, I DETECT OLDER CONSTRUCTION. ROMAN. AND BENEATH THAT—"**

"Something that was here before Rome."

**"SOMETHING THAT WAS HERE BEFORE ROME WAS A THOUGHT IN THE MIND OF A WOLF."**

*II. THE LAYERS*

The concrete ended at thirty-one metres.

What replaced it was travertine — Roman quarry stone, laid in the precise, modular blocks that Cian recognised as first-century construction. Flavian era, possibly Neronian. This deep beneath the Basilica, the architecture predated Constantine's original church by three centuries. He was passing through the foundations of something that had existed on this site before Christianity had a building to call its own.

The air changed. Warmer, wetter. The particular humidity of underground Rome, where ancient aqueducts and drainage systems had been sweating moisture into the substrate for two millennia. The scent of old stone — not the clean mineral smell of modern concrete but the dense, organic, almost biological odour of limestone that had been breathing in darkness since before the first Bishop of Rome had claimed the title.

And beneath the limestone smell: incense. Faint. Trapped. The ghost of frankincense laid down in layers over centuries, molecules embedded in porous stone the way memories embedded in ageing minds — faded but ineradicable.

Cian found the transition point seven metres past the Roman travertine. A vertical shaft — not modern, not Roman. *Old*. Carved from living rock with tools that had left marks in the stone that no chisel he'd ever seen had produced. The cut faces were too smooth. The angles too precise. The geometry of the shaft was not square but hexagonal, and the proportions — he measured them against his own body as he descended, a habit born of twenty-six centuries of entering places built by things that didn't think in human ratios — followed a progression he recognised.

Sacred geometry. The proportional language of beings who had been present when the mathematics of creation were first spoken.

Mo Chrá's hum deepened. The Cydonian ore in her blade was singing to the stone, and the stone was singing back.

The temperature dropped.

Not gradually. A boundary — a line in the air, invisible but absolute, like stepping through a curtain made of cold. On one side: Roman groundwater warmth. On the other: something else. A cold that wasn't meteorological. A cold that had *intent*. The particular chill that Cian had felt in every Watcher-era installation he'd ever entered — the ambient temperature of spaces designed by beings who had been accustomed to the cold of interstellar vacuum and had never quite adjusted their thermostats to human comfort.

He was inside Shemyaza's domain.

The hexagonal shaft opened into a chamber.

Cian stopped. Steadied his breathing. Let his eyes adjust.

The chamber was roughly twenty metres across and eight metres high. The walls were Cydonian metamaterial — the dark, light-absorbing stone that the Watchers had used to mark their domains, carved with the geometric glyphs that served as their written language. The glyphs did not glow. They did something worse. They existed at the edge of perception — visible only in peripheral vision, vanishing when looked at directly, as though the script had been designed to communicate with parts of the brain that conscious attention couldn't reach.

The floor was obsidian. Not volcanic glass — manufactured. Cydonian metamaterial polished to a molecular smoothness that reflected nothing. Looking down was like looking into a hole in the world.

And the chamber was not empty.

*III. THE BOUND*

They stood in the walls.

Not in front of the walls. Not leaning against them. *In* them. Recessed into the Cydonian metamaterial like statues in cathedral niches — but these were not statues, and the niches had not been carved for decoration.

Seven figures. Each occupying an alcove in the hexagonal chamber's perimeter, evenly spaced at approximately 2.5-metre intervals. Taller than human — the shortest was seven feet, the tallest closer to nine. Their silhouettes were wrong. The proportions that evolution had spent millions of years calibrating in the human form — the ratio of limb to torso, the size of the cranium relative to the body, the placement of joints — were *off*. Not grotesquely. Subtly. The way a word misspelled by one letter is harder to catch than a word misspelled by five.

They were wearing armour. Or the armour was wearing them. The distinction was unclear. The material — dark, iridescent, shifting in colour like oil on water when the light caught it wrong — appeared to have grown from their skin rather than been placed on it. Chitinous plates following the musculature beneath, articulated at joints that moved in ways human joints did not, the whole assemblage breathing with a slow, tidal rhythm that suggested the armour was alive, or at minimum, aware.

Beneath the armour, visible at the throat and wrists where the plates did not quite meet: skin. Pale. Faintly luminous. And threaded through the luminous skin, like veins carrying light instead of blood, sigils. Geometric. Precise. The same script that covered the walls, but written in the bodies of the beings who stood in them — glyphs that pulsed with a rhythm that matched the barely audible hum of the stele somewhere below.

They were not statues. They were sentries.

Mo Chrá went silent.

The sudden absence of her hum was more alarming than any frequency she'd ever produced. The sword had been humming continuously since the descent began. Her silence was a tactical communication: *I see them. They have not heard me. If I make one more sound, they will.*

Cian pressed himself flat against the shaft opening. Controlled his breathing to near-zero displacement. Assessed.

**"SHEDIM,"** Liaigh said, and his voice was barely pressure — the quietest Cian had ever heard him, the empyreal register compressed to a whisper that arrived more as temperature change than sound. **"NEPHILIM SPIRITS. SHEMYAZA'S BLOODLINE. BOUND TO THIS SITE BY THE OATH ITSELF — THEY CANNOT LEAVE, AND THEY CANNOT DISOBEY THE PROTOCOLS THAT WERE SET WHEN THE DOMAIN WAS CONSECRATED."**

*Consecrated* was a generous word for what the Watchers had done to their domains. *Contaminated* was closer to the truth. But Cian understood the implication: these were not freelance demons squatting in an abandoned ruin. These were oath-bound guards, operating under a standing order that had been in effect for five thousand years. They had a mission. They had protocols. They had a chain of command — even if the commanding officer was currently sealed in the Abyss.

"Movement pattern?" Cian breathed.

**"SLOW ROTATION. THEY CYCLE THROUGH THE ALCOVES ON A RHYTHM THAT MATCHETH THE STELE'S HARMONIC PULSE. WHEN THE PULSE CRESTETH, THEY SHIFT."**

"How long between pulses?"

**"NINETY-THREE SECONDS. BUT HEED THIS: THEIR SENSES ARE NOT SIGHT AND SOUND AS THOU KNOWEST THEM. THEY READ ACOUSTIC DISPLACEMENT. EVERY FOOTFALL, EVERY BREATH, EVERY HEARTBEAT ALTERETH THE ACOUSTIC PROFILE OF THE CHAMBER. THEY DO NOT SEE THEE. THEY** ***HEAR*** **THY EXISTENCE."**

Cian looked at the obsidian floor. Twenty metres of open space between him and the far wall, where a passage appeared to descend further. Seven shedim guards reading acoustic displacement with senses evolved — *designed* — for exactly this purpose.

He had ninety-three seconds between rotations. The chamber was twenty metres wide. The guards could hear his heartbeat.

"Wonderful," he murmured.

Mo Chrá offered no opinion. She was, for the first time since the Epilogue, genuinely silent. Not withholding — *concentrating*. The new harmonic registers she'd acquired were doing something Cian could feel through the hilt as a faint internal vibration, like the sword was running calculations.

**"SHE IS CALIBRATING,"** Liaigh said. **"THE BINDING FREQUENCY. SHE HATH NEVER USED IT IN THE FIELD. SHE PREPARETH."**

"Is she ready?"

A pause. A hum — barely there, but warm. The acoustic equivalent of a raised eyebrow and the words: *I was built by a prophet who saw this exact moment five thousand years ago. Am I ready. Honestly.*

"Right. Stupid question."

*IV. THE CROSSING*

The pulse crested. Seven shedim shifted simultaneously — a movement that was not walking but *translation*, a lateral displacement from one alcove to the next that happened without the intermediate steps that physics normally required. One moment they occupied positions one through seven. The next: positions two through eight. The eighth alcove, previously empty, was now occupied. The first was vacant.

Ninety-three seconds.

Cian moved.

He crossed the obsidian floor in a technique he had perfected in the thirteenth century while infiltrating a Mamluk fortress in Cairo: the *siúl cúin* — the quiet walk. Not the absence of sound but the management of sound. Each footfall placed with the ball of the foot absorbing impact before the heel descended, weight transferred through the kinetic chain in a sequence that distributed acoustic energy across the widest possible surface area, minimising the peak amplitude of any single step.

The shedim did not react.

He reached the centre of the chamber in twenty-two seconds. Mo Chrá was managing her own acoustic profile — dampening the natural resonance of Cydonian ore against the obsidian floor by producing a counter-frequency so precise that the two cancelled to silence. The technical term for what she was doing was active noise cancellation. The practical term was *holding her breath*.

At the centre of the chamber, directly beneath the apex of the hexagonal ceiling, Cian paused. Not by choice. By necessity.

There was a ward on the floor.

He couldn't see it. He could *feel* it — through Mo Chrá, who had gone rigid in her scabbard, every harmonic locked in a posture of extreme attention. The ward was embedded in the obsidian, invisible to human eyes, but Mo Chrá's Cydonian ore could read the frequency signature the way a dog reads scent. Watcher-era. Complex. Multi-layered.

An alarm glyph.

If he stepped on it, every shedim in the domain would know.

**"TWO METRES AHEAD,"** Liaigh said. **"CIRCULAR. APPROXIMATELY ONE POINT FOUR METRES IN DIAMETER. THE GLYPH IS KEYED TO ACOUSTIC DISPLACEMENT ABOVE A THRESHOLD OF—"**

Cian didn't need the number. Mo Chrá was already providing it — a specific frequency pulsed through the hilt, calibrated to the ward's activation threshold. She was telling him exactly how quietly he needed to move, expressed in the language of vibration that twenty-six centuries of partnership had made more precise than words.

The answer was: quieter than his heartbeat.

His heartbeat was currently fifty-eight beats per minute. The resting rate of a man whose cardiovascular system had been optimised by Methuselah's Sword for twenty-six centuries of sustained combat readiness. Fifty-eight beats per minute produced a mechanical vibration in the chest cavity that propagated through the skeleton at approximately 0.8 newtons of force.

The ward's threshold was below that.

"I can't cross it on foot," he breathed. "My heart's too loud."

There was a moment of silence. The kind of silence in which an angel and a sword both processed the same tactical problem and arrived at the same solution at exactly the same time, and neither of them wanted to be the one to say it.

Mo Chrá said it first. A hum — tentative, questioning. The acoustic equivalent of: *So... about that new healing frequency...*

**"SHE PROPOSETH,"** Liaigh said, with the particular careful neutrality of a being who was about to describe something theologically permissible but tactically absurd, **"THAT SHE TEMPORARILY SUPPRESS THY HEARTBEAT."**

"She proposes to *stop my heart*."

**"BRIEFLY. THE RESTORATION FREQUENCY CAN SUSPEND CARDIAC FUNCTION AND RESTART IT WITH NO TISSUE DAMAGE. THE PRINCIPLE IS SOUND. THE EXECUTION HATH NEVER BEEN—"**

"Tested."

**"—ATTEMPTED."**

"On a living person."

**"ON ANY PERSON. THE CAPABILITY WAS ACTIVATED SIX WEEKS AGO. SHE IS CONFIDENT."**

Mo Chrá hummed. The hum was indeed confident. It was the hum of a five-thousand-year-old sentient weapon who had just identified an elegant solution to a complex problem and was entirely unbothered by the fact that the solution involved temporarily killing her wielder.

Cian looked at the ward he couldn't see. At the shedim he could feel cycling through their positions. At the passage on the far side of the chamber that led down to the stele — and the intelligence he needed to plan the Dudael operation.

"How long?"

**"THE WARD IS ONE POINT FOUR METRES. AT THY CURRENT STRIDE LENGTH AND THE REQUIRED ACOUSTIC PROFILE: FOUR SECONDS OF CARDIAC ARREST."**

"Four seconds."

**"THREE POINT SEVEN, IF THOU ART PRECISE."**

"And if she can't restart it?"

**"THEN I SHALL HEAL THEE. BUT THE HEALING WILL NOT BE SILENT, AND EVERY SHEDIM IN THIS DOMAIN WILL KNOW PRECISELY WHERE THOU ART."**

Cian weighed four seconds of clinical death against the alternative — fighting seven oath-bound Nephilim spirits in a hexagonal room made of Watcher-era metamaterial that would amplify their home-field advantage into something approaching mathematical certainty.

"Do it," he said.

Mo Chrá didn't hesitate.

The restoration frequency — her upper register, the harmonic that tasted of sunlight and clean water — inverted. Reversed polarity. The same authority that could speak damaged creation into wholeness could, it turned out, speak a functioning heart into temporary silence with the delicacy of a conductor bringing an orchestra to a fermata. Cian felt his pulse stutter, skip, and stop.

He moved.

The world without a heartbeat was a strange place. Quieter. The constant percussion that had accompanied every second of his existence for twenty-six centuries — the steady, rhythmic thrum that was so fundamental to the experience of being alive that consciousness didn't even register it as sound — was gone. In its absence, everything else amplified. The hum of the stele below. The sub-audible pulse of the shedim's rotation cycle. The faint, impossibly distant sound of Gregorian chanting from the Basilica above, where a dawn choir was rehearsing for a mass that was still hours away, their voices threading through a kilometre of stone as ghosts of melody.

One step. Two. Three.

His vision was narrowing. Four seconds of cardiac arrest was survivable. Five was concerning. Six was the boundary where the brain began to express displeasure through the medium of unconsciousness.

Four steps.

He was past the ward.

Mo Chrá restarted his heart.

The sensation was — *extraordinary* was the wrong word. *Unpleasant* was insufficient. *Like being kicked in the chest by a horse made of lightning* was closer but still inadequate. The creation frequency reversed again, and the organ that Methuselah's Sword had been maintaining in peak condition for twenty-six centuries received a restart command that went through it like a defibrillator designed by God.

His heart hammered back to life. His vision whited. His knees buckled and he caught himself on the wall of the passage beyond the chamber, breathing in harsh, controlled gasps that he throttled to silence through sheer force of will.

Mo Chrá hummed. Smug. Satisfied. The hum of a sword who had just performed a cardiac arrest and resuscitation on a moving target in under four seconds and wanted full credit.

"We are going to discuss this later," Cian breathed.

*Later is fine. I'm flexible.*

**"THE GUARDS HAVE NOT REACTED,"** Liaigh said. **"THY PASSAGE WAS BELOW THRESHOLD. WELL DONE."**

"I was *dead*."

**"FOR THREE POINT NINE SECONDS. WELL WITHIN ACCEPTABLE PARAMETERS."**

"Whose parameters?"

**"MINE."**

Cian pressed his back against the ancient stone and waited for his hands to stop shaking. They stopped in four seconds — Methuselah's Sword metabolised adrenaline with the efficiency of a chemical processing plant, which was useful in combat and extremely annoying when a man wanted to feel the perfectly reasonable physiological consequences of having his heart stopped by his own weapon.

The passage descended.

He followed it.

*V. THE CAPTAIN'S STELE*

The stele was singing.

Not the way the Mars steles had sung — those had been recordings, acoustic memories replaying in response to Titan-5's approach. This was different. This was a stele that had been singing continuously for five thousand years, its voice sustained by the acoustic properties of Cydonian metamaterial that didn't decay, didn't fade, didn't forget. The sound it produced was below human hearing — a frequency so deep that it existed more as vibration in the bones than as sound in the ears. But Mo Chrá could hear it. And through Mo Chrá, so could Cian.

*Shemyaza.*

The name was in the song. Woven through it. Not spoken but *encoded* — a frequency signature that carried identity the way DNA carried biological instruction. This was the Captain's marker. His claim. His domain, proclaimed in the language of creation and sustained by the technology the Watchers had stolen from Heaven.

The chamber that held the stele was vast.

Cian stood at the entrance and felt the scale of it settle into his bones the way altitude settles into a climber's lungs — gradually, oppressively, with the dawning recognition that the space he had entered was not built for beings his size. The ceiling was thirty metres above. The walls curved in a parabolic arc that followed acoustic principles Cian recognised from Watcher-era construction — every surface calculated to focus sound toward the stele at the centre, creating an amphitheatre of resonance where the Captain's voice could fill the space with the authority of a being who had once commanded a third of the Watchers in all of creation.

The stele itself was twelve metres tall.

Black. Cydonian metamaterial, polished to the same molecular smoothness as the obsidian floor above. Covered — *covered* — in script. The geometric language of the Watchers carved into every surface in lines so dense and so precise that the stone appeared to be made of text. And the text was not static. The glyphs shifted at the edge of perception — not physically moving but acoustically cycling, each line of script producing a frequency that interacted with the lines above and below it in a harmonic cascade that made the entire surface of the stele into a single, continuous, impossibly complex musical score.

And at the base of the stele, carved deeper than the rest, in characters that Cian's five-thousand-year-old cartographic training identified instantly:

*Coordinates. Watcher domain boundaries. The acoustic architecture of twenty territories, laid out with the precision of a celestial surveyor who had mapped the earth before humanity had invented the concept of property.*

Binding protocols.

"There," Cian said. "The lower register. Can you read it?"

Mo Chrá was already reading it.

Her creation frequencies — the harmonics that the Watchers had lost and Enoch had preserved — reached out to the stele's surface and began to interact with the recorded data. Not copying. *Conversing*. The sword's frequencies and the stele's frequencies meeting in the space between metal and stone, exchanging information in a language older than language, the acoustic equivalent of two databases synchronising across a five-thousand-year air gap.

The scan would take time.

"How long?" Cian asked.

Mo Chrá pulsed a rhythm. Twelve minutes. The data was dense, and she was being thorough — not just reading the binding protocols but also extracting the harmonic architecture of each domain, the acoustic specifications of the binding sites, the frequency keys that the Watchers had used to lock and unlock their installations.

Including Dudael.

Twelve minutes. In a room the size of a cathedral, directly beneath the Vatican, surrounded by oath-bound shedim, with a five-thousand-year-old alarm system between him and the exit.

"I've been in worse positions."

**"NAME ONE."**

Cian opened his mouth. Closed it. Thought about it.

"Carthage. 146 BCE."

**"CARTHAGE WAS THY FAULT."**

"Everything's my fault according to you."

**"THAT IS BECAUSE MOST THINGS ARE. BEGIN THY VIGIL. I SHALL MONITOR THE GUARD ROTATION FROM—"** Liaigh paused. The pause contained information. **"THE UPPER CHAMBER'S ACOUSTIC ENVIRONMENT IS CHANGING."**

"Changing how?"

**"AN EIGHTH SHEDIM HATH MANIFESTED. LARGER THAN THE OTHERS. THE PULSE FREQUENCY HATH SHIFTED TO ACCOMMODATE ITS PRESENCE."**

"A commander."

**"OR THE EQUIVALENT. ITS ACOUSTIC SIGNATURE IS... DENSER. OLDER. THIS IS NOT A FOOT SOLDIER, WARRIOR. THIS IS SOMETHING THAT REMEMBERETH SHEMYAZA."**

Mo Chrá's scanning frequency didn't waver. She was six minutes into a twelve-minute extraction and she was not going to stop because the tactical situation had escalated. This was, in her five-thousand-year-old estimation, a staffing problem, and staffing problems were Cian's department.

Cian moved to the chamber's entrance and looked up the passage toward the hexagonal room above. He couldn't see anything. But he could feel it — through the floor, through the walls, through the Cydonian metamaterial that carried vibration the way copper carried current. A new presence. Heavier. More complex. The acoustic weight of a shedim that had been accumulating power for millennia, fed by the stele's harmonic output, sustained in a state of semi-manifestation that was closer to physical reality than any demon had a right to be.

"Mo Chrá. Time."

Five pulses. Five minutes remaining.

He drew the sword.

The sound she made when she cleared the scabbard was different now. Pre-Epilogue, her unsheathing had been a clean, bright tone — a single note of creation frequency that announced her presence the way a lion's roar announced territorial dominion. Post-Epilogue, the note was richer. Layered. The original creation tone was still there, still bright, still carrying the authority of the Word. But beneath it, in her new lower register: the binding frequency. Dark. Heavy. The sound of chains that had been forged in heaven and had been waiting five millennia for someone to use them.

The shedim in the chamber above heard her.

All of them. Simultaneously. Including the commander.

The rotation pattern broke.

*VI. THE TAKING OF PRISONERS*

They came down the passage in a formation that Cian had never seen a shedim use — because he had never encountered shedim in their home territory, on ground they had defended for five thousand years, near a stele whose acoustic output gave them more substance than they could achieve anywhere else on earth.

The first one materialised three metres from the passage entrance. Not walked. *Materialised* — acoustic displacement condensing into form the way fog condensed into rain, the chamber's resonance providing enough energy for the spirit to construct something approaching a physical presence. It was eight feet tall. The elongated silhouette that Liaigh had described was worse in person — the proportions just *wrong* enough to trigger every predator-detection algorithm that evolution had wired into the human nervous system. The bioluminescent sigils under its skin pulsed in time with the stele's harmonic, and the grown-not-forged armour articulated as it moved with the organic, segmented precision of a centipede.

It saw Cian. Or — more accurately — it *heard* him. Its head tilted at an angle that human necks couldn't achieve, and the sigils across its throat flared.

It spoke.

The language was pre-Flood. Cian recognised fragments — his Hebrew was excellent and his Sumerian was functional — but this was older than both. This was the language the Nephilim had spoken before Babel, the tongue their Watcher fathers had used in their homes, and it carried acoustic weight that made Cian's teeth ache.

He didn't need to translate. The tone was universal: *Intruder. Kill.*

The second shedim materialised behind the first. The third flanked left. They were forming a stack — a tactical entry formation. Coordinated. Intelligent. These were not the feral, disorganised spirits Cian had been fighting for twenty-six centuries. These were soldiers, and the standing order they were executing had been written by the being who had commanded the descent from Heaven.

"Three in the passage," Cian said. "Mo Chrá — how many behind them?"

Four pulses. Four more materialising in the hexagonal chamber. And behind them, slower, heavier, displacing more acoustic energy than the rest combined: the commander.

"Eight total. One heavy."

**"THE COMMANDER IS PROBLEMATIC,"** Liaigh said, with the precise understatement of a being who had been observing warfare since before Cian's species evolved the capacity for it. **"THE OTHERS ARE SOLDIERS. THE COMMANDER IS A PRIEST. NEPHILIM PRIESTLY CASTE — AN APKALLU-CLASS SPIRIT WITH FIVE MILLENNIA OF ACCUMULATED POWER FROM PROXIMITY TO THE STELE."**

"Can I fight it?"

**"THOU CANST FIGHT ANYTHING, WARRIOR. THE QUESTION IS WHETHER THE ENGAGEMENT WILL PERMIT THEE TO COMPLETE THE SCAN AND DEPART. IF THE COMMANDER ENGAGETH THEE DIRECTLY, THE ACOUSTIC OUTPUT WILL DESTABILISE THE EXTRACTION MO CHRÁ IS CONDUCTING."**

Three minutes. Mo Chrá needed three more minutes.

"Delay. Contain. Don't destabilise the scan."

**"CORRECT."**

The first shedim charged.

It moved the way Liaigh had warned — too smooth, too silent. No footfall. No battle cry. It flowed across the passage floor like dark water running uphill, its armoured limbs reconfiguring mid-stride as it transitioned from bipedal to something that used all six of the appendages visible beneath the chitinous plating. The sigils along its body blazed. Its mouth opened, and from that mouth came a sound — a Watcher-era war frequency, a combat harmonic that the Nephilim had used against each other in the antediluvian conflicts that had torn the old world apart before the Flood finished the job.

Mo Chrá *sang*.

Her creation frequency met the war harmonic in the passage, and the collision was — if you were the kind of person who enjoyed watching fundamental forces of reality argue about who had right of way — spectacular. The Nephilim's frequency, encoded in its spirit for five thousand years, encountered a harmonic that predated it by the entire span of creation. The war-song met the Word. And the Word did not compromise.

The creation frequency shattered the combat harmonic like glass.

The shedim staggered. Its manifestation flickered — the semi-physical form destabilising as the acoustic energy that sustained it was disrupted. For half a second, it was less a being and more a smear of malevolent intention, a spiritual pressure without shape.

Cian stepped forward and cut.

Mo Chrá's edge passed through the flickering form, and the binding frequency — her new lower register, the dark authority of holy chains — activated on contact. The creation frequency in the blade didn't destroy the shedim. It *caught* it.

The spirit screamed. A sound no human throat could produce — the shriek of a being that had existed for five millennia in a state of terrible freedom and was suddenly, violently, *not free*. Acoustic chains — visible only to Mo Chrá's perception and, through her, to Cian — wrapped the shedim's spirit in a frequency lattice that held it the way a diamond held carbon. Compressed. Immobilised. Unable to flee, unable to fight, unable to do anything except exist in the grip of a harmonic that was older than its father.

The first prisoner Mo Chrá had ever taken hung suspended in the air, wrapped in chains of sound, and the look on its face — if you could call the expression on the face of a semi-manifested Nephilim spirit a *look* — was pure, incandescent shock.

*Got you*, Mo Chrá hummed. The satisfaction in the vibration was unseemly.

"Hold it. Don't interrogate — there's no time. Just *hold it*."

The second shedim had watched the first get bound. It had also watched the creation frequency shatter a combat harmonic that had been reliable for five thousand years. The intelligence that Shemyaza had bred into his children expressed itself immediately: it didn't charge. It retreated.

The third followed.

And the commander stepped into the passage.

*VII. THE PRIEST*

The Apkallu-class shedim filled the passage the way a river fills a channel — not by occupying space but by becoming indistinguishable from it. Where the soldier-class spirits had been eight feet tall with wrong proportions and grown armour, the commander was something else. Something that had been feeding on the stele's acoustic output for five thousand years and had used that energy to become more *real* than any spirit Cian had encountered.

It was ten feet tall. Its manifestation was nearly solid — the translucent quality of the soldiers replaced by an opacity that suggested actual physical substance, skin and bone and muscle assembled from acoustic energy into something that could, if it chose, pick up a stone and throw it. The sigils under its skin were not faint but brilliant — geometric patterns of light that moved across its body in waves, each one carrying a data-load of Watcher-era script that told the story of its life, its lineage, its five-thousand-year vigil.

It wore no armour. It didn't need armour. Its presence was its armour. The acoustic pressure it exerted as it entered the passage pushed against Cian's chest like a physical weight — a spiritual gravity that had nothing to do with mass and everything to do with five millennia of accumulated malice, compressed into a form that had once been a giant, that had drowned when God broke the world, and that had spent every moment since planning what it would do when the chains came off.

It looked at Cian. And it smiled.

The smile was worse than the war-frequency. It was *human*. It was the smile of a being that had been watching humanity for five thousand years and had learned, with terrible precision, how to mimic the facial expressions that caused the most disquiet.

It spoke. This time, Cian understood — not the words, which were still in the antediluvian tongue, but the meaning, which Mo Chrá translated through vibration: *The Irishman. My father spoke of you.*

"Everyone's father spoke of me," Cian said. "It's a character flaw of the family."

The commander tilted its head. The gesture was curious. Almost amused. *You carry a blade that sings the old song. But you are just a man. You are mortal. Breakable. One heart. One spine.*

"That's what the last one said. Ask him how it worked out." Cian shifted his grip on Mo Chrá. "Actually, don't ask. He's a bit preoccupied."

The bound shedim, still hanging in chains of sound behind him, made a noise that might have been agreement.

The commander's smile widened.

Then it sang.

Not spoke. *Sang*. A frequency that Cian recognised with a jolt of ancestral terror — the *binding song*. The same harmonic structure that Armaros had used to propose the Cydonian oath, repurposed as a weapon. A frequency designed not to kill but to *bind* — to freeze, to root, to lock a living being in place the way the Watchers themselves had been locked in the Abyss.

The binding song hit Cian like a wall of pressurised air.

His legs locked. His arms froze. The ancient acoustic weapon found every joint, every tendon, every muscle fibre and spoke to them in the language of paralysis — *stop, hold, be still*. It was a master-class in the Watcher technology of constraint, executed by a spirit who had learned it from the source.

For one second, Cian couldn't move.

Mo Chrá could.

The sword's response was instantaneous. The creation frequency — the harmonic that predated the binding song by the entire span of existence — detonated through the binding like sunlight through shadow. Not cancelled. *Denied*. The creation frequency did not argue with the binding song. It simply asserted a prior claim on reality, and reality agreed.

The paralysis shattered.

Cian moved in the same instant — forward, fast, closing the distance before the commander could sing again. Mo Chrá was already cycling to her lower register, the binding chains building in her bass frequencies, preparing to do to the Apkallu-class spirit what she had done to the soldier.

But the commander was faster.

It dissolved. Not retreated — *dissolved*. Its manifestation collapsed into pure acoustic energy and flowed through the passage walls like water through limestone, reforming behind Cian in the stele chamber. Between him and the stele. Between him and the data Mo Chrá was still extracting.

*Two minutes*, Mo Chrá pulsed. *I need two more minutes.*

The commander was standing beside the stele. Its hand — nearly physical, nearly real — touched the surface. And the stele's output *surged*. The harmonic frequency that had been sustaining the shedim's manifestation amplified, and the commander's form solidified further. It was drawing power directly from the stele. Feeding. Growing more real, more physical, more capable with every second of contact.

"It's using the stele as a battery," Cian said.

**"CORRECT. AND THE INCREASED OUTPUT WILL ALSO STRENGTHEN THE SOLDIERS. THEY WILL RE-ENGAGE WITHIN APPROXIMATELY—"**

Behind him, in the passage, the sound of five shedim re-manifesting.

"Now. They'll re-engage now."

**"—NOW. YES."**

*VIII. THE SCAN*

The next ninety-seven seconds were the most demanding of Cian mac Morna's twenty-six centuries of continuous combat.

He fought the way Methuselah's Sword had trained him to fight — not with the explosive, decisive strikes of a warrior seeking to end an engagement quickly, but with the precise, economical, *sustaining* efficiency of a man who needed to be alive and in this room for two more minutes, no matter what came through that passage.

The five soldier-class shedim came in pairs. Rotating. The same tactical discipline they had used in the guard rotation — adapted to combat, applied to Cian's position like a siege protocol. Two engaged while three regrouped. When the two were driven back by Mo Chrá's creation frequencies, a fresh pair stepped in. The rotation was designed to exhaust. To erode. To reduce a single defender's capacity through attrition until the inevitable arithmetic of one-against-many achieved its conclusion.

Cian didn't let it.

Twenty-six centuries of combat experience compressed into a single tactical mode: *hold this ground*. He planted himself at the passage entrance — the chokepoint, the only geometry that favoured a lone defender — and Mo Chrá became a wall. Her creation frequencies swept the passage in arcs calculated to deny the shedim's approach without overcommitting to any single attacker. The binding chains in her lower register caught two more soldiers in succession — wrapping them in acoustic shackles that pulled them from the fight and suspended them in harmonic lattices that they would not escape.

Three down. Four remaining, plus the commander.

And the commander was still touching the stele.

Mo Chrá pulsed. *Ninety seconds.*

The stele's output climbed. The remaining soldiers' manifestations were thickening — their forms gaining the near-opacity of the commander, their armour hardening, their war-frequencies growing more powerful as the stele fed them. The commander was deliberately sacrificing tactical engagement to amplify its subordinates. It was, Cian realised with the reluctant appreciation of a professional assessing another professional's strategy, playing general.

"Liaigh. Can you do anything about the stele's output?"

**"I CANNOT INTERVENE IN THE COMBAT. THOU KNOWEST THIS."**

"I'm not asking you to fight. I'm asking if you can dampen the stele's output. It's not a shedim. It's a *rock*."

A pause. The pause of a celestial being re-examining the rules.

**"THE STELE IS AN ACOUSTIC DEVICE. NOT A COMBATANT. NOT A LIVING BEING."** Another pause. **"DAMPENING AN INANIMATE ACOUSTIC SOURCE DOTH NOT VIOLATE THE BAN."**

"Then *dampen it*."

The pressure in the chamber changed.

Liaigh's intervention was not visible. It was not dramatic. It was the angelic equivalent of placing a hand over a speaker — a targeted, precise suppression of the stele's output that reduced its harmonic amplitude by perhaps forty percent. Not enough to silence it. Not enough to interrupt Mo Chrá's scan. But enough to cut the power supply that was feeding the shedim's manifestations.

The effect was immediate. The remaining soldiers' forms flickered. Their near-opacity thinned back to translucence. The commander — still touching the stele — shuddered. Its smile disappeared.

It looked up. Not at Cian. At the ceiling. At a point beyond the ceiling — beyond the stone, beyond the Basilica, beyond the sky — at the source of the suppression.

It spoke one word.

The word was not in any language Cian recognised. Not Hebrew. Not Sumerian. Not the antediluvian tongue its soldiers had used. This was something *older* — a celestial designation, spoken in a register that human vocal apparatus should not have been capable of producing. The sound of it passed through Cian without depositing meaning, the way light passes through glass without warming it.

But Mo Chrá understood. And through Mo Chrá's sudden, sharp vibration — not translation but *recognition* — Cian understood one thing: the commander had just named his angel.

The temperature in the chamber dropped ten degrees.

"It knows you," Cian said.

**"EVERY SHEDIM KNOWETH ME."** Liaigh's voice was steady. Calm. The calm of a being who had been identified by an enemy that could do nothing about it. **"I BORE WITNESS AGAINST ITS FATHER BEFORE THE THRONE. I WAS PRESENT WHEN MICHAEL CAST THE CAPTAIN INTO THE ABYSS. EVERY CHILD OF SHEMYAZA REMEMBERETH THE FACES OF THOSE WHO TESTIFIED."**

Mo Chrá pulsed. *Thirty seconds.*

The commander released the stele.

It turned to face Cian, and its expression was no longer curious, no longer amused. It was *terrified*. Not of Cian. Not of Mo Chrá. Of the presence it had felt suppressing the stele — the presence of one of the archangels who had testified against the Watchers before the Throne, who had bound Azazel in the darkness of Dudael, and who had watched without flinching as Michael cast Shemyaza into the Abyss. Not the executioner. The prosecutor. And the prosecutor had found the Captain's children still guarding a domain that should have been abandoned five thousand years ago.

Terror in a five-thousand-year-old Apkallu priest-spirit expressed itself as violence.

It came at Cian like a collapsing building.

The binding song. The war-frequency. Something else — a harmonic Cian had never heard, a *command frequency* that tried to seize control of the acoustic environment itself, to turn the chamber's resonance into a weapon. The sound was overwhelming. Disorienting. The kind of acoustic assault that would have killed an unaugmented human through sheer mechanical damage to the inner ear.

Cian was not unaugmented.

Mo Chrá *screamed* — not in pain but in answer. Creation frequency against command frequency. The Word against the stolen science of angels who had forgotten the Word. And this time, the sword did not merely deny. She *overwhelmed*. The new harmonics — the richer, denser registers she'd gained in the Epilogue, the amplified authority that Liaigh's resonance had unlocked — deployed in full for the first time.

The commander's attack broke against Mo Chrá's frequency like a wave against a cliff that had been there since before the ocean existed.

And in the breaking, Mo Chrá's binding chains — all of them, every bass harmonic she possessed, the full weight of holy authority expressed through five-thousand-year-old Cydonian ore — reached out and caught the Apkallu-class spirit the way a net catches a shark.

The chains wrapped.

The priest-spirit *fought*. For three seconds, it fought with everything five millennia of accumulated power could produce — and for three seconds, the chains held, and the creation frequency held, and the binding held, because the authority that had forged those chains predated the universe that had produced the spirit, and primacy was not negotiable.

The commander went still.

Bound. Silenced. Held.

Mo Chrá pulsed once.

*Scan complete.*

*IX. THE EXFILTRATION*

"Release the soldiers. Keep the priest."

Mo Chrá obeyed. The three bound soldier-shedim dissolved as their chains opened — snapping back to the dispersed, incorporeal state that was their natural condition away from the stele's output. With the stele dampened and their commander bound, they had no tactical cohesion and no power source. They scattered like sparks from a struck anvil.

The commander hung in chains of holy sound, suspended above the stele chamber floor like a grotesque chandelier. Its sigils still pulsed, but dimly. Its mouth still moved, but silently. The binding frequency's truth-compulsion meant that anything it said would be involuntarily honest, and the spirit had solved this problem by choosing to say nothing at all.

*Intelligent*, Mo Chrá hummed. Grudgingly respectful.

"Can you hold it and move?"

*Yes. But not comfortably.*

"Define 'not comfortably.'"

*I need to split my frequencies. Creation register for wards and environmental countermeasures. Binding register for the prisoner. That leaves me with no reserve for your heartbeat trick if we encounter another alarm glyph.*

"So we go around the wards."

*Or through them. I can unmake them. But unmaking Watcher wards is loud.*

"How loud?"

*Louder than your heartbeat.*

**"THERE IS AN ALTERNATIVE,"** Liaigh said. **"THE WARD IN THE UPPER CHAMBER WAS KEYED TO ACOUSTIC DISPLACEMENT ABOVE A SPECIFIC THRESHOLD. THE STELE'S OUTPUT IS CURRENTLY SUPPRESSED. IF THE WARD'S ACTIVATION THRESHOLD IS TIED TO THE STELE'S HARMONIC—"**

"—then with the stele dampened, the threshold is higher."

**"SIGNIFICANTLY HIGHER. THY HEARTBEAT MAY NOW PASS."**

"*May.*"

**"I AM OFFERING THEE INFORMED SPECULATION, NOT A GUARANTEE. THE ALTERNATIVE IS MO CHRÁ'S LOUD OPTION."**

Cian looked at the sword. Mo Chrá looked back — not with eyes, because swords didn't have eyes, but with the particular vibration that communicated *your call, boss*.

"We go quiet. If the ward triggers, we go loud."

He moved.

The passage back up to the hexagonal chamber was empty — the scattered soldiers had fled deeper into the domain rather than face the creation frequency again. The chamber itself was dark, silent, the alcoves vacant. Without the stele's full output and without their commander, the shedim had no anchor and no orders. They were, for the first time in five thousand years, unsupervised.

Cian crossed the obsidian floor.

Mo Chrá's binding register hummed steadily, maintaining the acoustic chains on the Apkallu priest-spirit that floated behind Cian like the world's most theologically disturbing balloon. The creation frequency in her upper register probed ahead, reading the chamber's acoustic profile for the ward's signature.

There. In the floor. The alarm glyph.

Its activation threshold — she measured it, compared it to the reading from the descent — had risen. The stele's suppressed output meant the ward was running on residual power, its sensitivity reduced by the same forty percent that Liaigh's dampening had achieved.

Cian's heartbeat: fifty-eight beats per minute.

Ward threshold: above sixty-two beats per minute equivalent displacement.

Margin: adequate.

He stepped onto the ward.

Nothing happened.

He stepped off the ward.

Still nothing.

"That was anticlimactic," he said.

*You're welcome*, Mo Chrá hummed.

**"ANTICLIMACTIC IS THE PREFERRED OUTCOME OF ANY INFILTRATION,"** Liaigh observed. **"CLIMAX IS WHAT HAPPENETH WHEN THE PLAN FAILETH."**

The hexagonal shaft. The old stone. The Roman travertine. The modern concrete.

Cian climbed. Mo Chrá maintained the binding chains. The Apkallu priest-spirit floated behind him in enforced silence, its ancient face frozen in an expression that combined existential fury with the particular indignity of being towed through a maintenance shaft by a mortal man and his opinionated sword.

He emerged through the maintenance hatch at 0347. Ninety-three minutes. Total time below: one hour, thirty-three minutes. Well within Liaigh's seven-hour window.

The air above was cool. Roman. Scented with cut grass and diesel and the ancient stone-dust smell of a city that had been built and rebuilt and rebuilt again on the bones of everything that came before it.

Dawn was an hour away. The obelisk stood against the sky, lit by floodlights that made it glow like a finger pointing at heaven. Behind it, St Peter's Basilica — the largest church in Christendom, built on the grave of a fisherman, above the domain of the being who had led two hundred angels into rebellion.

Cian closed the maintenance hatch. Locked it. Stepped into the colonnade's shadow.

"Mo Chrá. Release the priest."

*Are you sure? I could interrogate it. The truth-compulsion is—*

"Not here. Not now. We have what we came for. The binding protocols. The domain architecture. The acoustic specifications."

*But it knows things. Five thousand years of intelligence. Naamah's operations. The drilling—*

"And if I interrogate it within a hundred metres of the Vatican, the acoustic output will be detected by whatever monitoring systems the Swiss Guard don't know they're sitting on top of."

Mo Chrá considered this. The consideration lasted approximately one second.

*Fine. But I'm registering my objection.*

"Registered."

The binding chains opened. The Apkallu priest-spirit hung for a moment in the pre-dawn air — free, disoriented, its manifestation already thinning as the distance from the stele robbed it of the acoustic energy it needed to hold form. It looked at Cian. At Mo Chrá. At the sky above, where the presence of Liaigh was a pressure that even a freed spirit could feel.

It spoke. One sentence. Cian understood this time — the priest-spirit used Aramaic, a concession to being addressed by an Irishman.

*You are too late. She is already through.*

Then it dissolved. Scattered. Returned to the incorporeal state from which five thousand years of sustained acoustic energy had briefly delivered it, and was gone.

"She is already through," Cian repeated.

**"NAAMAH,"** Liaigh confirmed. **"THE PRIEST'S INTELLIGENCE WILL BE CURRENT. IF IT SAITH SHE HATH BREACHED THE OUTER PERIMETER—"**

"Then the timeline just accelerated."

Mo Chrá hummed. Not smug. Not playful. The hum she made when operational reality was worse than the planning assumptions, and the only correct response was to move faster.

Cian pulled out his phone. Secured line. A number in Austin, Texas, where a woman who had built the operational profile that had got him in and out of the Vatican in ninety-three minutes was — he checked the time zone — probably still awake, because Miriam Ashford did not sleep when there was work to be done.

The phone rang twice.

"It's done," Cian said. "I have the binding protocols. Dudael specifications. Full domain architecture for all twenty."

A pause. The sound of keys clicking — Miriam processing, even at this hour, because her mind did not have an off switch.

"And?" she said.

"And we need to move faster than we planned. Naamah is through the outer perimeter. The drill has reached Watcher-era substrate."

Silence on the line. Not stunned silence. Processing silence. The silence of a financial intelligence operative recalculating every timeline, every logistical assumption, every operational constraint in light of information that had just invalidated the comfortable margins.

"How fast?"

"Vatican findings plus Victoria's data plus Chris's acoustic analysis. I need the full team on a secure call in six hours."

"I'll set it up."

Cian paused. He was standing in the shadow of Bernini's colonnade, holding a phone, carrying a sword that was still vibrating with the satisfaction of having taken her first prisoners, listening to the voice of a woman whose operational competence had just saved him hours of blind navigation beneath the most heavily surveilled square kilometre on earth.

He wanted to say something.

"Your operational profile was flawless," he said.

It wasn't what he wanted to say. It was close. It was as close as a man born in 586 BCE could get to what he meant without crossing a line that twenty-six centuries of emotional scar tissue had built around the part of himself that had once known how to speak to women about things that weren't operational profiles.

"Thank you," Miriam said. Professional. Steady.

Then: "Come home safe."

She hung up before he could respond.

Mo Chrá hummed.

The hum was warm. Knowing. The particular frequency she produced when she perceived something Cian couldn't see yet — or wouldn't. The same tone from the end of Chapter 2, but louder. More certain. As if the intervening hours had provided additional data points that confirmed a hypothesis she'd been running since the corridor in Austin.

"Don't," Cian said.

*I didn't say anything.*

"You hummed."

*I hum. It's what I do. I'm a sword. Humming is literally my primary mode of communication.*

**"SHE HATH A POINT,"** Liaigh said.

"You're both fired."

**"THOU CANST NOT FIRE ME. THIS HATH BEEN ATTEMPTED TWENTY-THREE TIMES ACROSS SIX CENTURIES, IN FOUR LANGUAGES, AND ON ONE MEMORABLE OCCASION IN WRITING, AND NOT ONE ATTEMPT HATH PRODUCED ANY DISCERNIBLE EFFECT UPON MY CONTINUED PRESENCE. I HAVE OUTLASTED EMPIRES, PLAGUES, EVERY OCCUPANT OF THE BUILDING THOU JUST SPENT NINETY-THREE MINUTES BREAKING INTO, AND THE ENTIRE DECORATIVE SWORD COLLECTION THAT HANGETH ON THY WALL AND HATH NEVER ONCE BEEN PERMITTED TO DO ANYTHING ABOUT IT. THE POSITION IS PERMANENT."**

"Watch me."

Mo Chrá hummed louder. It was, unmistakably, *laughter*.
