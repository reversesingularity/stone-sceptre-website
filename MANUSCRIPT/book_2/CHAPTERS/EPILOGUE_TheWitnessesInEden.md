# EPILOGUE: THE WITNESSES IN EDEN

✦

*"And I will give power unto my two witnesses, and they shall prophesy a thousand two hundred and threescore days, clothed in sackcloth."* — Revelation 11:3

✦

The Garden had no weather.

This was the thing that visitors noticed first — if visitors were a category that applied to a place that had not received one since a chariot of fire had deposited its passenger twenty-eight centuries ago and the cherubim had re-established the perimeter with the particular diligence of guardians who had been told *no one enters* and who interpreted the instruction at its most liberal as *almost no one, and only when the Almighty personally countersigns the admission*. The Garden had no weather because weather was a consequence of the Fall — the atmospheric instability that followed the fracture of creation's original acoustic harmony, the turbulence that arose when the sustained note of perfection was disrupted and the disruption propagated through every system that the note had been sustaining: geological, biological, meteorological.

Eden had not fallen.

Eden had been *removed*. Set apart. Hidden in a fold of creation that occupied the same spatial coordinates as the Tigris-Euphrates confluence and simultaneously occupied no spatial coordinates at all, because the Garden existed in the frequency that creation had been spoken into and the frequency had not changed here — had not degraded, had not been corrupted by the accumulation of human rebellion and angelic transgression and the slow entropic decay that the theologians called *the curse* and that the physicists, had they been able to measure it, would have called *the deviation from the original harmonic*.

The light was not sunlight. It was the luminescence of a creation that operated as designed — photons produced not by nuclear fusion but by the sustained creative Word that had said *Let there be light* and had not, in this place, been contradicted. The light fell on leaves that had never known autumn. On grass that had never known frost. On the bark of the Tree of Life, whose roots descended into substrate that had been created before the concept of soil existed — substrate that was not geology but *intent*, the material expression of a Creator's decision that life should be sustained.

The Tree stood in the centre of everything.

It always had. The Garden's geometry was not Euclidean — distances here were relational, not metric, measured by purpose rather than metres. The Tree of Life was the centre because it was the point from which sustenance radiated, and the radius of that sustenance was the radius of the Garden, and the Garden was precisely as large as it needed to be to accomplish its function: the preservation of the things placed within it until the appointed time required their deployment.

Three things had been placed within it.

Two prophets. One tree.

The tree sustained the prophets. The prophets sustained the purpose. And the purpose — patient, long-seeded, calibrated across millennia with the particular precision of a divine plan that operated on timescales that made human strategic planning look like a child's game played with sticks — waited.

✦

Enoch sat beneath the Tree and transcribed.

He had been transcribing for five thousand years. Not continuously — even a prophet translated directly into Paradise required intervals of contemplation, of prayer, of the particular stillness that came from sitting in the presence of the Almighty and listening to the sustained note of creation's original harmony and allowing the note to recalibrate the soul's alignment with the frequency it had been designed to resonate at. But the transcription was his nature. The recording. The act of witness reduced to its most fundamental expression: *I saw. I wrote. The writing endures.*

The scroll before him was not papyrus. Not parchment. Not any medium that human civilisation had developed in the five millennia since his translation. The medium was *creation substrate* — material that the Garden produced for the purpose of recording, the way the Tree produced fruit for the purpose of sustaining. The ink was not ink. The stylus was not a stylus. The act of transcription in Eden was the act of *impressing truth into the fabric of unfallen reality*, and the impressions did not fade because unfallen reality did not experience entropy.

He wrote in the language that existed before Babel.

He wrote because writing was what he was.

Enoch, seventh from Adam. The man who had walked with God for three hundred and sixty-five years on the fallen side of the perimeter, and who had been taken — *translated*, the theologians said, because *taken* implied force and force implied resistance and Enoch had not resisted — into the Garden where walking with God was not an act of faith but the ambient condition of existence. Five thousand years. Fifty centuries of transcription, of recording the proceedings of the celestial court as they were relayed to him through the sustained connection that his translated state permitted, of documenting the sins and judgments and interventions and the slow, patient, agonising unfolding of the plan that would, in the fullness of time, bring everything to its appointed conclusion.

He had written Azazel's crimes.

He had written them five thousand years ago, in the language that the fallen world would receive as *1 Enoch*, in the script that the Book of the Watchers would preserve in fragmentary Aramaic and Ge'ez translation, in the words that Jude would quote in his epistle and that the Ethiopian church would canonise and that the Western church would lose and rediscover and argue about for centuries while the man who had written them sat beneath a tree in a garden that the arguing theologians did not believe was literal and transcribed the continuation.

*To Azazel ascribe ALL sin.*

The words were in him. Had been in him since the vision — the revelation in which the Lord had shown Enoch the full scope of the corruption that Gadreel's son had seeded into human civilisation. Not merely weapons. Not merely the metallurgical innovations that Gadreel himself had taught. Azazel had taken his father's teachings and expanded them into a comprehensive corruption: enchantments, pharmakeia, cosmetics deployed as vanity-engines, warfare elevated from tribal necessity to industrialised slaughter, the transformation of human conflict from something that could be survived into something that could be *optimised*. Azazel had taught humanity to be efficient at destruction, and the efficiency had compounded across millennia, and the compounding was still running.

And Raphael had sealed him.

Enoch had watched the sealing. Had been present — translated, removed from the physical plane but acoustically connected to creation's fabric — when Raphael composed the note. The perfect, self-sustaining frequency that would contain the False Prophet in the prison beneath the ice for five thousand years. Enoch had heard the note begin. Had felt it establish itself in the Cydonian metamaterial of Dudael's architecture — the same stone, the same Martian ore, the same acoustic-memory substrate that Enoch had gathered on Mount Hermon and forged into a blade and given to his son and that had passed, through channels he had documented and channels he had not, into the hands of a boy in Connacht who would carry it for twenty-six centuries.

He had heard the note for five millennia.

It was part of the Garden's ambient frequency — not dominant, not disruptive, but present. A low, sustained bass note beneath the chord of creation's perfection, the sound of mercy holding judgment in abeyance, the acoustic expression of a divine decision to *delay* rather than *destroy*. Enoch had sat beneath the Tree of Life and transcribed the proceedings of heaven and listened to that note every day for five thousand years, and the note had become as familiar as his own breathing — more familiar, because his breathing had changed when he was translated but the note had not changed at all.

Until now.

✦

The stylus stopped.

Not because Enoch willed it to stop. The stylus stopped because the hand that held it had ceased to move, and the hand had ceased to move because the body — translated, sustained, perfected by five millennia of Tree-of-Life renewal — had received a signal that bypassed cognition entirely and arrived in the nervous system as a *physical event*. Not sound. Not the perception of sound through ears that functioned perfectly in the unfallen atmosphere of the Garden. Something deeper. Something that operated at the level where the soul's frequency intersected with creation's fabric — the level that Enoch, alone among the three residents of Eden, could perceive because Enoch had forged a blade from the same substrate that the signal was propagating through, and the forging had permanently attuned his acoustic perception to the frequencies that Cydonian metamaterial carried.

The bass note was gone.

Raphael's seal — the sustained, perfect, self-reinforcing harmonic that had contained Azazel for five thousand years — had stopped.

Enoch felt the absence the way a musician feels a missing string: not as silence but as *wrongness*. The chord of creation's ambient frequency in the Garden recalibrated around the gap, the harmonic relationships adjusting to accommodate the loss of a component that had been present for so long that its presence had been absorbed into the baseline. The Garden was still perfect. The Tree still sustained. The light still fell on leaves that had never known autumn.

But the note was gone. And the absence was *louder* than the note had ever been.

Enoch set the stylus down.

He did not need to consult the Empyreal Register. He did not need to petition the celestial court for confirmation. He did not need to verify through the channels of divine communication that his translation permitted. He knew. He had forged the metal. He had written the crimes. He had heard the seal for five thousand years, and now he heard its ending, and the ending was not ambiguous.

He stood.

Then — a second disturbance. Not absence this time but *presence*. A sharp, bright reverberation through the Cydonian substrate of creation's fabric — a frequency he knew the way a father knows his daughter's voice in a crowd. Mo Chrá. The blade he had forged on Hermon from ore that remembered the Watchers' oath was *singing*. Not the combat frequency. Not the creation-frequency overtone series. Something new — an active, deliberate, exploratory harmonic that Enoch recognised instantly because he had built the capacity into the metamaterial five thousand years ago and had wondered, across all the intervening centuries, whether the blade would ever encounter a target complex enough to activate it.

She was recording.

Mo Chrá was mapping the acoustic fingerprint of the thing that had emerged from the broken seal — cataloguing every harmonic, every overtone, every molecular vibration of Azazel's unfolding. The blade that Enoch had forged as testimony was *gathering testimony*. Doing what she had been made to do. What *he* had made her to do.

Enoch smiled.

The smile was small and private and carried the particular satisfaction of a craftsman who has built something five millennia ago and who has just felt it perform the function he designed it for. The boy in Connacht had carried her well. The engineer in An Daingean had housed her well. And now, in the moment of greatest crisis, the blade was not merely surviving — she was *learning*. When the boy raised her against the False Prophet again, she would know him. She would carry the acoustic map of his every frequency. She would be ready.

*Good girl*, Enoch thought. And the thought was not anthropomorphism. It was a father's pride.

The Garden responded to his movement the way the Garden responded to everything: with the organic precision of a biome that operated under direct creative authority. The grass beneath his feet was not trampled — it was *received*. The air around him did not resist — it *accompanied*. Five thousand years in Paradise had not diminished Enoch's humanity. He was still the seventh from Adam. He was still the scribe. He was still the man who had walked with God and who understood, with the particular understanding of a prophet who had been shown the full scope of creation and judgment, that the things he had been shown were not metaphorical.

He looked toward the figure seated on the stone by the eastern brook.

✦

Elijah was sharpening his knife.

He had been sharpening the same knife for twenty-eight centuries. Not because the blade required it — blades did not dull in Eden, where entropy's jurisdiction ended at the cherubim's perimeter — but because sharpening was an act of *preparation*, and preparation was what Elijah did while he waited for the thing he had been placed in the Garden to wait for.

The knife was bronze. Tishbite metalwork, crude by any standard that post-Hellenistic metallurgy would consider worth measuring. The handle was wrapped in leather that should have rotted three millennia ago and had not, because the Tree of Life's sustaining radius extended to the possessions of those it sustained — a biological generosity that Elijah had never questioned and that Enoch, the more scientifically inclined of the two, had documented in a seven-scroll treatise on the arboreal mechanics of divine sustenance.

Elijah did not look up when Enoch approached.

He did not need to. Twenty-eight centuries of cohabitation in a garden that contained exactly three human residents had calibrated the two prophets' awareness of each other to a precision that exceeded the capabilities of any surveillance technology the fallen world had produced. Elijah knew Enoch's footfall. Knew his breathing pattern. Knew the particular cadence of the scribe's approach when the scribe was carrying news that would change the nature of their waiting.

This was that cadence.

"The note hath ceased," Enoch said.

The language was the pre-Babel tongue — the language that Eden had preserved, the acoustic medium that human speech had been designed to operate in before Babel shattered it into fragments that the fallen world called *languages*. In this tongue, precision was not a discipline. It was ambient. Words meant what they meant, and the meaning did not require interpretation because the gap between intent and expression had not yet been opened by the corruption that Babel had introduced.

Elijah's hands stopped.

The whetstone — Eden granite, smooth, grey, the geological expression of unfallen mineral structure — rested against the bronze. The prophet's hands were the hands of a man who had spent his pre-translation life in the wilderness of Gilead and the courts of kings and the summit of a mountain where fire had fallen from heaven at his word. They were not smooth. Translated or not, the body that the chariot of fire had carried into Paradise bore the testimony of the life it had lived: calluses, sun-darkened skin, the particular grip strength of a man who had survived drought and exile and the wrath of a queen who had served the Siren and who had sworn his death in the name of a goddess who was not a goddess but a Nephilim abomination wearing divinity as a mask.

"When?" Elijah said.

"Moments past. I felt the frequency collapse. The cascade. The silence after."

Elijah set the whetstone down. He looked at Enoch with eyes that carried the fire of Carmel — the same fire, sustained, undimmed, burning at a lower intensity in Paradise because Paradise did not require the flame's full expression but at the same intensity in the *spirit*, because the spirit's fire did not modulate for comfort.

"Then the Teacher of Secrets walketh free."

"He walketh free."

The words settled between them with a weight that was not acoustic — not the vibration-based phenomenon that the Nephilim Chronicles' physics would have measured and classified — but *historical*. The weight of personal experience. Enoch had stood before the Watchers' council and read the indictment. Had looked into the faces of the two hundred as he pronounced the judgment. Had recorded, in the precise, neutral, devastating clarity of a scribe who understood that the record was more powerful than the rhetoric, every sin that the fallen angels and their offspring had committed against the creation they had been designed to serve.

And of all the sins he had recorded, the sins ascribed to Azazel were the worst.

Not because Azazel's acts were the most violent. Shemyaza's were. Not because Azazel's were the most blasphemous. Penemue's corruption of sacred writing exceeded Azazel's in theological severity. Azazel's sins were the worst because they were the most *comprehensive*. The son of Gadreel had not corrupted one domain of human existence. He had corrupted *every* domain. Weapons. Enchantments. Cosmetics. Pharmakeia. Warfare as industrial process. Destruction as systemised enterprise. Azazel had looked at humanity and seen not a species to corrupt but a *civilisation to optimise for its own annihilation*, and the optimisation had been so thorough that the Almighty had ascribed the totality of the resulting sin — not Azazel's personal sins alone, but the cascading downstream consequences across every generation that inherited his teachings — to a single being.

*To him ascribe ALL sin.*

Enoch had written those words. And now the being to whom all sin had been ascribed was walking free for the first time in five thousand years.

"The seal was delay," Enoch said. "Not permanence. We always knew."

"We knew." Elijah rose from the stone. The motion was fluid — the economy of a body that had been maintained by the Tree of Life for twenty-eight centuries and that had been seasoned, before its maintenance began, in the particular crucible of a prophet's life in a fallen world. "And the delay hath served its purpose. The Bride hath had her centuries of preparation. The testimony hath been sown. The remnant endureth." He paused. "And the boy?"

The boy. Twenty-six centuries old, and Elijah still called him the boy. The prophet who had thrown his cloak over a farmer's son and called him to ministry — who understood the act of commissioning and the weight it placed on the commissioned — recognised the same pattern in the relationship between a healer and a warrior he had observed from Paradise for twenty-six centuries.

"The boy held," Enoch said. "He reached the prison. He fought. He was told to stand down."

"By Raphael."

"By Raphael."

Elijah closed his eyes. The fire behind them did not close. "And the boy stood down."

"He did."

Elijah exhaled. The breath was not physical necessity — translated bodies in Eden did not require respiratory gas exchange. It was *expression*. The prophetic body's response to information that carried emotional weight, the physical gesture of a spirit processing something complex enough to require the body's participation.

"Good," he said. "Then he hath learned what I could not learn on Horeb. That the earthquake is not the Lord. That the fire is not the Lord. That the still small voice—" He stopped. The reference was personal. A thousand years before Christ, a prophet who had just slaughtered four hundred and fifty prophets of Baal and four hundred prophets of Asherah on the summit of Carmel had fled into the wilderness because a queen had promised him death, and the promise of one woman had undone the courage that had faced down eight hundred and fifty servants of darkness. On Horeb, the Lord had spoken in the silence after the earthquake and the wind and the fire, and the silence had contained more authority than all three combined.

Twenty-eight centuries in a garden of perfect quiet had taught Elijah the lesson of Horeb in a curriculum that could not be compressed.

"The Siren is moving," Elijah said. Not a question.

"Naamah departed with Azazel. The ship turned south into the storm."

Elijah's jaw tightened. The muscle memory of Carmel — of the moment when the fire fell and the priests died and the people shouted *The Lord, He is God!* while the blood of eight hundred and fifty false prophets stained the Kishon — surfaced in the set of his mouth. Naamah. The thing that Jezebel had served. The corruption that had worn the face of Asherah and the body of a goddess and the smile of a being who had survived the Flood by means that the Almighty had permitted for reasons that the prophet did not claim to understand but that he trusted, because trust was the residue of Horeb and Horeb's lesson had not faded.

"Four hundred prophets of Asherah," Elijah said. "On the mountain. I slew them at the brook. Every one. And behind every one was *her*. She sent them. She trained them. She gave them the rituals and the words and the pharmakeia and the blood-craft. I killed her instruments, and she made more. Three thousand years she hath been making more." The fire in his voice was not the fire of a young prophet in the grip of righteous fury. It was the fire of a furnace that had been burning for twenty-eight centuries and that had been refined, by the patience of Paradise, into something that burned hotter because it burned *slower*. "I am ready to finish it."

"Thou art ready," Enoch said. "We are both ready. We have been ready since before the boy who carries the blade was born."

"Then the twelve hundred and sixty days—"

"Are at hand."

The words fell into the Garden's perfect acoustic environment and were absorbed without echo. Eden did not produce echoes. Echoes were the by-product of sound waves reflecting off surfaces that were not designed to receive them — the acoustic consequence of a world where surfaces and sounds existed in imperfect relationship. In Eden, every sound was received. Every word was absorbed. The acoustic harmony of unfallen creation accepted speech the way the ground accepted rain: as fulfilment of design.

Twelve hundred and sixty days. Forty-two months. Three and a half years. The duration of their ministry as the Two Witnesses of Revelation 11 — the prophesied period during which they would stand in Jerusalem and prophesy and call down fire and shut the heavens and turn water to blood and testify against the Beast and the False Prophet and the Whore and the entire assembled machinery of the mystery system that had been building toward this moment since the Watchers descended on Hermon and taught humanity the knowledge that God had reserved for the angels.

And at the end of the twelve hundred and sixty days, they would die.

This was also known. Also documented. Also *accepted* — not with resignation, because resignation implied unwillingness, but with the particular readiness of two men who had never died and who understood that their mortality was not a price to be paid but a testimony to be delivered. *They overcame him by the blood of the Lamb, and by the word of their testimony; and they loved not their lives unto the death.*

They would die. They would lie in the streets of Jerusalem for three days and a half while the world mocked. And then the voice would come — the voice that Enoch had heard at his translation and that Elijah had heard from the chariot, the voice that said:

*COME UP HITHER.*

And they would rise.

But the rising was three and a half years and three and a half days away, and between the rising and this moment — this breath, this garden, this final interval of sacred waiting — lay the fire.

✦

The third resident of the Garden did not speak.

He sat beneath a branch of the Tree of Life that extended toward the eastern boundary — the direction of Jerusalem, the direction of the hill, the direction of *the afternoon* — and he listened to the two prophets with the particular stillness of a man who had arrived in Paradise by a route that neither prophet had taken and that no theology had predicted and that the grace it represented was so extravagant, so unearned, so devastatingly *free*, that twenty centuries of contemplating it had not diminished its magnitude.

He had not been a prophet. Had not walked with God. Had not called down fire or written scrolls or forged blades from Martian ore. He had been a thief. A criminal. A man crucified on a Roman instrument of execution for crimes that Rome's jurisprudence had deemed worthy of the penalty, and Rome's jurisprudence, for all its imperfections, had not been wrong. He was guilty. He had lived guilty. He had hung on a cross guilty.

And he had turned his head to the left.

That was all. A turn of the head. A look at the man dying beside him — the man who was not dying the way criminals died, who was dying the way *something else* died, something that the thief's untrained, unlettered, theologically vacant mind had nevertheless perceived with the crystalline clarity that comes to men in extremis when the body's failure strips the mind of every pretension and leaves only the raw, unfiltered capacity to *recognise*.

*Lord, remember me when thou comest into thy kingdom.*

Nine words. No title. No lineage. No three hundred and sixty-five years of walking with God, no chariot of fire, no prophetic resume. Nine words from a dying criminal who had looked at a dying carpenter and had seen — through the blood and the flies and the Roman efficiency and the screaming crowd — the King.

*To day shalt thou be with me in paradise.*

And paradise had received him. And the Tree had sustained him. And twenty centuries later, the thief sat beneath its branches and listened to two prophets discuss the end of the world with the quiet attention of a man who understood, from personal experience, that the distance between damnation and paradise was exactly one act of recognition.

He would not go with them. The twelve hundred and sixty days were not his commission. His commission had been fulfilled on a Roman cross twenty centuries ago, and the fulfilment had been so complete, so absolute, so theologically sufficient that no additional ministry was required. He was in the Garden because grace had placed him there. He would remain in the Garden because grace sustained him. And when the final trumpet sounded and the dead in Christ rose first and the tree gave its final fruit and the Garden was no longer a hidden fold in creation but the open, unveiled, restored reality of Earth remade — he would be there. Standing. Whole. The proof that the grace Enoch and Elijah were about to step into the fire to proclaim was not theoretical.

It was biographical.

He sat. He listened. He waited.

The Tree of Life dropped a leaf on his shoulder, and the leaf carried the fragrance of a creation that had never known the Fall, and the fragrance was the fragrance of home.

✦

Enoch looked toward the Garden's boundary.

The boundary was not visible — not a wall, not a fence, not the kind of perimeter that human architecture produced. It was an acoustic phenomenon: the frequency of Eden's perfection encountering the frequency of the fallen world and producing an interference pattern that the cherubim maintained and that manifested, to the translated eye, as a shimmer. A distortion. The visual equivalent of the heat-haze that rose from desert sand, except that this shimmer was not heat but *difference*. The Garden's frequency meeting the world's frequency and the meeting producing the boundary that separated preservation from corruption.

Beyond the boundary was the world they were about to re-enter.

A world that had not heard their voices for millennia. A world that had been given the delay — five thousand years for Enoch, twenty-eight centuries for Elijah — and had used the delay to build and destroy and rebuild and to accumulate, in the process, the very conditions that the prophecy described: the Beast, the False Prophet, the Whore, the ten-king confederation, the global deception, the mark, the image, the abomination. The conditions were complete. The chess pieces were on their squares. The Teacher of Secrets was free. The Siren was sailing. The Beast vessel that Azazel's biotechnology would animate was in development. The Bride was scattered but alive. The remnant endured.

Everything was in place.

Twelve hundred and sixty days. And everything that followed.

"We walk out of Paradise," Enoch said, "and we do not walk back."

"We walk out of Paradise," Elijah said, "and we walk into fire."

"As thou hast done before."

"As I have always done." The prophet looked at the scribe. The fire in his eyes met the patience in Enoch's, and the meeting was the same meeting it had been for twenty-eight centuries — the complementary frequencies of two men whom God had chosen for the same mission and equipped with opposite temperaments because the mission required both. The patience to endure. The fire to proclaim. The record that could not be falsified and the flame that could not be extinguished.

"The boy will need us," Elijah said.

"The boy hath the blade. The blade hath the record. And the healer walketh beside him still."

"The healer. Raphael." Elijah said the name with the respect of a prophet who understood archangels not as abstractions but as colleagues — beings he had served alongside in the economy of heaven's operations, each assigned a function, each operating within the constraints that the Almighty's design imposed. "Raphael hath borne the weight of that commission for twenty-six centuries. When we arrive, his burden easeth."

"And ours beginneth."

Elijah sheathed the bronze knife. The motion was final — not the casual return of a blade to its resting place but the deliberate conclusion of an activity that had sustained him through twenty-eight centuries of waiting. He would not sharpen the knife again in Eden. The next time the blade was drawn, it would be drawn in Jerusalem, in the fallen air of a world that was about to hear two voices it had not heard since before Christ walked the same streets.

The Garden's light fell on both prophets — the scribe and the fire-caller, the patient one and the fierce one, the man who had walked with God in the seventh generation from Adam and the man who had called down fire in the reign of Ahab. Five thousand years and twenty-eight hundred years, united in the same garden, sustained by the same tree, commissioned for the same twelve hundred and sixty days.

Behind them, the thief sat beneath the Tree of Life and watched them look toward the boundary.

Enoch turned. The scribe's eyes — patient, ancient, carrying five millennia of recorded truth — met the eyes of the man who had arrived in Paradise with no record at all. No scrolls. No prophecies. No ministry. Nine words on a Roman cross and the recognition that those nine words contained.

"Dismas."

The redeemed name. The name that Paradise had given him — not the name his mother had spoken over a cradle in Judea, not the name that Roman courts had inscribed on the sentencing tablet, but the name that grace had written on the white stone that Revelation promised to those who overcame. *Dismas.* The word meant *sunset* in the tongue his mother had spoken, but in the pre-Babel language of the Garden it meant something that could not be compressed into a single English equivalent — something between *the one who arrived at the last hour* and *the one whose arrival proved that the last hour is sufficient*.

"We go," Enoch said.

Dismas nodded. The nod carried everything his silence had always carried: *This is what grace defends. This is what you are stepping into the fire to proclaim. The distance between damnation and paradise is one act of recognition. Go. Testify. I will be here when you come home.*

The cherubim's flames burned at the perimeter. The frequency shimmered. Beyond the boundary, Azazel was walking free and Naamah was sailing south and Cian mac Morna was standing on the edge of the world with a sword forged by the scribe's own hands, enduring.

The twelve hundred and sixty days were at hand.

Enoch picked up the scroll. Five thousand years of transcription — the Empyreal Register's complete record of every celestial proceeding since his translation, the continuation of the testimony that 1 Enoch had begun — rolled beneath his fingers with the weight of history and the lightness of purpose.

He would carry it out of Paradise with him.

The testimony was not finished.
