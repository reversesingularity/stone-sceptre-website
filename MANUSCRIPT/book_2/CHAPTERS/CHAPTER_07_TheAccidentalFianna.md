# CHAPTER 7: THE ACCIDENTAL FIANNA

✦

Sound came first. It always did.

The body remembered before the mind could — this was the oldest truth Cian mac Morna knew, older than any doctrine he had learned in any century, older than the sword on his back. The body processed the world through vibration before it processed it through thought. And the vibrations that reached him now were wrong.

Four Rolls-Royce AE 2100D3 turboprops. He catalogued them the way a drowning man catalogues air: with desperate, animal specificity. Constant RPM. Consistent harmonic. The C-130J's cargo bay resonated at a frequency he knew — he had ridden in Hercules airframes across four decades of conflicts in which the aircraft's airframe had been the only thing between him and a drop into geography that wanted him dead. The engines were correct. The airframe was correct.

Everything else was wrong.

His heartbeat was too slow. He could feel it in his temples — a thick, reluctant pulse, the cardiac rhythm of a body that had been emptied of something more fundamental than blood. The Chains had drawn on his acoustic resonance, the deep vibrational connection between wielder and sword, and what remained was the particular silence of a man whose instrument had played its last note and gone still.

Liaigh was not there.

This registered not as thought but as absence — a void in the acoustic architecture of his perception where the guardian's presence had been a constant for twenty-six centuries. Not a voice. Not a hum. A *pressure* — the subsonic assurance of a being whose attention had rested on Cian's heartbeat since 586 BCE, monitoring, adjusting, keeping the margin between wound and death wider than reflexes alone could manage. That pressure was gone. Had been gone since the Romanian border. Would remain gone until—

He did not finish the thought. The thought led to a place he could not afford to visit while his body was reporting damage from systems he did not fully understand.

Mo Chrá.

The sword was on his chest.

He felt her weight before he understood her position — the familiar density of Cydonian metamaterial, the particular distribution of mass along a blade he had carried since a prophet from Jerusalem had placed it in his hands with words that still burned in his acoustic memory. She was lying crosswise across his sternum. Crossguard beneath his collarbones. Point aligned with his left hip. Edge turned away from his body.

Silent.

For the first time in two thousand six hundred years, Mo Chrá was not humming.

The terror was immediate and absolute. It bypassed every layer of his training — the Roman discipline, the Byzantine patience, the Cold War compartmentalisation, the twenty-six centuries of emotional fortification that had taught him to process loss as data rather than feeling. The terror went straight to the substrate. The Iron Age floor. The cognitive fossil beneath everything else: the boy from Connacht who had been given a sword by a being of fire and had never been without her voice since.

She was silent. She was cold. The Cydonian ore that should have been radiating warmth against his chest was producing nothing — no frequency, no signature, no presence. The acoustic void where her hum should have been was a wound more specific than anything Dragan's cursed greatsword had inflicted.

His hand moved. Fingers closing around the hilt with the desperate precision of a man reaching for the one thing that had never left him. The grip was familiar — twenty-six centuries of muscle memory, the particular way his palm fitted the wire-wrapped handle, the placement of each finger worn into the metal by repetition measured in millennia rather than years.

Nothing. No warmth. No response. No hum rising to meet his grip.

Then — faint. So faint that he questioned whether he was manufacturing signal from desperation. A pulse. Not a hum. A single, cardiac throb of warmth that moved through the hilt into his palm the way a heartbeat moves through a chest wall. Not a word. Not a greeting. A *reflex* — the instrument confirming, at the absolute limit of its depleted capacity, that it was still present. Still bonded. Still alive.

The relief hurt worse than the terror.

He exhaled. The breath shuddered. He did not permit his face to show what the breath had contained.

Then he opened his eyes.

The cargo bay resolved. Grey metal. Ribbed fuselage. Tactical lighting casting everything in the particular amber glow that military aircraft designers had determined was optimal for maintaining night-adapted vision in personnel who might need to exit into darkness at speed. The bench-seat opposite was occupied by equipment cases. An Daingean's olive-drab bulk filled the centre of the bay, every drone cell empty, the swarm expended, the launch doors sealed over hollow cradles.

And Miriam Ashford was two feet away from his face, holding a penlight.

His Iron Age substrate registered several things simultaneously. Her proximity. Her breathing — elevated, controlled, the particular respiration rate of a person who had been maintaining alertness for longer than her body approved. Her hands — steady. Her eyes — focused with the clinical precision of someone performing a medical assessment, not the emotional aperture of someone sitting vigil.

And the sword. The crosswise placement. Crossguard to collarbone. Point to hip. Edge away. The geometry that his body recognised before his mind could intervene, because the body had been born in an age when that geometry meant something specific and irrevocable.

*Bean-choimhdeachta.*

The word surfaced from the deepest archive of his memory — from the place where language existed before writing, where protocol existed before law, where the rituals of the Fianna were not history but lived practice. A woman placing a warrior's blade across his chest in the formation of guardianship. Not a marriage. Something older. Something that predated marriage by centuries and carried weight that marriage could not. A public declaration that this warrior's life was under her protection. That she would answer for his honour while he could not answer for himself. That she claimed him — not as property, not as lover, but as *charge*. A responsibility accepted before witnesses and sealed by the placement of steel.

The last woman who had done this was Siobhan. In 1170. Eight hundred and fifty-seven years ago.

His mouth opened. The register that emerged was not the English he had been speaking for four centuries or the operational flat-tone he used with the team. It was the old language. The first language. The one that lived beneath all the others the way bedrock lived beneath topsoil.

*"A bhean-choimhdeachta, glacaim—"*

"Hold still, your left pupil is sluggish."

The penlight moved across his field of vision. Left to right. His pupil tracked, or failed to track — he neither knew nor cared, because he was attempting to deliver a formal response to a declaration of guardianship that had not been made to a woman who was checking his pupillary reflex and had no idea that what she had done with a sword and a stretcher in a Carpathian car park constituted the most significant interpersonal commitment in Fianna culture short of a blood oath.

"Ashford—"

"Your left pupil is responding approximately point-three seconds slower than your right. That's within acceptable range for post-concussive presentation but I want to monitor it. Can you track my finger?"

Her index finger replaced the penlight. She moved it in a horizontal arc, watching his eyes follow. He was following her finger and simultaneously trying to restart the sentence that had been interrupted — the formal Old Irish that his cognitive fossil was insisting he complete because you did not leave a *bean-choimhdeachta* declaration unanswered, you did not leave *anything* unanswered in Fianna law, the silence itself was a form of response and the wrong one—

"Tracking is symmetrical. Good. Blood pressure?"

She reached for his left arm. The blood pressure cuff was already in place — she had placed it while he was unconscious. The squeeze of the cuff inflating interrupted whatever his mouth had been preparing to say, which was probably a mercy, because based on the first four words, what his mouth had been preparing to say was going to create a diplomatic incident between the sixth century BCE and the twenty-first century that no amount of operational context could resolve.

"One-ten over seventy-two. Low for your baseline but functional. IV is running — saline plus electrolytes. You've been out for approximately four hours." She made a note on a pad. An actual paper pad. Miriam Ashford, who had spent twelve years running from supernatural entities with nothing but encrypted devices and paranoia, was keeping medical notes with a pen. "Heart rate fifty-eight. Your resting is normally in the low forties."

"I know what my resting heart rate is."

"Then you know it's elevated. Which tells me your system is still compensating." She clicked the penlight off. Her expression was the one he catalogued as *professional assessment with zero tolerance for patient opinions*. It was the expression she wore when she was managing something she couldn't control by controlling everything adjacent to it. "You need another two hours minimum before I'm comfortable with you weight-bearing."

"I've been weight-bearing since before the Roman Republic."

"And your left pupil is sluggish. The two facts are not in competition."

He tried again. The words were there — sitting in the register that had not been accessed in centuries, the formal vocabulary of acceptance and obligation that the Fianna had used when the world was young enough to need its warriors consecrated by the people who loved them. The architecture of what he wanted to say was precise: acknowledgement of the claim, acceptance of the guardian's right, a counter-declaration of worthiness that was also, in the old law, a confession of vulnerability. *I accept your protection because I am worth protecting* — the particular humility of a warrior admitting that his life had value beyond his function.

"Ashford. The sword. You placed—"

"I put it where it would fit." She was adjusting the IV drip. Not looking at him. The particular focus of someone performing a task that required exactly the amount of attention she was giving it and not one fraction more, which meant the excess attention was being directed elsewhere, and he could not read where. "The stretcher was narrow. Mo Chrá is long. Your hands were the only safe cradle — she objects to being handled by anyone else. It was logistics."

Logistics.

Cian mac Morna lay in the cargo bay of a C-130J Hercules at thirty thousand feet and stared at the riveted ceiling and contemplated the distance between *bean-choimhdeachta* and *logistics*. It was a distance measured in approximately twenty-six centuries and the entire breadth of a civilisation that had forgotten what swords meant when you placed them on the bodies of the men who carried them.

She had no idea. She had performed the single most significant act of personal commitment in Fianna warrior culture, and she had no idea she had done it, and the reason she had no idea was that the culture that would have taught her had been gone for two thousand years, and the only person alive who remembered it was lying on a bench with a sluggish left pupil and a sword on his chest that was too exhausted to laugh but would have if she could.

Mo Chrá pulsed. Faint. The cardiac warmth again — barely perceptible, the sword's depleted reserves producing one instinctive response. Not matchmaking. Not commentary. Something older: *she placed me here. I will stay.*

"Can you move her?" Miriam said. "I need access to your sternum for a proper cardiac assessment."

She reached for the sword.

Mo Chrá became heavy.

Not gradually — *instantaneously*. The blade that weighed three and a half pounds in Cian's hand tripled in apparent mass within a fraction of a second. Miriam's hands, positioned to lift the crossguard, encountered resistance that should not have existed in an object governed by Newtonian physics. Her fingers tightened. Her forearms engaged. The sword did not move.

"She's—"

"I know." His hand was still on the hilt. He felt it — not the weight increase, which was acoustic rather than gravitational, a frequency-based density shift that the Cydonian ore could produce at will — but the *intent* behind it. Mo Chrá was spent. The Chains of Abyssal Damnation had emptied her. Her hum was gone, her luminescence was gone, her acoustic output was a flatline. But she had found, in the absolute basement of her depleted reserves, enough energy for one reflex.

*Do not separate me from my wielder.*

Not matchmaking. A sword that had been bonded to this man for twenty-six centuries, that had been silenced for the first time in her existence, clinging to the one connection that remained — the weight of steel on the chest of the man who had carried her since the world was younger.

His hand closed tighter around the hilt. The warmth was there. Faint. A heartbeat, not a hum.

She was alive. Spent, broken, silent — but alive.

Something moved behind his eyes that he could not afford to let reach his face. He managed it. Barely.

Miriam watched the weight shift. She did not attempt to move the sword again. Instead, she adjusted — shifting her assessment approach without comment, reaching for Cian's wrist instead of his sternum, placing two fingers on the radial pulse with the adaptive pragmatism of a woman who had learned that things in Cian mac Morna's vicinity did not always obey the rules she'd been trained on and the appropriate response was to work around them rather than argue.

She counted. Fifteen seconds. Multiplied. Noted the result.

"Pulse ox?"

She clipped the sensor to his index finger — the hand that was not holding the hilt of a sword that refused to be moved by anyone but him. The number appeared. She noted it.

"You're stable. Depleted, but stable." She clicked the pen closed. "When we land, James is carrying you to the vehicle. Don't argue."

"I wasn't going to argue."

"You were composing an argument in three languages. I can tell because your jaw does a specific thing when you're translating between registers."

He had no response to this. He had spent twenty-six centuries learning to conceal his internal processes from allies and enemies across dozens of cultures and civilisational epochs, and a twenty-nine-year-old GCHQ analyst had identified his translation tell from two feet away during a medical assessment.

Miriam gathered her supplies. Penlight, pad, blood pressure monitor — the collected instruments of a woman who had kept a two-thousand-six-hundred-year-old warrior alive through a depletion event that had no medical literature because medical literature did not account for the cost of channelling Gehenna fire through a sentient sword.

She moved toward the front of the cargo bay. Three steps. Four.

"Mac Morna."

She said it without turning. Flat. Professional. The wall rebuilt. The GCHQ composure firmly in place — the formal address that maintained the precise distance between *analyst assigned to a supernatural warfare team* and anything that might compromise that designation.

Cian heard "Mac Morna" and processed two things simultaneously, with the particular agony of a man whose Iron Age substrate and modern mind were providing contradictory assessments of the same data.

Relief: she was professional. The sword placement was logistics. She had not made a declaration. The crisis was his alone — manufactured by a cognitive fossil that had mistaken a practical decision for an Iron Age commitment.

Devastation: she was professional. The sword placement was logistics. She had not made a declaration.

He closed his eyes. Mo Chrá's heartbeat pulsed against his chest. One warmth in a cargo bay full of cold.

## II. THE RETURN

The silence ended without warning.

One moment the acoustic architecture of Cian's perception contained exactly three elements: the C-130J's turboprops, his own cardiac rhythm, and the faintest pulse of a sword that was learning to breathe again. Three sounds. A vocabulary reduced to monosyllables.

The next moment, with the sudden, absolute authority of a presence that had been restraining itself for twenty-four hours and had decided that restraint's utility was exhausted:

"THY MORTAL COMPANION HATH PLACED THY BLADE UPON THY BREAST IN THE MANNER OF THE BEAN-CHOIMHDEACHTA. THE SWORD APPROVETH. I ALSO APPROVE. I ADVISE SURRENDER."

Cian choked.

It was not a dignified sound. It was the sound of a man who had been lying in acoustic silence for hours, whose entire auditory world had been the muted roar of turboprops and the absence of everything he depended upon, and who had just received the Empyreal Register equivalent of a romantic ambush at a volume that bypassed his ears entirely and detonated inside his skull with the patient, devastating clarity of a being who had been composing this sentence since the aircraft crossed the Carpathian ridgeline.

Miriam, two seats forward, looked up from her notes. "Pain?"

"No."

"You made a sound."

"Turbulence."

"The aircraft isn't experiencing turbulence."

"Internal turbulence."

She assessed him with the expression that said *I am filing this under 'requires further investigation'* and returned to her notes.

In the space behind his sternum — not physical space, the acoustic space where Liaigh's presence had resided for twenty-six centuries — the guardian settled back into position with the particular satisfaction of someone returning to a duty they had never wanted to leave. The Ban had been absolute: no intervention, no speech, no presence within the Principality's jurisdiction. The C-130J had cleared Romanian airspace seven minutes ago. The domain boundary was behind them. And Liaigh — the being who had argued for Cian's life before powers that could unmake continents, who had restrained himself from acting while his charge fought for survival without his voice — had evidently spent the entirety of his enforced silence preparing *this*.

*She doesn't know,* Cian said. Internal. The sub-vocalised communication that looked, to anyone observing, like a man with his eyes closed and his jaw slightly tightened. *She doesn't know the protocol. The placement was practical. She was managing equipment on a narrow stretcher.*

"I offer mine assessment as one who hath observed both thy nature and hers with discernment spanning considerable duration."

A pause. Cian braced.

"Thou art outnumbered. Surrender is the strategically sound option."

*She called me Mac Morna. Formal. Professional. She reverted.*

"I have observed her behaviour when thou wast stricken. The custom is irrelevant. The woman claimed thee. The sword merely noticed first."

*Liaigh—*

"She called thee by thy name."

The words landed with weight that had nothing to do with volume. Liaigh's voice — which was not a voice, which was a form of communication that predated human speech and operated at a frequency that the guardian had spent twenty-six centuries calibrating to a single mortal's perception — carried the particular gravity of a statement delivered not as opinion but as testimony.

"Not thy title. Not thy designation. Thy name. This I heard even through the silence of the Accord."

Cian's hand tightened on Mo Chrá's hilt. The heartbeat pulsed.

*I was unconscious. I didn't hear it.*

A silence. Not the silence of absence — the silence of a being choosing his next words with the care of someone who had watched civilisations rise and fall and understood precisely which truths could be delivered gently and which could not.

"I did."

Two words. The simplest construction in the Empyreal Register. Subject and verb, carrying the full weight of an archangel's perceptual authority — a being whose senses operated at frequencies that registered the acoustic signature of every heartbeat within his charge's vicinity, who had heard Miriam Ashford say *Cian* — not *Mac Morna*, not the formal address, the *name* — while the man who bore it lay bleeding and silent on Carpathian stone.

Cian stared at the ceiling.

He had been alive for twenty-six centuries. He had survived four marriages, each ending in the particular grief of a man who did not age watching the woman he loved grow old and die while he remained. He had sworn, after the fourth — after Maria, in 1847, during the Famine, when the hunger took her because the blessing that preserved his flesh did not extend to hers — that he was done. That the oath was sealed. That he would not open that door again because the cost of opening it was paid not in years but in the specific cruelty of outliving everyone who stepped through it.

His Iron Age substrate — the cognitive fossil, the ancient bedrock beneath the sediment of empires — said: *she named thee*.

His modern mind said: *stress response. Adrenaline. The amygdala overriding the prefrontal cortex. She reverted to "Mac Morna" the moment composure returned. The name usage was reflexive, not deliberate.*

Both assessments were correct. Neither was sufficient.

"Liaigh."

"I am here."

"You've been waiting twenty-four hours to say this."

"I have been restrained by the terms of a Territorial Accord for twenty-four hours. I have been composing this assessment for considerably longer."

Despite everything — the depletion, the silence, the dead weight of a sword that could barely pulse, the fact that somewhere beneath his chest his heart was beating at fifty-eight instead of its resting forty-two because his body was still paying the price for channelling judgment-fire through Cydonian ore — despite all of it, something in Cian's chest moved. Not the heart. Something adjacent to the heart, in the space where the capacity for laughter had been filed alongside grief and duty and the four names he carried in the vault he never opened.

"You are the worst guardian in celestial history."

"I am the guardian who hath kept thee alive for twenty and six centuries. The woman is a recent complication. A welcome one."

Cian couldn't respond. Not because the words weren't there — they were, lined up in three languages, none of them adequate — but because Mo Chrá chose that precise moment to produce a pulse that was fractionally warmer than the previous ones. Not a hum. Not agreement. But the sword equivalent of a nod — the acknowledgement of a weapon that had spent twenty-six centuries reading her wielder's emotional state through the vibrations of his hands and had heard everything Liaigh said and found it, despite her exhaustion, worth responding to.

He closed his eyes. The engines droned. Miriam's pen scratched on paper. Liaigh's presence settled into its accustomed position in his perception — the steady, ancient pressure that whispered *I am here, I have always been here, and I am not leaving* — and the cargo bay of a C-130J Hercules became, for the first time since the Carpathian mountains, a space in which Cian mac Morna could breathe without counting.

## III. THE OPERATIONAL COMPROMISE

McNeeve Galway smelled like antiseptic and gun oil.

James Madigan considered this appropriate. The safehouse's medical bay occupied the ground floor of a building that had been a coastguard station before McNeeve Industries had acquired it in 2019 and converted it into a facility where people who fought supernatural entities could be treated for injuries that no civilian hospital would understand. The antiseptic was standard. The gun oil was James — he cleaned weapons the way other men drank tea: compulsively, ritually, and with the particular focus of someone who found in the mechanical ritual a form of order that the world outside the rifle's action did not provide.

He was cleaning his SCAR-17 when he decided it was time.

Cian was in the recovery room. Vertical, which was an improvement — he'd been carried to the vehicle and carried from the vehicle and carried into the building, and each instance of *carried* had produced the expression of a man whose dignity was being removed by pliers while his body refused to provide an alternative. The IV was out. The blood pressure was normalising. The sword — propped against the wall beside the bed, silent and dark — had produced what Brennan described as "micro-pulses" on the spectrograph: the acoustic equivalent of a coma patient's eyelid flutter.

Miriam was in the corridor.

She had been in the corridor for six hours. She had showered, changed, eaten half a sandwich that James had left on the console table, and returned to the chair outside the recovery room door with the particular determination of a woman who was not keeping vigil because keeping vigil would imply emotional investment and what she was doing was *monitoring a team member's post-operational recovery status*. The distinction existed only in Miriam Ashford's architecture of self-deception, and James Madigan had not survived thirty years in the special operations community by failing to recognise self-deception when it positioned itself outside a recovery room door for six hours.

He finished the SCAR's bolt carrier group. Reassembled it. Function-checked the action. Set it aside.

He walked into the recovery room and closed the door behind him.

Cian looked up. The look was the one James had catalogued across six years of working with a man who was older than Christianity: the assessment. The instant, comprehensive evaluation that registered James's posture, breathing rate, pupil dilation, and the micro-tells of facial musculature that communicated emotional state, all processed in the fraction of a second it took a normal person to register that someone had entered the room.

"James."

"Mac Morna." James sat in the chair beside the bed. He did not ease into the chair. He occupied it — the way he occupied every space, with the economical precision of a man who understood that comfort was a luxury and efficiency was a discipline. "After-action."

"The Armaros engagement? I'll debrief—"

"Not the Armaros engagement."

A silence. The specific silence of a man whose twenty-six centuries of experience had just informed him of his tactical error — assuming that the after-action review would be about the operation, when the operator sitting across from him had a different action under review entirely.

James did not waste words. He had learned, across three decades in the Regiment and two more in Cian's service, that economy of language was not merely a preference but a tactical doctrine. Words were ammunition. You used exactly as many as the target required and not one more.

"The Tea Incident." He held up a finger. One. "Audio lab, Chapter House. She brought you tea. Your voice changed register when you took it. You don't change register."

A second finger. "Field medical response, sub-foundation. When you went down, she broke formation. An intelligence analyst with twelve years of field survival training abandoned a tactically sound position to reach you. In a chamber full of active-threat contacts."

Third finger. "First-name break. She said 'Cian.' Not 'Mac Morna.' Under duress, in a Watcher domain, with her hands on your wound and her composure in pieces on the floor." James paused. "I don't have audio. I have visual confirmation from my position eleven metres away."

Fourth finger. "The sword. She placed Mo Chrá across your chest. I don't know the cultural significance — I imagine you do — but I know what I saw. She didn't *put* the sword on you. She *placed* it. There's a difference."

Fifth. "She's been in the corridor for six hours. She will not leave this building. She has been asked."

He lowered his hand.

"Two counts of compromised tactical judgment. One breach of her own operational security protocol — the formal address protocol she's maintained since Austin. One act of—" He searched for the word. James Madigan was not a man who searched for words, which made the search itself communicative. "—significance. And six hours of surveillance that she is calling recovery monitoring."

James leaned forward. The posture carried weight that had nothing to do with his frame and everything to do with the thirty years of operational authority compressed into it.

"You are operationally compromised. Both of you. I am stating this as your tactical officer, not as your friend, because the distinction matters. Operational compromise means degraded decision-making under emotional load. It means she will break formation to reach you. It means you will position yourself between her and the threat axis when formation discipline says otherwise. It means that in the next engagement — and there will be a next engagement — one or both of you will make a decision that prioritises the other over the mission."

He paused.

"I've worked with you for six years. I have watched you kill things that would make a theologian question God's creation. I have never seen anything frighten you." James's voice dropped. Not to a whisper — to the flat, tired register of a man delivering an assessment he wished he didn't have to make. "She *terrifies* you. Sort it out before Greenland, or she'll get herself killed trying to save you again. And if that happens, I will shoot you myself. That is not a metaphor."

The room was quiet. The medical bay's fluorescent lighting buzzed at sixty hertz — a frequency Cian could hear and James could not, a difference that encapsulated twenty-six centuries of perceptual divergence in a single electrical hum.

Cian looked at his hands. The hands that had held Mo Chrá. The hands that had failed to complete a sentence in Old Irish because a woman with a penlight had interrupted him. The hands of the most dangerous mortal combatant in history, which were currently doing nothing because their owner had been told to sort out his feelings by a man who could, and would, shoot him.

What came next surprised both of them.

"James." Cian's voice shifted. Not to the operational flat-tone, not to the wry deflection he used to navigate modern emotional conversations. To something older. Quieter. A register that James had never heard — the cadence of a language that predated English by millennia, spoken through English words because the man speaking them had spent long enough in this century to translate on the fly but not long enough to strip the original architecture from his voice.

"In the law I was born under — the law of the Fianna, the law that governed how warriors lived and fought and loved and died in my father's time — a man who wished to court a woman under another man's protection was required to petition the protector. Not the woman. Her protector. The man who answered for her safety. The man who would kill the petitioner if the petition was made in bad faith."

James's expression did not change. It was the expression of a man who had prepared for several dozen possible responses and had not prepared for this one.

"The petition was formal. It was binding. It required the petitioner to declare his intention before a witness — to state, before the woman's guardian, that his interest was honourable, that he understood the weight of what he was asking, and that he accepted the guardian's right to refuse."

Cian met James's eyes.

"You are the closest thing Miriam has to a guardian. You have watched over her. You have protected her. You have answered for her safety in ways that she may not fully know. And you have just told me, in the language of your own century, to sort it out."

A pause. The fluorescents buzzed.

"I am sorting it out. In the language of mine."

James stared at him.

The silence extended for approximately five seconds. In that silence, James Madigan — a man who had been decorated for valour in three theatres, who had survived ambushes and interrogations and the particular horror of friends dying in places whose names were classified — processed the fact that he had walked into a recovery room to deliver a tactical threat and had instead received a formal petition for courtship rights from a man who had been alive since the construction of Solomon's Second Temple.

"You are..." James started. Stopped. Started again. "You are asking me for *permission* to court Miriam Ashford."

"I am declaring my intention and submitting to your authority to refuse it. The distinction matters. In Fianna law, the petitioner does not ask. He declares and offers. The guardian decides."

"I'm not her father."

"No. You're the man who would shoot me if I hurt her. In Fianna terms, that makes you her guardian. The willingness to kill is the qualification."

James sat back. The SCAR-17 was in the next room, freshly cleaned, function-checked, seven hundred and twenty-three confirmed kills across its operational life. The metaphor was not lost on him.

"You're telling me that the formal response to 'sort it out or I'll shoot you' is a *medieval courtship petition*."

"Iron Age. Predates medieval by approximately a millennium."

James looked at the ceiling. Then at the floor. Then at Cian, with the expression of a man who had been given information he had not requested and was now responsible for processing.

"And if I refuse?"

"Then I am bound by the oath to withdraw. No contact beyond operational necessity. No provision. No shield position. The petition is closed and may not be reopened." Cian's voice was steady. "I have not made this petition in nine hundred years. The last time, the guardian was a Norman lord in Connacht whose response was considerably less measured than yours."

"What happened to the Norman lord?"

"He said yes. His daughter and I were married for forty-one years. She died of old age. I did not."

The words landed with the quiet devastation of a fact that could not be argued with because it was not an argument — it was a history. The history of a man who had loved and buried and loved again and buried again until the weight of burial exceeded the weight of love and he had sealed the door and sworn it would remain sealed.

James was quiet for a long time.

"You're not asking me for permission," he said finally. His voice had changed — not softened, James Madigan's voice did not soften, but shifted from the assessment register to something more personal. "You're asking me to hold you accountable."

"Yes."

"Because you've outlived every woman you've loved and you're terrified it will happen again."

Cian did not respond. The response was in the silence.

"I'm not going to refuse." James stood. "I'm not going to say yes. What I'm going to say is this: she makes her own decisions. If she decides she wants you — and the data suggests she's already decided and hasn't admitted it to herself — then my job is to make sure neither of you gets killed because of it. That's not a guardian's ruling. That's an operational assessment."

He moved to the door. Stopped.

"For what it's worth. The petition — the fact that you asked at all, that you submitted instead of just..." He gestured vaguely at the space between them. "That tells me more about you than six years of combat operations."

He opened the door. Paused.

"Sort it out, Mac Morna. I mean it."

The door closed.

Mo Chrá, propped against the wall, produced a pulse. Warmer than the previous ones. Not a hum — the sword was days away from humming — but a distinct, volitional warmth that radiated through the Cydonian ore into the room's ambient temperature. Agreement. Approval. The particular satisfaction of a weapon that had watched its wielder do something stupidly brave that had nothing to do with combat and everything to do with the kind of courage that doesn't require a sword.

Cian sat in the recovery room and listened to his sword learn to breathe again and did not think about the woman in the corridor.

He failed.

## IV. THE GIFT OF PROVISION

The medical bay was quiet at three in the morning.

Quiet in the way that safehouses were quiet when the operation was over and the adrenaline had metabolised and what remained was the particular exhaustion that came not from effort but from the absence of the threat that had been sustaining the effort. James was at the far end of the corridor, reassembling a sidearm with the meditative precision of a man whose hands needed occupation the way his lungs needed air. Brennan was in the signals room, running the Armaros stele data through acoustic analysis software, the glow of three monitors painting his face in the specific blue light of a man who would rather solve problems than process what had happened. Chris Guilder was asleep in a chair in the briefing room, laptop open, glasses folded on the keyboard, his breathing the slow, regular rhythm of someone who had learned in his twenties that sleep was a resource to be seized whenever opportunity presented.

Miriam was in the corridor.

She had not moved. The chair — metal-framed, government-surplus, designed by someone who believed that comfort encouraged complacency — had become a fixed position. She had a mug of tea on the floor beside her, cold. She had her medical notes open on her lap, the pen resting in the crease of the notepad. She was not writing. She was not reading. She was existing in the space outside the recovery room door with the determined endurance of a woman who would not name what she was doing because naming it would require admitting why.

Cian's monitors were blaring red.

Not the critical red of cardiac arrest or respiratory failure — the amber-edged red of a system registering elevated activity in a patient flagged for bed rest. Heart rate climbing from fifty-eight to sixty-four. Blood pressure rising. Movement detected: the particular sensor profile of a man whose body had been told to stay horizontal and whose body was not inclined to obey.

He sat up. The pain was immediate and comprehensive — not localised, not the sharp specificity of a wound, but the full-body depletion of a man who had served as a conduit for acoustic energy that his physiology was never designed to channel. Every joint protested. Every muscle reported damage that was not damage in any medical sense but felt like it in every practical one. His ribs ached where Mo Chrá's acoustic resonance had drawn on the vibrations of his chest cavity. His hands — the hands that had held the sword through the transformation, through the chains, through the fire — trembled.

His exoskeleton's health display, still active on the wrist-mount, offered a detailed summary of his condition in the precise, emotionless language of biomedical telemetry. He ignored it. The display told him things his body had already reported through older channels.

Mo Chrá was against the wall. He looked at her.

Dark. Still. The Cydonian ore that should have been radiating its characteristic teal luminescence was producing nothing visible — the sword's energy reserves at a level so low that even the passive frequencies she maintained during rest had been suspended. But the micro-pulses that Brennan had detected were there. He could feel them through the floor — faint, irregular, the acoustic heartbeat of an instrument that was rebuilding itself one frequency at a time.

She hummed. Once. Low. The particular hum of a sword that had watched its wielder try to stand and had opinions about the decision but lacked the energy to express them fully.

"I know," he said.

He stood. The room tilted. He let it tilt and waited for the tilt to resolve itself, which it did after two seconds — two seconds during which he was grateful that no one was watching, because two seconds of vestibular instability would have provided James with enough ammunition to confine him to bed rest through the application of a lecture so clinical it would qualify as a medical procedure.

He walked to the door. Opened it.

Miriam looked up.

She had not been sleeping. Her eyes were clear — fatigued, but clear, with the particular alertness of a person running on something that was not caffeine and was not adrenaline and was the specific, sustained wakefulness produced by a mind that would not permit itself to rest while the thing it was monitoring remained at risk.

"You should be horizontal."

"You should be asleep."

They regarded each other across the threshold of the recovery room — the particular geometry of two people who had survived a Watcher domain together and had emerged with injuries that medicine could treat and injuries that medicine could not name and were now standing three feet apart in a corridor at three in the morning, each telling the other to rest while neither moved to do so.

Cian did not go back to bed.

He walked past her. Down the corridor, toward the galley — the small kitchen that McNeeve maintained in every safehouse because the proprietors of McNeeve Industries understood that operational personnel needed food and drink at hours when restaurants were closed and civilisation was sleeping and only soldiers and sentries and the sleepless were awake.

The galley had what he needed.

He found the blanket first. Military surplus. Heavy. The kind of blanket that weighed on you and pressed you into the surface beneath you and made the body feel held in a way that had nothing to do with arms and everything to do with mass. It was folded on a shelf above the supply cabinet, and he took it down with hands that protested the reaching and were overruled by intent.

He found the tea. Barry's Gold Blend. It had not been stocked at McNeeve Galway six months ago. It was stocked now. He did not know who had placed the order — whether James had noticed, or Sarah had communicated a preference through the logistics chain, or whether Miriam herself had mentioned it to someone who had acted on the information. He did not care. The tea was here. He boiled water. He brewed it to the strength he had observed her drink eleven times — counted, each one, because the Iron Age substrate catalogued the preferences of people it was responsible for the way it catalogued the behaviours of enemies: compulsively, completely, without conscious decision.

Milk. A specific amount. Not too much. He had watched her adjust the colour against the mug until it reached the particular shade that satisfied her, and he had memorised the shade the way a painter memorised a pigment — not by name but by recognition.

He found bread. Toasted it. Found butter. Applied it. The galley did not offer much at three in the morning, but what it offered was warm and real and the act of preparing it — the simple, physical labour of making food for another person — steadied his hands in a way that medical treatment had not.

He carried it back to the corridor.

Miriam was still in the chair. Her eyes tracked his approach — the mug, the plate, the blanket over his arm. The expression that crossed her face was not readable by any framework he had developed across twenty-six centuries of studying human emotional response, because the expression contained multiple simultaneous states that his training insisted were contradictory: surprise, recognition, gratitude, resistance, and something beneath all of them that lived in the same register as the word she had used in the Carpathians — the name she had called him when she thought he was dying.

He set the mug on the floor beside her chair. Set the plate beside the mug. Unfolded the blanket and draped it across her lap, over her legs, with the care of a man whose hands were shaking from physical depletion and whose hands were not going to be permitted to shake.

He did not sit down. He stood in the corridor — too stiff to bend easily, too stubborn to show it — and looked at her with the expression of a man who had carried four women to their graves and sworn on the last grave that he was done and was now standing in a corridor at three in the morning holding nothing because he had given what he held to the woman in the chair.

"You held the line for me," he said. "Now let me hold it for you."

The words were not poetry. They were not romance. They were the particular idiom of a man who had been born in an age when *holding the line* meant standing between the people you loved and the things that would kill them, and who had spoken those words in that order to exactly four women in twenty-six centuries, and who was saying them now to a fifth because the oath he had sealed in 1847 had crumbled in a Carpathian sub-foundation when a woman who claimed not to believe in miracles had placed his sword across his chest and called him by his name.

Miriam's hands closed around the mug.

The warmth transferred. She felt it — he watched her feel it, watched the heat move from the ceramic through her fingers into the cold that she had been accumulating for six hours in a metal chair in a corridor that was not designed for vigils. She looked at the tea. The colour was right. The shade she adjusted for. The exact ratio of milk to brew that she had never told him and that he had learned by watching because the man who had been watching her was the kind of man who watched, who catalogued, who memorised the shade of a woman's tea the way he memorised the sound of her breathing — because to the Iron Age substrate, these things were not separate. The way a woman took her tea and the sound of her breathing and the way she placed a sword across a wounded man's chest were all data points in the same assessment, and the assessment was one he could not stop making because the capacity to stop had been filed in the vault alongside the four wedding rings and the four names and the four graves, and the vault was open now and he did not know how to close it.

She did not cry. Miriam Ashford did not cry. She had not cried when Victoria died. She had not cried when the Director's people found her in Warsaw. She had not cried in twelve years of running and hiding and fighting for a survival that some days didn't feel worth the effort.

She stopped fighting the tiredness.

It was visible — the exact moment when the resistance left her body. Not collapse, not surrender in the dramatic sense. More like the release of a weight that had been held so long the muscles had forgotten they were holding it. Her shoulders dropped. Her spine settled against the chair back. Her hands wrapped further around the mug and brought it to her lips and drank.

"Thank you," she said. "Mac Morna."

The formal address. The wall. The last brick in a defence that had been rebuilt from the rubble of the Carpathians with the stubborn, magnificent engineering of a woman who refused to be undone by the things she felt. Miriam Ashford would not say *Cian* to him while she was conscious and in possession of her composure. The name she had used in the Carpathians had been torn from her by duress — the amygdala overriding the prefrontal cortex — and she had sealed that breach the way she sealed every breach: by rebuilding the wall higher and pretending the breach had never existed.

He nodded. The nod was acceptance. Not of the wall, but of everything the wall was protecting — the woman behind it, the feelings she would not name, the fact that she had placed his sword across his chest and called him by his name and was now drinking his tea and calling him Mac Morna because the alternative was admitting what she had done and what it meant and that the twelve-year run was ending not with capture but with something she had not been running from and therefore had no protocol to resist.

She ate the toast. She finished the tea. She pulled the blanket higher — the heavy, military-surplus weight of it pressing her into the chair the way gravity pressed you into the earth — and her eyes closed.

She was asleep within minutes.

Cian stood in the corridor and watched the door.

Not her. The door. The threshold between the corridor and whatever existed beyond it — the world outside, the war they were fighting, the next engagement and the next after that and the chain of conflict that extended forward into a future he could not see and backward into a past he could not escape. He watched the door because watching the door was the thing he knew how to do. Provision and protection. The two pillars of the old law. Feed her. Guard her. Do not ask for anything in return because the asking is not the point — the providing is the point, the standing between her and the dark is the point, and the man who stands does not sit down until the woman who sleeps has finished sleeping.

Mo Chrá hummed.

Soft. Tentative. The first sustained frequency she had produced since the Chains — not the battle-cry of combat frequencies, not the analytical mapping of ward architecture, not the warm comedy of romantic commentary. A lullaby. A frequency he had not heard in eight centuries — the gentle, protective oscillation that the sword produced when the person it wished to protect was sleeping and the person it was bonded to was watching and the space between them was safe.

Eight hundred and fifty-seven years since she had last played this frequency. She had spent it then on a woman in Connacht who slept while her husband stood guard and whose husband was the same man standing guard now, in a different corridor, in a different century, for a different woman.

Mo Chrá's first recovered energy — the first sustained expenditure of acoustic reserves rebuilt from the absolute silence of post-Chains depletion — spent not on combat diagnostics, not on ward analysis, not on any function that served the war. Spent on this.

The sword understood priorities.

In his perception, behind his sternum, in the space where the guardian's presence had been restored since the aircraft cleared Romanian airspace, Liaigh spoke.

"WELL DONE."

Two words. Empyreal Register. No elaboration.

The most dangerous mortal combatant in history stood in a corridor at three in the morning and held a line that required no sword and no training and no twenty-six centuries of combat experience. Only the willingness to stand. Only the ancient, simple courage of a man who had finally done the thing that the archangel who had watched him for twenty-six centuries considered genuinely brave.

Not the killing. Not the Chains. Not the fire.

This.

The medical bay was quiet. James, at the far end of the corridor, set down the sidearm he had reassembled and stripped and reassembled again across the past hour. He looked at the scene — the man in the corridor, the woman in the chair, the sword humming from the recovery room — and he said nothing. He did not need to say anything. The after-action was complete.

Brennan's monitors glowed blue in the signals room. Chris Guilder slept with his laptop open. Somewhere above Galway, the sky was turning from black to the deep grey that preceded dawn — the colour of a world that had survived another night and was preparing, without enthusiasm but with persistence, to try again.

Miriam slept. Cian watched the door. Mo Chrá hummed a lullaby she had not played in eight centuries for a woman she had decided, in the ancient and irrevocable grammar of a blade that remembered everything, was worth the energy.

Outside, the Atlantic sounded against the Galway shore — the same frequency it had been producing since before the Watchers fell, since before the Flood, since before any of them were born. The oldest sound. The first lullaby.

It harmonised with the sword.
