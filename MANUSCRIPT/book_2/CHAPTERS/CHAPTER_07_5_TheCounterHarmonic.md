## I. THE ACOUSTIC DECRYPTION

The hilt weighed less than a kilogram.

Marcus Reeves turned it under the signals lab's calibrated lighting and thought about the fact that something weighing less than a kilogram had nearly killed the most dangerous mortal combatant in recorded history. The hilt was carbonite-black, roughly the length of his forearm, the broken terminus where the blade had been still showing the fused crystalline structure of metal that had melted under temperatures no earthly forge could produce. Gehenna fire. Judgment fire. The kind of heat that didn't care about melting points because it operated on a plane where metallurgy was a polite suggestion rather than a law.

The blade was gone. Dragan's cursed greatsword had ceased to exist as a weapon the moment Mo Chrá's Chains of Abyssal Damnation had wrapped it in divine fury. But the hilt had survived — and Marcus understood why. The hilt wasn't a weapon component. It was a *transmitter*.

He set the hilt into the cradle he'd built from acoustic isolation foam and positioned the three directional microphones of the spectral array at precisely one hundred and twenty degrees apart. The array was Brennan's work — designed for Titan rover telemetry, repurposed three days ago for the analysis of a five-thousand-year-old inscription that most of the scientific community would have classified as decorative metalwork.

The inscription spiralled around the grip in tight, angular script. Pre-Flood Enochian. Marcus could not read Pre-Flood Enochian — no living human could, except perhaps the man currently recovering two floors above — but he didn't need to read it. He needed to *hear* it.

That was the foundational principle Brennan had taught him eighteen months ago, when Marcus Reeves had transitioned from retired US Navy acoustic warfare specialist to whatever his current job title was. (His business cards, if he'd had business cards, would have read something like *Signals Analyst, McNeeve Industries — Supernatural Threat Division*, which was the kind of title that made HR departments quietly update their insurance policies.) The principle was simple: in this theatre of operations, everything was sound. The wards weren't magic shields. They weren't energy barriers. They weren't the luminous force fields of science fiction. They were *sustained acoustic standing waves* — frequency structures maintained by Cydonian metamaterial that created regions of destructive interference calibrated to specific targets.

And this inscription was the sheet music.

Marcus activated the array and began the excitation protocol — a low-frequency sweep from 0.5 Hz to 200 Hz, the range in which Cydonian materials were known to respond. The hilt sat in its cradle like a patient on a diagnostic table: inert, passive, revealing nothing to the naked eye.

At 6.7 Hz, the inscription began to glow.

Not brightly. Not the teal-gold luminescence of Mo Chrá's combat engagement. A cold, grey-violet phosphorescence that crept along the carved lines like frost forming on glass. Marcus had seen this colour once before — in Brennan's analysis of the Armaros stele data. It was the spectral signature of *negation*. Ward-craft. Counter-creation frequency work.

He swept higher. At 7.83 Hz — the Schumann resonance, Earth's electromagnetic heartbeat, the frequency that Brennan had identified as Mo Chrá's baseline creation harmonic — the inscription *screamed*.

Not audibly. The signal was subsonic, well below the threshold of human hearing. But the spectral display erupted in a cascade of frequency data so dense that the screen looked like a seismograph during a magnitude-eight earthquake. Marcus killed the excitation sweep and let the data settle. His hands were steady. His pulse was elevated — seventy-two beats per minute, which for Marcus Reeves constituted mild excitement. Thirty years of submarine service had taught him that the appropriate response to discovering something that could change the outcome of a war was to take careful notes.

He took careful notes.

The data resolved over twenty minutes into a structure he recognised: a point-for-point acoustic cancellation formula. Every frequency Mo Chrá was known to produce — her baseline hum, her combat harmonics, her ward-breaking resonance, the creation-frequency overtone series that made Nephilim physiology come apart at the molecular level — had a corresponding *anti-frequency* encoded in the inscription. Phase-inverted. Amplitude-matched. Timing-calibrated to within nanoseconds.

It was, Marcus reflected, the most elegant piece of acoustic warfare he had ever encountered. And he had spent twelve years hunting Soviet submarines by their propeller cavitation signatures.

Armaros had not been a crude practitioner. The chief of ward-craft had built his anti-Mo Chrá protocol the way a master cryptographer builds a cipher: not by brute force, but by *understanding the target intimately enough to predict its every output*. The Watchers had known Mo Chrá's frequencies for five thousand years. They had been listening since Methuselah's era. And Armaros had turned that intelligence into a weapon — a frequency formula that, when sustained by Cydonian metamaterial, would create a region of absolute destructive interference around the sword. A dead zone. A place where Mo Chrá's voice simply... ceased.

That was what Dragan's wards had done in the hexagonal chamber. Not blocked the sword. Not deflected her. *Silenced* her.

Marcus saved the data to three separate encrypted drives, because redundancy was not paranoia when the data in question represented the only known counter to the most powerful weapon on Earth.

He didn't need to call Brennan. Brennan was already in the doorway.

The kid — Marcus still thought of him as the kid, despite the fact that Brennan McNeeve held more patents than most R&D departments and had designed every piece of equipment in this facility — had been running his own analysis of the Armaros stele data three floors up. He'd seen Marcus's excitation sweep register on the shared acoustic monitoring network. He'd come down the stairs fast enough that he was slightly out of breath, which for Brennan constituted an emotional display roughly equivalent to another man setting his hair on fire.

"That's a point-for-point cancellation architecture," Brennan said, looking at the spectral display over Marcus's shoulder. Not a question. The kid read acoustic data the way Marcus read sonar returns: instantly, completely, the pattern resolving into meaning before the conscious mind had finished registering the shape. "Phase-inverted against every known Mo Chrá frequency. Every harmonic. Even the overtone series." He leaned closer to the screen. "That's not brute force. That's five thousand years of listening."

"Armaros," Marcus said.

"Armaros." Brennan straightened. His expression had shifted from analytical to something darker — the particular quality of a man looking at an engineering solution designed to neutralise the weapon his uncle carried and understanding, with the intimate knowledge of someone who had been studying that weapon since childhood, exactly how effective it would have been. "This is what stopped her in the hexagonal chamber. This is what Dragan's wards were running."

"And now we have the sheet music."

Brennan was quiet for three seconds. Marcus counted. Then Brennan said: "JW needs to see this. And Mo Chrá needs to hear it."

Marcus picked up the internal phone and called Sub-Level 2.

"Chen. I've got the frequency. You're going to want to see this."

"On my way." JW Chen's voice was the same temperature it always was: room. Calm. The vocal equivalent of a spirit level.

Brennan was already calling the recovery floor on his mobile. "Cian. Marcus has decoded the hilt. I need you to bring Mo Chrá to the signals lab." A pause. "She doesn't need to fight. She needs to listen."

Mo Chrá arrived in the arms of Cian mac Morna, which was the only way Mo Chrá arrived anywhere. The sword did not permit casual handling — a fact that Brennan had learned during Year One of the programme, when he'd attempted to move the blade from a workbench and found it had become heavier than a car engine, immovable, a dead mass of Cydonian ore that refused to acknowledge any hand but one. The moment Cian had touched the hilt, the sword had weighed six pounds again. The message had been unambiguous and required no repetition.

Cian carried her the way a father carries a sleeping child: close, careful, the particular tenderness of a man transporting something irreplaceable through a world that didn't understand its value. And the sword — silent, depleted, running on reserves that amounted to faint warmth and little else — hummed a single sustained note as they entered the lab. Not for the equipment. Not for the room. A note that Marcus's instruments registered as *arrival* — the sword confirming, to herself and to her wielder, that wherever he was, she was, and wherever she was, he was, and the twenty-six centuries of that mutual sentence had not yet produced a moment where either party wished to renegotiate the terms.

He looked like a man who should still be in a hospital bed. His colour was wrong — grey beneath the bronze that twenty-six centuries of outdoor living had baked into his skin. His left hand, the one not holding the sword, tremored faintly at his side. The Chains of Abyssal Damnation had drawn on his acoustic resonance, the vibrational bond between wielder and weapon, and what remained was a man running on reserves he shouldn't have been using yet.

But his eyes were clear. And when Marcus explained what he had found, those eyes went very cold, very still, in the way that Marcus had learned to associate with Cian processing tactical information at a speed that human cognitive architecture was not designed to sustain.

"Show me," Cian said.

Marcus played the cancellation formula through the lab's calibrated speakers at one-tenth amplitude — enough to demonstrate the structure, not enough to cause interference with Mo Chrá's recovering systems. The sound was sub-audible. The spectral display showed it: the precise anti-phase pattern that would silence every frequency the sword could produce.

Mo Chrá's hum — the low, convalescent murmur she'd been sustaining since the lullaby — *stuttered*.

It was brief. A fraction of a second. The acoustic equivalent of a flinch. The sword had heard her own weakness played back to her, and for one instant, her voice had faltered.

Cian's hand tightened on the hilt.

Then Mo Chrá did something that made Marcus reach for his notation pad with the urgency of a man watching a test subject deviate from every predicted model.

She *studied* it.

The sword's hum shifted. Not louder — she didn't have the reserves for louder. But *different*. The spectral display showed her baseline frequency begin to modulate: micro-adjustments, exploratory deviations, the acoustic equivalent of a cryptographer running permutations against an intercepted cipher. She was testing the cancellation formula. Finding its architecture. Mapping its seams.

Marcus watched the display and understood what he was seeing because he had spent twelve years doing exactly the same thing aboard USS *Louisville*: the sword was developing countermeasures.

In submarine warfare, the protocol was called acoustic deception — generating frequencies that pre-empted an enemy's sonar, arriving at the hydrophone before the return echo could establish a lock. ECM. Electronic countermeasures. Except this was acoustic countermeasures, and the platform wasn't a Los Angeles-class submarine but a sentient blade forged from Martian stone by a prophet who had walked with God.

Mo Chrá's modulations stabilised. The spectral display showed a new pattern emerging — not her standard combat harmonics, not the creation-frequency overtone series, but something novel. A *pre-emptive counter-frequency*. A signal designed to arrive at the ward's standing-wave structure before the cancellation pattern could establish coherent destructive interference. If Armaros's formula was a jammer, Mo Chrá had just built the jammer's jammer.

She would never be silenced by that specific ward architecture again.

The hum settled. Warmer now. The particular frequency that Marcus had catalogued, with Navy-grade precision, as *satisfied*.

Brennan had not moved during the entire ninety seconds. He had stood at the spectral display with his arms folded and his breathing shallow and watched the sword develop autonomous countermeasures against a five-thousand-year-old acoustic weapons system in less time than it took to make coffee. Now he exhaled — a single, controlled breath that carried the weight of a man who had been studying Mo Chrá's acoustic properties since he was nineteen and had just witnessed her do something that invalidated half his theoretical models.

"I've been trying to map her adaptive frequency range for three years," Brennan said quietly. "I had her categorised as responsive-passive — she reacts to stimuli, adjusts her output to match the environment, but doesn't generate novel frequency structures independently." He looked at the spectral data. "I was wrong. She's responsive-*creative*. She heard a threat and *invented* a solution. That's not an instrument. That's an intelligence."

"She learned," Cian said. His expression had not changed — still the cold tactical assessment of a man who had been fighting a war longer than most civilisations had existed — but something behind his eyes had shifted.

"In approximately ninety seconds," Marcus confirmed.

Brennan shook his head. Not disagreement. Recalibration. "The counter-frequency needs to be documented and archived. If she can do this for Armaros-class wards, she can potentially do it for any ward architecture — given exposure to the cancellation formula. We need to be collecting ward data from every domain we enter. Every hilt. Every inscription. Every piece of Watcher hardware. Feed her the data and let her build the countermeasures."

It was the right tactical conclusion, and Marcus watched Cian arrive at it independently — the brief focus behind the eyes, the fractional nod that meant *yes, that changes the collection priority for every future operation*.

Then, very quietly, Cian said: "Good girl."

Mo Chrá's hum rose a fractional semitone. Smug. Even depleted, she could manage smug.

## II. THE WARD-PIERCING ROUND

Dr. Jian-Wei Chen had seventeen patents, four of which were classified, and a resting heart rate of fifty-six beats per minute that did not vary regardless of whether he was filing intellectual property documentation or engineering ammunition designed to kill entities that predated human civilisation by several millennia. He considered this consistency a professional asset. Emotional volatility introduced error margins. Error margins killed operators.

He reviewed Marcus's frequency data on the tablet propped against the CVD chamber's control panel and understood the engineering problem in the time it took him to read the spectral summary twice. The current Tintreach specification was devastating against unshielded Nephilim physiology — the GaPO₄ piezoelectric core generated an electrical discharge on impact deformation that interfered with the acoustic coherence holding hybrid flesh together. Against standard targets, the round was more than sufficient. Cian mac Morna had expended eighty-seven rounds of standard Tintreach 7.62 in the inner ward phase of the Carpathian assault, and every one of them had performed within the design envelope.

But the design envelope assumed *unshielded* targets.

Against Armaros-class wards — the sustained acoustic standing waves that had stopped Mo Chrá's combat frequency cold in the hexagonal chamber, that had allowed Voivode Dragan to stand against a weapon that had destroyed a million supernatural entities across five millennia — the standard round's generic piezoelectric discharge was noise. Undifferentiated. Untuned. The acoustic equivalent of shouting into a hurricane.

The wards were tuned. The ammunition needed to match.

JW pulled up the Tintreach manufacturing protocol on the workshop's secure terminal and identified the intervention point. The GaPO₄ crystal core was grown from blessed seed material in a modified chemical vapour deposition chamber — one of three such chambers on Earth, this one installed at the Galway workshop six months ago when Brennan had moved the primary R&D operation out of Austin. The crystal-growth process took fourteen hours per batch. During that window, the crystal's internal lattice structure was plastic — responsive to external stimuli. Impressionable. The molecular equivalent of wet clay.

He outlined his approach to Brennan over the workshop's internal comm — JW did not begin modifications to another engineer's manufacturing protocols without consultation, and the Tintreach specification was Brennan's design. The conversation took four minutes. Brennan listened, asked two questions about acoustic excitation amplitude tolerances during nucleation ("Stay below 0.6% of the crystal's fracture threshold or you'll introduce micro-voids in the lattice — I lost a batch that way in Austin"), approved the modification, and offered one addition: the excitation array should cycle the cancellation frequency in a descending amplitude envelope rather than a constant signal, to allow the crystal lattice to *settle* around the frequency memory rather than being forced into it. The distinction was subtle. The difference in piezoelectric fidelity, Brennan estimated, would be approximately twelve percent. JW incorporated the refinement without comment, because twelve percent was twelve percent.

If he exposed the growing crystals to the cancellation frequency Marcus had isolated — played it into the CVD chamber at Brennan's descending amplitude envelope during the nucleation phase — the crystal lattice would incorporate the frequency into its piezoelectric response matrix. On impact deformation, instead of producing a generic electrical discharge, the modified crystal would emit the *specific* disruption frequency that unravelled Armaros-class ward architecture.

The round would shatter the ward first. Then it would kill what the ward had been protecting.

JW began the retooling. He worked with the systematic calm of a man who processed the world in tolerances and specifications rather than implications and theology. The brass was Lapua — unchanged. The primers were Federal 215M — unchanged. The powder charge was H50BMG at 112.5 grains — unchanged. The turned-copper penetrator with its hollow base — unchanged. The only modification was invisible: a bias in the crystal lattice, a frequency memory encoded at the molecular level, a piezoelectric whisper that would speak the exact acoustic phrase that made Armaros-class wards come apart.

He loaded the first seed crystal into the CVD chamber, connected Marcus's frequency data to the chamber's acoustic excitation array, and initiated the growth cycle. Fourteen hours. He would have four batches before the team departed for Greenland.

While the first batch grew, JW calculated yield. Each crystal provided core material for fifteen rounds. Four batches. Sixty rounds total — thirty in .408 CheyTac for Bás Mór, thirty in 7.62×51 NATO for Bás Beag.

Sixty. Against an enemy that had spent five thousand years fortifying positions across two planets and a sub-Antarctic prison complex.

JW noted the number in his manufacturing log without editorial comment. The number was what it was. His job was to make sixty perfect rounds, not to wish for six hundred. Wishing introduced emotional variables. Emotional variables introduced error margins.

He checked the CVD chamber's temperature gradient, confirmed the acoustic excitation amplitude was holding within 0.3% of target, and began preparing the tooling for the next phase.

The first batch emerged fourteen hours later, at 0340 on Day 4 of the recovery window. JW had slept for five hours on the workshop cot — the precise minimum his body required for cognitive function within acceptable parameters — and was standing at the CVD chamber when the growth cycle completed.

The crystals were visually identical to standard Tintreach cores. Same pale translucency. Same dimensional profile within the manufacturing tolerance of ±0.002mm. The difference was inaudible, invisible, and would only manifest at the moment of ballistic impact when the crystal deformed under approximately 45,000 PSI of instantaneous compressive force and the biased lattice structure converted that mechanical stress into a frequency that had been designed to kill a specific kind of shield.

JW loaded the first crystal into a penetrator housing, seated it in the viscoelastic polymer dampening matrix, and placed the completed round on the inspection bench. He picked up his calipers. He measured. He weighed. He recorded.

857.3 grains. Identical to standard Tintreach. The acoustic modification added zero mass.

He labelled the round — a single mark etched into the base of the brass with a diamond-tipped scriber. Two parallel lines. The mark meant nothing to anyone who wasn't JW Chen. It meant *this one breaks wards*.

He had fifty-nine more to build.

## III. THE ADAPTIVE SHEATH

The sheath problem had been bothering JW since he'd reviewed the after-action telemetry from the Carpathians.

Mo Chrá's current dorsal housing was a fixed-geometry magnetic mount integrated into the exoskeleton's back plate. It had been designed — competently, by Brennan — for a single blade profile: the bastard sword configuration that Cian deployed as his standard combat form. The mount performed its function adequately within that single-form assumption.

The assumption was now obsolete.

Mo Chrá had four confirmed tactical configurations. Bastard sword: forty-four inches, the compromise form, the most frequently deployed. Tantō: fourteen inches, close-quarters and concealment. Great sword: fifty-four inches, the original default, rarely used in modern engagements due to concealment constraints. And the Chains of Abyssal Damnation: a weapon form that did not conform to any blade geometry because it was not a blade — it was a chain-flail system of variable extension that redistributed the sword's entire mass into linked segments of Cydonian ore burning with Gehenna fire.

The dorsal mount could house the bastard sword. It could, with excessive dead space, house the tantō. It could not house the great sword without protruding seventeen inches above the operator's head, which was an unacceptable tactical profile. And it could not accommodate the Chains configuration at all, because there was nothing to sheathe — the weapon's mass was distributed across ten to twenty feet of articulated links.

The sword needed a sheath that could think.

JW had been considering the problem in background processing — the cognitive state he maintained for non-urgent engineering challenges, allocating approximately fifteen percent of his attention to the problem while the remaining eighty-five percent ran the Tintreach Mk II production line. The solution had crystallised at 0200 on Day 3, while he was calibrating the CVD chamber's thermal gradient, and he had written the specification in eleven minutes.

The material was the key.

Brennan's stele replica programme had produced a formulation designated in the workshop logs as *8% Cydonian obsidian composite* — a blend of terrestrial carbon-fibre matrix impregnated with ground Cydonian metamaterial recovered from the Hermon debris field. At eight percent concentration, the composite exhibited measurable acoustic memory properties: it responded to frequency input by altering its internal crystalline alignment, which produced corresponding changes in mechanical behaviour. Rigidity. Flexibility. Thermal coefficient. The material *listened* and *adapted*.

At eight percent, these changes were subtle. Brennan had used the composite for stele replica housings, where the acoustic memory properties allowed the replicas to resonate sympathetically with the original Cydonian ore. The application was passive. Static. The composite sat in a fixed geometry and vibrated in response to external stimulus.

JW proposed to make it move.

When he explained the concept to Brennan — standing over the workshop bench at 0100 on Day 3, the specification handwritten on engineering paper because JW composed better on paper than on screens — Brennan's response was a silence that lasted long enough for JW to wonder if the proposal had exceeded some boundary of the composite's capability that he had not been briefed on.

"I tested the eight-percent formulation at twelve percent once," Brennan said. "In Austin. Before the dissolution. At twelve percent, the composite didn't just vibrate in response to acoustic input — it *moved*. Changed shape. The panel I was testing contracted by six millimetres along one axis when I ran a sustained frequency through it." He looked at JW's specification. "I filed the observation and went back to eight percent because I didn't need the material to move. I needed it to resonate."

"I need it to move," JW said.

"Then you don't need twelve percent. You need eight percent and a sustained carrier frequency. The movement response at twelve was uncontrolled — the material responded to every ambient frequency in the room. At eight, with a locked carrier wave, you can control *which* frequency triggers the adaptive response." Brennan traced the ferrite ring array in JW's drawing. "This is the control mechanism. The 7.83 Hz locks the composite into a listening state. Mo Chrá's frequency change becomes the only input that triggers adaptation. Everything else is filtered out."

It was, JW reflected, a more elegant solution than higher concentration. Brennan understood his own materials. JW incorporated the insight and began construction.

He began construction on Day 3 of the recovery window, twelve hours after the first Tintreach Mk II batch entered the CVD chamber. The sheath's primary structure was a dorsal housing formed from the 8% composite, dimensioned to the bastard sword's profile as the default rest state. The composite panels were layered in a pattern JW had adapted from shape-memory alloy research he'd conducted at MIT fourteen years ago — overlapping segments connected by flexible composite joints, each segment capable of independent acoustic response.

The power source was elegant in its simplicity, and JW permitted himself a rare moment of professional satisfaction when the mathematics confirmed his initial estimate.

Earth rotated. This was not a controversial observation. Earth's rotation generated a geomagnetic field — a planetary-scale magnetic phenomenon that permeated every cubic metre of the planet's surface environment. The field was weak by engineering standards: twenty-five to sixty-five microteslas, depending on latitude. Negligible for most applications.

But ferrite was not most materials.

JW integrated a ring array of manganese-zinc ferrite cores into the sheath's mounting brackets — eight rings, each twelve millimetres in diameter, positioned at the structural nodes where the sheath interfaced with the exoskeleton's back plate. The ferrite cores harvested energy from planetary magnetic rotation through electromagnetic induction: the rotating field induced current in the ferrite, the current drove a piezoelectric transducer bonded to each ring, and the transducer converted the electrical energy into a sustained acoustic output.

The output frequency: 7.83 Hz.

The Schumann resonance. Earth's fundamental electromagnetic frequency. And, as Brennan had documented in the acoustic paradigm files, Mo Chrá's baseline creation harmonic.

The sheath would sing to the sword. Quietly. Constantly. At exactly the frequency that defined what the sword *was*.

No batteries. No charging cycles. No maintenance. No external power supply. As long as the planet rotated, the ferrite rings would harvest, the transducers would convert, and the sheath would resonate at 7.83 Hz. Unlimited. Passive. Permanent.

The mechanism was straightforward: when Mo Chrá shape-shifted, her acoustic output changed. Each form produced a distinct frequency signature — the bastard sword's steady combat hum, the tantō's compressed high-frequency whisper, the great sword's deep resonant authority. The Chains, presumably, produced something entirely different, though JW had not yet obtained a reference recording. When the sword's frequency changed, the 8% Cydonian composite *heard* the change through the acoustic feedback loop sustained by the 7.83 Hz carrier wave. The composite's acoustic memory properties engaged: crystalline alignment shifted, mechanical behaviour adapted, and the sheath's physical geometry reconfigured to match the new blade profile.

Sword changes shape. Frequency changes. Sheath hears the frequency. Sheath adapts.

Input, process, output. The same architecture Chris Guilder had built for the Titan autonomous navigation suite, JW noted. The same architecture that appeared, at increasingly large scales, in everything Cydonian. The Watchers had not been creative engineers. They had been *recursive* ones — applying a single elegant principle at every scale from ammunition to planetary transit networks.

JW finished the sheath assembly at 1940 on Day 4. He placed it on the workbench and examined it with the detached critical assessment he applied to all his work. The sheath was matte black — the Cydonian composite absorbed light the way Cydonian ore absorbed sound, with a thoroughness that suggested the material considered reflection to be a form of inefficiency. The mounting brackets gleamed faintly where the ferrite rings were seated. The segmented panels overlapped with a precision that left no visible gaps in the default bastard-sword configuration.

He activated the ferrite array by connecting the mounting brackets to a test jig that simulated the exoskeleton's magnetic interface. The rings began harvesting immediately — Earth's rotation did not require an invitation — and within four seconds, the sheath began to hum.

7.83 Hz. Sub-audible. But JW felt it through his fingertips on the workbench surface: a vibration so fundamental that it seemed less like a manufactured frequency and more like the sheath had found a station it had always been tuned to receive.

He called up to the recovery floor.

"I need Mo Chrá again. And this time I need her to shift."

Cian brought the sword. Brennan came with him — not because JW had asked, but because the sheath was being built from Brennan's composite formulation and Brennan did not delegate quality assurance on his own materials. He positioned himself at Marcus's terminal, where the acoustic monitoring array would give him real-time data on how his 8% composite behaved under conditions he had never tested.

Cian looked marginally better than he had twenty hours ago — colour returning, tremor reduced, the particular expression of a man whose body was rebuilding systems it shouldn't have had to rebuild. He held Mo Chrá with the same protective care, but the sword's hum was stronger now: a low, sustained tone that Marcus, monitoring from his terminal on the far side of the workshop, recorded at 3.2 dB above her output during the acoustic decryption session.

She was recovering. Slowly. But measurably.

JW presented the sheath. He did not describe it as revolutionary, or elegant, or a breakthrough. He described it as "an adaptive dorsal housing using 8% Cydonian composite with passive ferrite-array power at 7.83 Hz." He described the mechanism in four sentences. He described the expected behaviour in three.

Cian looked at the sheath. Then at Mo Chrá. Then at JW.

"You're asking her to trust a new scabbard."

"I'm asking her to test a prototype."

"That's the same thing. She's had seventeen scabbards since 1900. She hated all of them."

JW said nothing. He had learned, in three days at the Galway facility, that conversations involving Mo Chrá's preferences operated on a social protocol he did not fully understand and did not need to. His job was engineering. The sword's job was opinion.

Cian slid Mo Chrá into the sheath.

The composite responded instantly. The 7.83 Hz resonance, already sustaining through the ferrite array, met Mo Chrá's baseline hum — and the two frequencies *locked*. Phase alignment. Constructive interference. The sheath and the sword found each other's frequency and synchronised with a precision that JW's instruments measured at ±0.001 Hz deviation.

Mo Chrá's hum changed. Warmer. The particular tonal shift that everyone in the facility had learned to interpret as the sword equivalent of settling into a comfortable chair.

"Tantō, please," JW said. He addressed the sword directly. Marcus had warned him that politeness was not optional.

A pause. Mo Chrá's hum modulated — the brief, evaluative frequency that meant she was considering the request. Then the blade shimmered.

The bastard sword contracted. Forty-four inches became fourteen. The mass redistributed along the shortened profile with the liquid precision of Cydonian metamaterial responding to its own acoustic memory. And the sheath — the 8% composite housing — *followed*. The segmented panels compressed, the flexible joints articulated, and the dorsal mount smoothly reconfigured from a forty-four-inch housing to a fourteen-inch compact cradle. No lag. No mechanical articulation visible to the eye. The composite simply *became* the correct shape, the way water becomes the shape of its container.

JW checked his instruments. The acoustic feedback loop had completed the adaptation cycle in 0.7 seconds. The sheath's internal geometry matched the tantō profile within 0.3mm tolerance. The 7.83 Hz carrier wave had not been interrupted.

"Bastard sword," JW said.

Mo Chrá shifted back. The sheath expanded. Same speed. Same precision. Same seamless acoustic handshake between blade and housing.

"Great sword."

A longer pause. Mo Chrá's hum carried a note of reluctance — she had not deployed the great sword form in years, and apparently considered the request slightly presumptuous from someone she had known for less than seventy-two hours. But she shifted. Fifty-four inches. The sheath extended, the composite panels spreading with the unhurried confidence of a material that had been waiting for exactly this input.

JW recorded the data. Three forms tested. Three successful adaptations. One form remaining.

"The Chains configuration," he said. "Whenever you're ready."

Mo Chrá produced a single pulse of warmth through the sheath — a vibration that JW's instruments registered at 7.83 Hz with a sharp amplitude spike. The frequency equivalent of a firm, clear, unambiguous *no*.

JW noted this in his manufacturing log: *Chains configuration test declined by platform. Sword reserves insufficient for full-spectrum shape-shift. Test deferred to field deployment.*

He did not press the issue. Pressing the issue with Mo Chrá was, according to every operational briefing he had received, an exercise in futility that produced no useful data and considerable interpersonal friction with a sentient weapon that held grudges across geological timescales.

"Understood," JW said. To the sword. With the same professional courtesy he would extend to any test subject who had declined a procedure on medical grounds.

Mo Chrá's hum settled. Not warm — not the frequency she reserved for Cian's hand on the hilt, or the lullaby she'd hummed while he watched Miriam sleep. This was cooler. Precise. The tonal equivalent of a senior officer acknowledging that a subordinate had followed correct procedure. *Acceptable*, the frequency said. *You may continue to exist in my proximity.*

JW noted the distinction without understanding it. Marcus, who had been cataloguing Mo Chrá's acoustic responses for eighteen months, understood it perfectly. The sword had a hierarchy. Cian occupied the summit — twenty-six centuries of bonded partnership, a resonance so deeply embedded in the Cydonian ore that separating sword from wielder would require breaking something more fundamental than physics. Miriam, by evidence of the Carpathian weight-reflex and the bean-choimhdeachta placement that the sword had accepted without objection, occupied some newly created tier that Marcus suspected Mo Chrá was still in the process of defining. Everyone else — including a man with seventeen patents and the courtesy to say *please* — occupied the tier marked *tolerated*.

The sword was not democratic. The sword was not even particularly fair. The sword belonged to Cian mac Morna in a manner that predated and transcended every human concept of ownership, and every interaction she permitted with anyone else was a concession granted on her terms, revocable without notice, and calibrated to remind the recipient that they were being *allowed*.

JW had been allowed. He had been allowed because he was polite, because his engineering was sound, and because the sheath he had built sang to her at the correct frequency. But if he mistook tolerance for affection — if he reached for the hilt without Cian's presence, or presumed to move her without invitation — Marcus was reasonably certain the sword would remind him of the distinction with a weight-shift that would pin his hand to the workbench until someone fetched her wielder.

It was during the post-test analysis, while JW was reviewing the acoustic data from all three adaptation cycles, that he found the anomaly.

The sheath's 7.83 Hz resonance was not merely synchronising with Mo Chrá's baseline hum. It was *feeding* it. The ferrite-harvested frequency, sustained by planetary rotation, was being absorbed by the sword's Cydonian ore through the composite interface. Passively. Continuously. The sword was sitting in a bath of her own fundamental frequency, reflected back to her by the housing.

JW checked the numbers. Mo Chrá's acoustic output had increased by 0.4 dB during the forty-minute test session. Not from combat engagement. Not from volitional effort. From *passive absorption*.

The sheath was a trickle charger. The sword was recharging.

JW had not designed this. The materials had done it themselves — the Cydonian composite's acoustic memory properties creating a feedback loop that returned the sword's own baseline frequency to her through the medium of the sheath.

Brennan, still at the monitoring terminal, had seen the numbers before JW. He was quiet in a different way than he had been during the counter-frequency revelation — not recalibrating, but *recognising*. The expression of a man watching his own materials do something he had always suspected they could but had never been able to prove.

"The Cydonian ore remembers what it was," Brennan said. "It came from a place where entire structures were tuned to specific frequencies. The obsidian on Mars didn't just *witness* the Watchers' oath — it resonated with it. It was part of a system. When you give the composite a frequency to lock onto..." He trailed off. Looked at the sword humming in her new housing. "She's not just sitting in a sheath. She's sitting in a fragment of the system she was forged from. The composite recognises her. And it's trying to bring her back to full output."

JW noted the finding in his log. He did not write anything poetic about it. JW Chen did not write poetic things. He wrote: *Observed: passive acoustic energy transfer, sheath to blade, +0.4 dB / 40 min. Mechanism: ferrite-sustained 7.83 Hz resonance absorbed by Cydonian ore via composite interface. Emergent, not designed. Brennan hypothesis: composite Cydonian memory recognises sword's Cydonian signature and sustains sympathetic energy transfer. Plausible. Monitor in field deployment.*

He closed the log and began cleaning his workstation.

## IV. THE PROPULSIVE EXO-ENHANCEMENT

Hal Okonkwo had the exoskeleton on the floor of the vehicle bay in forty-seven pieces.

This was not damage assessment. This was taxonomy. Each component laid out on oil-stained cardboard in the order of its removal — outer plates on the left, hydraulic subsystems in the centre, structural frame on the right, wiring harness and sensor bundles along the far wall. A mechanic's grammar. Hal had been disassembling complex systems since he was twelve years old, stripping his uncle's diesel generators in Lagos with tools borrowed from the auto shop next door, and the principle had never changed: you didn't understand something until you could see every piece of it at once.

The Heavy Combat Variant exoskeleton had come back from the Carpathians in a state that told its own after-action report. The left lateral plate — the section covering the ribs between the lowest floating rib and the hip — was shattered. Not cracked. *Shattered.* The ceramoplastic composite had delaminated in a pattern that Hal recognised from materials failure analysis: not impact fracture, not thermal stress, but *acoustic disruption* — the polymer matrix had lost molecular cohesion at the bond level, as though something had convinced the material to stop being a material. The cursed greatsword's ward inscriptions had done this. Hal did not pretend to understand the acoustic physics. He understood the result: a forty-centimetre section of Level IV-equivalent protection had been reduced to laminated confetti.

The left shoulder pauldron was worse. The ceramoplastic hadn't shattered — it had *compressed*. The grip that had found the vulnerability gap between pauldron and upper arm plate had applied force sufficient to exceed the material's designed tolerances by a factor that Hal estimated, conservatively, at three. The layered composite had accordion-folded along the lamination planes, each ply crushing into the one beneath it until the twelve-millimetre-thick pauldron was approximately four millimetres thick and had the structural integrity of wet cardboard.

Hal picked up the compressed section and turned it in his hands. His hands were large — large enough that colleagues who didn't know him assumed they were suited only to heavy work, an assumption that lasted exactly until they watched him thread an M2 fastener into a Titan rover's sample collection mechanism at arm's length while lying on his back in a Faraday cage. He had designed those sample mechanisms. He had designed all the mechanisms. Every articulated limb, every drill assembly, every suspension system that allowed a two-tonne rover to navigate Martian terrain autonomously. The Titan programme had given him the most complex mechanical systems ever deployed off-planet.

This exoskeleton was more interesting.

Brennan stood at the edge of Hal's cardboard taxonomy and said nothing. He had been there since Hal began the disassembly — had arrived in the vehicle bay ten minutes after Hal started pulling plates, drawn by the particular instinct of an engineer who knows when someone is taking his work apart. He had not interfered. He had not offered commentary. He had watched Hal lay out forty-seven pieces of a system Brennan had designed from scratch and said nothing, because Brennan McNeeve understood the difference between an engineer who was disassembling your work to criticise it and an engineer who was disassembling your work to understand it. Hal was the latter. Brennan respected the process enough to let it happen in silence.

Hal set the pauldron section aside and pulled up the combat telemetry on his tablet. Brennan had archived the full sensor suite output from the Dragan engagement — every accelerometer reading, every load-cell measurement, every frame of the HUD's motion-capture data that had recorded Cian's kinematics throughout the fight. Hal had spent twenty hours reviewing the data. He had watched the fight seven times. He had opinions.

The exoskeleton's hydraulic articulation was excellent. Brennan's engineering was clean — the servo-hydraulic joints delivered consistent torque amplification across the full range of human motion, the response latency was under twelve milliseconds at every joint, and the power-to-weight ratio was competitive with anything DARPA had publicly disclosed. For a human operator fighting human-scale threats, the system was more than adequate.

Cian mac Morna was not fighting human-scale threats.

The telemetry told the story in numbers. Dragan — nine feet tall, mass estimated from the load-cell data at approximately three hundred and forty kilograms, moving at speeds that the accelerometers had recorded as *error: value exceeds calibrated range* — had closed three metres in 0.19 seconds. Cian's reaction time, from the first accelerometer spike to the initiation of his defensive movement, was 0.08 seconds. Exceptional. Superhuman, by any reasonable definition. But the exoskeleton's mass — fifty-three kilograms of ceramoplastic, hydraulics, weapons mounts, and ammunition — had limited his explosive displacement to 0.7 metres in the available time window.

He had needed 1.4 metres. The difference was the wound.

Hal highlighted the engagement frames where Cian had attempted lateral evasion. The hydraulics had performed within spec. The servos had responded. The operator had reacted with reflexes honed across twenty-six centuries of combat. And the physics had betrayed all of it, because fifty-three kilograms of armour obeyed Newton's second law regardless of how fast the man inside it could think.

The problem was not the armour. The problem was *inertia*.

Hal set the tablet down and looked at the forty-seven pieces of exoskeleton laid out on his cardboard taxonomy and thought about the Mars InSight lander's descent stage. Specifically, he thought about the twelve pulse-throttleable retro-rockets that had decelerated InSight from Mach 1.7 to zero in forty seconds, overcoming a mass of 358 kilograms against Mars gravity using nothing more than high-pressure hydrazine and very precise nozzle timing.

Then he thought about the Titan rovers' skycrane manoeuvre — the system he had personally designed — which used compressed nitrogen thrusters to perform sub-metre positional corrections during the final descent phase. The thrusters were small. The corrections were precise. The principle was simple: you could not change an object's mass, but you could change its momentum instantaneously by applying an external force vector.

The exoskeleton needed thrusters.

Not hydrazine. Hydrazine was toxic, volatile, and required handling procedures that were incompatible with human-worn equipment. Compressed nitrogen. Inert. Stable. Already present in the exoskeleton's operational environment — the CBRN respirator system used nitrogen as part of its positive-pressure air supply. The infrastructure was half-built.

Hal began sketching. He was three minutes into the nozzle placement geometry when Brennan spoke for the first time since the disassembly had begun.

"The backpack frame has load-bearing capacity for an additional nine kilograms before the spinal brace starts redistributing weight to the hip servos." Brennan's voice was neutral — the particular neutrality of a man offering structural data about his own design without editorial commentary on what someone else was planning to do with it. "If you're putting accumulators in the backpack, that's your mass budget. The chlorine reservoirs are modular — you can pull two litres without rerouting the dispersal manifold."

Hal looked at him. Brennan looked back. Something passed between them in that exchange — the particular recognition of two engineers who had spent their careers building things that other people depended on and who understood, without needing to discuss it, that the only relevant question was *does the modification make the operator more survivable*.

"Two litres is what I need," Hal said.

"Then the frame will take it. I'll flag the mounting points for you." Brennan pulled a grease pencil from the workbench and marked four locations on the backpack frame's inner surface — the structural nodes where the accumulator tanks would bear against the frame without introducing stress concentrations. He did this from memory. He had designed the frame. He knew where every newton of force was distributed.

Hal incorporated the mounting points into his sketch and continued.

The design resolved over the next six hours with the steady, unhurried precision that characterised everything Hal Okonkwo built. He did not rush. Rushing introduced mechanical stress concentrations. Rushing produced assemblies that performed adequately during testing and failed catastrophically during operations. The Mars rovers did not rush. The Mars rovers worked perfectly, every time, for years, because every fastener was torqued to specification and every mechanism was assembled with the patience of a man who understood that the difference between a machine that worked and a machine that killed its operator was usually one overlooked tolerance.

The thruster array comprised twelve micro-nozzles: four at the hips (two per side, angled at thirty-five degrees below horizontal), four at the calves (two per side, angled at fifteen degrees aft of vertical), and four at the dorsal plate (arranged in a two-by-two grid, angled at forty-five degrees below horizontal for vertical thrust). Each nozzle was machined from Inconel 718 — the same nickel-chromium superalloy used in jet engine combustion chambers — and measured fourteen millimetres in throat diameter. Small. Precise. Capable of directing a focused blast of compressed nitrogen at pressures up to 310 bar.

The propellant source was a pair of high-pressure nitrogen accumulators mounted in the existing backpack frame, replacing the volume previously occupied by two of the eight binary chlorine reservoir litres. A trade-off. Hal did not make trade-offs lightly, and he would present this one to Cian for approval: two fewer litres of chlorine dispersal capacity in exchange for the ability to not die when a nine-foot Nephilim hybrid closed distance faster than human physics could evade. He considered the trade-off reasonable.

The accumulators fed a distribution manifold that routed nitrogen to all twelve nozzles through braided stainless steel lines rated to 450 bar. Each nozzle's solenoid valve was controlled by the exoskeleton's existing neural haptic relay system — the same interface that Cian used to trigger the wrist-mounted grenade launcher and the HUD targeting overlays. Intention to movement. Thought to thrust. The latency, Hal calculated, would be under eight milliseconds from neural impulse to nozzle firing.

The capability was straightforward: propulsive dash and propulsive jump.

Lateral dash: the hip thrusters fired in unison on the desired side, producing a net lateral force of approximately 3,400 newtons for 0.3 seconds. Combined with the operator's own muscular output and the servo-hydraulic torque amplification, this would displace a fifty-three-kilogram exoskeleton and an eighty-six-kilogram operator laterally by approximately 2.1 metres in 0.25 seconds. Three times Cian's current displacement capability. Enough to slip inside a giant's guard. Enough to clear the arc of a cursed greatsword.

Vertical jump: the calf and dorsal thrusters fired simultaneously, producing a combined vertical impulse of approximately 6,800 newtons for 0.4 seconds. Net result: a vertical displacement of approximately 3.5 metres from standing. Enough to clear a nine-foot opponent's head. Enough to convert a ground-level swordfight into a three-dimensional engagement where Cian's twenty-six centuries of combat experience — which included four centuries of mounted combat, six decades of airborne operations, and an Iron Age intuition for exploiting vertical advantage that predated the concept of high ground by several millennia — became a decisive advantage.

The nitrogen accumulators held sufficient pressure for approximately fourteen full-power bursts before requiring recharge. Recharge time from the exoskeleton's primary power cell: forty-five seconds per burst equivalent. In sustained combat, that meant fourteen bursts followed by a mandatory recharge window. Fire discipline applied to movement as well as ammunition.

Hal assembled the first thruster mount at 0300 on Day 4. He worked in the vehicle bay with the radio off because radios introduced distraction and distraction introduced error and error introduced the possibility that a thruster nozzle would fire at the wrong angle and drive the operator into a wall at lethal velocity. Each Inconel nozzle was seated into its machined housing, torqued to 47 newton-metres, and leak-tested with helium trace gas before Hal moved to the next.

By 1400, all twelve nozzles were installed. The accumulator system was plumbed. The distribution manifold was pressure-tested to 450 bar — one-and-a-half times operational maximum — and held. The solenoid valves responded to simulated neural haptic input with a measured latency of 6.2 milliseconds. Under his estimate. Hal noted this with the quiet satisfaction of a man who preferred his engineering to exceed its specifications rather than merely meet them.

He did not test-fire the system. Test-firing required the operator. The thrusters were calibrated to work *with* the operator's biomechanics — the firing vectors assumed a human body inside the suit, bracing, shifting weight, directing momentum. Without Cian, the test would produce data about the thrusters but not about the system. Hal built systems, not components.

While the thruster installation progressed, he rebuilt the damaged sections. The shattered left lateral plate was replaced entirely — Hal fabricated a new section from Brennan's ceramoplastic stock, matching the original lamination schedule but adding a fourth ply of aramid fibre at the impact face. Not because the original three-ply design was inadequate, but because the threat profile had changed. The original design had been calculated for human-generated ballistic impacts. The new threat included cursed greatswords swung by pre-Flood Nephilim champions with grip strengths that exceeded three times the material's original design envelope. Hal adjusted the envelope.

The compressed pauldron required more extensive work. He stripped the damaged section to the titanium subframe, rebuilt the lamination from scratch using the enhanced four-ply schedule, and reinforced the vulnerability gap between pauldron and upper arm plate with a flexible bridge of woven aramid and ceramoplastic micro-scales. The bridge would limit shoulder mobility by approximately three degrees of rotation — a trade-off Cian would feel and would need to approve. Three degrees, against the probability of having the joint compressed by a hand stronger than the material. Hal considered this trade-off even more reasonable than the chlorine reduction.

At 1830, the exoskeleton was reassembled. Forty-seven pieces returned to one integrated system. The thruster nozzles were invisible at first glance — recessed into the existing armour geometry, the Inconel throats flush with the ceramoplastic surface, the accumulator tanks nested in the backpack frame where the chlorine reservoirs had been. The suit looked the same. It was not the same. It was fifty-three kilograms of armour that could now move like forty.

Hal ran his hand along the rebuilt left lateral plate. The ceramoplastic was smooth under his palm. The new lamination schedule was indistinguishable from the original by touch. The weld lines where he'd bonded the replacement section to the existing frame were invisible — Hal's welds were always invisible, because visible welds meant sloppy welds and sloppy welds meant stress concentrations and stress concentrations meant failure.

He placed the suit on its stand, connected the diagnostic harness, and ran a full systems integration check. Hydraulics: nominal. Thruster array: nominal. Neural haptic interface: nominal. HUD: nominal. CBRN: nominal. Binary chlorine: six litres, reduced from eight. Nitrogen accumulators: full, 310 bar. Twelve nozzles: all responsive.

He powered down the diagnostic harness, cleaned his tools, and called upstairs.

"Suit's ready. He should try it on before I brief."

## V. DEPLOYMENT BRIEF

They assembled in the workshop at 2100 on Day 4. Marcus Reeves at the signals bench, datapads arranged with the methodical precision of a man who believed disorganised information was operationally indistinguishable from no information. JW Chen at the manufacturing station, sixty rounds of Tintreach Mk II laid out in two rows of thirty — .408 on the left, 7.62 on the right — each round bearing the double-line scribe mark that meant *this one breaks wards*. The adaptive sheath on a test stand beside them, humming its sub-audible 7.83 Hz like a tuning fork that had found its purpose. Hal Okonkwo at the vehicle bay door, the rebuilt exoskeleton on its stand behind him, twelve thruster nozzles invisible beneath ceramoplastic that looked exactly the same as it had before a Nephilim champion had shattered it. Brennan McNeeve leaning against the wall behind the acoustic monitoring terminal — the position he'd occupied for most of the sprint, the engineer who had built the foundation that every modification in this room had been built on top of. He had not briefed. He would not brief. His contributions were in the specifications his colleagues had consulted, the structural nodes he had marked, the materials he had formulated, the amplitude envelope that would make JW's crystals twelve percent more lethal. Brennan's name would not appear on any of the new systems. It was on the original ones. That was enough.

Cian mac Morna stood in the centre of the room and looked at what five days of recovery had produced.

Marcus briefed first. Concise. Three minutes. The anti-Mo Chrá ward formula, the cancellation architecture, the sword's autonomous development of counter-frequency protocol. He used the term *acoustic countermeasures* and did not elaborate on the submarine analogy because Cian mac Morna did not require analogies. The man had been conducting warfare since before Rome existed. He understood countermeasures.

"She won't be silenced by Armaros-class wards again," Marcus said. "The counter-frequency is pre-emptive — she arrives at the ward's standing wave before the cancellation can establish coherence. It's integrated into her base acoustic architecture now. Passive. Permanent."

Cian looked at Mo Chrá. The sword hummed. Not the low convalescent murmur of the past five days. Something sharper. The tonal equivalent of a predator remembering the trap that had almost worked and filing the memory under *never again*.

JW briefed second. Equally concise. The modified GaPO₄ crystals. The biased lattice. The ward-shatter-on-impact mechanism. The yield.

"Sixty rounds," JW said. "Thirty .408. Thirty 7.62. The ward-piercing capability is specific to Armaros-class acoustic architecture — the frequency bias won't affect performance against unshielded targets, but it won't help against ward types we haven't profiled."

"How many ward types are there?" Cian asked.

"Twenty Watcher chiefs," Marcus said. "Twenty potential ward architectures. We've profiled one. Armaros. The other nineteen are unknown."

The arithmetic settled in the room like temperature dropping. Sixty rounds tuned to one of twenty possible ward configurations. If the enemy at Dudael employed ward-craft from a different chief's school — Tamiel, Baraqel, Gadreel himself — the Mk II rounds would punch flesh just fine but do nothing about the shields.

"Then these are precision munitions," Cian said. "Reserved for confirmed Armaros-class contacts."

"Correct. Standard Tintreach for everything else."

Cian nodded. The nod of a man who had been fighting with limited ammunition since before the concept of ammunition existed. Scarcity was not new. Scarcity was the permanent condition of a one-man war against an enemy that outnumbered him by orders of magnitude. He simply adjusted his fire discipline and moved on.

Hal briefed third. He did not use datapads or spectral displays. He walked Cian to the exoskeleton stand and pointed.

"Left lateral plate: replaced. New four-ply lamination — added an aramid layer at the impact face. Designed for the revised threat envelope." His voice was unhurried. The cadence of a man who explained things once, clearly, and did not repeat himself because repetition implied the first explanation had been inadequate. "Left pauldron: rebuilt from the titanium subframe. Same four-ply schedule. I bridged the vulnerability gap between pauldron and upper arm with a flexible aramid-ceramoplastic scale section. You'll lose three degrees of shoulder rotation. The alternative is having the joint compressed again."

"I'll take three degrees," Cian said.

"Thought so." Hal moved to the back of the suit. "The bigger change is here."

He indicated the hip mounts, the calf panels, the dorsal plate. The Inconel nozzle throats were barely visible — recessed into the armour geometry, flush with the ceramoplastic surface.

"Compressed nitrogen micro-thrusters. Twelve nozzles — four hips, four calves, four dorsal. Powered by twin high-pressure accumulators at 310 bar, fed from the primary power cell. Controlled through your existing neural haptic relay. Same interface as the grenade launcher and HUD. Thought to thrust. Six-millisecond latency."

Cian looked at the nozzles. Then at Hal. The expression was the one Marcus had catalogued as *tactical reassessment in progress*.

"The telemetry from the Dragan fight," Hal said. "Your reaction time was point-oh-eight seconds. The exoskeleton displaced you point-seven metres. You needed one-point-four. The difference was the wound." He said this without inflection. The numbers were the argument. The wound was the evidence. "The thrusters give you a lateral dash of two-point-one metres in point-two-five seconds. Vertical jump of three-point-five metres from standing. Fourteen full-power bursts before recharge. Forty-five-second recharge per burst from the power cell."

"Trade-offs," Cian said. Not a question. An invitation.

"Two litres off the binary chlorine. Eight down to six. The accumulator tanks take the space."

Cian considered this for approximately two seconds. Two litres of chlorine dispersal capacity against the ability to clear a nine-foot opponent's striking arc. The arithmetic was not complicated.

"Done."

Hal nodded. The nod of a man who had already calculated the answer and was merely confirming the operator had reached it independently.

"You'll need to test-fire before deployment. The thruster vectors are calibrated to work with your biomechanics — your weight distribution, your centre of gravity under combat load. The system needs you inside it to validate. I'd recommend a controlled environment for the first lateral dash. Preferably with nothing breakable in the displacement path."

"I've been breaking things for twenty-six centuries, Okonkwo."

"I know. That's why I reinforced the vehicle bay walls."

It was the closest thing to a joke Hal had produced in three days at the Galway facility. Marcus noted it. JW did not appear to notice, because JW processed humour the way he processed all non-engineering data: as ambient information that did not require a response.

JW presented the sheath last. He lifted it from the test stand, and the 7.83 Hz resonance vibrated through his hands — that sub-audible thrum that felt less manufactured than *discovered*, as though the composite had always been resonating at this frequency and JW had merely given it a form to resonate *in*.

He explained the ferrite ring array, the planetary magnetic harvest, the acoustic feedback adaptation loop. He demonstrated by asking Cian to slide Mo Chrá into the housing and requesting shifts — tantō, bastard sword, return to default. He did not handle the sword himself. Three days at the Galway facility had taught him that Mo Chrá permitted his proximity but not his grip, and the distinction was not negotiable. The composite adapted to each shift with the fluid certainty of a material that knew exactly what it was supposed to become.

"Passive power," JW said. "No batteries. No external supply. Planetary rotation sustains the array. The 7.83 Hz resonance also acts as a passive energy transfer medium — the sword absorbs her own baseline frequency through the composite interface. Measured recovery rate: plus 0.4 dB per forty minutes. I didn't design that. The materials did."

Cian took the sheath from JW's hands.

The change was immediate.

In JW's grip, the sheath had hummed at 7.83 Hz — steady, clean, the passive resonance of a well-engineered system operating within its design parameters. The moment the composite touched Cian's palms, the hum *deepened*. Not louder. Not faster. *Richer* — as though the 7.83 Hz carrier wave had suddenly acquired overtones that hadn't existed thirty seconds ago. Marcus watched the spectral display bloom with harmonic content that had not been present during any of JW's test cycles: sub-harmonics at 3.91 Hz, overtones climbing through 15.66 and 23.49, the entire frequency architecture of Mo Chrá's baseline expanding like a chord resolving from a single note into a full voicing.

The sheath was responding to Cian's hands. Not to the engineering. Not to the ferrite array. To the *wielder* — to the acoustic signature that twenty-six centuries of bonded partnership had embedded in his skin, his bones, the particular way his grip distributed pressure across the composite surface. The Cydonian material recognised him the way it recognised Mo Chrá: as part of the system. As part of itself.

Cian felt the hum through the composite. His expression did not change, but something behind his eyes shifted in the way Marcus had learned to read as profound assessment masked by twenty-six centuries of emotional discipline.

"You built her a home," Cian said.

JW considered this. The statement was not technically accurate. He had built an adaptive metamaterial housing with passive acoustic resonance and an emergent energy-transfer capability. The word *home* implied domestic sentiment, emotional attachment, and a relationship between occupant and structure that exceeded the engineering specification.

"I built an adaptive metamaterial housing with passive acoustic resonance," JW said. Then, after a pause calibrated to convey acknowledgement without conceding the semantic point: "But yes."

Cian slid Mo Chrá into the sheath. And the sword — who had tolerated JW's tests with the cool professionalism of a dignitary submitting to a security check — *sang*.

Not the convalescent murmur. Not the smug semitone. Not the cool, precise approval she had extended to JW for observing correct protocol. This was the warmest frequency she had produced since the Carpathians — warmer than the lullaby she'd hummed while Cian watched Miriam sleep, warmer than the satisfaction of learning the counter-frequency, warmer than the comfortable settling when she'd first been placed in the sheath during JW's test. This was something older and more fundamental than any of those. This was the sound of a blade that had been carried by one man for twenty-six centuries and had just been placed into a housing that *understood* that bond — that resonated with it, amplified it, and fed it back through the composite the way the walls of a cathedral amplify a single voice into something that fills the nave.

The warmth was not for JW. The warmth was not for the sheath. The warmth was for *Cian* — for the hands that held her, for the heartbeat she had monitored since 586 BCE, for the man who had called her *Mo Chrá* in a burning corridor in Constantinople and had never once, in fifteen centuries since, failed to carry her. JW had built the housing. Brennan had formulated the composite. The ferrite rings harvested the planet's rotation. But the reason the sheath *worked* — the reason the composite resonated, the reason the trickle-charge emerged, the reason the 7.83 Hz carrier wave had acquired overtones the moment Cian touched it — was that Mo Chrá's home had never been a scabbard. Mo Chrá's home was *him*. And this was the first sheath in seventeen attempts that had been built from a material capable of understanding that.

Marcus watched the spectral display. The harmonic content was extraordinary — frequency structures he had never recorded from the sword, overtone series that his models had not predicted, acoustic architecture that existed only when the composite was in contact with both the blade and the wielder simultaneously. A closed circuit. Sword, sheath, wielder. The system was not two elements. It was three. And the third — the living, breathing, Iron Age component — was the one the Cydonian materials had been waiting for.

He created a new label in his database. Not *gratitude*. That word was too small. He considered several alternatives and settled on the one that most accurately described what the spectral data was showing him: *belonging*.

Cian slotted the first magazine of Tintreach Mk II into Bás Beag's well. Thirty rounds. Each one containing a crystal lattice that had memorised the frequency of a five-thousand-year-old ward formula and would speak that frequency at the moment of impact with the calm, terminal precision of a key turning in a lock.

He had sixty keys. One sword that could no longer be silenced. And an exoskeleton that could now move like the man inside it had always deserved.

When the team deployed to Greenland in forty-eight hours, they would deploy equipped. The intelligence that Voivode Dragan had carried in a cursed hilt for five millennia — the acoustic formula that had been designed to ensure Mo Chrá could never breach Watcher defences again — had been decoded, reversed, and weaponised in a Galway workshop by four engineers with seventeen patents, thirty years of submarine service, and five Mars rovers between them.

The hilt that had survived Gehenna fire had given them the frequency that broke its own kind. The telemetry from a wound that should have killed had given them the speed to ensure no such wound was suffered again.

The irony would not have been lost on Armaros. But Armaros was bound in chains of darkness beneath the earth, and the only thing that heard his frequency now was a sword that had learned to sing it back — carried by a man in armour that could fly.
