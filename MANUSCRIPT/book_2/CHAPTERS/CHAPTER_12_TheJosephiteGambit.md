## I. The Empty Room

SIGNAL LOST.

White text on three black screens, and Chris Guilder was alone in a signals lab in Galway with nothing to do but count.

He counted. 1423 hours. That was when the last frame of telemetry had populated — Brennan's instrument suite broadcasting real-time atmospheric, acoustic, and positional data from a Greenland nunatak at sixty-seven degrees north. Air temperature: minus thirty-five Celsius. Pressure gradient: stable. Mo Chrá's harmonic output climbing through the gate activation sequence at the exact frequency profile Chris had predicted, because the mathematics were elegant and the engineering was clear and elegance in engineering meant the sequence would do exactly what the sequence was designed to do.

The standing wave had formed. The aperture had opened. Four people and a sentient sword had crossed the threshold from a frozen nunatak into whatever existed on the other side of a planar fold that translated matter instantaneously between Cydonian metamaterial nodes.

Mars.

They were on Mars, and the satellite uplink could not transmit through a standing wave that folded space across two hundred and twenty-five million kilometres, and the moment Cian mac Morna stepped through the gate, every data stream that connected the field team to this room ceased to exist.

Chris pushed his glasses up.

The gesture was nervous. The information was not. He had modelled the comms implications of gate transit six weeks ago — had run the electromagnetic propagation calculations three times, because the number was too important to risk approximation and the conclusion was the kind of conclusion that needed to survive its own scrutiny before he presented it to the team. The standing wave would sever all electromagnetic communication. The acoustic architecture of the gate did not transmit photons the way it transmitted matter. Whatever EM signals existed on the Martian side would propagate on the Martian side, and Chris Guilder, sitting at his three-monitor arc with his laptop and his hardened comms console and his encrypted satellite links, would receive exactly none of them.

He had known this would happen.

Knowing did not help.

The gate transit window was approximately six hours — his calculation, based on the standing wave's structural coherence and the Cydonian metamaterial's rated harmonic tolerance, extrapolated from three weeks of on-site data that Brennan had collected at the nunatak with the particular obsessiveness of an engineer who understood that every measurement was a vote against uncertainty. Six hours during which four people would be conducting an operation that Chris had helped plan and could not support and could not monitor and could not influence in any capacity.

Six hours of SIGNAL LOST.

He had been in this condition before. In the Carpathians, his comms had died for eleven minutes. Eleven minutes. The acoustic wards in the Vârcolac stronghold's corridors had disrupted the encrypted tactical channel between his command post in An Daingean and the assault team two hundred metres below, and for eleven minutes Chris had watched thermal signatures on a screen and made the only decision available to him. He had called it *human-on-the-loop* because the situation did not have a name and naming it gave him something to hold, and the something was the only thing between him and the particular species of helplessness that came from watching someone you served with walk into a threat they could not see.

He had sent eight drones through those wards. Seven died — stripped of function and dropped like stones as the layered counter-frequencies found their rotational harmonics and cancelled them. One survivor, trailing sparks from two melting motor housings, had made it through by whatever margin separated functional from destroyed. The drone had descended to three metres above an elite approaching Cian's blind side and had delivered two hundred grams of consecrated calcium hypochlorite onto a stolen body.

Eleven minutes. One decision. One survivor out of eight.

Six hours was not eleven minutes.

Chris closed the satellite link management software. Monitoring a dead link was not analysis. It was anxiety wearing a technical costume, and Chris Guilder had spent three years learning to distinguish between the two because Mars rovers did not respond to anxiety and neither did autonomous drone swarms and neither did the tactical environment he now occupied, which was the environment of a man who had sent people into a place he could not reach and now had to sit in a room and wait for the universe to tell him whether they were alive.

He opened the secondary monitoring suite — the passive collection architecture that he and Marcus had built over the preceding months. Distributed intelligence. Commercial satellite passes on scheduled orbits. Hydroacoustic monitoring feeds from the Southern Ocean. Financial transaction networks. The particular species of open-source data mining that was not technically illegal if you did not think too carefully about where the source data originated or how Marcus's thirty years of submarine service had equipped him with access credentials that a civilian signals analyst should not possess.

The Ross Sea feeds were active.

The *Ashtoreth* — Naamah's research vessel, the floating operations centre for a seven-year drilling campaign — was visible on the latest synthetic-aperture radar pass. Fifty-six metres of hull, positioned at the same coordinates it had occupied for three years, anchored in the seasonal pack ice at the edge of the Ross Ice Shelf. The infrared overlay showed the thermal plume of the drilling operation: a diffuse heat bloom at the drill shaft, the residual warmth of industrial friction converting mechanical energy into thermal output proportional to depth and rock resistance.

Still drilling.

Chris ran the calculation. He ran it every seventy-two hours and did not quote from memory because the number was too important to risk approximation. Drill progress rate accumulated over seven years of continuous operation, adjusted for geological density at estimated depth, corrected for the reduced penetration rate that would result from encountering Cydonian metamaterial — the same acoustic-memory ore that the Watchers had used to build their gate network and that Mo Chrá was forged from. Material hardness extrapolated from resonance characteristics that Brennan had measured at the Greenland nunatak.

The arithmetic was worse than it had been three weeks ago. It was worse every time he ran it. The drill was close. Not months. Not weeks. Days. Possibly hours.

He made a note. Checked the time. 1431. Eight minutes since SIGNAL LOST. Five hours and fifty-two minutes remaining.

Chris opened the swarm controller software. There were no drones deployed. No swarm configured. No tactical environment that required fifty autonomous quadrotors running his targeting algorithms. But the firmware needed a revision pass — three sensor integration modules were flagged for update, and updating sensor modules was precise and precise was a discipline and discipline was the only available treatment for the condition of sitting alone in a room where three screens said SIGNAL LOST and five hours and fifty-two minutes stretched ahead like a corridor with no doors.

His hands moved. The code scrolled. The fluorescent strips buzzed at fifty hertz. Outside, Galway went about its evening under December clouds that had nothing to do with Greenland or Mars or what lay beneath two and a half miles of Antarctic ice.

Chris waited.

Four hours and twenty-four minutes. Chris had the elapsed time calculated before his hand reached the handset, because his mind tracked time the way his fingers tracked keystrokes — automatically, continuously, the number always running in the operational layer of cognition that remained active even when the conscious mind was occupied with firmware revisions that served no tactical purpose and everyone in the room knew it, including Chris, who was the only person in the room.

Marcus's voice came through the encrypted satellite link with the compression artefacts of a transcontinental connection — Austin to Galway, routed through relay nodes that Marcus had configured himself because Marcus Reeves did not trust communication architectures he had not personally verified, a habit formed over thirty years of submarine service where a misconfigured sonar array meant the difference between detecting a threat at forty nautical miles and detecting it at four.

1448\. Twenty-five minutes after the team entered the gate. Chris filed the number. He did not run the tree yet — not from a single datum, not from a thermal anomaly that could be equipment reconfiguration or drill head replacement or any of a dozen operational explanations. One datum was a point. Two data were a line. Three were a trend. Chris Guilder did not act on points.

Four hours ago.

He went back to the firmware. The code scrolled. The numbers did not change. The screens did not change. SIGNAL LOST. SIGNAL LOST. SIGNAL LOST.

He waited.

## II. The Alert

The second call came at 1943 hours.

Five hours and twenty minutes since SIGNAL LOST. Forty minutes remaining in the transit window. Chris had the handset before the second ring because he had not stopped tracking the elapsed time and forty minutes was not six hours — it was the closing margin, the narrowing window, the phase of the operation where probability trees began to collapse toward their terminal branches and the branches were labelled with words that Chris had taught himself not to think about in advance because thinking about terminal branches before you had data was not analysis.

It was fear.

"Chris."

Marcus's voice was different. Not the measured cadence of a routine check. The particular flattening of affect that thirty years of submarine service produced in a man who had just identified a contact that he did not want to be identifying — the vocal equivalent of a sonar operator reaching for the compartment phone with one hand while the other hand was already writing the bearing on the plotting board because you reported what you heard, not what you wanted to hear.

"I went back to the hydroacoustic archive."

Chris's jaw tightened. Marcus maintained passive access to the IMS hydrophone network through the Southern Ocean — the same International Monitoring System that the Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty Organisation operated to detect nuclear detonations. Underwater microphones positioned at the SOFAR channel depth, where sound propagation was optimal and acoustic signals could travel thousands of kilometres without significant attenuation. Marcus had been monitoring the Ashtoreth drill's acoustic signature through these feeds for eleven months, because a man who had spent twelve years cataloguing the propeller cavitation signatures of Soviet *Akula*-class submarines did not stop listening to things simply because he had retired.

"The drill has been producing a consistent acoustic signature for eleven months of continuous monitoring," Marcus said. "I know that signature the way I knew a Los Angeles-class reactor coolant pump — intimate, granular, a sound profile I could pick out of background oceanic noise at ranges that would constitute showing off if I were still wearing dolphins. Grinding rock. Steady state. Repetitive."

"Past tense."

"Past tense. At 1447 UTC — one minute before the thermal shift I reported — the acoustic signature changed. The grinding stopped. Not gradually. Not the way a drill bit dulls and the frequency rolls off. Instantaneously. One second: rock-on-steel contact at the rated RPM of a heavy industrial drill operating at depth. Next second: nothing."

"Equipment failure?"

"That's what I thought. For about ten seconds. Then the hydrophone picked up something else."

Marcus paused. Chris recognised the pause — it was the same pause Marcus had used when presenting the anti-singularity data to the team in Galway, the deliberate gap that a career intelligence professional inserted before delivering an assessment that would change the operational picture. Not for drama. For emphasis. Because what followed the pause needed to land without interference.

"For approximately twelve seconds after the drill stopped, the hydrophone recorded an acoustic event that I have never encountered in thirty years of listening to every ocean on this planet. The resonance profile changed from solid-medium contact — rock, steel, the kind of dense-material acoustics I've catalogued a thousand times — to open-cavity reverberance. A sudden, massive increase in the reflected-energy signature consistent with a mechanical penetration into a large subterranean void."

Chris's pulse rate did not change. He noted the fact clinically. His pulse *should* be changing. The data warranted it. But his body had learned, somewhere between the Carpathians and this moment, that physiological arousal was not compatible with analytical processing, and analytical processing was the only thing he had to offer the people on the other side of three dead screens.

"How large?"

"Based on reverberation time and the acoustic impedance mismatch between the original drill medium and the cavity — very large. Kilometres of cross-section. Hundreds of metres of vertical clearance. I'd need better sensor data to be precise, but the cavity is not a void pocket or a geological anomaly. It's a *structure*."

The drill had broken through. Into the prison. Into Dudael.

"There's more," Marcus said, and his voice dropped into the register that Chris had learned to associate with information that Marcus did not fully understand but reported anyway because a sonar operator reported his contacts and let the tactical team interpret them. "After the breakthrough reverberance faded — maybe ninety seconds after the drill stopped — the hydrophone began recording a sustained acoustic emission from the cavity. Not mechanical. Not geological. A complex harmonic in frequency bands that do not correspond to any catalogued oceanic, geological, or anthropogenic source in the IMS database."

"Characterise it."

"I can't. That's the problem. It's a stable standing wave — a self-sustaining, non-decaying harmonic structure operating at frequencies below the threshold of human hearing but well within hydrophone detection range. It has been running continuously since the breakthrough. It has not degraded. It has not varied. It is *perfect*."

A sound that had been playing for five thousand years without interruption. Without degradation. Without a single microsecond of silence.

Raphael's seal.

Chris did not say it. He did not need to. The implications assembled themselves in his mind with the same clarity that the gate activation sequence had assembled — input, process, output, the engineering made visible once you understood the architecture. Marcus was hearing the binding frequency that held Azazel in isolation from the rest of creation. Marcus did not know what it was. Chris did.

"The thermal data confirms it," Marcus continued. "The drill plume is gone — completely. Not reduced, not reorganised. *Gone*. The drill has stopped because there's nothing left to drill through. And the subsurface thermal blooms I flagged — four point sources in a roughly rectangular distribution around the former drill axis — have intensified. They're consistent with human activity. Equipment deployment. Movement of personnel and materiel through a newly opened access point into the cavity below."

Chris's jaw was clenched tight enough to ache. He noted the ache the way he noted his pulse — clinically, distantly, a biological datum filed and set aside because the datum did not help and what helped was the picture, and the picture was assembling itself with the merciless clarity of a targeting solution.

The drill stopped 4.5 hours ago. Naamah breached Dudael 4.5 hours ago. She had 4.5 hours to establish operations. Her advance team was inside. Her command post was operational. Her people were mapping the prison, deploying equipment, and assessing Raphael's seal.

The team was still on Mars. Or they had already come through the gate. Chris did not know which. The standing wave was either still active — holding the aperture open for the return transit — or it had collapsed, and the team was already inside Dudael with no comms, no extraction, and no knowledge that the prison they had gate-jumped into was occupied.

"Pull everything," Chris said. "FININT — the full picture. Ashtoreth supply chain, last seventy-two hours. Wire transfers. Air traffic into any Antarctic-adjacent staging point. Punta Arenas charter companies. Military flights. Anything."

"Chris, what are you—"

"Just pull it. Now."

Marcus pulled it.

Personnel: Chris cross-referenced the charter company's booking records — accessed through the FININT monitoring architecture Marcus had built from Victoria Ashford's encrypted files — against the House Satar and House Tamiel front company databases that Sarah McNeeve maintained on McNeeve Industries' secure servers. Naamah had known the breach was imminent. The drill telemetry had told her. The synchronisation problem was absolute. He could not reach Cian. He could not transmit through the standing wave — if it was still active. The team would walk into a kill-zone.

If they had not already.

Chris's mind ran the timeline: the gate transit window was approximately six hours. They had been gone for five hours and twenty-five minutes. At some point during their Mars operation — the Araqiel bunker exploration, the Cartographer's Library, whatever they found in the Labyrinth of Night — the intelligence would compel them to redirect to Antarctica. They would use the gate's remaining transit capacity to jump from Mars to Dudael. They would step through. And they would be inside.

If Cian stepped through now — right now, while Chris was reading Marcus's data — the team would face twelve Synagogue operatives in a cavern beneath two and a half miles of ice with no warning and no reinforcements. Cian had the HCV, Mo Chrá, sixty Tintreach Mk II ward-piercing rounds, James with his SCAR-H, Miriam, Brennan. Against six House Satar intelligence specialists and six House Tamiel spiritual warfare practitioners. The interior arithmetic was actually favourable — Cian mac Morna in the rebuilt Heavy Combat Variant against unshielded operatives was not a fair fight. It was not supposed to be.

But the interior was not the problem.

The surface was the problem. The drill shaft. The *Ashtoreth*. The perimeter that Naamah's An-74-delivered reinforcements were currently establishing around the only exit from the prison. If Cian won the fight below — and he would win, because two thousand six hundred and thirty-six years of combat experience wrapped in an acoustically enhanced exoskeleton with a creation-frequency sword did not lose to surveillance technicians and pharmakeia practitioners — he would emerge through the drill shaft into a surface security cordon that he did not know existed.

And if Cian arrived too late — if the standing wave collapsed and the team was delayed on Mars for hours they did not have — then by the time they reached Dudael, the twelve-person advance team would have been reinforced. Forty on the surface. Eighty within seventy-two hours. A hundred within a week. The ticking clock ran in one direction and it ran against everything Chris cared about.

He could not control the timing. He could not control whether Cian came through too early into a twelve-on-four ambush or too late into a fortified encampment. He could not control any of it. He had three screens that said SIGNAL LOST and a satellite phone and a picture that was clear and terrible and complete.

What he could control was what happened on the surface when the team tried to get out.

Chris pushed his glasses up. The gesture was precise. The decision behind it was already made.

## III. The Executive Decision

The chain of command was simple. Chris had mapped it the way he mapped system architectures — nodes, connections, authority flows, failure modes — because mapping was what he did and the map was the only thing between clear thinking and the kind of panic that got people killed.

Cian mac Morna: operational authority. Unreachable.

James Madigan: military second-in-command. Unreachable.

Brennan Webb: McNeeve Industries CEO. Unreachable.

Sarah McNeeve: legal and corporate authority. Available. In Galway, one floor above Chris's signals lab, running McNeeve Industries' legitimate operations — the aerospace contracts and the mineral rights applications and the R&D programmes that kept the company functional and funded and provided the institutional infrastructure that a twenty-six-hundred-year-old Irish warrior used to fight a five-thousand-year-old conspiracy in the modern world.

Marcus Reeves: intelligence and technical advisory. Available. In Austin, monitoring the passive collection architecture that was currently delivering the worst operational picture Chris had ever seen.

Chris Guilder: drone and signals specialist. Available. Sixth in a chain of command where the first three links were on another planet.

He was not a commander. He was a twenty-nine-year-old software engineer from El Paso who had been rejected by Google three times — by *choice*, not by Google, because Google built ad platforms and Brennan built things that mattered and the distinction had been the clearest professional decision Chris had ever made. He wrote targeting algorithms. He ran autonomous systems. He managed the swarm controller architecture that could coordinate fifty quadrotors in a simultaneous engagement envelope with sufficient precision to dissolve two hundred and six reanimated corpses in forty-one minutes.

Drones.

He commanded drones. Machines that expended their operational utility and became components. Seven of eight destroyed in the Carpathian wards and the number had not kept him up at night because machines did not have night and machines did not have the specific vulnerability of a nervous system responding to the chemical reality of fear. Chris had built the swarm. The swarm had killed. Chris had sat back afterward and had not known how he felt about that.

This was not drones.

He picked up the satellite phone and called Sarah McNeeve.

She answered on the second ring. The voice was Sarah's professional register — precise, unhurried, the cadence of a woman who had spent fifteen years turning chaos into billable paragraphs and who answered phones at 2000 hours on a weekday with the same composed efficiency she brought to a 0900 board meeting, because Sarah McNeeve did not have off-hours. She had working hours and she had hours during which she worked from home.

"Chris."

"I need seven minutes." He heard his own voice — even, controlled, the compressed data-delivery cadence of a man who had trained himself to front-load critical information because seventeen-minute light delays and dead comms channels did not forgive preamble. "The drill stopped. Naamah has breached. Hydroacoustic confirmation from IMS feeds — the drill's acoustic signature terminated four and a half hours ago and Marcus is registering a sustained harmonic from the cavity that does not match any known source. Thermal confirms breakthrough — drill plume gone, subsurface thermal blooms consistent with personnel and equipment deployment. FININT confirms pre-positioning — wire transfers through House Baraqel accounts to Punta Arenas charter companies, two An-74 cargo flights delivered twelve to eighteen hours ago, passenger manifests matching House Satar and House Tamiel front companies."

"How many inside?"

"Estimate twelve. Advance team. Six Satar intelligence, six Tamiel spiritual warfare. Plus approximately twenty-eight on the surface — eight ship security, sixteen from the An-74 deliveries, four Satar logistics. The team is going to gate from Mars into a prison that's been occupied for four and a half hours by an enemy that pre-positioned reinforcements before the breakthrough."

Silence. Two seconds. Sarah McNeeve processing at the speed of a woman who did not waste time on denial.

"What do you need?"

"I need to deploy the NZ contingency. Josephite Tier-1 from Christchurch. Twelve operators, three fire teams, surface assault on the drill site perimeter. The team can handle the interior — Cian has the HCV and Mo Chrá and sixty ward-piercing rounds. But they cannot fight their way out through a surface security cordon they don't know exists. I need the surface cleared."

"You need my authority."

"I need your authority and I need your architecture."

Another silence. One second. Then Sarah McNeeve's voice changed — not in register, not in composure, but in *density*. The shift from the woman who managed corporate operations to the woman who had spent six months in Chapter 4 constructing a legal framework for exactly this contingency, filing mineral rights applications and incorporating survey companies and building the layered operational cover that would make an unsanctioned military insertion into the Antarctic Treaty zone look like something other than what it was.

"The NZ-incorporated environmental survey company — that's our entity of record. Registered in Wellington, legitimate field operations history, current Antarctic access credentials through MFAT. The addendum is approved and sitting in MFAT's system. I activate it with one email."

"Do it."

"The Christchurch logistics node has pre-positioned cold-weather equipment, communications gear, and what the inventory manifest describes as 'scientific monitoring instrumentation' — which is accurate, in the sense that Guardian Network consecrated ammunition monitors the continued existence of supernatural threats and then resolves the monitoring situation with terminal velocity. Basis: our contracted survey vessel has reported a subsurface seismic event at the drill site, crew safety is a concern, we are requesting emergency personnel and equipment delivery to the site via military transport. The C-130 is already allocated to Antarctic operations on a rotational basis. A priority humanitarian request puts our payload on the next available departure. MFAT confirmation of the survey company's credentials and the emergency fieldwork permit addendum will pass RNZAF scrutiny because the paperwork is *real* — I didn't fabricate cover documents, I built legitimate operational infrastructure and waited for the moment it would be needed."

Chris's jaw was tight. The legal architecture that Sarah had constructed — the months of filings and applications and corporate shells and permits that had looked like paperwork — was a weapon. She had built it the way Chris built targeting algorithms: to perform a specific function at a specific moment with a specific outcome. The function was stealth. The moment was now. The outcome was twelve Josephite Tier-1 operators arriving at an Antarctic drill site in approximately six hours under cover so thorough that House Satar's surveillance infrastructure — the most sophisticated intelligence apparatus in the Synagogue of Satan — would see a routine New Zealand Antarctic logistics flight responding to a seismic event, not a military assault force inbound for a sub-ice extraction.

Corroborating paper trail."

"I need forty minutes. The Maritime NZ filing is electronic. The MFAT activation is one email. The RNZAF liaison will require a phone call because military transport requests move faster with a voice on the other end of the line, and the voice needs to be mine because the survey company's director is a woman named Sarah McNeeve who has been filing Antarctic permits for eighteen months and whose name is in the system."

"Forty minutes. Then you'll have a C-130 departing Christchurch with twelve operators, cold-weather equipment, and enough consecrated ordnance to contest a surface perimeter against twenty-eight hostiles. Call your Josephites."

She hung up. No farewell. No *be careful*. Sarah McNeeve did not waste words on sentiments that did not advance the operational objective, and the operational objective was getting twelve people from Christchurch to the Ross Ice Shelf before eighty more enemies arrived from Punta Arenas.

Chris set the phone down. Picked it up again. His hands were steady. They had been steady in An Daingean when the comms went dead. Steady in the armoury when James had inspected the SCAR-H with the methodical precision of a man who knew that the arrangement in which you set your weapon down was the arrangement in which you picked it up. Steady now, when what he was about to do was not push a button that launched fifty autonomous quadrotors into a forest full of stolen bodies, but give an order that would put twelve human beings onto ice two thousand kilometres from the nearest friendly territory with consecrated weapons and Josephite field habits and the specific, irreducible vulnerability of flesh that could not be rebuilt from components.

*They don't die. They expend their operational utility and then they're components.*

His own words. From the armoury. He had said them about drones because drones were what he commanded and the language of expenditure was the language of machines and machines did not have the problem of being alive.

He was not deploying machines.

A pause. Authentication verified against whatever ledger the Josephites maintained for this purpose.

"Authenticated. Go ahead."

Chris ran the intelligence picture. He did not spare details and he did not pad the briefing, because his voice carried the cadence of a man who had spent three years training on seventeen-minute light delays and the cadence did not waste bandwidth on words that did not advance the mission and the mission was simple and terrible and clear.

The field team had the Heavy Combat Variant exoskeleton, Mo Chrá, sixty Tintreach Mk II ward-piercing rounds, and a man who had been killing things since the sixth century BC. The interior fight belonged to Cian mac Morna.

The mission: secure the surface perimeter. Neutralise the twenty-eight enemy combatants controlling the drill shaft. Prevent the team from being bottled beneath two and a half miles of ice with no exit. Hold the perimeter until the team completed their objective and extracted via the drill shaft.

The arithmetic: twelve Tier-1 operators against twenty-eight surface combatants. Outnumbered 2.3 to 1.

Shield Seven did not hesitate. "How fast do you need us?"

"How fast can you deploy?"

"C-130 from Christchurch to the coastal staging point: four hours at best air speed. Storm Herald shuttle from staging to target: ninety minutes. We can be on site in under six hours."

Six hours. If Cian was already inside, six hours was an eternity. If the team was fighting right now — if the gate had opened and the twelve Satar and Tamiel operatives had detected the acoustic bloom and were converging — then six hours of surface hold time was the margin between extraction and entombment.

"Deploy." Chris's voice did not waver. The word tasted different from "full deployment" in the Carpathians — *all fifty, thermal targeting authorised, engagement zone is everything between the ridgeline and the stronghold entrance*. In the Carpathians, the deployment had been drones. Fifty autonomous quadrotors following targeting algorithms that Chris had designed, executing engagement protocols that Chris had coded, deploying payloads that dissolved stolen bodies into the chemistry they should have been left to. The swarm had killed two hundred and six ghouls in forty-one minutes. Zero friendly casualties. Clean. Algorithmic. The mathematics of expenditure applied to machines that had no stake in the outcome.

"Deploy" now meant twelve people stepping onto a C-130 at Christchurch. Twelve operators checking consecrated weapons and zipping cold-weather gear and briefing the assault plan during the four-hour flight south, each of them running their own arithmetic — the personal calculation that every soldier made before every engagement, the one that weighed training and experience and faith against the specific, irreducible probability that the mission would cost more than the mission was worth.

Chris could not make the calculation for them. He could only make the call.

"Rules of engagement?"

"Surface targets only. Anyone in the perimeter who is not field team is hostile. Guardian Network anti-Nephilim protocols authorised. Expect enhanced-bloodline combatants — eight minimum, possibly more. Standard Tintreach loadout. Consecrated munitions." He paused. The next words mattered. "Shield Seven. The field team has no comms. They don't know you're coming. If you see a man in heavy armour with a sword that hums — that's our principal. Do not engage him."

"Understood. Six hours. Shield Seven out."

The line went dead.

Chris set the phone down. His hands did not shake. They had not shaken in An Daingean. They had not shaken when one drone on two melting motors had screamed through acoustic wards to deliver a payload that ended something that used to be a person. They did not shake now, and the steadiness was not bravery — it was the particular discipline of a nervous system that had learned to defer its tremors until the operation was over, and the operation was not over and would not be over for six hours and Chris Guilder's hands would be steady for every one of those hours because the steadiness was the only thing he could offer the twelve people who were about to fly south on his word.

His phone buzzed. Sarah. Text message, the brevity of a woman who had just executed the first three steps of an operational cover sequence that would have taken a less prepared legal mind a week:

*MFAT activated. Maritime NZ filed. RNZAF liaison in 20 min.*

Twenty minutes. Then the C-130.

Chris looked at his screens. SIGNAL LOST. SIGNAL LOST. SIGNAL LOST.

Somewhere on Mars — or beneath the ice — four people were either alive or they were not, and Chris could not tell which, and it did not matter because the decision was made and the C-130 was warming and the twelve were going south regardless. The mission did not wait for confirmation. The mission waited for nothing. That was the nature of sending humans instead of drones: you could not call them back with a command override, could not reroute them with a waypoint update, could not expend them and replace them with the next unit in the swarm queue. They were people. They went, and they did the thing, and they came back or they did not come back, and the man who sent them lived with the sending.

James would buy him a drink if they survived.

James would buy him several.

## IV. The Cost

Marcus called at 2015 hours.

Chris answered immediately. The satellite phone had not left his hand since the Josephite deployment order, because the phone was the only thread that connected him to the operational picture and the picture was the only thing he had and having it was both essential and insufficient — essential because the picture was data, insufficient because data was not comms and comms was the thing he actually needed and the thing he could not have.

"Secondary FININT snapshot." Marcus's voice had settled into the flat, metronomic register of a man delivering an intelligence assessment that he had already processed and digested and stripped of emotion because emotion was not Marcus Reeves's professional output. Data was Marcus's professional output. Emotion was what Marcus did with the data after he had delivered it to someone else, alone, in whichever room he happened to occupy, with whatever Naval tradition of private reckoning served the purpose. "The Synagogue's financial activity around Antarctica has surged in the last twelve hours. Wire transfers to aviation charter companies, equipment suppliers, and what I assess to be House Aram security contractor front companies. The financial pattern is consistent with a major operational escalation."

"Give me numbers."

"Within seventy-two to ninety-six hours, based on the logistics lead time from Punta Arenas and the throughput capacity of the Antarctic staging infrastructure, Naamah will have an occupying force of eighty to one hundred personnel in and around the prison complex. Armed. Supplied. Dug in. The forty that are there now will nearly triple."

Eighty to one hundred. Chris ran the dual picture — the same bifocal analysis he had been running since Marcus's first call, the interior and the exterior, the two halves of a fight that was being waged in different dimensions by different people against different enemies.

Inside Dudael: Cian with the HCV — rebuilt by Hal, propulsive dash, full articulated armour, the acoustic enhancement suite that turned a man into a weapons platform. Mo Chrá in the adaptive sheath with the 7.83 Hz ferrite ring generating the resonance field that made the sword hot-deployable. Sixty Tintreach Mk II rounds — ward-piercing, designed specifically for targets behind acoustic defences. James with the SCAR-H and the instincts of a man who had been tier-one in a former life and who had transitioned from military operator to something without a job title because the job did not exist in any military's organisational chart. Miriam. Brennan. Four people against twelve Synagogue operatives.

The interior was asymmetric in the team's favour. Cian mac Morna in the Heavy Combat Variant against intelligence analysts and pharmakeia practitioners was not combat. It was clearance.

Surface: twenty-eight enemy combatants against twelve NZ Josephite Tier-1 operators arriving in — Chris checked the elapsed time since his deployment order — approximately four hours and forty minutes. Outnumbered 2.3 to 1. Quality against quantity. The Josephites had surprise, anti-Nephilim protocols, eight centuries of institutional combat experience against the Watchers' legacy, and the very specific advantage that Sarah McNeeve's operational cover would blind the enemy's early warning until the C-130 was already on approach and the Storm Herald shuttles were inbound at wavetop height and it was too late to reinforce the perimeter.

The arithmetic was tight. Not hopeless. Not comfortable. Tight. The particular grade of tight that meant every variable mattered and every margin was the margin and the difference between success and catastrophe was the difference between the variables you controlled and the variables you did not.

Chris controlled the deployment. He controlled the timing — such as it was, constrained by the physics of distance and airspeed. He controlled the intelligence picture, continuously updated via Marcus's passive collection feeds.

He did not control the gate. He did not control when Cian would step through. He did not control whether the team was currently alive, or fighting, or trapped, or any of the states that occupied the branching possibilities of a probability tree whose terminal nodes he refused to examine because examining them would require acknowledging that three of the people in that tree were people he had eaten dinner with and one of them was a woman he had watched teach Brennan how to read Sumerian cuneiform over coffee in the Galway signals lab and none of them knew what was waiting for them.

"Marcus."

"Here."

"The acoustic emission you detected from the cavity — the sustained harmonic. Is it still active?"

"Continuous since 1447 UTC. Stable. No degradation. No variation. If I didn't know better, I'd classify it as a navigation aid — a fixed-frequency reference beacon, the kind of thing we used to calibrate sonar arrays against. Except navigation aids don't operate in frequency bands that shouldn't exist and they don't maintain coherence over five hours without external power."

Five thousand years. The harmonic had been maintaining coherence for five thousand years. It would maintain coherence until it was broken — until House Tamiel found a frequency that could crack a note played by an archangel who understood sound the way a surgeon understood anatomy.

"Has the acoustic environment changed at all? Anything new in the cavity?"

"One thing. Approximately forty minutes ago — 1935 UTC — I registered a transient acoustic event inside the cavity. Brief. Maybe three seconds. Frequency profile inconsistent with the drilling infrastructure or the sustained harmonic. It had characteristics of... Chris, it sounded like a gate activation."

1935 UTC. That was 1935 hours. Eight minutes before Marcus's previous call. While Chris and Marcus had been discussing hydroacoustic data, the team had stepped through the gate into Dudael.

They were inside. Right now. While he sat in Galway.

Chris took off his glasses. Cleaned them on his shirt. Put them back on.

The gesture sequence — the full version, push then removal then clean then replace — reserved for processing content he could not compartmentalise. In the Carpathians, had cleaned his glasses once: after two hundred and six ghouls had dissolved into chemistry and his screens showed the aftermath and his mind had needed the four seconds of blurred vision to transition from operational mode to whatever mode came after operational mode, the mode that did not have a name because naming it would mean admitting that it existed and admitting it existed would mean acknowledging that Chris Guilder, twenty-nine, El Paso, three Google offers rejected, was a man who built things that killed other things and sat in rooms watching screens and did not know how he felt about it.

But he knew now. Sitting in a signals lab in Galway with three monitors that said SIGNAL LOST, he knew exactly how he felt. He felt the weight of twelve operators on a C-130 somewhere over the Southern Ocean, flying south on his authority toward a fight he had ordered, against enemies he had identified, in defence of people he had served with and could not reach. He felt the weight of the gap between drones and humans — the gap he had been traversing since the Carpathians, since the moment he had sent eight through wards that killed seven, since James had said *Chris, good lad* with the particular emphasis of a man who did not give praise he did not mean. The gap between *they don't die* and *they die*. The gap between pushing a button that launched expendable machines and picking up a phone and saying *deploy* and meaning *go and fight and I cannot guarantee you will come back*.

*They expend their operational utility and then they're components.*

He heard the words. His own words. The armoury in Galway. The clinical language of a software architect discussing autonomous systems whose destruction was a design parameter. He had believed the words when he said them. He had believed them because the alternative — that the things he sent into harm's way mattered in a way that transcended operational utility — was a belief that would make him unable to function, and functioning was the requirement and the mission was the priority and the mission did not accommodate belief systems that interfered with execution.

He did not believe the words now.

Twelve people on a plane. Twenty-eight enemies on the ice. Four friends beneath it.

And there was nothing left to do. No algorithm to write. No firmware to update. No swarm to deploy, no targeting solution to optimise, no calculation to run that would change the variables or narrow the margins or close the gap between a C-130 over the Southern Ocean and a gate that had already opened beneath the ice. The arithmetic was complete. The arithmetic had been complete for thirty minutes. Everything that could be controlled had been controlled, and everything that could not be controlled remained exactly as uncontrollable as it had been when Marcus had said *it sounded like a gate activation* and Chris had understood that his friends were in a prison and he was in a chair and the distance between the two was measured in units that mathematics could not bridge.

Chris Guilder closed his eyes.

He had not prayed before. Not once. Not as a child in El Paso, where his mother had taken him to Mass on Easter and Christmas and he had stood in the pew and watched the ritual with the detached analytical interest of a boy who was already writing his first sorting algorithms on a TI-84 and who processed the liturgy the way he processed everything — as architecture, as system, as a series of inputs and outputs whose underlying logic he could admire without inhabiting. Not in college, where the question of God had occupied the same cognitive shelf as the question of dark matter — theoretically significant, empirically underdetermined, filed under *interesting but not actionable*. Not in the Carpathians, where two hundred and six stolen bodies had dissolved under his drones and the question of whether they felt anything had kept him up at two in the morning but had not driven him to his knees because the question was philosophical and philosophy did not require kneeling.

Not until now.

He did not kneel. He was sitting in a signals lab chair with three dead monitors in front of him and fluorescent lights buzzing overhead and the satellite phone on the desk and the particular gracelessness of a man who did not know the protocol for this because there was no protocol, no API call, no sequence of inputs that guaranteed an output, and the absence of protocol was the thing that made it prayer instead of engineering.

*God.*

The word formed in his mind and he did not know what to attach to it. He did not have the Empyreal cadences that Cian would have used — the ancient, formal structure of a man who had been speaking to the divine for twenty-six centuries. He did not have James's quiet, private faith — the particular species of belief that a special forces operator carried in the same interior space where he carried his tactical instincts, unspoken, unglamorous, load-bearing. He did not have Miriam's scholarly relationship with the text, or Brennan's stubborn engineering-adjacent conviction that the universe was designed and the designer was benevolent because the mathematics were too elegant to be accidental.

He had nothing. He had a chair and three dead screens and a phone and the knowledge that four people were beneath the ice and twelve were flying toward it and he could not help any of them.

*God. I don't know how to do this. I build systems. I write algorithms. I solve problems by reducing them to their components and optimising the components until the solution emerges from the architecture. I have reduced this problem to its components and the components do not optimise. The variables do not converge. The solution does not emerge. I have reached the boundary of what I can do and the boundary is here, in this chair, and on the other side of the boundary are sixteen people whose lives depend on timing I cannot control and outcomes I cannot influence and I do not have an algorithm for this. I do not have a targeting solution for this.*

*I am asking You to be the variable I cannot control.*

*I am asking because I have nothing else to ask with and nowhere else to ask and no one else to ask and the arithmetic is done and the arithmetic is not enough. The arithmetic has never been enough. I have been pretending it was enough because pretending was the only thing between me and the knowledge that the systems I build and the algorithms I write and the elegant autonomous architectures that I am so proud of are not sufficient for the problem of keeping people alive in a world where things want them dead.*

*Keep them alive.*

*That's it. That's the whole prayer. It's not elegant. It's not optimised. It does not have the formal structure of a well-designed system call. It is a man in a chair asking the God he has never spoken to for the only thing that matters.*

*Keep them alive. All of them. The four beneath the ice and the twelve on the plane and the man with the sword and the woman who reads cuneiform and the engineer who counts everything and the soldier who would buy me a drink if we survive.*

*Keep them alive and I will figure out the rest later. The theology. The protocol. The whatever-comes-after. Later. Right now: keep them alive.*

The fluorescent strips buzzed. The monitors glowed. The satellite phone did not ring.

Chris opened his eyes. His hands were in his lap. They were not steady. For the first time since the Carpathians — since the armoury, since An Daingean, since one drone on two motors — his hands were not steady. They trembled. The tremor lasted four seconds. Five. Then it stopped, and the steadiness returned, and the steadiness was different now. It was not the discipline of a nervous system suppressing its physiological response. It was something else. Something he did not have a name for because he had never experienced it before and naming things he had never experienced was not his competency.

It felt like the arithmetic was still incomplete. But it felt like the incompleteness was not his problem anymore.

Chris Guilder pushed his glasses up one more time. His jaw ached from clenching. His hands were steady. They had been steady the entire time — steady in An Daingean, steady in the armoury, steady when one drone on two motors screamed through the dark to save a man's life, steady now in a signals lab where three screens glowed blue-white with the words SIGNAL LOST and the fluorescent strips hummed at fifty hertz and the satellite phone sat on the desk beside a laptop running firmware updates that no one needed.

The next satellite pass was in forty-seven minutes. In forty-seven minutes, he would have an updated infrared picture of the drill site — and the thermal signature of a C-130 on approach from Christchurch, and the heat blooms of a surface perimeter that twelve Josephite operators would assault with consecrated weapons and the professional competence of an order that had been doing this since the Crusades.

In four hours and thirty-five minutes, the Josephites would be on site. Twelve against twenty-eight. Challenging. Not impossible. Winnable with surprise and the particular advantage that House Satar's surveillance network was, at this moment, looking at a legitimate New Zealand environmental survey response to a seismic event, because Sarah McNeeve's lawfare was as lethal as any weapon Chris had ever deployed and twice as precise.

Inside Dudael, beneath the ice, Cian mac Morna was either fighting or preparing to fight or already beyond fighting. Chris did not know which. The screens did not tell him. The satellite did not tell him. The hydrophone told him only that the binding harmonic was still singing and the cavity was still occupied and forty minutes ago something that sounded like a gate had activated in the dark.

He had done everything he could do. The data was gathered. The intelligence was delivered. The Josephites were airborne. Sarah's cover architecture was operational. Marcus was monitoring. The picture was as complete as it would get until the satellite passed or the Josephites landed or the phone rang with a voice on the other end that was not Marcus.

Now he waited.

Chris Guilder did not close his laptop. He did not close the monitors. He did not leave the room. He had been in this chair since the team had entered the gate — six hours, no food, two coffees, the particular endurance of a young man whose body had not yet learned that it could not sustain this level of cortisol indefinitely and whose mind had learned something that his body hadn't, which was that there were things worse than cortisol and one of them was leaving your post with screens still dark and people still deployed and the mathematics of the thing still unresolved.

He understood, now, why James Madigan sat the way James Madigan sat — upright, forward, weight on the balls of his feet even in a chair, the posture of a man who had never fully left the operational condition because the operational condition was not a state you entered and exited. It was a state you acquired. It stayed. It lived in the space between what you had done and what it had cost and the gap between the two was the space in which the person you had been became the person you were.

Chris had been a software engineer from El Paso who wrote targeting algorithms for autonomous drones. That person had sent fifty machines into a Carpathian forest and had dissolved two hundred and six stolen bodies and had sat back afterward and had not known how he felt about that.

Chris was a man who had sent twelve people to war. That person would know how he felt about it. The knowing would come later — after the screens changed, after the satellite passed, after the phone rang or did not ring, after the Josephites landed on the ice and did the thing and came back or did not come back. The knowing would come, and it would be specific and it would be heavy and it would not go away.

But that was later. That was after.

Now, there was the chair. The screens. The blue-white glow of SIGNAL LOST. The hum of fluorescent strips at fifty hertz. The satellite phone on the desk. The particular quality of waiting that separated the people who sent others into darkness from the people who went into darkness themselves — the waiting that Cian would have understood, and James would have understood, and Shield Seven and the twelve operators on the C-130 would have understood, because it was the waiting that happened on the other side of the decision and the decision was the thing that could not be taken back.

Outside, Galway was dark. The clouds had not moved. The evening had become night in the way that winter nights in western Ireland became night — gradually, then completely, the Atlantic wind carrying rain against the signals lab window in patterns that Marcus would have been able to read if he were here and that Chris could not read because Chris read screens, not weather, and the screens said the same thing they had said for six hours.

SIGNAL LOST.

The monitors glowed.

Chris pushed his glasses up.

He did not close the monitors.
