# PROLOGUE II

## The Tower and the Throne

---

### Babel — approximately 2200 BCE

The man on the throne was speaking in two voices, and one of them was her son's.

Naamah stood at the edge of the inner chamber — the room beneath the throne room, the one that did not appear on any builder's plan — and watched Nimrod's body do what Nimrod's body had been doing for three years: contain something it was not built to contain. The pharmakeia held. It always held. The problem was never the pharmakeia. The problem was the vessel.

Nimrod sat on the cedar chair they had brought down from the throne room for the extraction sessions — a chair, not a throne, because the distinction mattered to Naamah if to no one else. Thrones were for kings. This was a workstation. His body was large, muscled, dark, built for the hunt and the campaign and the public performance of power. A body designed to be looked at. A body that wanted to be seen. That was the problem. That had always been the problem.

Behind his eyes, intermittently, her son looked out.

She had learned to recognise the transition. It was not dramatic. There was no convulsion, no speaking in tongues, no theatrical shuddering of the kind the court priests performed for the crowds. The shift was acoustic — a slight change in the resonant frequency of the vocal cords, detectable only if you knew what to listen for. When Nimrod spoke, his voice carried the chest resonance of a large man accustomed to being obeyed. When Ohya surfaced, the overtone shifted. Higher. Thinner. The voice of something speaking through an instrument it had not chosen and could not properly tune.

She did not let herself feel anything when she heard that overtone. She had built the walls for this. Eleven decades of reconstruction after the Flood — stone by stone, decision by decision, identity by borrowed identity — until the woman who had screamed in the water was buried so deep beneath the woman who managed empires that the two could not hear each other.

The walls held. They were visible as walls, if you knew where to look. She knew where to look. She chose not to.

"The binding frequency," she said. Her voice was level. Clinical. The voice of an intelligence operative conducting a debrief, not a mother listening to her dead son speak through a stranger's mouth. "The third partial. You said you were close."

Nimrod's head turned toward her, and for a moment the eyes were wrong — too old, too knowing, carrying a weight that Nimrod's thirty years of life could not account for. Ohya. Looking at her from inside a cage of flesh and bone and the hunting king's ungovernable pride.

"I was there," Ohya said, using Nimrod's throat. The voice strained at the edges, like fabric pulled too tight. "When the archangel spoke the binding. I was in the valley above Dudael. I heard the frequencies. I *carried* them into the Abyss."

"And?"

"And the vessel cannot hold the memory at the resolution I need." The old eyes flickered. Nimrod's jaw tightened — a muscular reflex that belonged to the king, not the passenger. The two of them fought constantly for the body's autonomic systems, and neither of them noticed they were doing it, and Naamah noticed everything. "The third partial of the binding sequence is a complex harmonic. Sixteen simultaneous tones. I can reproduce eleven through this —" he gestured at Nimrod's throat, at the vocal apparatus that was designed for war cries and seduction and the performance of royal authority, not for channelling the acoustic architecture of an archangel's prison key — "but the remaining five require a frequency range this body does not possess. Human vocal cords cannot produce them."

She had known this. She had known this for months. She had been watching the extraction sessions degrade — not in accuracy, but in resolution. Each session pulled Ohya further to the surface and held him there longer, but the information he brought back was like a painting viewed through water: the broad shapes were clear; the fine detail dissolved at the interface between his memory and Nimrod's neurology.

Eleven of sixteen tones. Close enough to confirm the key existed. Not close enough to use it.

"The vessel is degrading," she said. Not a question.

"The vessel is *distracted*." Ohya's contempt surfaced through Nimrod's features — a curl of the lip that did not belong to the face wearing it. "He builds. He schemes. He wants his tower. Every hour I spend submerged, he spends building something higher. His ambitions are running perpendicular to your purpose, and I cannot hold the fine structure of the memory while he is dreaming of ziggurats."

This was the failure she had been mapping for six months. Not a catastrophic failure. Not a dramatic collapse. A slow, structural incompatibility between the vessel's nature and the intelligence it was required to contain. Nimrod's pride was not a flaw in the experiment — it was the experiment's load-bearing wall, the quality that made him strong enough to survive the pharmakeia in the first place. You could not seat an Abyssal spirit in a weak man. The vessel needed to be extraordinary. But extraordinary men have their own trajectories, their own appetites, their own gravitational fields, and Nimrod's field was bending the experiment toward his purposes faster than Naamah could correct for it.

The tower was the symptom. Every day it grew taller, and every day Ohya's extraction sessions yielded less, and the correlation was not coincidental. Nimrod was investing the body's acoustic energy — the same energy the pharmakeia channelled to maintain the seat — into the project that his pride demanded. He was siphoning the experiment's fuel to build a monument to himself, and he did not know he was doing it, because Nimrod did not know what he was for. Nimrod thought he was a king. He was a container.

"How long?" she asked.

"Before the vessel reaches its limit? Weeks. Months at the outside." The eyes shifted again — Ohya receding, Nimrod's hunting intelligence surfacing, the two of them trading control of the face like swimmers surfacing for air. "He is beginning to hear me. Not clearly. But the barrier between us is thinning. When he understands what he is carrying, he will fight it, and when he fights it, the pharmakeia will fail, and I will go back."

Back. To the Abyss. To the darkness and the commission and the four thousand years of preparation that Mastema had laid out for him, building the army he would one day command, an army of locusts that would pour out of the pit at the fifth trumpet and cover the earth.

Naamah did not flinch. She had not flinched since the water.

"The eleven tones," she said. "The partials you can reproduce. I need them recorded."

"You have them."

"I need them *verified*. Against the harmonic structure of the binding site. If the eleven partials are accurate, I can reconstruct the remaining five through substrate interpolation. It will take time."

"How much time?"

She looked at her son, wearing a dead king's future like an ill-fitting coat, and she told him the truth because there was no one else in the world she could tell it to, and even this was not intimacy — it was operational.

"Centuries," she said. "Perhaps longer. I need to find where Raphael bound him. The geography has changed. Everything has changed."

Ohya said nothing. Nimrod's chest rose and fell with the rhythm of a body that did not know it was a battleground.

"I will find it," she said. And that was all she said, because she did not make promises to her son — she had never made promises to anyone — and what she meant was not *I will find it for you* but *I will find it because I need what is inside it*, and the distinction was the architecture of everything she had built since the Flood, and she did not examine it, and she did not soften it, and she turned from the room before the overtone in his voice could shift back to the register that sounded like the boy she had not saved.

---

Babel fell eleven weeks later.

Not the tower — the conditions. The distinction mattered to Naamah, because everyone remembered the tower and no one remembered what the tower's fall actually cost.

God's judgment came as language. Not fire, not water, not the tectonic fury of the Flood. Something more surgical. The tongues divided. One morning, the builders understood each other; the next morning, they did not. The masons could not speak to the labourers. The engineers could not speak to the surveyors. The priests could not speak to the congregation. The entire apparatus of collective human endeavour — the thing that Nimrod had unified through force and charisma and the weight of his reputation — fractured along linguistic lines so precise that Naamah recognised the signature immediately.

This was not entropy. This was *design*.

The city emptied in stages. Families found families. Clans found clans. The human instinct for comprehension overrode the human instinct for power, and the empire Nimrod had assembled scattered to the four winds, each fragment carrying its new language like a seed, each seed containing the genetic code of a civilisation that would grow in isolation from every other.

Nimrod raged. The tower stood half-finished against the Mesopotamian sky, a monument to the exact moment when human ambition exceeded divine permission. He raged and he built and he ordered and he commanded, and his commands went out in a language that fewer people understood each day, and the tower did not grow, and the city emptied, and the pharmakeia — dependent on a stable acoustic environment maintained by the collective resonance of a unified population — began to fail.

Naamah watched the failure from the chamber beneath the throne room, where no one came because no one knew the room existed.

The light went out of Nimrod's eyes on a Tuesday. Not all at once. There was no convulsion, no scream, no dramatic severance. The overtone in his voice — the one she had learned to listen for, the one that meant her son was present — simply thinned. Attenuated. Became less and less distinct, like a sound moving away across water, until there was nothing in the vocal frequency but Nimrod, and Nimrod was confused, and Nimrod was alone, and Nimrod would spend the rest of his short life feeling haunted by something he could almost remember but never quite name.

Ohya was gone. Back to the Abyss. Back to the dark and the commission and the locusts.

She had eleven of sixteen tones. She had the approximate vector of Raphael's binding. She had four thousand years.

Mastema had known it would fail.

She understood that now. She had been mapping the failure's architecture — the cascade of conditions, the tower's siphoning of acoustic energy, Nimrod's pride as structural inevitability — and in the mapping she had found the shape of a hand she recognised. Not God's hand. God's hand was the language judgment, unmistakable, sovereign, indifferent to her specifically. The other hand was subtler. The hand that had cultivated Nimrod's pride. The hand that had whispered *higher, higher* to a man who was already inclined to build. The hand that needed Ohya in the Abyss for a very long time, because the fifth trumpet was not yet, and the army was not yet ready, and God's timeline did not serve Naamah's.

Mastema. The Accuser. The one God had permitted to retain a tenth of the fallen spirits to test and afflict the children of men. He had sabotaged the experiment — not by attacking it, but by feeding the vessel's pride until the vessel consumed itself. Elegant. Deniable. Executed entirely within the latitude God permitted him.

She mapped this. She filed it. She did not waste energy on rage.

She had lost the experiment. She had not lost the data.

---

Semiramis died that night.

It was quiet. There was no struggle. The queen had outlived her usefulness the moment Nimrod's eyes went empty, because Naamah had needed her alive only as long as the political architecture required a visible queen to manage while the real work happened below the throne room. The visible queen was no longer needed. The visible queen's *identity* was.

Naamah closed the door behind her. She opened the window to change the air. She sat on the edge of the dead woman's bed and she looked at the dead woman's face and she began the process she had perfected in eleven decades of post-Flood survival: she studied the bone structure, the habitual expressions, the way the skin gathered at the corners of the eyes and mouth. She listened to the acoustic signature of the room — how Semiramis's voice had resonated in this particular space, against these particular walls, and she began to tune.

By morning, she was Semiramis. By the end of the week, Semiramis's household could not tell the difference. By the end of the month, Naamah had assumed not only the name and face but the political network, the priestly contacts, the intelligence infrastructure, and the religious system she had been building through Nimrod's reign — the system that would outlast Nimrod, outlast Babel, outlast every political entity in Mesopotamia, and survive in various forms across every civilisation she would touch for the next four thousand years.

The Mystery. Babylon. The mother of harlots and abominations of the earth.

She built it. She wore it. She made it beautiful and terrible and self-sustaining, a system of worship and commerce and control that operated in the space God's sovereignty permitted — the gap between divine decree and human free will where a sufficiently patient architect could build an empire that scripture itself would name.

She was patient. She had learned patience in the water. She had learned it from the sound of her son's voice disappearing into a flood she could not stop, and from the one hundred and forty-four years she had spent afterward floating in a body she did not recognise, waiting for the land to appear, and she would never be impatient again.

### *Ashtoreth*, Ross Sea — March 2026

Naamah set the drilling report on the nightstand and stood.

She dressed without turning on the light. Black wool. Practical layers. Boots that made no sound on the steel deck. The movements were automatic, precise, calibrated by four millennia of practice at being someone whose body never betrayed uncertainty. The sweating had stopped. The shaking had stopped. The walls were back in place, and the woman who had screamed in the water was buried again, and the woman who managed empires was the only one in the room.

She walked the corridor to the bridge. The ship moved beneath her in the long rolls of the Ross Sea, and the dark outside the portholes was absolute, and she did not look at the water.

The night watch stiffened when she entered. They always did. She had been aboard the *Ashtoreth* for three years and no member of the crew had ever seen her startled, uncertain, or afraid. They did not know her name. They knew her as the Director. They knew the project was deep-ice acoustic survey, which was true in the way that describing a wolf as a quadruped was true. They knew not to ask questions.

She crossed to the sonar station. The operator moved aside without being told.

The display showed the familiar topography of the sub-ice landscape — the Ross Ice Shelf above, nine hundred metres of ancient ice, and beneath it the sea floor descending toward the continental shelf. And there, at 1,247 metres, the anomaly. The thing the drills had been chasing. The substrate that returned an acoustic signature consistent with Cydonian metamaterial — the building blocks of Watcher infrastructure, the material the Twenty had used to mark their domains before the world broke.

Gadreel's stele. Gadreel's domain. The place where Raphael had spoken the binding over Azazel and sealed him in the desert of Dudael — a desert that had been a desert when the Watchers walked the earth, and was now under a kilometre of Antarctic ice, and the irony of that was the kind of thing she would have found amusing four thousand years ago and did not find amusing now.

She was close. Closer than she had been in four thousand years of searching. The acoustic resonance at the boundary layer was increasing — the stele responding to the drill's approach the way Watcher metamaterial always responded to sustained acoustic contact. Waking up. Remembering what it was. Beginning to broadcast.

Eleven of sixteen tones.

She had spent four thousand years on the remaining five. Continental drift calibration using the Tamiel stele in Peru as a vector reference. Substrate interpolation across six acoustic libraries she had assembled from every Watcher site she had located on the post-Flood earth. Iteration after iteration, century after century, refining the reconstruction one partial at a time, and she was not finished but she was close, and when the drill reached the stele she would have the resonance data to complete the key, and when the key was complete she would open Dudael, and when Dudael was open she would have Azazel, and Azazel knew how to build a vessel that could hold her son, and her son would ride out of the Abyss at the head of the army he had been building for four thousand years, and she would ride him, and the Whore of Babylon would mount the Beast, and Revelation would unspool exactly as written, and every step would be hers, and every step had always been hers, and God had known it, and she had known God had known it, and she did not care.

She leaned forward. The sonar display pulsed with the slow heartbeat of something ancient waking beneath the ice.

"Increase drill speed to maximum sustainable rate," she said. Her voice was the voice of a woman who had survived the remaking of the world and the remaking of herself and the loss of the only thing she had ever loved without reservation, and the voice did not shake, and the voice had not shaken in four thousand years.

The night watch obeyed.

Below the ice, the door was opening.
