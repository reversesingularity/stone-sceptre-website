## II. THREE WORDS THE ENGLISH BURIES

### Word 1: חָלַל (chalal) — "began"

The form **הֵחֵל** (*he'chel*) is the Hiphil (causative) stem of the
root חלל (*chalal*). The KJV renders it "began." This is grammatically
defensible. It is also incomplete.

The root חלל across its stems:

| Stem | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Qal** | to wound, to pierce, to slay |
| **Niphal** | to be defiled, to be profaned, to be polluted |
| **Piel** | to profane, to desecrate, to defile, to violate |
| **Pual** | to be profaned |
| **Hiphil** | to begin — but also: to cause profanation |

Hebrew roots carry their semantic weight across all stems. A reader
fluent in Hebrew does not hear **הֵחֵל** ("he began") without hearing
the root beneath it — a root saturated in profanation, defilement, and
wounding. The word is not neutral. It is loaded. Moses had cleaner
verbs available for "begin." He chose this one.

**The reading within the semantic range:**

*"He was profaned/defiled into becoming a gibbor in the earth."*

This is not eisegesis. This is the root doing what Hebrew roots do —
carrying connotation across stems. Multiple rabbinical commentators
have noted it. Rashi connects Nimrod to rebellion against God.
The Targum Pseudo-Jonathan renders the passage as Nimrod becoming
"a mighty rebel/sinner before the LORD" — the translators read
something darker than "began" in the Hebrew.

**For TNC:** The pharmakeia that seated Ohya's Abyssal spirit in
Nimrod's body is precisely the mechanism by which a post-Flood human
is "profaned into becoming" a gibbor. The Hebrew preserves the
violation. The English flattens it.

### Word 2: גִּבּוֹר (gibbor) — "mighty one"

**Gibbor** is not a compliment. It is an ontological category.

Its two critical appearances in Genesis:

**Genesis 6:4** — *"the same became mighty men* (**gibborim**) *which
were of old, men of renown."*

These are the Nephilim offspring. The children of the Watchers and
human women. The gibborim of Genesis 6 are explicitly hybrid beings —
angelic-human crosses whose physical and cognitive capabilities
exceeded anything the human genome could produce. The word gibbor
here describes what they *are*, not what they achieved. It is a
category of being, not a degree of accomplishment.

**Genesis 10:8** — *"he began to be a* **gibbor** *in the earth."*

Same word. Same category. The text places Nimrod in the identical
classification as the pre-Flood Nephilim offspring.

**The question the Hebrew forces:**

How does a post-Flood human — the son of Cush, grandson of Ham, a
man born from the line that survived on the ark — "begin to be" a
gibbor? You do not gradually become a demigod through training or
ambition. The gibbor classification in Genesis 6 is not earned. It
is *constitutive*. It describes what something is at the level of
its nature.

Unless something was done to him from outside.

Unless the category was imposed — violently, supernaturally, through
an act of profanation (חלל) that changed what he was at a level
deeper than diet and exercise could reach.

**For TNC:** The Nimrod experiment — Naamah's pharmakeia seating
Ohya's Abyssal spirit into Nimrod's body — is the mechanism the
Hebrew describes. Nimrod did not "begin to be" a gibbor through
natural means. He was *profaned into* the gibbor category by having
a Nephilim spirit seated in his flesh. The experiment worked. It
made him what the text says he was. What it could not do was make
him *stable*.

### Word 3: לִפְנֵי יְהוָה (liphnei YHWH) — "before the LORD"

The Hebrew **פָּנִים** (*paniym* — face, presence) with the
preposition **לְ** (*l'* — to, toward, before) creates a phrase
that carries a range:

- In the presence of (neutral/positional)
- In the face of (confrontational)
- In opposition to / against

**The ancient translators read it as confrontational — and gigantic:**

**Septuagint (LXX):** The LXX makes *two* interpretive moves that
the English buries. First, it translates **gibbor** not as "mighty"
but as **γίγας** (*gigas*) — literally **"giant."** The Alexandrian
Jewish translators (~250 BCE) heard gibbor in Genesis 10 and
recognised the same ontological category as the Nephilim offspring
of Genesis 6:4. They did not translate it as "strong man" or "warrior."
They said *giant*. They placed Nimrod explicitly in the Nephilim
category by name.

Second, it translates *liphnei YHWH* as **ἐναντίον κυρίου τοῦ θεοῦ**
(*enantion kuriou tou theou*). This is unambiguous. *Enantion*
means "against," "in opposition to," "contrary to."

The full LXX of Genesis 10:9: **οὗτος ἦν γίγας κυνηγὸς ἐναντίον
κυρίου τοῦ θεοῦ** — "This one was a *giant* hunter *against*
the Lord God."

The Greek-speaking Jews of Alexandria — working approximately
250 BCE, much closer to the original Hebrew context than any
modern translator — heard both the Nephilim classification and
the adversarial posture. Modern English hides both.

**Targum Pseudo-Jonathan:** Renders the passage explicitly as
Nimrod being "a mighty rebel before the LORD" and adds that he
led the people of the earth to rebellion against God. The Aramaic
paraphrase does not hedge. It reads the Hebrew as adversarial.

**Targum Onkelos:** More restrained but still translates with a
sense of defiance rather than neutral proximity.

**Josephus (Antiquities 1.4.2):** Describes Nimrod as gradually
turning the people away from God, changing the government into
tyranny, and building the tower in defiance of God's command to
scatter.

**For TNC:** Nimrod, with Ohya riding behind his eyes, was a
gibbor-hunter *against* God. The Tower of Babel was not merely
hubris — it was a man-turned-gibbor, animated by a Nephilim
spirit commissioned in the Abyss, building upward in direct
opposition to the God who had drowned the world his spirit came
from. The text says so. The ancient translations confirm it. The
English hides it.

**Four ancient witnesses — all reading the Hebrew the same way:**

| Source | Date | Reading of Nimrod |
|--------|------|-------------------|
| **Septuagint (LXX)** | ~250 BCE | γίγας (*gigas*) — "giant" (Nephilim category). ἐναντίον κυρίου — "against the Lord" |
| **Targum Onkelos** | ~100 CE | "a strong rebel" |
| **Targum Pseudo-Jonathan** | ~4th–7th C | "mighty in sin, and to rebel before the Lord in the earth" |
| **Josephus** (*Ant.* 1.4.2) | ~93 CE | Turned the people away from God; tyranny; defiance of the divine command |

Every ancient translator who touched this text heard something
darker than "began to be a mighty one before the LORD." The only
tradition that reads it admiringly is the modern English one.

The English "began to be a mighty one before the LORD" sounds almost
admiring. The Hebrew is horrified.

## V. THE GIBBOR COUNTERARGUMENT — AND ITS ANSWER

### The Challenge

If gibbor carries Nephilim-category weight in Genesis 10:8, what do
we do with the 150+ other occurrences of the word across the Tanakh?

David's Mighty Warriors (2 Samuel 23:8-38) are called **gibborim**.
Gideon is addressed as a gibbor (Judges 6:12). Joshua's fighting men
are gibborim. Lions, warriors, kings, and hunters across the Hebrew
Bible carry the word. If every gibbor is a Nephilim-class entity,
the Old Testament becomes incoherent.

This is the strongest objection to the argument above. It deserves a
serious answer.

### The Cynic's Case (Stated Fairly)

The cynic says: gibbor is a common Hebrew term for strength, power,
and martial prowess. It means "mighty one" — sometimes very mighty,
sometimes merely strong, always human-scale unless explicit
supernatural context overrides it. The word functions the way English
"champion" does — it can describe a boxing champion or a champion of
justice. Context determines meaning, not etymology. Applying the
Genesis 6:4 classification to every gibbor in the Tanakh is
overreading.

Michael Heiser, among the best scholars of the Watchers tradition,
has argued that the Septuagint's use of γίγας (gigas) for gibbor
is a loose and inconsistent translation choice. The LXX does not
translate gibbor as gigas in Joshua 1:14, 2 Samuel 23, or the vast
majority of its occurrences. This inconsistency, the argument goes,
undermines the claim that gigas was a deliberate Nephilim-category
marker for Nimrod.

The cynic is right about the data. The word is common. The
translation varies. The inconsistency is real.

The cynic is wrong about what the inconsistency proves.

### The Smoking Gun: Selective Translation

The LXX translators had multiple Greek words available for gibbor
and deployed them **differently depending on context**:

| Context | Hebrew | Greek Translation | Category |
|---------|--------|-------------------|----------|
| Genesis 6:4 — Nephilim offspring | גִּבּוֹרִים (gibborim) | **γίγαντες** (gigantes) | Nephilim |
| Genesis 10:8-9 — Nimrod | גִּבֹּר (gibbor) | **γίγας** (gigas) | Nephilim |
| 2 Samuel 23:8-38 — David's warriors | גִּבּוֹרִים (gibborim) | **δυνατοί** (dunatoi) | Human |
| Joshua 1:14 — fighting men | גִּבּוֹרֵי (gibborei) | **δυνατοί** (dunatoi) | Human |
| Judges 6:12 — Gideon | גִּבּוֹר (gibbor) | **δυνατός** (dunatos) | Human |
| Judges 11:1 — Jephthah | גִּבּוֹר (gibbor) | **δυνατός** (dunatos) | Human |

The translators **chose differently**.

When they heard gibbor in military and heroic contexts — David's
warriors, Gideon, Joshua's soldiers — they reached for **δυνατός**
(dunatos) and its plural **δυνατοί** (dunatoi): "capable,"
"powerful," "strong." A human-scale word. An achievable category.

When they heard gibbor in Genesis's primeval narrative — where the
gibbor category had been established four chapters earlier as the
classification of Nephilim offspring — they reached for **γίγας**
(gigas): "giant." A Nephilim-scale word. A category that implies
something other than human merit.

This was not mechanical translation. This was **interpretive
selection**. The Alexandrian Jewish scholars who produced the Genesis
portion of the LXX had *just* translated Genesis 6:4, rendering
gibborim as γίγαντες (gigantes) — the Nephilim offspring, the
hybrid beings who prompted the Flood. Four chapters later, they
encountered gibbor again for Nimrod and made the **identical lexical
choice**. Same translator. Same book. Same session of work. Same
word — different Greek — by conscious, contextual, theological
decision.

### Why the Inconsistency IS the Evidence

The inconsistency *across different books* of the LXX (different
translators, different centuries, different source contexts) is
irrelevant to the Genesis argument. What matters is the consistency
*within Genesis* — where a single translation team, working through
a single book, chose γίγας for the Nephilim offspring in 6:4 and
then chose γίγας again for Nimrod in 10:8.

If anything, the variation across the broader LXX *strengthens* the
case. It proves the translators were not mechanically rendering
gibbor as gigas every time they saw it. They had δυνατός in their
vocabulary. They used it frequently. When they reached for gigas
instead — specifically in Genesis, specifically for Nimrod, four
chapters after the Nephilim — it was because they heard something in
the Hebrew that the military contexts did not carry.

The selective translation pattern is the strongest evidence. The LXX
translators were not inconsistent. They were *interpreting*. And when
they interpreted Nimrod, they put him in the Nephilim category and
left David's men out of it.
