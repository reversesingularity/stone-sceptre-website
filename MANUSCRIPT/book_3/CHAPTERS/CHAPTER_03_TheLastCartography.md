## I. THE BLIND SPOT

**Stewart Island, New Zealand — McNeeve Safe House, Sub-Level 2 (Lab)**

**March 16, 2028 — 0547 Hours (NZDT)**

---

What the instruments had never been designed to detect was the acoustic signature of *unfallen* creation.

Miriam pulled the bearing data from Mo Chrá's nine-second tone. The destination wasn't the problem; it was the twelve thousand kilometres between here and there, and what was listening along the way. She overlaid the Josephite intelligence map of the Thirteen Houses against Chris Guilder's acoustic leyline network.

"It's not hidden," Miriam said to the empty laboratory and the sword that wasn't empty. "It's *inaudible*."

If they maintained the sword's 432 Hz resonance envelope during transit, they would pass through House Khem's surveillance grid like a dog whistle through a room full of people listening for bass notes. Undetectable. Not stealth — simply singing in a key the enemy's ears had been surgically removed from hearing.

The only question was the perimeter. The cherubim.

She couldn't map it with instruments. She couldn't assign coordinates to a divine rotational harmonic. But she could feel its edge — the threshold where the fallen world's ambient corruption thinned enough for unfallen physics to assert themselves. It wasn't a barrier. It was an invitation that could only be accepted on its own terms.

She marked the approximate boundary somewhere between the eastern Mediterranean and the Iraqi basin.

"I can find it," she told the sword. "When we get close, I'll know."

Mo Chrá's hum warmed. One-tenth of a hertz. The tonal equivalent of a nod.

## II. THE THIRD SEAL

**Stewart Island, New Zealand — McNeeve Safe House, Communications Room**

**March 16, 2028 — 1034 Hours (NZDT)**

---

Brennan saw the rider before the rider had a name.

He was monitoring nine feeds — the same configuration he had maintained since the team's arrival from Antarctica, the same wall of screens that had shown him Dr. Ezra Adon's face at the UN and the cure that was not a cure and the beginning of the thing he was now watching accelerate beyond his capacity to model. Nine screens. Three financial data terminals: Bloomberg, Reuters Eikon, the Josephite dark-pool monitor that tracked capital flows through the Synagogue's known settlement architecture. Three news aggregators: BBC World, Al Jazeera, the decentralised Josephite SIGINT feed from the MEREWYN Division's passive intercept network. Two satellite feeds: Brennan's own pre-dissolution telemetry cache — sixty days of coverage remaining before orbital decay rendered the data worthless — and the Josephite southern hemisphere GEOINT platform. One screen dark — reserved for Chris Guilder's encrypted video link from Galway, currently idle because it was three in the morning in western Ireland and Chris had been awake for thirty-one hours before Brennan ordered him offline.

The Bloomberg terminal updated at 1034:17 NZDT.

It was not dramatic. The most consequential economic events in human history rarely were — they did not arrive with sound effects or visual cues; they arrived as numbers, as data points, as adjustments to indices whose names meant nothing to ninety-nine percent of the species whose survival depended on them. The CBOT Wheat Futures contract for May 2028 delivery opened at $14.72 per bushel. It had closed the previous session at $8.40.

Brennan stared at the number for four seconds. In those four seconds, the engineer's mind — the mind that translated everything into physical parameters, that reduced revelation to resonance and prophecy to phase angles — performed the calculation that the markets would spend the next seventy-two hours failing to complete.

Adon's cure had been adopted by forty-seven national health ministries. Fourteen more were in regulatory fast-track. The combined pharmaceutical production capacity required to manufacture four billion doses of a protein-synthesis protocol that used Cydonian molecular geometry demanded raw materials — reagents, lipid nanoparticle substrates, cold-chain logistics, specialised bioreactor capacity — that the global supply chain could not produce while simultaneously sustaining normal agricultural output. The manufacturing pipeline commandeered industrial capacity. Shipping lanes redirected. Chemical feedstock that had been allocated to fertiliser production was reallocated to pharmaceutical synthesis because the cure was more immediately profitable and the governments that had adopted Adon's protocol were contractually obligated to prioritise its production.

The result was not a shortage. It was a *substitution*. The cure replaced food as the primary output of the global industrial economy — not because anyone decided it should, but because the economic incentives Adon had structured into his "free" IP release made it inevitable. He had donated the cure without licensing fees, which meant governments bore the manufacturing cost, which meant they needed capital, which meant they issued bonds against the projected healthcare savings, which meant the bond markets priced the cure's distribution as the most reliable revenue stream on Earth, which meant institutional investors reallocated from agricultural commodities to pharmaceutical infrastructure bonds, which meant grain futures collapsed and then inverted when the supply contraction became visible, and by the time the CBOT opened on March 16, 2028, the price of wheat had risen seventy-five percent overnight because the money that used to grow food was now growing salvation and the market had not yet understood that the two were the same equation with different denominators and that the entity who had written the equation had five thousand years of experience designing systems whose true function was invisible until the victims were dependent on them.

*A measure of wheat for a denarius.*

Brennan had read Revelation. Every Josephite did — not as theology but as operational intelligence, the way a submarine commander reads Soviet naval doctrine: not because he believes in the ideology but because the ideology predicts the fleet movements, and the fleet movements will kill him if he cannot predict them first. He had read the Third Seal the way he read every prophetic passage — as a specification document for a system that had been designed and that would be deployed and that had a discoverable architecture if you understood the engineer who built it.

The Black Rider carried a balance. *A pair of scales.* Not a weapon — a measuring instrument. The angel did not bring famine. The angel *measured* the famine that the Beast's economic architecture had already produced, and announced the exchange rate that the world would pay.

"A quart of wheat for a denarius," Brennan said aloud, to the screens and to the silence and to the empty chair beside him where Miriam had been monitoring Asian markets until 0300 hours before he had sent her to sleep. A denarius was a day's wage in first-century Rome. A quart of wheat was enough to sustain one person for one day. The Third Seal was not the imposition of poverty. It was the revelation that the cure — the miracle, the salvation, the thing the world had wept with gratitude to receive — had restructured the global economy so that a day's labour purchased exactly enough food to survive until the next day's labour, and nothing more, and the surplus that had defined human civilisation since the agricultural revolution was gone, not destroyed but *redirected*, channelled into the pharmaceutical infrastructure that kept the cure flowing, and the cure kept flowing because the disease kept threatening, and the disease kept threatening because the acoustic frequency embedded in every dose ensured that the cure was also the leash.

It was the weaponization of desperation. Brennan watched the numbers climb, realizing that Azazel hadn't just cornered the pharmaceutical market; he had calculated the exact caloric intake required to keep a human body functioning just enough to work, but too exhausted to rebel. The algorithm didn't want them dead. It wanted them compliant, perpetually teetering on the edge of starvation, entirely dependent on the Beast's supply chain for tomorrow's breath. It was a mathematical guillotine, lowering by fractions of a cent per hour.

The Bloomberg terminal updated again. Corn futures. Rice futures. Soybean contracts. Each one climbing in the same impossible trajectory — not panic buying but structural repricing, the market discovering in real-time that the cure's supply chain and the food supply chain were the same chain and that Adon had welded them together with the quiet precision of a craftsman who had been sealed in a dark place for five thousand years with nothing to do but design.

Reuters Eikon showed the geopolitical layer. The Red Horse had already done its work — the regional conflicts that Azazel's emergence had triggered were burning across four continents. Three African nations had declared martial law over cure distribution disputes. A naval standoff in the South China Sea over pharmaceutical shipping lanes. India and Pakistan exchanging artillery fire across the Line of Control after a contested vaccine allocation. The wars were not about ideology or territory. They were about *access* — who controlled the cure, who received it, who was excluded — and the nations fighting over it did not understand that the conflict itself was the product, that the Red Horse and the Black Horse were not separate riders but sequential operations in the same campaign, that war created the conditions for famine the way a surgeon's incision creates the conditions for the suture, and both were designed by the same hand.

*And see thou hurt not the oil and the wine.*

The Bloomberg terminal confirmed it. Luxury commodities — petroleum derivatives, high-end agricultural products, the material infrastructure of wealth — remained stable. The rider's scales weighed wheat and barley, the staples of the poor. The oil and the wine, the provisions of the powerful, were exempt. The famine was *selective*. It crushed the base of the pyramid and left the apex untouched, and the apex was the Ten Kings, and the Ten Kings were Azazel's operational infrastructure, and the infrastructure was being fed while the world starved, and the world would be grateful for the starvation because the man who caused it had also cured the plague that would have killed more, and gratitude is the most effective chain ever forged because the prisoner polishes it himself.

Brennan reached for the intercom. Paused. Looked at the wheat price one more time — $14.72, still climbing — and felt the particular kind of recognition that comes when an engineer reads a system specification and realises that the system has already been built, that it is already operational, that the question is not whether it will work but how long before the operators notice that they are inside it.

He pressed the intercom.

"James. You need to see this."

## III. THE WEIGHT THE SWORD WON'T CARRY

**Stewart Island, New Zealand — McNeeve Safe House, Ground Level**

**March 16, 2028 — 2203 Hours (NZDT)**

---

The rain had stopped.

Cian noticed because Mo Chrá noticed — the sword's subsistence hum shifted when the ambient acoustics changed, the way a musician's posture shifts when the room's reverb alters, and the cessation of rain on the corrugated-steel roof of the safe house's ground-level structure changed the room's acoustic profile enough that the blade adjusted her baseline by a quarter-hertz. A change too small for any human instrument in the building to detect. Cian detected it because he had been listening to her adjustments for twenty-six centuries and his nervous system no longer distinguished between her frequency responses and his own proprioception. She was, in the ways that mattered, a limb.

They were in the kitchen. Not the briefing room, not the lab, not the communications centre — the kitchen, because Brennan McNeeve had spent six weeks eating at workbenches and sleeping in server rooms and the last meal he would share with the man who had recruited his great-great-grandmother's great-great-grandmother into a war against entities older than human agriculture would not take place beside a spectrograph.

Cian had cooked. Lamb — procured from the Stewart Island Josephite supply cache, which maintained provisions for twelve operators for ninety days and which Cian had supplemented with the particular instincts of a man who had provisioned Celtic war-bands before the Romans arrived and who understood that the meal before a separation carried ceremonial weight regardless of whether anyone acknowledged the ceremony. He had cooked it simply: seared, rested, served with potatoes and the last of the fresh rosemary from the safe house's small garden plot, which would not be tended again because the people who tended it were about to scatter to different wars on different continents and the garden would grow wild and the rosemary would survive because rosemary always survived, which was why he had planted it, six weeks ago, with the unconscious foresight of a man who chose resilient things because resilient things were what he understood.

Brennan ate. James ate. Miriam ate in the communications room — her choice; she had said she wanted to monitor the European markets' overnight response to the grain repricing, and she did, but she also understood that some conversations between men who had shared a particular kind of proximity required the mercy of a closed door. She was learning this. She was learning many things, at a pace that the other three in the building tracked with the careful attention of people who recognised an operational asset reaching capability and who did not discuss it because discussion would have been a form of diminishment.

At 2203 hours, James excused himself. "0430 departure prep," he said, and meant it, and also meant *this is between you two*, and left with the efficiency of a man whose emotional intelligence was expressed through tactical logistics rather than speech.

The kitchen was quiet. Mo Chrá hummed at 7.83 Hz. The stove ticked as it cooled. Outside, the absence of rain revealed sounds that fourteen days of continuous storm had been masking: wind in the native bush, the distant percussion of surf on the southern coast, a morepork calling from the tree line with the two-note insistence of a bird that had been saying the same thing since before the Polynesian navigators arrived and that would be saying it long after the safe house rotted back into the soil.

Brennan placed a USB drive on the table between them.

"Satellite access. The Cayman trust keys. Dead-drop locations for all twenty-three safe houses — five nations, rotating encryption, ninety-day cycle." He looked at the drive the way an engineer looks at a component that has been tested and certified and that he is now handing to someone who will use it in conditions the testing protocol could not simulate. "Sarah built the trust architecture. The encryption is her design. The rotation schedule is automatic — no human intervention required. If I'm compromised, the system continues."

Cian picked up the drive. Small. Plastic. The weight of a civilisation's mercy, reduced to forty grams and a USB-C connector.

"The Mark system," Cian said. "How long?"

"Layer Zero is in prototype. Biometric enrolment, financial exclusion architecture — the infrastructure is already in Adon's distribution network. He didn't build a pharmaceutical supply chain. He built a digital identity platform that *happens* to deliver medicine." Brennan's voice carried the flat register of an engineer describing an elegant solution to a problem he wished did not exist. "Six months to full deployment. Nine months to ubiquity. By Christmas 2028, anyone without the Mark — whatever form it takes — won't be able to buy food at the prices the Third Seal has set. The Josephite safe houses are the only alternative."

"Twenty-three safe houses. For how many people?"

"The houses can sustain twelve hundred at capacity. The satellite network can coordinate resupply for three thousand. Beyond that —" Brennan stopped. The sentence had run into the wall that every logistics calculation eventually hits — the wall between what can be sustained and what cannot, between the people you can feed and the people you cannot, between the engineer's mandate to solve problems and the theologian's awareness that some problems are judgments and judgments are not engineering challenges. "Beyond that, we're in the Lord's hands."

Cian nodded. It was the nod of a man who had spent twenty-six centuries living in the Lord's hands and who had learned that the Lord's hands were larger than human logistics but that the Lord had a documented preference for using human logistics as the mechanism of His providence, and that the mechanism required maintenance, and that maintenance was Brennan McNeeve's gift, and that the gift was about to be tested at a scale that neither of them could model.

"You can't come where I'm going," Cian said. Not a question.

"No." Brennan did not elaborate. The architectural decision was settled — confirmed by the operational assessment, ratified by the reality that the journey to Eden required Mo Chrá's creation harmonic as both compass and shield and that the people who could travel inside that harmonic's protective envelope were limited to those whose spirit-signatures were compatible with unfallen frequency, and Brennan's spirit-signature was Josephite and consecrated and loyal but it was *human*, and the place they were going was not a human place, and the sword would protect him but the cherubim's perimeter would not open for him because the perimeter was not a security system that could be bypassed with the right credentials — it was a *resonance that admitted resonance*, and Brennan's resonance, for all its consecrated integrity, was the resonance of a fallen world.

He knew this. He had known it since the briefing. He had not said it because the knowledge was a private wound and he honoured private wounds by not displaying them.

"Your war is harder than mine," Cian said. His voice carried the Irish cadence — the lilt that surfaced when the man beneath the operator surfaced, when the twenty-six centuries thinned enough to reveal the Iron Age chieftain's son from Connacht who had learned grief before he learned sword-craft and who had never found a weapon that could cut the particular kind of pain that lived in the space between people who loved each other and could not follow each other. "No sword that hums direction. No frequency that tells you you're walking the right way. Just people. Frightened people. And the mercy to keep feeding them when the numbers say you can't."

"I know," Brennan said.

"You're the best of us. You always were."

Brennan McNeeve looked at the table. At the USB drive. At the plates that still held the remnants of the last meal. At the kitchen that he had built into a laboratory and a command centre and a home, in that order, because that was the order in which he understood the world — build it, run it, then allow yourself to live in it — and that was about to become something else. A headquarters. A bunker. A place where frightened people would come because a man they had never met had dissolved a billion-dollar space company to build them a sanctuary, and the man would be here, in this kitchen, making the calculations that kept them alive, and the calculations would be insufficient, and the mercy would be sufficient, and the tension between the two would be the architecture of his war for the next twelve hundred and sixty days.

Mo Chrá's hum shifted.

Not the subsistence note. Not the combat frequency. Not the diagnostic pulse that Brennan had catalogued across fifteen months of observation. This was 8.1 Hz — the recognition frequency. The note the sword produced when Cian lifted her. The note that meant *I know the hand*. The note that, in all of Brennan's recordings, in every spectral analysis, in every dataset he had compiled since the Austin laboratory, had been reserved exclusively for one person.

She was producing it for Brennan.

8.1 Hz. Clear. Sustained. Three seconds. Then it folded back into the subsistence baseline, and the sword returned to 7.83, and the kitchen was quiet again except for the cooling stove and the morepork and the wind.

Brennan had heard it. His eyes moved to Mo Chrá — leaning against the wall beside the door, unremarkable, a blade in a sheath, an object — and in his expression was the particular quality of recognition that occurs when an engineer who has spent his career measuring a phenomenon from the outside is, for the first and only time, measured by the phenomenon in return.

Neither man spoke about it.

They did not need to. Some frequencies carry their own translation.

Cian stood. He extended his hand. Brennan took it — the grip of operators, firm, brief, communicating in the grammar of physical contact everything that the grammar of language could not carry — and then Cian walked to the door and lifted Mo Chrá from the wall and the sword's weight settled into his grip with the particular rightness that meant *home* in the only language the blade had ever spoken, and he left the kitchen.

Behind him, Brennan McNeeve sat in the quiet and began his war.

## IV. THE BEARING

**Stewart Island, New Zealand — Surface**

**March 17, 2028 — 0517 Hours (NZDT)**

---

The world smelled different.

Cian registered it first as the absence of concrete — fourteen days underground, breathing filtered air and volcanic-rock ventilation and the particular recycled staleness of a bunker that was never designed to house people who were deciding the fate of species, and now the surface, the open air, the Stewart Island pre-dawn that carried salt and native bush and the mineral tang of exposed granite and the faint sweetness of rosemary from the garden plot that no one would tend.

Then he registered what was beneath the smell.

Not rain. Not wind. Not any meteorological phenomenon that the fourteen days of continuous storm had prepared him for. The storm had broken overnight — Miriam had noted the pressure change on the comms-room barometer at 0313 hours, a rapid stabilisation unusual for the latitude and the season but not unprecedented. The sky was clear for the first time since they had arrived from Antarctica. Stars. The Southern Cross riding the horizon like a bearing marker placed there by a cartographer who understood that some travellers needed celestial confirmation of what their swords already knew.

Three packs stood by the jetty. Josephite field loadout — standardised since the Greenland operation, refined after Antarctica: seventy-two-hour sustainment, communications gear, medical kit, water purification, ammunition in calibres that assumed the enemy would not be entirely human. Compact. Efficient. The weight of operational necessity and nothing more, because the journey they were beginning did not permit luxury and did not need it and would supply, at its terminus, the only luxury that mattered — a place where the weight could be set down.

Miriam carried the acoustic mapping tablet — ruggedised, Josephite Engineering Division modification, its software loaded with the ingress corridor she had plotted twelve hours ago and the cherubim-perimeter approximation that no software had been designed to model and that she had entered as a hand-drawn boundary because the data that produced it came not from the instruments but from the part of her that the instruments could not reach.

Cian carried Mo Chrá.

The sword hummed at 7.83 Hz. Baseline. Patient. The directional frequency was there — the 432 Hz creation harmonic threaded beneath the subsistence note like a compass needle sewn into the fabric of the blade's identity — but subdued, resting, the way a guide waits at the trailhead for the party to assemble before stepping onto the path.

James Madigan stood at the safe house entrance. He had not come to the jetty. The distance between the door and the water — forty metres of weathered timber boardwalk over native scrub — was the distance between two wars, and James had chosen his war, and the choice required that he remain on his side of the distance. Southern hemisphere Josephite operations. Twelve hundred souls who did not yet know they would need sanctuary. The logistics of mercy and the tactics of defiance.

He raised one hand. The economy of the gesture said everything a longer farewell would have diluted.

Cian returned it. Same economy. Same grammar.

The *Abyss Warden* waited at the jetty — a fifteen-metre aluminium monohull, her diesel idling at a frequency that Brennan's engineering modifications had tuned to harmonic neutrality so that the engine's vibration pattern would not interfere with Mo Chrá's acoustic output. Enochian deck wards hummed beneath the non-skid surface — a subsonic lattice bonded to the hull during consecration, invisible, inaudible to the unconsecrated ear, producing a continuous acoustic perimeter that turned the vessel's hull into a moving ward. The bow-mounted M2HB sat beneath its rain shroud, the .50-calibre barrel angled at the fifteen-degree declination that Josephite maritime doctrine prescribed for surface engagement — the geometry of a weapon that knew what it was for and did not pretend to be decorative.

A Josephite crew of four. No names exchanged. The crew knew who their passengers were. The passengers knew the crew did not need to be known. Operational security was its own form of intimacy — the trust of anonymity, the acknowledgment that in the Josephite Order, function superseded identity because the mission superseded everything.

Miriam stepped aboard first. Her boots found the deck with the particular sureness of a woman who had spent three years learning to move in operational environments and who had discovered, in the process, that her body's spatial awareness was sharper than it should have been — that she navigated physical spaces the way she navigated financial datasets, with an instinctive geometry that preceded calculation, and that this instinct had grown since the Carpathian operation, since the moment Armaros's Gehenna frequency had failed to reach her and the awareness that she was *tuned differently* had shifted from hypothesis to certainty.

The air was different.

She felt it the moment she stepped from the jetty to the deck — not a temperature change, not a pressure change, not anything the barometer in the comms room would have registered. A *resonance*. The same quality she had perceived in the laboratory twelve hours ago, when she had placed her hand on the spectrograph housing and felt the cherubim perimeter through twelve thousand kilometres of planetary crust. But here, on the surface, in the open air, with the stars above and the water below and the pre-dawn stillness carrying every frequency without the filtering that concrete and steel imposed — here, it was stronger. Clearer. The acoustic equivalent of standing inside a cathedral nave one breath before the organ plays — the space *ready*, the air *held*, the resonance of the architecture itself anticipating the sound that would complete it.

She looked at Cian.

He felt it too. She could see it in the set of his shoulders — the particular straightening that occurred when Mo Chrá's frequencies aligned with something external, when the sword's internal compass locked onto a bearing and the bearer's body responded by orienting itself the way iron filings orient to a magnet. Not conscious. Not voluntary. The alignment of a man and a weapon and a frequency and a direction, occurring at a level beneath choice, in the acoustic substrate where physics and prophecy were the same discipline.

Mo Chrá's hum shifted. The subsistence note climbed — not to the 432 Hz creation harmonic; that would come later, when the perimeter was close, when the sword needed to sing in the key that the cherubim's ward-craft would recognise. For now, she settled at 12.4 Hz. The alert state. The frequency that Brennan had catalogued as *Mo Chrá detecting a relevant signal and adjusting baseline awareness accordingly*. Not threat. Not combat. *Orientation*. The sword finding north. The compass engaging.

The *Abyss Warden*'s engines deepened. The crew cast off. The vessel turned north, cutting a clean wake through the Foveaux Strait's pre-dawn surface, and behind them Stewart Island's silhouette diminished with the unhurried patience of a landmass that had existed since the continental shelf was carved and that would exist long after the safe house's lights went dark and the rosemary garden grew wild and the man in the kitchen finished his calculations and began the work of feeding the remnant.

The safe house lights went dark. One by one. Ground level, then sub-level one, then the communications room, then the lab. Brennan, moving through the building, securing the sections that would not be needed until the first refugees arrived. Closing doors. Activating automated systems. Beginning the solitary architecture of his war with the methodical discipline of an engineer who understood that mercy, like metallurgy, required sustained heat at precise temperatures over extended duration, and that the fire had to be tended, and that the tender had to remain.

Miriam watched the lights go dark. She felt each one as a small subtraction in the acoustic landscape — the electrical hum of each circuit ceasing, the building's frequency signature simplifying, resolving from the complex harmonic of an occupied facility into the single sustained note of a structure serving one purpose and one man.

Then the last light went dark, and Stewart Island was a shadow, and the Southern Cross rode the horizon, and the *Abyss Warden*'s consecrated hull hummed with Enochian ward-craft, and Mo Chrá's 12.4 Hz alert frequency pulsed beneath Cian's hand with the steady patience of a compass that had been waiting five thousand years to point this direction and that would not stop pointing until they arrived.

Then Mo Chrá's hum changed.

Not gradually — not the gentle modulation of a blade adjusting to ocean thermals or compass drift. A spike. The 12.4 Hz alert frequency leapt to 14.8 and held — a register that Brennan's acoustic logs had catalogued exactly twice in the blade's operational history, both times in the proximity of active Cydonian infrastructure. Not remnant. Not archaeological trace. *Active*. The frequency of Cydonian metamaterial receiving a signal and resonating in response.

The spike held for four seconds. Then the blade dropped back to 12.4 — alert, not alarmed — but the silence aboard the *Abyss Warden* had changed the way silence changes when everyone in a confined space has heard the same wrong note and no one wants to be the first to name it.

Miriam's hand moved to the acoustic mapping tablet. The ingress corridor she had plotted twelve hours ago showed clean passage — frequency-null lanes threading through the Synagogue's geomantic web, the gaps in the surveillance architecture where the Watchers' constitutional deafness to the 432 Hz creation band rendered the sword's emissions invisible. Clean. Verified three times. The math was good.

But Mo Chrá had heard something on the bearing that the math had not predicted.

Cian's hand adjusted on the hilt. The shift was millimetric — the difference between holding a compass and holding a weapon — and his body performed it without consulting his mind, the way it had performed it for twenty-six centuries whenever the acoustic environment produced data that the cognitive fossil classified as *unresolved*.

The Southern Cross rode the horizon. The consecrated hull hummed. The bearing held.

Something on it did not.

The sword pointed. They followed.
