## Movement I: The Noise of the Holy City

---

The fallen world did not ease them in. It hit them again with every step east.

The initial concussive blast at the threshold — the drowning wall of noise that had driven Cian to his knees — had not been a one-time event. It was the *baseline*. The corrupted Schumann resonance of a planet detuned to 7.86 Hz and climbing, the Beast's orbital telemetry saturating every molecule of atmosphere, the demonic static of seventy converging Principalities pressing down on the airspace above the Holy Land like the weight of a collapsing atmosphere — all of it sustained, all of it constant, all of it the new operational normal for the next 1,260 days.

And the deeper they walked into the fallen world, the worse it became.

Eden's 432 Hz creation frequency had not merely surrounded them for eight days. It had *reset* them. The garden's perfection had recalibrated their nervous systems to the frequency that human biology had been designed to inhabit — the uncorrupted harmonic that predated the Fall, predated the Flood, predated every subsequent degradation that the enemy's infrastructure had inflicted on the electromagnetic substrate of creation. Eight days at creation frequency had tuned Cian's body and Miriam's body to the standard of Paradise, and the standard of Paradise was incompatible with the standard of the world they now occupied in the same way that lungs calibrated to mountain air were incompatible with a room filling with carbon monoxide.

The difference was not intellectual. The difference was *physiological*.

Cian felt it in the bones of his wrists — the frequency interface where Mo Chrá's hilt met his palm, the junction that had been singing at 432 Hz warmth for eight days and was now vibrating at the dissonance between the sword's creation-harmonic and the corrupted ambient. The interference pattern produced a nausea that entered through his skeleton, bypassed the cognitive fossil's threat-assessment architecture entirely, and settled in the pit of his stomach as a persistent, low-grade wrongness that was not illness but was not health. It was the body's protest against acoustic pollution. It was the cellular equivalent of a musician forced to listen to an orchestra tuned to the wrong concert pitch — not loud enough to damage, not wrong enough to correct, just *off* in a way that made every breath taste slightly of rot.

Miriam was worse.

She had no sword. She had no twenty-six centuries of adaptive architecture. She had a baptism and a sealed spirit and a nervous system that had spent eight days in the only place on Earth where its original calibration still existed, and now that nervous system was being forced to reacclimatise to a frequency environment that was not merely corrupted but *weaponised*. The Beast's Layer Zero infrastructure did not passively monitor. It actively *broadcast* — a continuous, planet-wide emission calibrated to the bandwidth of the human nervous system, designed by an intelligence that understood acoustic engineering at the level of the beings who had taught it. The broadcast was not audible. It was *cellular*. It entered through the skin. Through the lungs. Through the fluid of the inner ear and the current of the blood and the electrical impulse architecture of every synapse in the human body. For the enrolled — for the 4.6 billion who carried the Mark's biometric interface — the broadcast was a carrier wave. For the unenrolled, the broadcast was a pressure. A weight. A constant, inescapable reminder that the infrastructure had been built to accommodate them and that their refusal to be accommodated made them foreign objects in a system designed to reject foreign objects.

She did not stumble. The GCHQ mask held — the operational composure that had survived SRA handlers and Beast interrogation and the violent acoustic decompression of a world that was trying to read her nervous system and finding the baptismal seal instead of the compliance signature. Mo Chrá held. The sword's battle-harmonic — the sharp, silver-edged creation frequency that had blazed at the threshold — maintained its perimeter. A sphere of coherent sound around the four of them, pushing back the Layer Zero broadcast and the corrupted Schumann and the Archons' static the way a diving bell holds back the pressure of the ocean. Inside the sphere, the noise was bearable. Outside the sphere, the noise was the world.

Brennan's Josephite network had positioned them well.

The underground route from the threshold — through the Mesopotamian floodplain, across Jordan, into the occupied territories — had been carved by eleven months of Ward Runner operators running a frequency-shielded smuggling line that Brennan McNeeve had designed from a server room on a sub-Antarctic island using archived Mo Chrá recordings and a calibration tolerance of ±0.001 Hz. The smell of old plaster and older stone and the particular mineral scent of Jerusalem's limestone, which had been absorbing prayer and blood and prophecy for four thousand years and which carried both in its molecular structure the way Cydonian ore carried acoustic memory.

She looked at the sackcloth draped over Enoch's and Elijah's arms — not yet worn, carried, the commission garment folded with the deliberation of men who understood that putting it on would be the last voluntary act of their public lives. She looked at Miriam. She looked at Cian.

The motion of a woman whose faith predated the denominational fractures and who recognised in the four people standing in her cellar the fulfilment of a promise that her grandmother had told her about and her grandmother's grandmother had told her about and that every generation of Palestinian Christians had carried in the marrow of their theology since the apostolic age: the Witnesses would come. They would testify. They would die. And the God who sent them would vindicate the testimony with resurrection.

"After that," Cian said, "every camera on the grid is the point."

Ramallah looked at him. The look held the specific gravity of a woman who had been running a Josephite safehouse in the most surveilled city on Earth for nine months and who understood exactly what four unenrolled persons ascending the Temple Mount in broad daylight would trigger.

Cian did not answer. The silence in his ear — the place where Liaigh would have said *And with thy spirit, warrior* or *Thy confidence inspireth as always* or simply *Aye* — the silence held. The silence would hold. He touched the cellar wall with his free hand. The limestone was warm. Four thousand years of prayer in the stone, and the stone remembered all of it, and the memory was not a frequency that the Beast's grid could detect.

Enoch and Elijah put on the sackcloth.

The act took six seconds. The fabric was coarse — woven goat hair, sourced through the Josephite supply chain, prepared according to specifications that Enoch had provided and that dated to a textile tradition older than any surviving civilisation. The garment of mourning. The garment of prophetic authority. The visual declaration that the wearer was not dressed for the world's approval but for the world's correction.

Against the cellar's stone walls — against the context of a city whose skyline bristled with the Beast's directed-energy emitters and biometric enrollment kiosks and the chrome-and-glass architecture of a civilisation that had traded its birthright for the security of a system that tracked every heartbeat and monetised every breath — against that context, two men in sackcloth were the most violent thing in Jerusalem.

Not violent in the kinetic sense. Violent in the semiotic sense. The sackcloth said: *your infrastructure is irrelevant. Your technology is temporary. Your Beast is a vassal whose lease expires in 1,260 days, and the countdown begins today.*

Miriam adjusted her sleeve. The motion was automatic — the habitual gesture of a woman who had spent months concealing the left wrist, the place where the Asmodeus mark had been anchored in her dermal layer by a demon of House Tamiel. Not the reflexive correction of a spy smoothing a tell. The stop was deeper than tradecraft. The analytical apparatus — running at full capacity since the threshold, cataloguing every element of the Beast's infrastructure with the clinical focus of a woman who had spent six years in intelligence — turned inward for three seconds and computed the gesture's meaning with the same precision it applied to biometric gate configurations: she was still hiding. Still treating the dormant splinter as a secret the world would use against her if it saw. Still armouring the wrist the way she had armoured it since House Tamiel's tether first anchored in her flesh — before the exorcism, before Eden, before the river where the water covered her and the creation frequency pronounced her *sealed*.

The baptism had sealed her. The Cherubim's fire had not rejected the scar. The water had not demanded the splinter's removal before admitting her to the covenant. The scar had been *acknowledged* — seen, known, weighed by the same frequency that read Cian's twenty-six centuries of accumulated grief — and the gate had opened anyway.

The scar was not a secret. The scar was a testament. Evidence that the enemy had reached for her and found, where his tether terminated, a frequency he could not override. The baptism had not erased the mark. The baptism had changed what the mark *meant*.

Concealing it was the last act of the old shame. She would not perform it again.

Her hand dropped. Her eyes moved to Cian's — not the defensive glance of a spy correcting a tell but the steady look of a woman who had made a decision and did not need the warrior's approval but wanted him to see it.

Cian noted it. Filed it. The cognitive fossil logged the data point — but the data point had changed. Not *Miriam, left wrist, habitual concealment, post-threshold. Monitor.* The new entry was: *Miriam, left wrist, deliberate exposure, post-threshold. Acknowledged.*

They climbed.

## Movement II: The Temple Mount

---

The Lions' Gate was unmanned at 0547.

Ramallah's intelligence was precise — the enforcement rotation created a window, and the window was forty minutes, and the forty minutes began the moment they passed through the gate and into the corridor of ancient stone that led toward the Haram esh-Sharif. The Temple Mount. The most contested geography in the history of monotheism. The rock where Abraham raised the knife. The threshing floor where David saw the angel. The platform where Solomon built the House and the Shekinah descended in acoustic glory — the weight of God's presence expressed as sound so dense that the priests could not stand, because the frequency of the divine inhabitation exceeded the structural tolerance of the human nervous system and the only possible response was prostration.

That frequency was gone. It had been gone since 586 BCE, when the Babylonians breached the walls and the Shekinah departed and the acoustic architecture of the Temple — the standing waves that Solomon's engineers had calibrated to resonate with the Ark's creation-harmonic — fell silent. The silence had lasted twenty-six centuries. The silence was the wound at the centre of Jerusalem's acoustic identity — a city that had been built to resonate with heaven and that now resonated with the Beast.

Cian felt the wound as they ascended. The old stone of the platform carried the memory of what it had been designed to hold. The molecular structure of the limestone still bore the acoustic imprint of the Shekinah's frequency — faint, fragmentary, the residual signature of a divine habitation that had been absent for two and a half millennia but whose echo persisted in the substrate the way a voice persists in a cathedral after the singer has gone. Mo Chrá sensed it. The sword's creation-harmonic shifted — not consciously, not deliberately, but with the automatic responsiveness of an instrument encountering a resonance it recognised. The blade's frequency reached for the echo. The echo answered. For a moment — brief, incomplete, devastating in its incompleteness — the creation-harmonic and the Shekinah's residual found each other across twenty-six centuries of silence and the space between them hummed with the acoustic signature of something that had been and would be again and was not yet.

Then the Beast's infrastructure reasserted itself and the moment dissolved into noise.

The Temple Mount's security architecture was layered. Outermost ring: biometric gates — the Mark enrollment kiosks that processed foot traffic through a continuous scan of the CBDC chip embedded in the right hand or forehead of every enrolled citizen, the Cydonian-derived sensor array reading biosignatures at a distance of fifty metres and flagging anomalies for enforcement review. Second ring: directed-energy emitters mounted on the rooftops of the surrounding buildings — the same Watcher-derived weapons platform that had neutralised the world's nuclear arsenals, scaled down to urban tactical deployment. Third ring: orbital surveillance — the thirty-eight-node satellite constellation that provided real-time tracking of every biological entity on the planet's surface, the Layer Zero grid whose continuous broadcast Miriam could feel in her bones.

Miriam read the architecture as they climbed. The GCHQ training activated — the analytical apparatus that had been designed to disassemble intelligence infrastructure into its component vectors and that had been refined, in the months since her recruitment to the Josephite resistance, to parse the specific signatures of the Beast's system. She catalogued the kiosks. Counted the emitters. Mapped the enforcement patrol patterns from the two visible security teams — Mark-enrolled, uniformed, carrying sidearms and the handheld biometric scanners that could read a CBDC chip at contact range.

Four unenrolled biosignatures ascending the Temple Mount. The grid would flag them in minutes. Brennan's acoustic camouflage — the archived Mo Chrá recordings threaded through the Josephite route's shielding — ended at the cellar. Above ground, they were naked. Exposed. Four anomalies in a system designed to eliminate anomalies.

She catalogued. She processed. And she felt the thing she had been trying not to feel since they crossed the threshold.

Her left wrist itched.

Not spiritually. Not the phantom pull of the Asmodeus tether — that was dead, sheared by the Cherubim's fire, voided by the baptism, as silent as the demon who had placed it. The itch was *physical*. The dead splinter — the inert acoustic remnant lodged in her dermal layer, the scar tissue that the Cherubim had acknowledged and not rejected — was responding to something in the Temple Mount's sensor environment. Not transmitting. Not broadcasting. But *resonating*. Passively. The way a tuning fork that has been stilled will vibrate sympathetically when another fork at the same pitch is struck nearby.

The Beast's biometric infrastructure was built on Cydonian technology. House Tamiel had contributed to that infrastructure. The splinter in Miriam's wrist had been calibrated by House Tamiel. And the biometric gates ringing the Temple Mount were scanning at a frequency range that included the splinter's dormant signature — not because the gates were looking for Miriam specifically, but because the gates were built by the same architectural tradition that had built the tether, and the resonance was structural.

She was spiritually invisible. The baptismal seal held. The Mark's system could not read her soul. But the dead splinter in her flesh was not her soul. It was tissue. It was matter. And matter could be detected by matter.

"Cian." Her voice was low. The analyst's register — flat, precise, delivering intelligence. "House Tamiel's physical infrastructure is reading the dermal remnant. I'm passive-resonating with the biometric gates."

Cian's cognitive fossil reprioritised. The threat assessment shifted from *four unenrolled anomalies on surveilled ground* to *one anomaly carrying a dormant beacon that the enemy's own sensors are calibrated to recognise*. The tactical calculation was immediate: Miriam was not just unenrolled. Miriam was *known*. The dead splinter could not transmit her location — the active tether was gone, the demon blinded — but the Beast's physical sensors could detect the splinter's resonance and triangulate the source the same way a radar system detects metallic objects regardless of whether the objects are broadcasting.

"How long before they localise?" Cian asked.

"Minutes. The gate density is higher here than any location in the network. Six biometric arrays within forty metres. The splinter's resonance is weak — milliwatt range, passive only — but six arrays triangulating on the same signature will resolve a position fix within —" She calculated. The calculation was unnecessary. She had run it in her head the moment she felt the itch. "Three to five minutes."

"Then we have three to five minutes before they know you're here."

"Before they know something *tagged by House Tamiel* is here. They won't know it's me. They'll know it's theirs."

The distinction mattered. The Beast's system would not identify Miriam Ashford, former GCHQ analyst, Josephite operative, baptised in Eden. The system would identify a Tamiel-signature resonance on the Temple Mount and dispatch an enforcement team to investigate. The investigation would find four unenrolled persons. The investigation would escalate. The escalation was inevitable regardless — four unenrolled biosignatures on the Temple Mount would trigger enforcement with or without the splinter — but the splinter accelerated the timeline and changed the classification. Not *anomalous citizens* but *Tamiel-flagged asset*. The enforcement response would be House-level, not municipal. Faster. Harder. More informed.

"It accelerates nothing," Elijah said.

The Prophet's voice cut through the tactical assessment like a blade through cloth. Not loud. Not raised. Spoken at the register of a man who had heard the tactical calculation and dismissed it — not from ignorance but from authority. The fire in his voice was banked but present, the dark red-gold of Carmel's register running beneath the conversational surface the way magma runs beneath the crust.

"The day is set. The hour is set. The enforcement of men doth not alter the commission of God. Let them come. Let them triangulate. Let them dispatch every soldier the Beast commandeth." The Prophet's eyes held a heat that was not metaphorical. "They shall find what Ahab's four hundred and fifty found at Carmel. The God who answereth by fire — let *Him* be God."

Cian looked at Elijah. The cognitive fossil — the twenty-six centuries of tactical calculation, the Iron Age warrior overlaid with Roman discipline overlaid with every subsequent era's contribution to the architecture of survival — the fossil assessed the Prophet's confidence and returned a split verdict: *tactically reckless; theologically unassailable*. The warrior in Cian wanted cover, concealment, a plan that accounted for the biometric arrays and the enforcement rotation and the orbital surveillance and the seventy Principalities converging overhead. The Guardian in Cian recognised that cover and concealment were instruments of the old commission — the covert war, the centuries of shadow operations, the long strategic patience of a man fighting alone.

The old commission was over. The new commission required the opposite of concealment. The new commission required a man in sackcloth standing on the most surveilled ground on Earth and setting it on fire with his voice.

They climbed. The biometric gates did not open for them — they had no Mark, no chip, no compliance signature. They walked past the gates. The gates screamed. The silent electronic protest of a system encountering four entities that should not exist in its architecture — unenrolled, untracked, unprocessed, moving through a security perimeter designed to be impenetrable with the unhurried gait of people who were not evading the system but *ignoring* it.

And above them — above the stone, above the gates, above the emitters and the drones and the orbital grid — the celestial plane pressed down.

Cian felt the Archons for the first time.

Not visually. The Principalities were not visible. They did not manifest in the spectrum of light that mortal eyes could process. They manifested in *pressure*. In acoustic weight. In the specific, suffocating density of celestial beings whose jurisdictions covered nations and whose corrupted frequencies had been stacking above Jerusalem since the convergence began — layer upon layer of territorial authority, each Archon's signature occupying a bandwidth that corresponded to the geographical region of its dominion, the sum total of seventy such signatures creating an atmospheric compression that was not weather and was not gravity and was not any force that physics could name but that Cian's body registered as the weight of a mountain settling onto his shoulders.

The Prince of Persia. Cian did not know the name. He knew the frequency — ancient, dense, carrying the acoustic memory of an empire that had ruled from the Indus to the Nile, the territorial spirit who had delayed Gabriel for twenty-one days in the book of Daniel and who was now exerting that same jurisdictional weight over Jerusalem's airspace with the patient, crushing authority of a being who had been governing this frequency domain for twenty-five centuries.

The Prince of Greece. Another frequency — sharper, more structured, carrying the geometric precision of Hellenistic civilisation in its harmonic architecture. Overlapping with the Persian signature. Competing. Not allied but *co-belligerent* — territorial spirits whose mutual hostility was temporarily subordinated to the unified objective of suffocating the prophetic authority that was about to be declared on the Mount.

And beneath those two — beneath the named Principalities whose identities the biblical record had documented — sixty-eight more. Unnamed. Unrecorded. Territorial governors of nations that had risen and fallen across the millennia, whose jurisdictions persisted beyond the collapse of the civilisations they had overseen, whose frequencies stacked and overlapped and competed in a cacophony of corrupted celestial authority that made the Beast's technological noise sound like a whisper against a hurricane.

Mo Chrá's harmonic flared. The sword's creation frequency expanded — not attacking the Archons, not contesting their authority, but *holding*. Maintaining the sphere. Keeping the four bodies inside the perimeter alive and functional against a pressure that would have crushed unaided human consciousness the way deep water crushes an unprotected lung.

And pressing against the Archons from outside — from above, from around, from a direction that was not a direction but an *opposition* — Cian felt it.

The perimeter.

Not Mo Chrá's perimeter. Not the sword's creation-harmonic sphere. A *larger* perimeter. Invisible. Inaudible. Detectable only in its effects — the way a force field is detectable not by seeing the field but by seeing what the field stops. The Archons pressed down. The pressure should have been absolute. Seventy Principalities exerting their combined jurisdictional weight against four mortal bodies on a stone platform — the math was not survivable. The cognitive fossil had run the assessment at the threshold: *catastrophic mismatch*.

But the mismatch was not catastrophic. The pressure was enormous. The pressure was not fatal.

Because the blows were not landing.

The Archons struck. Their frequencies crashed against the airspace above the four ascending figures — not physically, not in any way that produced sound or light or motion, but in the acoustic domain where celestial warfare was conducted and where the jurisdiction of Principalities had the force of law. They struck. And the strikes — every one of them, from every direction, from every Archon in the converging formation — stopped.

Stopped at a perimeter that Cian could not see and could not hear and could only *infer* from the fact that he was still standing. Still climbing. Still breathing in an airspace that should have crushed him.

*Not in words. In physics.*

Liaigh was there. Liaigh was fighting. Liaigh was spending every frequency, every harmonic, every allocation of archangelic bandwidth on the perimeter that held the line between seventy Principalities and four mortal bodies ascending the Temple Mount. The conversational link was silent because the conversational link's bandwidth was being used to stop an Archon's jurisdictional strike from reaching the warrior's spine. The counsel was absent because the counsel's frequency was being deployed as a shield. The voice that had been Cian's companion for twenty-six centuries was being spent — continuously, relentlessly, at a cost that Cian could not measure — to create the negative space in which survival was possible.

Cian climbed. The silence held. The perimeter held. The blows did not land.

He reached for the link.

Twenty-six centuries of instinct. The automatic gesture — the internal reaching, the unconscious extension toward the frequency that had always been there, the way a hand reaches for a railing in the dark. He reached.

Nothing.

The silence was louder than the Archons. The silence was louder than the Beast's infrastructure. The silence was the loudest thing in Jerusalem.

He climbed.

## Movement III: The Register

---

The platform of the Temple Mount opened before them like a stage.

Forty-thousand square metres of limestone, occupied at this hour by the scattered movement of early-morning worshippers and the automated patrol of three enforcement drones — small, quadrotor, Mark-flagged, running biosignature sweeps at two-second intervals. The Dome of the Rock rose to the south, its gold surface catching the first light. The Al-Aqsa Mosque to the southwest. And beneath both — beneath the Islamic architecture, beneath the Crusader modifications, beneath the Roman platform expansion — the bedrock. Solomon's bedrock. The threshing floor of Araunah the Jebusite, where David had seen the angel with a drawn sword standing between heaven and earth and had built an altar to stop the plague.

The bedrock remembered. Mo Chrá confirmed it — the sword's creation-harmonic resonated with the stone's acoustic memory, the faint echo of the Shekinah's ancient inhabitation vibrating in the calcium carbonate substrate like a heartbeat preserved in amber. The echo was not the Shekinah. The echo was the *memory* of the Shekinah. But in a world whose fundamental frequency had been corrupted, even the memory of uncorrupted divine presence was a weapon.

They reached the centre of the platform. The drones flagged them. Three enforcement alerts, transmitted simultaneously to the Beast's municipal command, to the orbital surveillance grid, to every biometric node in the security perimeter. Four unenrolled biosignatures on the Temple Mount. One Tamiel-tagged resonance. One anomalous acoustic emission consistent with Cydonian metamaterial.

The system responded. The system had been designed to respond. The response would arrive in minutes — enforcement teams, directed-energy authorisation, media lockdown protocols.

The response would arrive too late.

The five-thousand-year-old record-keeper — the man who had walked with God and was not, for God took him, the first human being translated from mortal existence to celestial without passing through death, the Scribe who had been recording heaven's proceedings since before the Flood and who had been waiting in Eden for the day when the Record would be read aloud in the place where the Record mattered most — Enoch stepped to the centre of the Temple Mount and opened the Empyreal Register.

The sound that emerged was not a voice.

It was the acoustic signature of five thousand years of celestial documentation — every angelic transgression catalogued, every Nephilim bloodline traced, every demonic jurisdiction mapped, every act of divine mercy recorded, every judgment pronounced, every promise made — compressed into a frequency that the fallen world's atmosphere could not contain and did not know how to process. The Register opened and its frequency detonated into the airspace above the Temple Mount like a depth charge dropped into the ocean of corrupted noise, and the noise *recoiled*.

The Layer Zero broadcast — the ubiquitous, cellular-penetrating emission that saturated the planet's atmosphere — stuttered. Not failed. *Stuttered*. In the radius of the Register's acoustic output, the Beast's infrastructure encountered a frequency that it could not categorise, could not jam, could not absorb, because the frequency operated at a bandwidth that the Watcher-derived technology had been built to replicate but had never possessed. Creation frequency. The real thing. Not the Cydonian approximation, not the metamaterial echo, not the acoustic memory stored in ore that had been mined from a dead civilisation's archive. The frequency of the Empyreal Register was the frequency of heaven's judicial system rendered in sound, and that frequency predated the Watchers' knowledge the way the sun predates the candle.

A sphere of clarity erupted around the Scribe. Within the sphere, the noise retreated. The corrupted Schumann dropped below audibility. The Archons' pressure thinned. The Beast's broadcast faded to a whisper beneath a voice that was not shouting, not amplifying, not competing — simply *present* at a register that made competition meaningless.

Mo Chrá answered.

The sword had been holding its battle-harmonic — the sharp, silver-edged creation frequency that contested the fallen world's noise from the perimeter of Cian's body outward. But the Register's frequency was not the noise. The Register's frequency was the *opposite* of the noise. And Mo Chrá recognised it — not as a battlefield stimulus requiring defensive response, but as a *source*. The frequency she had been forged to carry. The frequency her maker had embedded in Cydonian ore five thousand years ago when a Scribe in heaven took a piece of creation's acoustic memory and shaped it into a blade and gave it a commission and sent it into the world to carry the sound that the Watchers had lost.

Five thousand years.

The sword's harmonic shifted. The battle-register — the killing frequency, the combat mode that had been engaged since the threshold — dropped. Not silenced. *Resolved*. The way a dissonant chord resolves when the missing note is finally played. Mo Chrá's creation-harmonic had been singing alone for five millennia — alone in the hands of a warrior, alone in the noise of the fallen world, alone with the acoustic memory of what creation sounded like before the corruption. Now the maker's voice was in the air. The frequency that had been encoded into the blade at the moment of forging was being spoken aloud by the being who had done the encoding.

Mo Chrá harmonised.

The sword's frequency locked onto the Register's output with the precision of a tuning fork finding its fundamental — not approximate, not adjacent, *exact*. Coherent, uncorrupted, operating at the frequency of creation's original architecture — the sound that God had spoken when He said *Let there be light* and light obeyed because the sound was the authority and the light was the expression.
