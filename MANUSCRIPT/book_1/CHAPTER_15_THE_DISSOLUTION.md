# CHAPTER 15: THE DISSOLUTION

---

## I. THE DARK SCREENS

The screens had been dark for six days.

Brennan McNeeve sat in front of them anyway — the same way he'd sat in front of them every morning since he'd built this underground facility three years ago, coffee going cold at his elbow, notepad open to a page that would have been covered in equations by now if equations still mattered.

Five screens. Five Titans. Five billion dollars of engineering genius, twelve years of his life, the most ambitious private Mars mission in human history — all of it reduced to raw signal noise on instruments that would never receive another byte of data.

He wasn't mourning the rovers.

He wasn't even mourning the Cydonia complex — five thousand years of Watcher architecture unmade in seconds, the most significant archaeological discovery in human history returned to pre-differentiated substrate, a word he'd invented six days ago and already hated.

He was mourning the part of himself that had believed engineering could solve anything.

The part that had sat in a converted garage in Westlake Hills at twenty-three years old, hand-soldering circuit boards at two in the morning, telling Sarah — girlfriend then, not yet wife — that he was going to put a rover on Mars. Not NASA. Not SpaceX. *Him*. A kid from Austin with a master's in aerospace engineering, a talent for building things that shouldn't work, and the particular arrogance of a man who didn't know the word *impossible* applied to him.

That kid was dead now.

In his place sat a man who had heard fallen angels speak their own names across five thousand years of stone, felt reality shudder under the weight of two hundred cosmic oaths, and watched his five most brilliant machines get erased from existence by forces that didn't care about engineering.

*You need the same material. You need Cydonian ore.*

Cian's voice, six days ago. The moment everything shifted. Not the anti-singularity — that was horror, pure and cosmic and survivable. The shift had come earlier, in the quiet conversation about metamaterials and resonance matrices, when Cian had looked at Brennan's data and seen what eighteen months of the best engineering on Earth had missed.

Because the best engineering on Earth was the wrong *kind* of engineering.

The Acoustic Paradigm. Sound, not light. Vibration, not vision. A technology base built by beings who had witnessed God speak creation into existence and then spent a century replicating what they'd heard.

Human science couldn't touch it. *His* science couldn't touch it.

Mo Chrá could. A sword forged from pre-fall metamaterial by Enoch the Scribe could do in thirty seconds what Brennan's entire facility couldn't do in eighteen months.

That should have broken him. Six months ago, it would have.

But six months ago, he hadn't read Niamh's letter.

---

The brass cylinder sat in the top drawer of his desk — tarnished, ancient, inscribed with symbols that predated every alphabet Brennan had ever studied. He opened the drawer three or four times a day now, not to read the letter — he'd memorised every word — but to touch the cylinder itself. To feel the weight of it. The reality of two thousand years of waiting for someone to break the seal.

*If you are reading this, my brother has found you. Trust him. He has carried our burden alone for too long.*

Niamh mac Morna. His ancestor. Brennan had done the genealogy — traced the McNeeve line back through Irish parish records, through Norman conquest documents, through fragmentary Gaelic manuscripts that barely survived the Reformation. The name shifted across centuries: mac Niamh became MacNeave became McNeeve, the patronymic fossilising into a surname that nobody pronounced correctly and everyone assumed was Scottish.

It wasn't Scottish. It was a battle cry disguised as a family name.

*You are Machirite. You are mac Morna. You are a giant slayer.*

*Now rise, and hunt.*

He'd read those words the night the shedim came for Emma. Read them by lamplight in Cian's safehouse while his daughter slept in the next room, his son curled against Sarah's chest, the ancient sword humming a lullaby that his children heard and his wife didn't question because Sarah McNeeve was the kind of woman who decided demons were just another problem to solve and lullabies from supernatural weapons were simply part of the new operating parameters.

The letter had changed everything.

Not the revelation that his "Uncle Sean" was a twenty-six-century-old Celtic warrior. Not the proof that fallen angels existed, or that their offspring still walked the earth, or that the things that had attacked his family were the spiritual remnants of drowned giants from before the Flood.

The letter. Niamh's voice, reaching across two and a half thousand years, speaking directly to him:

*Help him. Fight with him. Do not let him carry this alone.*

Brennan closed the drawer. Picked up his cold coffee. Stared at the dark screens.

*Alright, Niamh. Let's talk about what "help" looks like when your life's work just got unmade by the voice of God's former employees.*

## II. THE FAMILY

McNeeve Space Systems employed eleven people.

Not the kind of eleven that a corporate HR department would recognise — no org chart, no middle management, no departmental silos. Brennan had built his company the way he built his rovers: lean, integrated, every component doing exactly what it needed to do and nothing else. He'd hired six of the eleven from his graduate programme at UT Austin. Two more from NASA's Jet Propulsion Lab, poached during a budget crisis that had left the agency haemorrhaging talent. The remaining three he'd found the way Brennan found everything — by recognising brilliance that other people had overlooked.

They were, by any objective measure, among the most talented aerospace engineers on the planet. They were also his friends. Some of them were closer than friends.

He knew their children's names. He'd been to their weddings. He'd sat in hospital waiting rooms with three of them — twice for births, once for a cancer scare that turned out to be nothing, thank God. When Marcus's divorce had gone nuclear, Brennan had driven to his apartment at midnight with a bottle of Jameson and hadn't left until Marcus stopped shaking.

These were not employees. They were family who happened to share a mission.

And six days ago, he'd exposed them to something that could have killed them all.

---

He called the meeting for 0800 on a Tuesday — seven days after the anti-singularity event. The underground facility was still showing damage: hairline fractures in the concrete, scorch marks on three consoles, the observation gallery's acoustic glass replaced with plywood sheeting that Marcus had installed with submarine-grade efficiency and zero aesthetic consideration.

They gathered in the main lab — the same space where Brennan had characterised the Cydonian ore, where the replica had detonated, where the oaths had nearly unmade reality. Someone had cleaned up the composite shrapnel. Nobody had cleaned the fear off the walls.

Brennan stood at the head of the table and looked at his people.

**Dr. Elena Vasquez.** Flight dynamics. Orbital mechanics. The woman who had calculated Titan-1's Mars transfer orbit by hand on a napkin during a late-night pizza session and been right to seven decimal places. Forty-one years old, Salvadoran-American, two doctorates from MIT, a laugh that could be heard through bulkheads. She'd designed the trajectory that put five rovers on Mars. She was also the one who, during the anti-singularity, had thrown herself over the youngest member of the team and shielded him with her body.

She sat now with her hands folded in her lap, her usual energy compressed into something watchful.

**Tomás Navarro.** Software architecture. The AI systems that ran the Titan rovers — the autonomous navigation, the adaptive decision trees, the machine learning subroutines that let a robot on Mars make independent choices seventeen light-minutes from human oversight — all Tomás. Twenty-nine years old, the youngest on the team, a Mexican-American kid from El Paso who'd been writing operating systems since he was fourteen and had turned down Google three times because, in his words, "Google builds ad platforms. Brennan builds things that *matter*." He'd been the one Elena shielded. He hadn't spoken in two days.

**Dr. Jian-Wei "JW" Chen.** Materials and structures. If Brennan was the architect of the Titan programme, JW was the man who decided what the architecture was built *from*. Every alloy selection, every thermal protection system, every structural calculation that kept a rover intact through Mars atmospheric entry — JW's work. Fifty-three, Taiwanese-American, seventeen patents, preternaturally calm. During the anti-singularity, the only person in the gallery besides Marcus who hadn't screamed.

**Priya Chandrasekaran.** Communications and deep space network liaison. The woman who had built McNeeve's comm relay architecture — the system that kept five rovers in constant contact with Earth across forty million miles of void. Thirty-six, Indian-American, a former ISRO engineer who'd crossed an ocean for the chance to work on a Mars mission that wasn't mired in government bureaucracy. When the telemetry feeds died, Priya had spent four straight hours trying to re-establish contact with the Titans, cycling through every frequency, every protocol, every backup channel — systematically, methodically, refusing to accept silence as an answer until the physics left her no choice.

**Hal Okonkwo.** Robotics and mechanical systems. The man who had designed the Titan rovers' physical bodies — the articulated limbs, the drill assemblies, the sample collection mechanisms, the acoustic emitter array that Marcus had specified and Hal had turned into hardware. Forty-four, Nigerian-American, built like a rugby prop, hands that could thread a needle or strip a transmission. He'd been the one to discover, during the post-event assessment, that every fastener in the facility had loosened by exactly three-quarters of a turn. Armaros's doing. Hal had spent two days re-torquing every bolt in the building, quietly and without complaint, because when Hal encountered a problem he fixed it.

**Dr. Anya Petrov.** Data systems and cybersecurity. The ghost in the machine — the woman who made sure McNeeve's most sensitive data stayed where it was supposed to stay and didn't end up where it wasn't. Thirty-eight, Russian-American, recruited from the NSA during a security conference where she'd casually demonstrated a zero-day exploit in their own presentation software. Anya handled the air-gapped systems, the encrypted backups, the offshore data mirrors that Brennan maintained against the possibility of exactly the kind of catastrophe that had just occurred. She'd been the first to recover after the anti-singularity — not because she wasn't afraid, but because her first instinct in any crisis was to secure the data.

**Marcus Reeves.** Acoustic specialist. Navy veteran. Best friend. Already committed. Already in.

Marcus stood at the back of the room with his arms crossed and his face unreadable. He'd been the one to suggest this meeting — had pulled Brennan aside the previous evening and said, with the blunt practicality that made him invaluable, "You can't leave them in limbo. They saw what happened. They deserve to know what comes next."

---

Brennan looked at them. Six faces — seven including Marcus — all watching him with the particular attention of people who had trusted him with their careers and were now waiting to find out if that trust extended to the end of the world.

"I'm going to tell you three things," he said. "The first is the truth. The second is a lie. And the third is a choice."

Elena's eyebrows rose. Tomás looked up for the first time in two days.

"The truth: what you experienced six days ago was real. Not a gas leak. Not an equipment malfunction. Not a shared psychotic episode, which is what a very expensive therapist would tell you if any of you were foolish enough to describe it to one." He paused. "You heard voices that weren't human. You felt reality destabilise. You saw my instruments record acoustic data that violates every physical law I've ever published a paper on. Some of you bled. Some of you lost consciousness. All of you survived something that should have been unsurvivable."

Silence.

"I am not going to explain what it was. Not because I don't trust you — I trust everyone in this room with my life, and I have the professional history to prove it — but because the explanation involves information that makes you a target. What I will tell you is this: the entities whose voices you heard are real, they are ancient, they are hostile, and they are part of a conflict that has been going on since before recorded history. The Mars mission — *our* Mars mission — stumbled into evidence of that conflict. And the people — the *things* — on the other side of it have resources, reach, and patience that make Lockheed Martin look like a lemonade stand."

JW spoke first. Of course he did — JW always spoke first when the room needed someone to ask the obvious question calmly.

"The sword."

Brennan nodded. "The sword."

"It shielded us. During the... event. The man carrying it — your uncle — he did something with the sword and it created a... I don't have a word for it. A zone of stability. Inside that zone, we survived. Outside—"

"Outside, you wouldn't have." Brennan kept his voice flat. Clinical. The voice he used when presenting failure-mode analyses to review boards. "The containment was provided by the sword and by another... asset... that I cannot identify. Without both, the propagation would have been uncontained. Austin would be a crater. Not a blast crater — a *nothing* crater. Pre-differentiated substrate. Matter that has been returned to a state prior to its organisation into atoms."

"That's not possible," Priya said.

"No," Brennan agreed. "It's not. And yet your ears bled and my rovers don't exist anymore."

The second silence was longer than the first.

"The lie," Brennan continued. "McNeeve Space Systems is going to file for Chapter 7 bankruptcy. The official story: catastrophic server failure destroyed our Mars telemetry archive. Equipment malfunction. Financial overextension. Poor management — specifically, *my* poor management. The narrative will be that Brennan McNeeve reached too far, built too fast, and when the technology failed, the company couldn't survive the loss."

"That's—" Elena started.

"A lie. Yes. A carefully constructed, legally defensible, forensically consistent lie that my wife is currently engineering with a precision that would make Lockheed's legal department weep." A ghost of a smile. "Sarah is very good at her job."

"Why?" Hal's voice was steady. Practical. The voice of a man who fixed things and needed to understand what was broken.

"Because every government agency with a three-letter acronym is going to want to know why a private Mars mission lost five rovers simultaneously. Because Lockheed Martin has a contractual right to mission data that, if they ever accessed it, would expose them to information that would either get them killed or make them complicit in something they cannot comprehend. Because NASA's Deep Space Network logged anomalous readings during the event that their physicists are already trying to explain. And because the organisations that *can* comprehend what happened — the ones my uncle has been fighting for twenty-six centuries — are watching. They are always watching."

"You said three things," Tomás said quietly. It was the first sentence he'd spoken in forty-eight hours. "Truth. Lie. Choice."

Brennan reached into his back pocket and pulled out a stack of envelopes. Seven of them. One for each person in the room besides himself.

"The choice." He set the envelopes on the table. "Inside each of these is a separation package. Eighteen months' salary, fully vested stock options — which will be worth something for about another six weeks before the bankruptcy filing zeroes them out, so exercise fast — continued health insurance for two years, and a letter of recommendation from me that will get you hired anywhere on the planet. MIT. JPL. SpaceX. ESA. Pick your dream job. I'll make the call personally."

He looked at each of them in turn.

"That's Door A. Walk away. Take the package. Forget what you heard. Build brilliant careers at organisations that will never ask you to listen to fallen angels. Live long, safe, productive lives. I will not think less of you. I will not blame you. I will write you the most glowing recommendation letter in the history of aerospace engineering, and I will mean every word of it."

He let that settle.

"Door B." His voice changed — dropped into something lower, harder. The voice that Sarah said sounded like his grandfather, and his great-grandfather, and every McNeeve man she'd ever met. A voice from a bloodline that had been fighting things larger than itself since before David picked up a stone.

"Door B is this: my uncle is assembling a team. Not a company — a *team*. The kind of team that operates outside governmental oversight, outside corporate structure, outside every system of accountability that normal life depends on. The mission is classified beyond any classification level that exists. The enemy is real, supernatural, and has been operating for five thousand years. The work will be dangerous, illegal by every statute I can think of, and absolutely essential."

He paused.

"He needs flight dynamics. He needs software architecture and autonomous systems. He needs materials science and structural engineering. He needs communications infrastructure that can operate independent of every government-controlled network on Earth. He needs robotics. He needs cybersecurity that can withstand scrutiny from entities that have been infiltrating human institutions since Sumer. And he needs acoustic engineering."

Marcus, from the back of the room, raised his hand. "I'm already in. For the record."

"Noted." Brennan almost smiled. "What I'm telling you is that your skill sets — the exact skills you developed building the Titan programme — are precisely what this fight requires. Not soldiers. Not spies. *Engineers*. People who build things that work in impossible conditions. People who solve problems that shouldn't have solutions. People who take a garage and twelve years and turn it into a Mars mission."

"You're asking us to join a war," JW said. Not a question.

"I'm asking you to build the infrastructure for one. There's a difference. Cian — my uncle — has been fighting the war for twenty-six centuries. He doesn't need more fighters. He needs support. He needs minds, and hands, and the kind of the stubborn creative genius that made McNeeve Space Systems the most improbable success story in aerospace history."

"And the pay?" Elena asked, with the pragmatism of a woman who had two kids and a mortgage.

"Better than McNeeve. Significantly better. My uncle's operational budget is... substantial."

"How substantial?"

"Twenty-six centuries of compound interest substantial."

Elena blinked.

"Take the envelopes," Brennan said. "Go home. Talk to your families, if you have them. Think about what you saw six days ago. Think about whether you can go back to building weather satellites and Mars rovers for NASA after hearing the voice of a being that helped create the universe."

He stepped back from the table.

"I need answers by Friday. No pressure, no judgment, no consequences either way. But by Friday, because on Saturday the bankruptcy filing goes public and the lie becomes the official story. Once that happens, the window closes."

## III. THE LAWYER

Sarah McNeeve had been awake for fifty-three hours.

This was not, by her standards, unusual. She'd pulled longer stretches during the Morrison-Hargrove merger, the one that had made her reputation — seventy-one hours of continuous negotiation, powered by espresso and the absolute certainty that the other side's counsel was hiding a subsidiary that would blow the deal apart if she could find it. She'd found it at hour sixty-eight. The opposing firm had never forgiven her.

But the Morrison-Hargrove merger hadn't involved fallen angels.

Sarah sat in the study of the Austin safehouse — one of Cian's properties, a nondescript ranch house in a quiet neighbourhood that was, beneath its suburban facade, wired with security systems that made the Pentagon look casual. Around her: three laptops, a legal pad covered in her meticulous handwriting, two empty coffee cups, and a stack of corporate filings that represented the dismantling of her husband's life's work.

She was, by training and temperament, a problem-solver. Corporate law was problem-solving with commas. You identified the threat, mapped the exposure surface, constructed a response that was legally airtight and strategically sound, and then you executed with the precision of a surgeon who happened to bill by the hour.

The current problem had a few more variables than usual.

**Variable one:** Lockheed Martin's partnership agreement gave them access rights to all McNeeve Space Systems mission data. This was standard aerospace contracting — the kind of clause that nobody questioned because nobody expected their mission data to include the recorded voices of fallen celestial beings speaking in a pre-human language that destabilised reality.

**Variable two:** NASA's Deep Space Network had logged the anomalous signal. Not the oaths themselves — the DSN wasn't configured to capture acoustic data from the creation harmonic's frequency range. But the *effect* of the signal — electromagnetic interference, gravitational fluctuations, a three-second disruption to every satellite in geosynchronous orbit above the Western Hemisphere — had been recorded by every monitoring station on Earth. Thirty-seven government agencies had already filed inquiries.

**Variable three:** Lockheed's board liaison, a man named David Halstrom, had been making "courtesy calls" to McNeeve's Austin facility twice a week for the past month. Halstrom was not discourteous, not aggressive, not overtly threatening. He was *patient* — the corporate variety of patient that Sarah recognised as the opening phase of a hostile data acquisition. He wanted the Mars telemetry. Not some of it. All of it.

And behind Halstrom, Sarah suspected, were people who wanted it for reasons that had nothing to do with aerospace engineering.

Cian had confirmed her suspicion three days ago. "The Synagogue has assets embedded in every major defence contractor. Lockheed, Raytheon, BAE, Thales — all of them. Not at the operational level. At the *board* level. The people making strategic decisions about what technology gets developed and who gets access to it."

Sarah, who had spent fifteen years navigating corporate power structures, had not been surprised. She'd been *validated*. She'd always sensed something beneath the surface of the aerospace industry — not a conspiracy exactly, but a *pattern*. Funding priorities that didn't match market logic. Research programmes that appeared and disappeared without explanation. Board appointments that seemed to serve an agenda nobody would articulate.

Now she knew what the pattern was. And she was about to drive a truck through it.

---

The cover story had taken her forty hours to construct. Not because the lie was complicated — lies were easy, any first-year associate could fabricate a corporate collapse. The difficulty was making the lie *boring*.

Boring was essential. Boring was the difference between a story that triggered investigation and a story that triggered a shrug. The aerospace industry was littered with bankruptcies — ambitious startups that burned through capital, over-promised on deliverables, and collapsed when the technology didn't materialise. McNeeve Space Systems needed to look like one more cautionary tale. The kind of failure that industry analysts would cite in articles about the "harsh realities of private space exploration" and then forget by the next quarterly earnings cycle.

Sarah's construction:

**The Narrative:** McNeeve Space Systems had overextended on the Mars mission. The five-rover deployment had consumed 340% of the original budget. Brennan's perfectionistic engineering standards had driven costs to unsustainable levels. The company had taken on debt — secured against future contracts that the rover failures made undeliverable. When the server infrastructure failed and the telemetry was lost, the company's sole asset — its data — ceased to exist, making the debt structure unsupportable. Chapter 7 filed. Assets liquidated. Story over.

**The Evidence Layer:** Sarah had spent eighteen hours constructing a forensic paper trail that supported this narrative. Financial statements showing accelerating burn rate — real numbers, selectively presented. Email chains documenting Brennan's refusal to compromise on engineering specifications — also real, pulled from actual correspondence, recontextualised. Board meeting minutes that didn't exist until Sarah created them — showing a pattern of ignored warnings from financial advisors, documenting a founder too passionate about his vision to see the cliff he was driving toward.

**The Server Failure:** This was the linchpin. Anya Petrov — who had agreed to stay on for two weeks to handle the technical side of the cover-up — had constructed a forensic trail of cascading hardware failures that would satisfy any digital forensics team short of the NSA's Tailored Access Operations. A RAID controller failure triggering a write cascade across the primary and backup arrays. A firmware bug in the storage controllers — a real bug, one that Anya had discovered and patched three years ago, now retroactively "undiscovered." Data corruption propagating through the backup tiers until the entire archive was cryptographically damaged beyond recovery.

"It's beautiful work," Anya had said, with the particular satisfaction of a cybersecurity specialist who had just engineered the most convincing data loss in history. "Any forensics team that examines these drives will find exactly what we want them to find — a plausible chain of hardware failures that destroyed an irreplaceable dataset. Tragic. Preventable. *Boring*."

**The Lockheed Response:** Sarah's masterstroke. Rather than fighting Lockheed's data access demands, she *gave them everything*. A full data dump — two hundred terabytes of mission telemetry, engineering specifications, test results, and operational logs. All of it genuine. All of it from the *public-facing* mission. The Titan rovers' photography, atmospheric readings, geological surveys, navigation data — everything that any legitimate aerospace partner would want.

What was missing: the acoustic data. Marcus's sonar readings. The stele activation frequencies. Anything related to the Cydonian ore, the creation harmonic, or the anti-singularity event. This data had never existed on McNeeve's corporate servers. It had been captured, processed, and stored exclusively on Brennan's air-gapped underground systems — systems that didn't appear in any corporate filing, any insurance policy, any asset inventory that Lockheed's lawyers could subpoena.

The acoustic data lived in three locations: an encrypted archive in Cian's Swiss facility, a backup in the safehouse network, and a set of drives that Anya had personally couriered to a location she referred to only as "the vault."

"Halstrom will push," Sarah told Brennan during their 0300 strategy session, the fifth night running. "He'll claim the data dump is incomplete. He'll demand access to on-site systems. He'll threaten breach-of-contract litigation."

"Let him."

"The litigation will take eighteen months minimum. During that time, the bankruptcy court controls all asset access. Lockheed's lawyers can file motions until the heat death of the universe — the trustee won't release systems that are part of an active liquidation proceeding. By the time they fight through the court process, there will be nothing left to find."

Brennan looked at his wife — the woman who had decided, on a night when a demon attacked their daughter, that monsters were just another problem to solve. The woman who had said *Kids, get your shoes* while the smell of ozone and corruption still hung in the air.

"Have I ever told you you're terrifying?" he said.

"Frequently." Sarah didn't look up from her legal pad. "It's one of my better qualities."

---

**The NASA Problem** was less amenable to legal firepower.

NASA didn't want McNeeve's data — they had their own Deep Space Network recordings of the anomalous signal, and those recordings were generating questions that Sarah couldn't suppress with paperwork.

The event had produced measurable effects across multiple government monitoring systems:

- The DSN had logged a three-second disruption to tracking stations in Canberra, Madrid, and Goldstone simultaneously. Official classification: "unexplained electromagnetic interference of unknown origin."
- NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center had recorded gravitational anomalies that didn't correspond to any known solar or geomagnetic event.
- The Air Force's Space Surveillance Network had briefly lost tracking on 847 objects in geosynchronous orbit — a "data gap" that the Space Force was treating as a potential cyberattack.
- The USGS seismograph network had registered micro-tremors at McNeeve's Austin location that were consistent with a localised seismic event of magnitude 2.1 — in an area with no geological fault activity.

These were breadcrumbs that Sarah couldn't sweep away. Government agencies had their own data, their own analysts, and their own institutional curiosity. Questions would be asked.

Her strategy: don't suppress the questions. *Redirect* them.

The cover story she fed through back channels — not directly, never directly, but through carefully positioned intermediaries in the aerospace community — was elegant in its misdirection: McNeeve Space Systems had been conducting high-energy acoustic experiments related to its Mars rover communication systems. An experimental sonic emitter — designed to test subsurface penetration frequencies for geological surveys — had malfunctioned catastrophically. The resulting acoustic discharge had produced the electromagnetic and seismic effects that various agencies had detected.

It was plausible. Private aerospace companies conducted experimental tests constantly. Acoustic experiments could produce electromagnetic effects through magnetostriction. The seismic readings were consistent with a localised industrial accident. And the DSN disruption? Sympathetic interference through the company's own transmitter array, which was linked to the Deep Space Network for rover communication.

Boring. Predictable. *Exactly* the kind of industrial accident that generated paperwork, insurance claims, and a stern letter from the FAA — but not the kind that generated congressional investigations.

"The key," Sarah told Cian during the 0300 brief, "is that nobody wants this to be interesting. Lockheed doesn't want their partnership with a failed company to attract scrutiny. NASA doesn't want to explain why they partnered with a startup that blew up its own lab. The Air Force doesn't want to admit they lost tracking on eight hundred satellites for three seconds. Every institutional incentive pushes toward accepting the boring explanation."

"And the Synagogue?"

Sarah paused. This was the variable she couldn't model. Corporations she understood. Government agencies she understood. A five-thousand-year-old network of occult financiers embedded in the infrastructure of global power — that was outside her professional experience.

"The Synagogue already knows what happened," Cian said quietly. "They felt the oaths. Liaigh — my guardian — said 'the oaths carried further than sound.' That means the signal propagated globally. Every entity with sufficient spiritual sensitivity felt two hundred fallen angels speak their names simultaneously. They don't need Lockheed's data. They don't need NASA's recordings. They know."

"Then what's the point of the cover story?"

"The cover story isn't for them. It's for the humans between us and them. The analysts, the investigators, the congressional staffers, the journalists — the *normal* people whose curiosity could draw them into something that would get them killed. Your cover story protects Brennan. It protects the team. And it protects every innocent person who might otherwise start asking questions that lead to doors they cannot survive opening."

Sarah met his eyes. "Then we make the cover story bulletproof."

"You already have." Cian regarded her with an expression that Sarah couldn't quite read — something old and quiet and almost tender. "Niamh would have chosen someone like you for Brennan. She'd have approved."

Sarah McNeeve — corporate lawyer, mother of two, woman who had reorganised her entire understanding of reality in a single evening and responded by getting her children's shoes — looked at the immortal warrior who had protected her husband's bloodline for twenty-six centuries.

"Don't patronise me," she said. "I'm working."

She turned back to her legal pad, and she didn't stop until the sun came up.

## IV. THE ANSWERS

The envelopes sat on the conference table until Friday.

Brennan spent those three days doing what he always did when he couldn't work on engineering — he worked on people. Quiet conversations in the facility's break room. A lunch run with Elena. An hour sitting next to Tomás in the server room, not talking, just *being there*, because Tomás processed things in silence and Brennan had learned years ago that the kid needed proximity rather than words.

He didn't push. He didn't recruit. He answered questions when asked and changed the subject when the questions got too close to information that would put them at risk.

On Thursday evening, JW found him in the lab — still staring at the dark screens, still drinking cold coffee.

"The crystal structure," JW said, without preamble. "The one you showed us before... everything. The material with the impossible lattice."

Brennan looked at him.

"I've been thinking about it for six days." JW sat down. "Every waking hour. The atomic geometry — the resonance matrix, you called it. I've worked with metamaterials for twenty years. Published thirty-one papers. I thought I understood acoustic impedance at a fundamental level."

"And now?"

"Now I know I was drawing stick figures and calling it art." JW's calm face held something Brennan had never seen there before — *hunger*. The intellectual hunger of a materials scientist who had just been shown that his entire field was a footnote to a chapter that hadn't been written yet. "I can't go back to graphene composites and carbon nanotube arrays after what I've seen. I can't sit in a lab at MIT and pretend that the most interesting material in the universe isn't sitting in a lead-lined case on your uncle's plane."

"That's not a question."

"No." JW held out his unopened envelope. "It's not."

---

Friday morning. 0800. The same conference room. The same seven faces.

Brennan stood at the head of the table. He'd asked for answers. He was about to get them.

Elena first. She placed her envelope on the table — opened, read, considered.

"Eighteen months' salary, fully vested options, and a letter that gets me hired anywhere." She tapped the envelope. "That's generous, Brennan. Genuinely generous. You're a good boss."

"I try."

"I called my husband last night. Told him I might be changing jobs. He asked if it paid well. I said yes. He asked if it was dangerous. I said probably. He said—" She paused, and for just a moment, the formidable Dr. Elena Vasquez looked like a woman who was scared and choosing to act anyway. "'Elena, the last time you came home this excited about work was the night you calculated the Mars orbit. Whatever this is, do it.'"

She pushed the envelope back across the table.

"I don't understand what I saw six days ago. I don't understand how a sword can produce a frequency that shouldn't exist. I don't understand how voices can travel forty million miles instantaneously. But I understand orbital mechanics, and I understand that whatever your uncle is doing, he's going to need someone who can move things through space." She looked at Brennan. "I'm in."

Tomás next. He didn't speak — he almost never spoke when a gesture would do. He simply placed his unopened envelope on the table, pulled out his laptop, and turned the screen toward Brennan.

On it: a software architecture diagram. Dense, complex, spanning three displays' worth of system design. At the top, a header:

**AUTONOMOUS INTELLIGENCE NETWORK — DISTRIBUTED, ENCRYPTED, SOVEREIGN**

"I started designing it on Wednesday," Tomás said. "Couldn't sleep anyway. If your uncle's fighting a network that's been operating for five thousand years, he needs a command-and-control system they can't penetrate, can't subvert, and can't shut down. This is the skeleton. I need six months and access to your cybersecurity person to build it."

Brennan looked at Anya. Anya was already studying Tomás's screen with the focus of a woman who had found something worth breaking into — or, in this case, worth making unbreakable.

"I was going to give myself until Friday," Anya said, not looking away from the diagram. "But that architecture needs me. I'm in since Wednesday."

JW: already committed. Envelope returned.

Priya placed her envelope on the table carefully, as if it contained something fragile.

"I need to ask one question."

"Ask."

"Can you promise me that what we're doing matters? Not matters to you, not matters to the mission — matters to *everything*. Because what I heard six days ago..." Her voice tightened. "Those voices. The way reality... *moved*. If that's what we're up against, I need to know that the fight isn't pointless. That building a communications network for your uncle isn't just rearranging deck chairs."

Brennan considered the question with the seriousness it deserved. Then he reached into the desk drawer, took out the brass cylinder, and set it on the table.

"This was written by my ancestor. Twenty-five centuries ago. She was rescued from a Nephilim blood cult by the same man who's asking for your help — my uncle Cian. She spent her life raising children who would continue the fight. And in her final year, she wrote a letter to every generation that would come after her."

He unrolled the scroll — carefully, reverently, the way he handled it every time.

"*The war is coming. The final war, the one the prophets wrote of and the angels whisper about in the halls of heaven. Stand with my brother. He will need you more than he knows.*"

Priya read the words. Read them again.

"*You are Machirite. You are mac Morna. You are a giant slayer. Now rise, and hunt.*"

"That was written to me," Brennan said. "But the fight she's describing — the fight against what you heard six days ago — that fight needs more than one bloodline. It needs people who build things. People who solve impossible problems. People who look at the end of the world and say *right, what's the communications architecture?*"

Priya folded the scroll back carefully and returned it to its cylinder. Then she pushed her envelope across the table.

"I'll need better encryption than anything I've worked with before."

"You'll have it," Anya said.

That left Hal.

The robotics engineer sat with his massive hands flat on the table, his unopened envelope between them. He hadn't spoken during Brennan's original pitch. He hadn't spoken during the answers. He'd sat and listened with the patient solidity of a man who built things that worked and didn't see the point of talking when building would do.

"My grandfather," Hal said, "was Igbo. From Nsukka, in southeastern Nigeria. He told me stories when I was small — before we moved to Houston, before I learned that stories were supposed to be *metaphors*." His deep voice filled the room without effort. "He told me about the Nephilim. Used a different name — *ndi ọjọọ,* the wicked ones, the giants who walked before the Flood. He said they taught men to make weapons. He said their fathers fell from heaven." Hal looked at Brennan. "I was eight years old, and I thought they were fairy tales."

He picked up the envelope. Turned it over. Set it down.

"Six days ago, I heard one of those fathers speak his own name. Gadreel. The weapons-teacher." His hands — the hands that had built five Mars rovers from blueprints to flight-ready hardware — were steady. "My grandfather would have said *the circle is closing*. He would have said *God doesn't waste hands that know how to build.*"

He stood up, took the envelope, and tore it in half.

"What do you need me to build?"

## V. THE COMBAT ENGINEER

Seven out of seven.

Brennan sat in his office after the meeting, staring at the torn halves of Hal's envelope, and felt something shift in his chest — not grief, not relief, but something between the two. The weight of responsibility. The strange, fierce gratitude of a man who had just asked people to follow him into darkness and been answered with loyalty he wasn't sure he deserved.

*Help him. Fight with him. Do not let him carry this alone.*

Not alone. Not anymore.

He was composing the operational brief for Cian — skill sets, clearance levels, personnel assessments — when the knock came. Three precise impacts, evenly spaced. Military cadence.

James Harrington stood in the doorway in running shoes and a plain grey t-shirt, his SAS-hardened frame filling the space with the particular economy of a man who never occupied more room than necessary but whose body was permanently calibrated for violence.

"Got a minute?" James said.

"Several. What's up?"

James entered the office, closed the door, and sat down across the desk. For a moment he said nothing — just studied Brennan with the assessing gaze that Brennan had learned to recognise as the look James gave to anything he was considering either recruiting or neutralising.

"Your people are impressive," James said. "Every one of them just signed on to a war they don't fully understand, based entirely on trust in you. That's not something you buy. That's leadership."

"That's friendship. I hired them because I liked them. Turned out they're also geniuses."

"Those two things aren't unrelated." James leaned forward. "Here's my problem. You've just assembled a team of seven bloody brilliant engineers for an operation that involves infiltrating hostile facilities, operating in contested environments, and potentially engaging with entities that can fold reality like a napkin. They're assets. *You* are an asset. And right now, the most dangerous thing any of you have ever done is solder a circuit board at three in the morning."

"I resent that. I've also operated a CNC mill while sleep-deprived."

"Hilarious. Here's what's not hilarious: Cian's going to take this team into the field. Not tomorrow, not next week, but soon enough. And when he does, the people in that field are going to include *things* — not people, *things* — that move faster than you can see, hit harder than you can imagine, and view engineers the way you view components. Useful when functional. Discard when not."

Brennan's smile faded. "You want to train us."

"I want to train *you*. First. Then you help me train them." James held up a hand before Brennan could object. "I'm not turning you into a soldier. I don't have the time and you don't have the temperament. What I'm going to give you is survivability. Situational awareness. Basic combat movement. How to clear a room, how to use cover, how to control your breathing when something unholy is trying to rip your face off."

"That's... specific."

"I work from experience. Tomorrow morning. 0500. The cleared lot behind the south wing. Wear something you don't mind bleeding in."

It wasn't a request.

---

*Day 1.*

James started with what he called "the baseline" — a comprehensive assessment of Brennan's physical capabilities delivered through a sequence of exercises that made Brennan's university rowing training look like gentle recreation.

"Run the perimeter. Now."

Brennan ran. The cleared lot behind the McNeeve facility was three hundred metres on the long side — an oblong of packed Texas dirt that Brennan had used for vehicle testing and James had apparently decided was an adequate killing field.

Three laps. Brennan finished the third lap with his lungs on fire, hands on his knees, the February sun already hot at 0530.

"Time?" he gasped.

"Doesn't matter. I'm not measuring your speed. I'm measuring your recovery." James watched him with clinical detachment. "Stand up. Breathe through the nose. Hands behind your head."

Brennan obeyed. His heart rate dropped. Faster than it should have.

James noticed. Said nothing. Filed it away.

Then came the hand-to-hand basics. James demonstrated a stance — feet shoulder-width, weight slightly forward, hands up, elbows in. "Combat platform. You fight from here. You move from here. Everything starts and ends from this position."

Brennan mimicked the stance. James adjusted his feet — a nudge here, a correction there. "Wider. Drop your centre of gravity. Good. Now — I'm going to push you."

James shoved him. Hard. Military hard. The kind of push that was designed to test not just balance but *instinct* — what the body did when the mind didn't have time to think.

Brennan stumbled back. Caught himself. Found the stance again without being told.

James pushed him again. Harder.

This time, Brennan didn't stumble. His feet shifted — a micro-adjustment, weight redistributing, his centre dropping a quarter-inch — and he absorbed the force. Not gracefully. Not skilfully. But *effectively*.

"Interesting," James said.

"What?"

"Do it again."

He pushed Brennan fourteen times. By the tenth push, Brennan was absorbing the impact like he'd been doing it for months, not minutes. His body was learning the movement pattern at a rate that James found... notable.

"What does Cian call your bloodline?" he asked.

"Machirite. Descended from Machir, son of Manasseh. The—"

"Giant-slayer clan. Yes. I've heard the briefing." James studied him. "Throw a punch. Your instinct. Don't think about it."

Brennan threw a right cross. It was clumsy — the punch of a man who'd never been in a fight — but the *mechanics* were there. Hip rotation. Weight transfer. The kinetic chain from foot to fist that took most trainees weeks to develop.

"Again."

Brennan threw again. Better. The second punch carried more force than the first, not because he was trying harder but because his body had *already corrected* the mechanical errors from the first attempt.

"You've never been in a fight," James said. Not a question.

"I got shoved in a bar in college. Does that count?"

"It doesn't. But that punch — the second one — that was the punch of someone who's been fighting for years. Not the precision, the *instinct*. Your body knew what to do before your brain figured out how to tell it." James paused. "It's in the blood."

"You're saying my ancestry makes me a natural fighter?"

"I'm saying that your bloodline has been killing things larger than themselves for three thousand years, and that kind of sustained generational selection tends to leave marks in the genome. Combat reflexes aren't entirely learned, Brennan. Some of them are inherited. *Yours* are inherited."

Brennan stared at his fist.

"We'll do two hours a day," James said. "Every day. Six weeks minimum. I'm going to teach you combatives, firearms, tactical movement, and threat assessment. The goal isn't to make you dangerous — the goal is to stop you from being a liability. What happens after that..." He shrugged. "We'll see what your blood remembers."

---

*Day 8.*

Breskov drill. James's own modification of the Russian systema approach — a cycle of stress positions, explosive movement, and controlled sparring designed to build combat conditioning in trainees who didn't have years to develop it.

Brennan was keeping up.

Not comfortably — every evening he collapsed into bed with muscles he hadn't known existed screaming in dialects of pain he hadn't known were possible. But he was *keeping up*. His body processed the physical stress with an efficiency that James measured and didn't comment on. Recovery times that should have taken hours took minutes. Movement patterns that should have required hundreds of repetitions were integrating after dozens.

"Your stamina is anomalous," James told him over breakfast on the eighth morning. This was the closest thing to a compliment the SAS sergeant had ever offered.

"I used to row in college."

"This isn't rowing conditioning. Your cardiovascular recovery rate is in the ninety-eighth percentile. Your fast-twitch muscle response is faster than some operators I've trained. And your proprioception — your body's awareness of itself in space — is extraordinary."

"Is that the bloodline thing again?"

"It's the bloodline thing. Cian moves the way he does because he's had twenty-six centuries of practice. You're moving the way you are because your DNA has been practising for thirty."

---

*Day 15.*

Firearms. James started with the fundamentals: weapon safety, grip, sight picture, trigger control. Standard military progression, adapted for a civilian with no prior experience.

Brennan put three rounds through the same hole on his first magazine.

James looked at the target. Looked at Brennan. Looked at the target again.

"Have you fired a weapon before?"

"Skeet shooting with my father-in-law. Twice."

"Skeet shooting doesn't teach you to group three rounds into a single hole at twenty-five metres. *Nothing* teaches that on the first day."

Brennan shrugged with the particular discomfort of a man who was discovering that a part of himself he hadn't known existed was very, very good at putting metal into flesh at high velocity.

"The sword," he said. "Mo Chrá. When she was in the safehouse, the night the shedim came — she hummed when I was near. Cian said she recognises the bloodline. He said..." Brennan frowned. "He said the Machirites were the giant-slayer clan because they were *bred* for it. The original Machir — Joseph's grandson, the one who conquered Gilead — was selected by God because his line carried specific gifts. Strength, speed, spatial processing, combat intuition. Mo Chrá can sense those gifts in the blood."

"And now you're wondering if the ancestor who wrote you that letter was right."

"*You are a giant slayer.* I thought she was being metaphorical."

"She wasn't." James took the weapon, cleared it, set it on the bench. "Brennan, in forty years of special operations, I've trained hundreds of people. Some of them were talented. A few were gifted. You are neither — you're *encoded*. Your body is running software that was installed thirty centuries ago, and all I'm doing is giving it hardware to execute on."

---

*Day 22.*

Tactical movement. Room-clearing drills. James had set up a shoot house in the facility's underground maintenance corridors — plywood walls, pop-up targets, simulated threats positioned according to scenarios he'd developed during his SAS career.

Brennan moved through the course at a speed that should have been reckless and was, instead, controlled. His corner work was clean. His weapon transitions were smooth. His threat identification — distinguishing targets from non-threats — was running at 94%, a number that James's SAS recruits typically didn't reach until week eight.

This was week three.

"You're compensating," James said after the run.

"For what?"

"For your engineering brain. Most trainees overthink — they see a threat, process it analytically, decide on a response, and execute. By the time they've finished thinking, the threat has moved. You're doing something different. You're *processing and moving simultaneously* — your body is executing while your brain is still analysing. That's the separation between trained and instinctive."

"And between instinctive and—"

"And between instinctive and *warrior*." James held his gaze. "Don't let it go to your head. You're still three decades behind any competent operator in experience, and experience is what keeps you alive when instinct isn't enough. But the foundation..." He paused. Reconsidered. Said it anyway. "I've only seen one other person learn combat movement this fast."

"Cian."

"Cian. Who has twenty-six centuries of practice and a supernatural sword." James's face held something that might, in a less disciplined man, have been wonder. "You have three weeks and a genetic memory."

---

*Day 36.*

Sparring. Full contact, controlled intensity. James had been building to this for five weeks — layering skills, developing reflexes, constructing the framework of competence that would let Brennan survive a physical confrontation with something that wanted to kill him.

Brennan stepped into the ring — a taped rectangle in the underground facility's cleared space — and raised his hands in the combat platform that had become second nature.

James came at him fast. SAS fast. The speed of a man who had spent forty years refining the art of controlled violence into something that looked effortless and felt like getting hit by weather.

Brennan blocked the first strike. Parried the second. The third caught him in the ribs — a controlled impact, enough to hurt, not enough to injure — and he absorbed it, pivoted, and threw a counter that James had to actually *work* to deflect.

"Good," James said. Not slowing down. "Again."

They sparred for six minutes. Brennan took hits — plenty of them — but he returned enough to matter. His movement was raw, unpolished, lacking the fluid economy of a trained fighter. But the *instincts* were operating at a level that made James revise his assessment upward for the fourth time in a week.

When they broke, both breathing hard, James regarded his student with an expression that Brennan had never seen from the SAS sergeant before.

Respect.

"You'll do," James said.

"High praise from Mr. Personality."

"Don't push it." James tossed him a water bottle. "Six more weeks. Intermediate combatives, advanced firearms, vehicle operations, and I'm going to teach you how to think like an operator — threat assessment, pattern recognition, operational planning. The engineering mind you've got is an asset. It processes tactical problems the same way it processes structural calculations — fast, systematic, three-dimensional. I want to sharpen that."

Brennan drank, wiped his mouth, looked at his hands. Bruised knuckles. Torn skin across the right index finger. Hands that had spent thirty years building rovers and were now learning to build something else entirely.

*You are Machirite. You are mac Morna. You are a giant slayer.*

"Six more weeks," he said. "And then?"

James's smile — rare, brief, and slightly terrifying — flickered across his face.

"Then we see if the button-smasher can hunt."

---

## VI. THE INHERITANCE

The night before the bankruptcy filing went public, Brennan sat in his office for the last time.

The McNeeve Space Systems logo — an angular M overlapping a stylised Mars — glowed above the reception desk, visible through his office window. Tomorrow, the power would be cut. The logo would go dark. The building would begin the slow process of becoming someone else's property, its clean rooms and fabrication labs stripped, catalogued, and auctioned to the highest bidder.

Everything he'd built. Everything except what mattered.

Sarah appeared in the doorway. "It's done. Filing goes to the court at 0800. Press release at 0900. Halstrom gets his data package at 0830 — I want him reading before the public statement, gives him just enough time to feel special and not enough to coordinate a response."

"Have I mentioned you're terrifying?"

"Twice today. You're losing your edge." She crossed the office, sat on the edge of his desk, and looked at the dark screens. "Any regrets?"

Brennan thought about it. Really thought about it — not the reflexive *no* he'd been offering everyone who asked, but the honest inventory of a man who was about to watch his life's work be dissolved for strategic purposes.

"The rovers," he said. "Not the company, not the contracts, not the brand. The rovers. Titan-1 through Titan-5. I built them with my hands. Designed them, tested them, watched them launch, tracked them across forty million miles of void. And they were *good*, Sarah. They were the best things I've ever built."

"They found the steles."

"They found the steles. And then the steles unmade them." He exhaled. "I keep thinking about Titan-5's last transmission. Seven seconds. The rover knew it was dying — the AI wasn't sophisticated enough for fear, but the diagnostic logs show a system that *recognised* terminal failure and prioritised data transmission over self-preservation. It spent its final seven seconds uploading everything its microphones could capture instead of trying to survive."

"That sounds like something you would do."

Brennan looked at his wife. The woman who had rearranged their entire life in a single night, who had said *kids, get your shoes* while demons stalked their hallway, who had spent the last six weeks constructing a legal fortress around a secret that could end the world.

"Niamh would have liked you," he said.

"I'd have liked her. Any woman who writes *if you want peace, prepare for war* in her farewell letter is my kind of ancestor."

Sarah took his hand. Squeezed. The grip of a woman who had made her choice and was not looking back.

"Come home, Brennan. The filing will be there in the morning. The team will be there in the morning. Your uncle's war will be there in the morning." She pulled him gently toward the door. "Tonight you're just a husband and a father. Tomorrow you can be a giant slayer."

Brennan stood. Took one last look at the dark screens. The logo glowing in the empty lobby. The facility he'd built from nothing and was now surrendering to nothing.

Then he turned off the lights and followed his wife into the night.

The screens stayed dark.

But in the underground lab — sealed, air-gapped, known only to the eleven people who had chosen Door B — Titan-5's seven seconds of acoustic data cycled through its analysis algorithms in silence.

Waiting to be decoded.
Waiting to speak.
Waiting for the giant slayers to listen.
