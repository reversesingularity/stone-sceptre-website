# CHAPTER 12: THE QUESTION

---

## I. THE WAITING

The Austrian safehouse had become a different place with James Madigan in it.

Where before it had been Cian's solitary command post — a converted wine cellar fitted with technology that would have seemed like sorcery to anyone who didn't know Brennan Webb — now it hummed with the particular energy of a team. Miriam's intelligence feeds ran on one console. James's tactical planning documents occupied another. Video feeds connected them to Brennan's Austin facility, where the engineer was working around the clock to solve a problem that had been vexing him for eighteen months.

Mars.

The Cydonia steles — twenty angular pillars arranged in a perfect circle around the Face complex — had been photographed, scanned, and acoustically mapped by Brennan's Titan rovers. The inscriptions on their surfaces were clearly Watcher script, predating every human civilisation by millennia. Mo Chrá had confirmed what Cian's instincts told him: the steles contained recordings. Memory. The acoustic archives of beings who had witnessed creation and then abandoned their first estate to corrupt it.

What the steles *said* remained a mystery.

"He's been at it for three days straight," Miriam said, watching Brennan's feed from across the operations table. The engineer's face was haggard — the particular exhaustion of a man who had given up on sleep as a concept and was running on caffeine, stubbornness, and the desperate need to succeed. "Has he even eaten?"

"MARCUS HATH BEEN PROVIDING SUSTENANCE," Liaigh reported. "THE SONAR SPECIALIST REMAINETH COMMITTED TO ENSURING BRENNAN'S PHYSICAL SURVIVAL, IF NOT HIS PSYCHOLOGICAL WELLBEING."

On the secondary feed, Marcus Reeves — Navy veteran, Brennan's contractor, and the closest thing the engineer had to a best friend — could be seen quietly placing sandwiches and energy drinks at the edge of Brennan's workspace. Brennan wasn't eating them. Marcus was eating them for him.

"We wait," Cian said. "When he's ready to brief us, he'll call."

James leaned back in his chair, the hydraulic frame of his exoskeleton powering down as he settled. The Veteran's Frame — Dara's adaptation of the MORTIS platform — sat in the corner like a patient sentinel, but James had been wearing the knee braces as a stand-alone unit. His movement had improved dramatically. Pain-free for the first time in fifteen years.

"So we wait," James repeated. "In a wine cellar. With an immortal Irishman and no whiskey. That seems like an oversight."

Miriam's lips quirked. "There's whiskey. Cian just hides it because he doesn't want to share."

"I don't *hide* it. I curate it. There's a difference."

"The difference being that you know where it is and I don't."

Cian sighed — the particular sigh of a man who had been keeping certain bottles in reserve for twenty-six centuries and was about to watch his reserves depleted by a Limerick man with SAS-conditioned tastes.

"Top shelf. Behind the encrypted server rack. The Midleton Very Rare."

James's eyebrows rose. "Midleton? You have *Midleton* in here and you've been serving me the Redbreast?"

"The Redbreast was *your* bottle. I wasn't going to criticise a man's choice in his own home."

"You're criticising it now."

"Now we're in *my* home. Different rules apply."

Miriam watched them — two Irishmen separated by twenty-six centuries of lived experience but connected by something older than either of them fully understood. The banter was familiar. The rhythm. It reminded her of her mother's voice, warm and sharp at the same time, trading barbs with colleagues who had become family through shared danger.

She excused herself quietly and went upstairs.

The confession she had demanded from Cian — the explanation for Victoria's death — had been given. The exoneration had been granted through action rather than words. But the wound was still there, still healing, and sometimes the safest thing to do was give it room to breathe.

## II. THE LONG VERSION

James poured two glasses of Midleton — generous measures, the kind that said *we're not here for taste, we're here for truth* — and settled into the chair across from Cian.

The operations table displayed a passive map of Brennan's Mars project. The Titan rovers, deployed across the Cydonia complex. The Face. The D&M Pyramid. The twenty steles arranged in their ancient circle. Red markers indicated areas where Brennan had achieved partial activation. Yellow markers showed areas where he'd failed entirely.

More yellow than red.

"The theology," James said. "You mentioned a long version."

Cian accepted the glass. The whiskey was warm against his palm — Irish single pot still, aged twenty-five years in sherry casks, the kind of bottle that cost more than some families spent on groceries in a month. He'd been saving it for a special occasion.

This would do.

"The theology is complex because it spans six thousand years of human history and an eternity before that," Cian said. "Where would you like me to start?"

"Start with the basics." James leaned forward, his eyes sharp despite the whiskey. "The Watchers. The Nephilim. The angel in your chest. All of it."

"You've already heard that version."

"I've heard the operational briefing. The tactical situation. Enemy strength, capabilities, disposition." James took a long pull of his whiskey. "That's not what I'm asking about."

Cian was quiet for a moment. Mo Chrá's hum was muted — present but restrained, as if the sword understood that this conversation belonged to him and not to her.

"What are you asking about?"

James's eyes met his. There was something there — not suspicion, not challenge, but the particular intensity of a man who had spent forty years navigating impossible situations and knew when he was approaching the heart of one.

"I'm not a praying man," James said. "Haven't been for years. The things I've seen — the things I've done — they make it hard to believe in a God who gives a damn. But I was raised Catholic. Limerick Irish, born in the Church, baptised before I could walk. The faith doesn't leave you, even when you try to leave it."

"I know the feeling."

"Do you?" James leaned forward. "See, here's my question, lad — and I know you're way older than me, but you look about as old as my Miriam, so I'm calling you lad. You've been fighting this war for twenty-six centuries. You carry an angel in your chest and a sword forged by the man who walked with God. You've told me about the Watchers, the Nephilim, the Harlot system, the whole bloody cosmic chess match."

He paused. Took another drink.

"But you haven't mentioned the one name that matters.

"Did you ever meet Him?

"In all your travels — twenty-six centuries of walking this earth, fighting the darkness, carrying that sword — did you ever meet the Lord Jesus Christ?"

## III. THE WOUND

The silence that followed was unlike any silence James had heard.

He'd been in rooms where men died. He'd been in briefings where impossible orders were given. He'd been in the moments after firefights when the adrenaline faded and the weight of what had just happened settled onto shoulders that had to keep carrying the load.

This silence was different.

This silence had *centuries* in it.

Cian's hand tightened on his whiskey glass. His jaw worked. The mask of "Sean Morna" — the flattened accent, the controlled expressions, the careful neutrality that had served him through a hundred different identities — cracked.

And beneath it, James saw something he hadn't expected.

Grief.

Not the fresh grief of recent loss. Not the fading grief of old wounds healed. Something older. Something that had been carried for so long that it had become part of the man carrying it.

Mo Chrá's hum shifted. The sardonic warmth, the tactical edge, the editorial commentary that James had come to expect — all of it was gone. In its place was something softer. Gentler.

A comforting hum.

The hum of a blade that had watched this conversation happen before, in different forms, across two thousand years.

"CIAN," Liaigh said quietly.

"Don't." Cian's voice was rough. Raw. "Not yet."

The angel fell silent.

James waited. The interrogator's patience. The confessor's stillness. His grandmother had been the same way — sitting in silence while the truth found its own way to the surface.

"I was commissioned in 586 BCE," Cian said finally. His voice was low. Controlled. But the control was costing him. "The prophet Jeremiah commissioned me to guard the people of Israel against the predators that had been hunting them since the Flood. I was twenty-six years old. The Babylonians had just destroyed Jerusalem, and the survivors were scattered across the known world."

"That's six hundred years before—"

"Before Christ. Yes." Cian's laugh was bitter. "Six hundred years. I spent six centuries learning to fight the monsters. Learning to wield Mo Chrá. Learning to hear Liaigh's voice and trust his guidance. I hunted shedim from Babylon to Rome. I killed Nephilim remnants in the Greek mountains and the Persian deserts. I did my job. I did it *well*."

"And then?"

"And then the Son of God came to earth." Cian's voice cracked. Just slightly. Just enough. "The Messiah. The one the prophets had promised. The one my entire mission was ultimately in service of. He was born in Bethlehem. He grew up in Nazareth. He taught and healed and performed miracles that shook the foundations of reality. He was crucified on a Roman cross. He rose from the dead three days later. He ascended to heaven in front of five hundred witnesses."

James said nothing.

"And I was in *Persia*." The words came out like a wound being opened. "I was hunting a cult of Nephilim-worshippers in the eastern provinces while the most important event in human history happened without me. I was fighting *shedim* while God Himself walked among men. I was doing my *job* while the Commander I served walked the earth and I never even *knew*."

Mo Chrá's hum intensified — warm, steady, the frequency of a blade that had been present for every moment of this grief and had never stopped trying to help.

"Did Liaigh know?"

Cian's eyes went to the ceiling. The question was not rhetorical. It was the question of a man who had asked it a thousand times and never received a satisfactory answer.

"HE KNOWETH WHAT I KNEW," Liaigh said softly. "WHICH WAS: EVERYTHING."

"Then *why*—"

"BECAUSE THY MISSION WAS PROTECTION. HIS MISSION WAS SALVATION. THEY COULD NOT INTERSECT."

"I could have *protected* Him—"

"HE DID NOT COME TO BE PROTECTED. HE CAME TO **DIE**."

The words hung in the air like a judgment.

"WHAT WOULDST THOU HAVE DONE, CIAN MAC MORNA, IF THOU HADST BEEN IN JERUSALEM THAT DAY? IF THOU HADST SEEN THE ROMANS LEADING HIM TO CALVARY? IF THOU HADST STOOD IN THE CROWD AND WATCHED THEM NAIL THE SON OF GOD TO A CROSS?"

Cian was silent.

"PETER TRIED TO STOP IT," Liaigh continued. "IN THE GARDEN OF GETHSEMANE. HE DREW A SWORD AND CUT OFF A MAN'S EAR. THE LORD REBUKED HIM. WHAT DOST THOU THINK THOU WOULDST HAVE DONE — WITH A BLADE THAT CANNOT BE RESISTED?"

"I would have tried to save Him."

"AND IN DOING SO, THOU WOULDST HAVE UNDONE EVERYTHING."

The silence stretched. James watched as something he hadn't expected unfolded — an argument that had clearly been happening for two thousand years, playing out in abbreviated form in an Austrian wine cellar while the world slept.

"I understand the logic." Cian's voice was a whisper. "I've understood it for twenty centuries. It doesn't help."

"I KNOW."

"You could have told me afterward. You could have *debriefed* me. Given me the history, the context, the—"

"AND WHAT WOULDST THOU HAVE DONE WITH THAT INFORMATION?" Liaigh's voice was gentle now. Gentle and terribly sad. "LIVED WITH THE KNOWLEDGE THAT THOU HADST BEEN HERE, ON THIS EARTH, WHILE THY LORD WALKED IT? KNOWN THAT IF THOU HADST ONLY TRAVELLED TO JUDEA, ONLY BEEN IN THE RIGHT PLACE AT THE RIGHT TIME, THOU COULDST HAVE SEEN HIM? HEARD HIS VOICE? LOOKED INTO HIS EYES?"

"I could have—"

"THOU WOULDST HAVE HATED THYSELF FOR EVERY MOMENT THOU SPENT ELSEWHERE. EVERY SHEDIM THOU KILLED INSTEAD OF BEING IN GALILEE. EVERY MISSION THAT KEPT THEE FROM JERUSALEM. THE GUILT WOULD HAVE DESTROYED THEE."

"The guilt is destroying me *anyway*."

The confession came out raw and unguarded. The voice of a man who had been carrying something for so long that he'd forgotten what it felt like to set it down.

And then — for the first time since James had met him — Cian mac Morna began to weep.

## IV. THE LISTENER

Upstairs, in the converted parlour that served as her sleeping quarters, Miriam Ashford froze.

She had been lying awake — the particular insomnia of a woman who had too many thoughts and not enough silence to process them — when the sound reached her. Muffled by distance and stone, but unmistakable.

Cian was crying.

She rose from her cot, padding silently to the door. The stairs leading down to the wine cellar were narrow and medieval, designed for servants who had been dead for centuries. Sound travelled strangely through them — sometimes swallowed entirely, sometimes carried with perfect clarity.

Tonight, it carried.

"I've asked the same questions for two thousand years," Cian's voice said, roughened by grief. "Why wasn't I there? Why didn't I feel anything when He died? Did He know about me? Did He *care*? And the one that haunts me most—"

"What's that?" James's voice. Gentle. Patient. The voice of a man who understood that some confessions had to find their own way out.

"Will He say it to me?"

"Say what?"

A pause. Long. Weighted with two millennia of hope and doubt.

"*Well done, good and faithful servant. Enter into the joy of your master.*"

The words from Matthew. The parable of the talents. The commendation that every servant hoped to hear at the end of their service.

"That's what I want," Cian whispered. "That's all I've ever wanted. To hear Him say those words. To know that the centuries meant something. That the fighting and the killing and the loneliness and the grief — that it *mattered*. That when I finally stand before Him, He'll look at me and say *well done*."

Mo Chrá's hum filled the silence — warm and steady and infinitely patient.

"And you're afraid He won't," James said.

"I'm afraid I've been doing this for twenty-six centuries and I still don't know if I'm doing it *right*. I'm afraid the mission I was given has somehow become the mission I gave myself. I'm afraid that when I finally meet Him — face to face, for the first time, at the end of everything — He'll look at me and I won't recognise what's in His eyes."

"What do you mean?"

"I mean I've been fighting so long I've forgotten what it feels like to be at peace. I've been hunting monsters so long I've forgotten what it feels like to be human. I've been carrying this sword so long I've forgotten what my hands felt like without her weight." Cian's voice broke. "What if I meet my Lord and He sees what I've become and finds it... wanting?"

The silence stretched.

And then James Madigan did something that twenty years of special operations had not prepared Cian for.

He reached across the table and put his hand on Cian's shoulder.

"Lad," he said quietly, "I've spent forty years in rooms where men confessed things they'd never told another soul. I've heard soldiers talk about kills they regretted and kills they didn't regret and the ones they're not sure about. I've listened to operators who couldn't sleep and operators who slept too well and operators who weren't sure which was worse."

He let the words settle.

"You know what I've learned? The ones who worry about whether they're good enough rarely are the bad ones. The bad ones don't *worry*. They sleep fine. They look in the mirror and see exactly what they expect to see. It's the good ones — the ones carrying the weight, doing the work, fighting the fight even when it costs them everything — those are the ones who lie awake at night wondering if they've done enough."

"That doesn't answer the question."

"No. It doesn't." James squeezed his shoulder. "But I'll tell you this: I've met a lot of men in my life who claimed to serve something bigger than themselves. Most of them were liars. Some of them were fools. A few of them were genuinely good people doing genuinely good work."

He paused.

"You're the first one I've ever met who's been doing it for twenty-six centuries."

Cian looked at him.

"If your Lord doesn't say 'well done' to that," James said, "then I don't understand theology nearly as well as I thought I did. And neither does He."

## V. THE WITNESSES

In Austin, Texas, Brennan Webb sat frozen at his console.

The audio feed from the Austrian safehouse had been running in the background — standard security protocol, maintained in case of emergencies. He'd been half-listening while he worked on the Cydonia problem, his attention split between spectral analysis and the quiet murmur of his team's conversation.

He wasn't split anymore.

Marcus Reeves stood beside him, a half-eaten sandwich forgotten in his hand. The Navy veteran's weathered face was still — the particular stillness of a man who had heard things in his career that he couldn't share and was now hearing something that made those things seem small.

"Is that—" Marcus started.

"Yeah." Brennan's voice was hoarse. "Yeah, it is."

On the feed, they could hear Mo Chrá's hum. James's quiet words. And beneath it — faint, but unmistakable — the sound of a man weeping for something he had lost before most civilisations had learned to write.

"I knew he was old," Marcus said quietly. "I knew the stories. But I didn't..."

"You didn't think about what it *meant*."

"No." Marcus set down the sandwich. "No, I don't think I did."

The feed continued. James's voice, steady and gentle:

*"...If your Lord doesn't say 'well done' to that, then I don't understand theology nearly as well as I thought I did. And neither does He."*

Brennan blinked. His eyes were wet.

"We should turn this off," he said. "This is... this is private. We shouldn't be listening."

"WE SHOULD," Marcus said. The words came out before he thought about them. "So we understand what we're fighting alongside."

They didn't turn it off.

---

Upstairs in the Austrian safehouse, Miriam Ashford sat on the top step of the cellar stairs and tried to remember how to breathe.

She had spent twelve years building herself into a weapon. She had trained under James, studied under experts, crafted an identity designed to survive in a world that wanted her dead. She had learned to suppress emotion, to channel grief into purpose, to convert love into fuel for the engine of vengeance.

And now she sat on cold stone stairs, listening to an immortal warrior weep, and felt something she hadn't felt since November 14th, 2014.

*Connection*.

Not to her mother. Not to the memory of what she'd lost. To the man downstairs — the ancient, exhausted, impossibly lonely man who had saved James's life without hesitation and was now confessing his deepest fear to a friend he'd known for days.

*We're the same*, she thought. *Different circumstances. Different centuries. But the same wound.*

The fear that you weren't enough. The terror that all your fighting had been for nothing. The desperate hope that somewhere, at the end of everything, someone would look at you and say: *well done*.

Mo Chrá's hum reached her through the stone. Warm. Comforting. As if the blade knew she was listening and wanted her to understand:

*You're not alone in this. None of you are alone in this.*

Miriam pressed her forehead against the cool stone wall and let the tears come.

For the first time in twelve years, she didn't fight them.

## VI. THE MORNING AFTER

Dawn found them still awake.

The Midleton was gone — both bottles that Cian had been "curating," along with a third that James had produced from somewhere and whose provenance neither of them could quite remember. The operations table displayed the same passive Mars feed, unchanged since the night before. Brennan hadn't called.

Cian's eyes were red. So were James's.

Neither of them mentioned it.

"So," James said, his voice rough from whiskey and confession. "Mars."

"Mars." Cian reached for a water glass — they had transitioned to water somewhere around three in the morning, a tacit acknowledgment that the serious drinking was done and the serious thinking needed to begin. "Brennan's been trying to unlock the Cydonia steles for eighteen months. The inscriptions are clearly Watcher script — Mo Chrá confirmed it when we first got the images. But the content..."

"THE CONTENT REMAINETH OBSCURED," Liaigh finished. "THE STELES WERE DESIGNED TO RESPOND TO SPECIFIC FREQUENCIES. BRENNAN'S TECHNOLOGY CAN APPROXIMATE THOSE FREQUENCIES, BUT NOT PERFECTLY REPLICATE THEM."

"Like trying to start a car with a dead battery," James said. "The key fits, but there's not enough power."

"Exactly."

The operations room door opened. Miriam stood in the threshold, her eyes slightly swollen, her posture uncertain.

"I heard you," she said quietly. "Last night. I wasn't trying to eavesdrop, but the walls..."

Cian met her eyes. Whatever he saw there made him nod — once, with the particular acknowledgment of one wounded soldier recognising another.

"It's all right," he said. "Some things shouldn't be carried alone."

She crossed to the table and took a seat. Her movements were careful — the caution of a woman who was processing something she hadn't expected to feel and wasn't sure how to proceed.

"Brennan's calling," she said. "I saw the alert on my way down. He says he's ready to brief us."

James looked at Cian. "You good?"

"I've been carrying this for two thousand years. One night of whiskey and confession won't fix it." Cian's smile was thin but genuine. "But it helps to know someone else has heard it."

He activated the communications feed.

## VII. THE MARS BRIEFING

Brennan's face appeared on the display looking worse than ever — three days of stubble, eyes bloodshot, the particular manic energy of a man who had been running on caffeine and desperation for far too long.

Behind him, Marcus Reeves was visible in the background, carefully not looking at the camera.

"I've been trying to solve this problem wrong," Brennan said without preamble. "For eighteen months, I've been trying to analyse the steles as archaeological artifacts. Photograph them. Scan them. Map their acoustic resonance. Approach them like a scientist approaches an unknown phenomenon."

"And?" James asked.

"And it doesn't work. The steles aren't artifacts — they're *recordings*. They're not designed to be studied. They're designed to be *played*." Brennan pulled up a display. "The inscriptions on each stele are unique. Twenty steles, twenty different scripts, twenty different acoustic signatures. But they're all part of the same system. They're meant to be activated *together*."

"A ritual," Miriam said.

"A recording. Like..." Brennan searched for an analogy. "Like the old vinyl records. Each one contains part of the song. To hear the whole piece, you need all twenty playing at once."

"CAN THY TECHNOLOGY ACCOMPLISH THIS?" Liaigh asked.

"That's what I've been trying to figure out." Brennan's expression shifted — frustration mixed with something that might have been hope. "The answer is: *almost*. I can approximate the frequencies. I can get the rovers positioned at each stele simultaneously. I can initiate a sequenced activation pattern that *should* trigger the playback."

"But?"

"But the frequencies aren't pure enough." Brennan leaned back, rubbing his eyes. "Mo Chrá can sing the creation harmonic. She *is* the perfect frequency. But she's here on Earth, and the steles are forty million miles away on Mars. I can't transmit what she produces — the waveform collapses when you convert it to electronic signal."

"So we need Mo Chrá physically present at Cydonia," James said.

"Or something that carries the same acoustic signature." Brennan pulled up another display — spectral analyses, frequency comparisons, material compositions. "The steles were carved from the same material as Mo Chrá's blade. Cydonian ore. Watcher metamaterial. They should be able to resonate with each other."

"Should?"

"I've run three hundred simulations. In theory, if I can trigger even a partial activation — get the steles to start resonating — they should be able to sustain themselves. Like a tuning fork that's been struck once and keeps vibrating."

"And if they can't sustain themselves?"

"Then we've wasted eighteen months and approximately four hundred million dollars of McNeeve Space Systems' research budget on the most expensive failure in human history." Brennan's laugh was hollow. "No pressure."

Marcus stepped into frame. "Tell them the other thing."

"The other thing?" Cian asked.

Brennan hesitated.

"Tell them," Marcus repeated.

"I got a partial activation two days ago." Brennan pulled up a recording — visual and audio from one of the Titan rovers. "Stele Seven. The activation lasted approximately four seconds before the resonance collapsed. But during those four seconds..."

He hit play.

The footage showed a close-up of one of the angular pillars — Watcher script carved deep into ancient stone, the geometric precision visible even through the thin Martian atmosphere. The rover's microphone was picking up ambient sound — the whisper of wind, the faint hum of the machine's own systems.

And then the stele began to glow.

Not with visible light. The camera showed the same gray stone, unchanged. But the *audio* — the audio captured something that made every person in the Austrian safehouse go still.

A voice.

Not words. Not language. A *tone*. A harmonic that contained within it the suggestion of intent, of meaning, of something that had been waiting millennia to be heard.

And then, cutting through the harmonic like a blade:

A single word.

**SHEMYAZA.**

The recording ended.

"The chief of the Watchers," Cian said quietly. "The one who led the descent."

"He's still speaking," Brennan said. "After all these millennia. His voice is still encoded in those steles. And if I can get them fully activated—"

"We'll hear everything they recorded."

"Their oaths. Their plans. Their knowledge." Brennan's eyes were bright with exhaustion and something else. Something that looked like hope. "Everything they stole from Heaven and brought down to Earth. Everything they used to corrupt humanity before the Flood."

James looked at Cian. "Is that what we want? To hear what these creatures had to say?"

"WHAT THEY HAD TO SAY," Liaigh answered, "INCLUDETH THE LOCATION OF THEIR EARTH-BOUND INSTALLATIONS. THE CONSTRUCTION METHODS OF THEIR TECHNOLOGY. THE WEAKNESSES THEY DELIBERATELY BUILT INTO THEIR SYSTEMS TO PREVENT EACH OTHER'S BETRAYAL."

"Intelligence," Miriam said. Her analyst's mind was already working. "Tactical intelligence on an enemy that's been operating for millennia."

"AND MORE." Liaigh's voice was quiet. "THE STELES MAY ALSO REVEAL THE LOCATION OF DUDAEL. THE PRISON OF AZAZEL. THE PLACE WHERE THE ANSWER TO THE BEAST'S VESSEL LIE WAITING."

The room was silent.

"How long until you can attempt a full activation?" Cian asked.

"Twenty-four hours. I need to reposition three of the rovers and recalibrate the frequency generators." Brennan's exhaustion was palpable through the screen. "I can have everything in place by tomorrow morning."

"Then do it." Cian's voice carried the weight of command — twenty-six centuries of decisions distilled into a single instruction. "Whatever you need. Whatever it costs. Get those steles talking."

Brennan nodded once. Then the feed cut out.

The silence that followed was different from the silences of the night before. This was the silence of a team processing a briefing, calculating implications, preparing for what came next.

"Mars," James said quietly. "We really are starting with Mars."

Cian looked at the display — the red planet rendered in satellite imagery, the Cydonia complex highlighted with its twenty ancient steles. Somewhere in those angular pillars, the voices of fallen angels waited to speak.

"We're starting with Mars," he confirmed. "And then we're going to Antarctica."

Mo Chrá hummed — anticipation and warning intertwined.

The war was about to expand.
