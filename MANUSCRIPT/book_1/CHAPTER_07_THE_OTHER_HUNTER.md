# CHAPTER 7: THE OTHER HUNTER

## MIRIAM ASHFORD POV

---

**Bucharest, Romania**
**November 2024**

---

## I. THE ACCOUNTANT

The man's name was Dragomir Petrescu, and he was going to die in approximately four minutes.

He didn't know this yet. He was sitting in the third booth from the door at Café Veranda on Strada Lipscani, nursing a *țuică* he couldn't afford on his legitimate salary, scrolling through messages on a phone he thought was encrypted. It was encrypted. Just not from Miriam.

She'd broken the cipher three weeks ago. A modified AES-256 implementation with a predictable initialization vector — the kind of encryption that looked impenetrable to anyone who hadn't spent six years studying applied cryptography at GCHQ's expense. Petrescu's handlers had given him military-grade security and left the back door wide open because they didn't believe anyone outside their network had the skills to find it.

They were wrong about that.

They were wrong about a lot of things.

Miriam Ashford sat four tables away, her back to the exposed brick wall, a paperback copy of Dostoevsky's *The Idiot* open on the table in front of her. She hadn't read a word in forty minutes. The book was a prop — appropriate cover for a dark-haired woman sitting alone in a Bucharest literary café on a Tuesday evening. Her coffee had gone cold. She hadn't touched it either.

She was watching Petrescu's reflection in the window behind the bar.

He was forty-three. Divorced. Two children he saw on alternate weekends. Chartered accountant by training, forensic auditor by profession, money launderer for monsters by circumstance. He processed transactions for a shell corporation called Carpathian Holdings Ltd — registered in Cyprus, administered from Bucharest, feeding into a network of seventeen subsidiaries across nine jurisdictions that ultimately serviced the financial interests of something that had been drinking human blood since before the Roman Empire.

Petrescu didn't know about the blood-drinking. He knew about the money. He knew the money was dirty. He processed it anyway because the alternative was a shallow grave in the Bărăgan Plain, and Dragomir Petrescu liked being alive.

Miriam understood the impulse. She'd spent a decade making the same calculation — survival first, questions later. The difference was that her survival served a purpose, and tonight that purpose required Petrescu to answer questions he didn't want to think about.

She closed the Dostoevsky and slipped it into her bag. Checked the exits one more time — front door, kitchen service entrance, bathroom window that opened onto a courtyard with a gate she'd already unlocked. Three routes out. She always needed three.

Then she stood, collected her cold coffee, and walked toward Petrescu's booth.

He looked up when she sat down across from him. The surprise on his face lasted approximately two seconds before it was replaced by the particular brand of wariness that men in his profession developed when strangers approached uninvited.

"Mr. Petrescu. My name is Sarah Goldstein. I'm an investigative journalist with the *Financial Times*, and I'd like to ask you about Carpathian Holdings."

The wariness became something closer to terror.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"Yes, you do." Miriam placed a manila folder on the table between them. Inside: bank statements, transfer records, corporate registration documents, and three photographs that proved Petrescu had personally signed authorizations for wire transfers totaling €47 million into accounts controlled by entities that did not officially exist. "These are copies. The originals are in a secure location. If anything happens to me tonight, they go to INTERPOL, the Romanian DNA" — *Direcția Națională Anticorupție* — "and six media outlets simultaneously."

The colour drained from Petrescu's face with the speed of a man who understood exactly what those documents represented. Not just prison. Not just professional ruin. Something worse. Something that would come for him in the dark.

"Who — who are you?"

"I told you. Sarah Goldstein. *Financial Times*."

"You're not a journalist."

"No," Miriam agreed. "I'm not."

She let the silence work. Silence was a weapon her mother had taught her — one of the last lessons Victoria Ashford had given before the night that ended everything. *Let them fill the void. People will tell you things in silence that they'd die before saying in conversation.*

Petrescu filled the void.

"What do you want?"

"I want to know where the money goes. Not the shell companies. Not the nominee directors. Not the correspondent banks. I want the *end point*. Who is the ultimate beneficial owner of Carpathian Holdings?"

"I can't —"

"You can." She leaned forward. Her voice dropped to a register that carried no warmth at all. "Because the alternative is that these documents reach people who will ask you the same questions in rooms without windows. And the people you work for will know that you talked, whether you did or not."

Petrescu's hand trembled around his glass. The *țuică* sloshed. He didn't drink it.

"There's a man. I've never met him. I've never seen his face. The instructions come through a law firm in Vienna — Kessler, Brandt & Volkov. They handle all client communications. I receive transfer orders, I execute them, I send confirmations. That's all."

"That's not all."

His eyes widened. "What —"

"Three months ago, you received an unusual instruction. A transfer of €2.3 million to a construction company in Brașov — a company that doesn't build anything. The payment was flagged for 'facility maintenance' at a location designated only by coordinates." Miriam slid a second document across the table. A satellite photograph. "These coordinates."

Petrescu stared at the photograph. His face told Miriam everything she needed to know.

"You recognize it," she said.

"I've never been there."

"But you know what it is."

A long silence. The café continued around them — espresso machines, quiet conversation, a waiter collecting glasses. Normal life. The kind of life that existed on the surface of things, thin as paper, covering the machinery underneath.

"They call it the Residence," Petrescu whispered. "Some kind of... private estate. In the mountains. The Carpathians, near Bran. Very old. Very private. Very —" He swallowed. "Very *secure*."

"Who lives there?"

"The client. The one I've never met." His voice dropped further. "The one nobody meets. Kessler called him *Domnul* once. Just that. *Domnul*. The Lord."

*The Lord.*

Miriam's heartbeat didn't change. Her hands didn't shake. Her breathing remained steady at fourteen cycles per minute — the controlled rhythm she'd trained into her autonomic nervous system over ten years of daily meditation and combat breathing exercises.

But inside — inside, something shifted. A tectonic plate of emotion grinding against the bedrock of discipline she'd spent a decade building.

*Domnul.*

She'd been looking for this man for three years. Tracking his money through fourteen countries. Cross-referencing corporate registrations with property records, flight manifests with shipping invoices, death certificates with police reports that never quite added up. Three years of financial forensics applied to the question that had consumed her since the night her mother died.

*Who gave the order?*

The Synagogue of Satan — her mother's name for the network, adopted from the biblical epithet that fit it with unnerving precision — had contracted the killing. But the Synagogue contracted everything. They were administrators, not operators. They issued instructions; they didn't carry them out.

The thing that came to their house in New Mexico. The thing with the ancient eyes and the patient smile. The thing that had watched her mother die with the detachment of a man reviewing a quarterly earnings report.

That was *Domnul*.

That was the Lord.

That was Lord Vârcolac.

"When was the last transfer to the Residence?" Miriam asked.

"Last week. Larger than usual. €4.7 million. Facility expansion, the instruction said. And —" Petrescu hesitated. "There was something else. A separate payment. €350,000 to a logistics company in Chișinău that handles — how do I say this — *biological cargo*. Refrigerated transport. Medical grade."

Corpses. He was transporting corpses.

Miriam kept her face neutral. She'd learned to keep her face neutral the way other people learned to ride bicycles — through repetition, falls, and the eventual muscle memory of not reacting to things that should make a person scream.

"How often do these biological shipments occur?"

"Monthly. Sometimes more. For years." He looked like a man who had just heard his own confession spoken aloud for the first time. "I never asked what was in them. I didn't want to know."

"I know."

"You think I'm a monster."

"No, Mr. Petrescu. I think you're a frightened man who made choices to survive. I think the monster is the one receiving those shipments." She stood. "You're going to leave Bucharest tonight. You're going to take your children and go to your ex-wife's sister's house in Timișoara — yes, I know about her — and you're going to stay there until I tell you it's safe to come back."

"You can't guarantee —"

"I can't guarantee anything. But if you stay in Bucharest, the people you work for will find out we had this conversation within forty-eight hours. I've disrupted their surveillance on you temporarily. *Temporarily*. Go."

Petrescu went.

Miriam sat back down. Picked up her cold coffee. Drank it in one swallow, tasting nothing.

The Residence. Carpathian Mountains. Near Bran.

She'd found him.

After three years of following the money — after ten years of preparing herself — she'd finally found the lair of the creature that had murdered her mother.

The question now was not *if* she would go.

The question was whether she would survive going.

## II. THE INHERITANCE

She walked back to her safehouse through three different routes, doubled back twice, switched jackets once, and spent eleven minutes sitting in a public park watching for surveillance before she was satisfied she was clean. Old habits. The oldest habits. The habits that had kept her alive since she was nineteen years old and the world revealed itself to be a place where monsters wore human faces and the night had teeth.

The safehouse was a one-bedroom flat in the Dorobanți district — nothing memorable, nothing expensive, rented under a name that connected to a legend she'd built four years ago. Romanian-born, British-educated, freelance translator. Neighbours who minded their own business. A landlady who accepted cash and didn't ask questions about the deadbolt Miriam had installed on the bedroom door or the blackout curtains or the go-bag that sat packed by the window at all times.

She locked the door. Checked the telltales — a piece of thread across the bedroom doorframe, a hair on the bathroom cabinet, the precise angle of the laptop screen she'd left at forty-three degrees. All undisturbed. Nobody had been inside.

Then she sat on the bed and opened the laptop, and for the first time in three years allowed herself to open the file she never opened unless she had to.

**VICTORIA_MASTER.enc**

The encryption on this file was not AES-256. It was a custom implementation her mother had designed herself — a woman who had graduated top of her class in mathematics at Cambridge before pivoting to forensic accounting — layered with a key derivation function so computationally expensive that brute-forcing it would require approximately 847 years on current hardware. The passphrase was a sentence that Victoria had made Miriam memorize when she was seven years old, long before Miriam understood why.

*The daughters of Zion walk in beauty, but their enemies shall know them by their scars.*

The file opened.

This was not the first time Miriam had read its contents. She had read them a hundred times. She had cross-referenced, annotated, verified, and expanded them over twelve years until the original 340 megabytes of data her mother had pressed into her hand on a flash drive in a New Mexico safehouse had grown to 2.7 terabytes of intelligence that would have made MI6's Middle East desk weep with professional envy.

Victoria's original dump contained:

The names, photographs, and financial profiles of 1,247 individuals connected to the Synagogue of Satan's operational network across forty-three countries. Account numbers for 893 shell corporations, nominee trusts, and charitable foundations that laundered an estimated $9.2 billion annually. Organizational charts showing the hierarchy from regional operatives up through continental directors to the inner circle — a body of twelve whose identities Victoria had never confirmed but whose existence she had mapped through the negative space of financial flows that converged on nameless accounts in jurisdictions that didn't ask questions.

But the crown jewel — the piece that had cost Victoria her life, the piece that had turned a routine audit into an execution order — was a single document. A memo. Four paragraphs. Marked with a classification system that didn't belong to any government Miriam had ever encountered.

The memo detailed a program designated *ACQUISITION*. Children. Specific children, identified through genealogical screening Miriam still didn't fully understand, selected for "integration" into what the document called "the Director's household."

Miriam's name was on the list.

She'd been seven years old.

The Director — unnamed in the memo, identified only by a code designation that translated from archaic Hebrew as *Husband of the Hidden Chamber* — had marked her. And Victoria, who had spent twenty years of her career being the smartest person in every room she entered, had recognized the memo for exactly what it was: a procurement order for her own daughter.

Everything that followed — the eighteen months of secret intelligence gathering, the dead-man switches, the carefully constructed escape plan, the desperate middle-of-the-night evacuation that would be the last time Miriam ever saw her mother alive — had flowed from that single moment of recognition.

What the Synagogue didn't know — what they had never discovered in twelve years of post-incident damage assessment — was that Victoria's dead-man switches were a decoy.

The files that went to journalists and law enforcement contacts after Victoria's death were real, but they were *curated*. Enough to cause damage, enough to trigger investigations that went nowhere because the investigators were either compromised or intimidated, enough to convince the Synagogue's internal security apparatus that the breach had been contained.

They were not the master archive.

The master archive was on the flash drive Victoria had pressed into Miriam's hand at 3:17 AM on a November night in the high desert of New Mexico, while something older than nations scratched at the front door.

The Synagogue of Satan believed — with the comfortable certainty of an organization that had operated in the shadows for millennia — that the Victoria Ashford breach had been neutralized. The files were out, yes, but manageable. The investigations had been suppressed. The journalists had been discredited or co-opted. The threat was contained.

They did not know that a twenty-nine-year-old woman with her mother's brain and her grandmother's faith carried the complete, unredacted, continuously updated intelligence archive of their global operations on an encrypted drive hidden inside a hollowed-out copy of the Talmud that she kept in a safety deposit box in Zurich.

They did not know because Miriam had spent twelve years making absolutely certain they would never know.

## III. THE FORGING

The year after her mother's death was the year Miriam Elizabeth Ashford ceased to exist.

Not literally. Her passport was still valid, her university records still on file at the London School of Economics, her National Insurance number still active in the HMRC database. But the woman who lived behind those documents — the girl who had studied archaeology because she loved the mystery of buried things, who had planned a career in museum curation, who had imagined a life of quiet scholarship in a Victorian house with a cat and too many books — that woman died in a safehouse in New Mexico on the same night as her mother.

What rose from the ashes was something else entirely.

*Uncle James* — James Whitfield, a retired SAS sergeant who had served with a man who owed Victoria a debt — gave Miriam three things when she arrived at his cottage in the Scottish Highlands with nothing but a backpack, a flash drive, and eyes that had seen things no nineteen-year-old should see.

The first was a roof over her head.

The second was a Browning Hi-Power 9mm and the knowledge of how to use it.

The third — the most dangerous gift — was a question.

*"Your mother died to give you the tools. What are you going to do with them?"*

Miriam knew exactly what she was going to do. But she also knew — with the crystalline clarity that comes from watching your mother torn apart by things that shouldn't exist — that wanting revenge and being capable of revenge were separated by a chasm that only discipline could bridge.

So she built the bridge.

---

*Year One: The Foundation.*

James taught her to shoot. Not the way civilians learned — standing at a range, paper targets, controlled conditions. He taught her the way the Regiment taught: moving, breathing, under stress, in the dark, in the rain, in conditions designed to break you before the enemy could try. She fired ten thousand rounds in twelve months. By the end of year one, she could put three rounds through a playing card at fifteen meters in under two seconds from concealment draw.

He taught her hand-to-hand. Not the choreographed violence of martial arts films — the brutal, efficient, anatomically precise combat methodology of a man who had killed people with his bare hands in places the Ministry of Defence still wouldn't acknowledge. Krav Maga for the foundation. Filipino martial arts for the blade work. British military combatives for the rest.

She was good. She was not good enough. She knew this because James told her, and James did not lie.

*"You're fast. You're smart. You're motivated. But the things that killed your mum are faster, stronger, and older than any human opponent you'll ever face. You will never beat them in a straight fight. So don't fight straight."*

---

*Year Two: The Money.*

Victoria's inheritance came through in month fourteen — probate delayed by the complexity of an estate that had been designed to be difficult to unravel. When the final accounting was complete, Miriam was worth £11.4 million.

Victoria had been very good at her job. Good enough to accumulate significant wealth through legitimate investments while simultaneously auditing the financial operations of an organisation that controlled more capital than most sovereign nations.

Miriam did not spend the money on comfort.

She spent it on becoming something that could survive what was coming.

She enrolled — under a false identity — at the King's College War Studies programme. Not for the degree. For the access. The programme attracted intelligence professionals, military officers, and security consultants from thirty countries. Miriam studied counterintelligence methodology alongside a colonel from the Indian RAW. She learned surveillance tradecraft from a retired Mossad case officer who taught an elective on "asymmetric threat assessment" and who, after six months, quietly told her that she was the most naturally talented intelligence analyst he'd ever trained — and asked no questions about why a twenty-one-year-old woman with Sephardic features and hands that never stopped shaking at night needed to learn these things.

She took his assessment and filed it alongside everything else: useful, but not sufficient.

---

*Year Three: The Tradecraft.*

The Mossad instructor — Miriam never learned his real name; she called him *Dov* because he moved like a bear and thought like a chess player — introduced her to a private security firm in Herzliya that trained corporate executives in counter-kidnapping protocols. The firm's actual purpose, which Dov never explicitly stated and Miriam never explicitly asked about, was retraining retired intelligence officers for the private sector.

Miriam spent six months learning: counter-surveillance detection routes, dead drops, secure communication protocols, identity management, the craft of building legends — false identities with enough documented history to withstand professional scrutiny.

She built seven.

Each identity had a passport, a bank account, a mobile phone registered to a verifiable address, a social media history going back three years, and a plausible biography that could survive a customs officer's casual questioning or a police database check. Three were British. Two were French. One was German. One was Israeli.

She maintained them the way a gardener maintained plants — watering them with small transactions, renewing documents, updating photographs, keeping each identity alive in the bureaucratic databases that constituted reality in the twenty-first century.

She was running her own intelligence service. Population: one.

---

*Year Four: The Violence.*

James had given her the fundamentals. But fundamentals didn't kill the things that had killed her mother.

She found a man in Lyon — a former DGSE operative turned mercenary turned monk who had retired to a monastery after seeing something in a basement in Beirut that no debriefing could explain. Brother Michel, Order of Saint Benedict. He wore a crucifix. He also kept a suppressed Heckler & Koch USP in his nightstand.

She told him the truth. Not all of it — but enough.

"I'm hunting something that isn't human. I need to learn how to fight things that are faster and stronger than me."

Brother Michel studied her for a long time. Then he poured two glasses of wine and told her a story about a cellar in Beirut where three DGSE operatives had cornered something that killed two of them before Michel put a magazine of silver-tipped hollow points into its chest and watched it dissolve.

"Silver doesn't always work," he said. "But it works often enough."

For eight months, Miriam trained with a man who had fought the supernatural and survived. He taught her things that weren't in any military manual — how to read a room for entities that displaced heat differently than humans, how to move through spaces where the light behaved wrong, how to listen for the particular quality of silence that preceded an ambush by something that didn't breathe.

He also taught her to kill.

Not the theoretical killing of a war studies programme. Not the controlled marksmanship of a firing range. The actual, deliberate, irreversible act of ending something's existence.

Her first kill was a ghoul.

Brother Michel had contacts in the Roma communities of Provence who reported supernatural disturbances the police wouldn't investigate. A family in a village outside Avignon had lost three members in six weeks to something that came at night through their cellar.

Miriam went with Michel. They found the ghoul in the cellar — a shambling thing, recently dead, recently reanimated, still wearing the clothes it had been buried in. Its eyes were milky. Its movements were wrong — too fluid for a corpse, too jerky for a living thing. It smelled of formaldehyde and copper.

Michel hung back. This was her test.

She shot it three times — chest, chest, head. It went down. It got back up. She shot it four more times. It kept coming.

"Fire," Michel said calmly.

Miriam pulled the incendiary cap she'd fabricated from a modified road flare, cracked it, and shoved it into the ghoul's chest cavity through the holes she'd just put there. The creature screamed — a sound she would hear in her dreams for years — and burned.

Michel nodded.

"Welcome to the war," he said.

---

*Years Five through Eight: The Hunting.*

She didn't go hunting monsters straight away. That would have been suicide, and Miriam hadn't survived this long by being stupid.

Instead, she hunted the money.

Victoria's files were the foundation. Miriam built on them the way archaeologists built on initial survey data — layer by layer, trench by trench, sifting through the financial strata of an organization that had been accumulating wealth since before the invention of double-entry bookkeeping.

She mapped the Synagogue's network.

Not the inner circle — those identities remained hidden behind layers of protection that even Victoria hadn't penetrated. But the operational infrastructure. The shell companies. The banks. The law firms. The shipping companies and logistics networks and property portfolios that constituted the physical manifestation of an entity that operated like a multinational corporation with a four-thousand-year head start.

She followed the money through:

Zurich. Geneva. Luxembourg. The Cayman Islands.  
London. New York. Hong Kong. Singapore.  
Vienna. Budapest. Bucharest.

Always Bucharest. The flows kept coming back to Bucharest, and from Bucharest into a cluster of companies that serviced a single client in the Carpathian Mountains.

A client the lawyers called *Domnul*.

A client who received monthly shipments of biological cargo.

A client who paid €4.7 million for "facility maintenance" and whose security protocols suggested someone with resources, paranoia, and operational experience that transcended anything the modern world should have produced.

During these years, Miriam also killed fourteen ghouls across six countries, disrupted two trafficking operations connected to the Synagogue's peripheral networks, and survived three incidents that should have been fatal — the last of which involved a man in a hotel bar in Prague who offered to buy her a drink and whose hand, when she shook it, was cold as marble and strong as a hydraulic press.

She broke his wrist. He smiled. She ran.

That was the night she understood that the creatures at the top of this hierarchy were nothing like the ghouls she'd been burning in cellars. The thing in Prague was *old*. Fast. Intelligent. And it had been watching her.

She changed identities the same night. Burned the Sarah Goldstein legend she'd been using in Central Europe and activated Elise Bergmann, a German art dealer with impeccable documentation and seven years of simulated transaction history.

For three months, she disappeared entirely. Scotland. James's cottage. The firing range in the hills. Ten thousand more rounds. More training. More preparation.

She wasn't ready.

She made herself ready.

---

*Years Nine and Ten: The Target.*

The Synagogue of Satan did not know that Miriam Ashford possessed the Victoria Master Archive.

This was the single most important fact of her operational existence, and everything she did — every identity she created, every safehouse she established, every investigation she conducted — was designed to preserve it.

The dead-man switches had done their job. Victoria's curated files had reached journalists and law enforcement. The Synagogue's internal security division — Miriam had mapped their structure, though she didn't know who ran it — had conducted a damage assessment and concluded that the breach was contained. The files were public but manageable. The source was dead. The daughter had disappeared into the civilian population — a traumatized orphan who posed no operational threat.

They were looking for a victim. Not a hunter.

The Asmodeus Director who had marked her — the demon-possessed corporate executive whose acquisition order had started this entire cascade — still wanted her. His desire was not related to the files. He didn't know about the files. He wanted her the way collectors wanted rare objects: with patient, obsessive, dehumanizing single-mindedness. His agents continued to search for her through conventional channels — private investigators, database searches, the kind of human-scale intelligence operations that Miriam's tradecraft training had made her invisible to.

She existed in the gap between what they were looking for and what she actually was.

They were looking for Miriam Ashford, frightened daughter of a dead whistleblower.

She was something else entirely.

It took nine years of financial forensics to identify the entity behind Carpathian Holdings. Nine years of following shell companies through jurisdictions, matching transaction patterns to historical records, cross-referencing property ownership with census data going back to the 18th century. Nine years of the meticulous, unglamorous, deeply tedious work that separated real intelligence analysis from the Hollywood fantasy of instantly accessible databases and dramatic revelations.

When the identification finally came, it came not as a single breakthrough but as a convergence of evidence so overwhelming that doubt became mathematically impossible.

The entity behind Carpathian Holdings was the same entity that had maintained a network of financial accounts across European capitals for at least six hundred years. The same entity whose earliest documented transaction — a property purchase in Wallachia in 1314 CE — predated the formation of the Romanian state by five centuries. The same entity whose financial behavior exhibited patterns of institutional memory that exceeded any human lifespan by an order of magnitude.

The same entity that had walked into a safehouse in New Mexico twelve years ago and nodded for Miriam's mother to be killed.

She didn't know the word *vampire*.  Not then. Not yet. She didn't believe in vampires. She believed in evidence, and the evidence told her that *Domnul* was old, powerful, inhuman, and responsible for the systematic murder of hundreds of people across centuries.

What she called him didn't matter.

What mattered was that he was going to die.

## IV. THE WIRE

Bucharest. Two hours after leaving Petrescu in the café.

Miriam sat cross-legged on the safehouse bed, the satellite photograph spread before her, her laptop displaying a topographic overlay of the Carpathian region surrounding the coordinate designation she'd extracted from the accountant's records.

The Residence.

A fortress, Petrescu had said. Very old. Very private. Very secure.

The satellite imagery — purchased through a commercial provider using one of her European legends — confirmed this assessment and added details that the accountant couldn't have known. The structure occupied a mountain ridgeline southwest of Bran, set back from the main road network by roughly seven kilometres of unpaved track that wound through dense forest before terminating at a gated perimeter. The gates were new — thermal-hardened steel, motion sensors on the pillars, camera coverage on all approaches. The outer wall was old — medieval stonework, patched and reinforced over centuries but fundamentally original construction.

Inside the perimeter: a main structure, three outbuildings, and something that didn't show up properly on the satellite images. An area in the northwest quadrant that should have been garden or courtyard but registered as a thermal anomaly — cooler than surrounding terrain by approximately fifteen degrees.

Underground construction. Something large. Something deep.

Miriam opened a second window on her laptop and began building an operational assessment.

**TARGET ANALYSIS — CLASSIFIED**

**Target Designation:** DOMNUL  
**Location:** Carpathian Residence (coordinates [REDACTED])  
**Threat Assessment:** EXTREME  
**Operational Readiness of Target:** HIGH

**Known Defenses:**
- Perimeter security (modern): cameras, sensors, gated approach
- Structural defense (historical): medieval fortress architecture, single primary approach
- Underground facilities: extent unknown, thermal anomaly suggests significant subterranean construction
- Personnel: unknown number, unknown capability
- *Note: The "biological cargo" shipments suggest the presence of non-standard defensive assets. Classification uncertain. Cross-reference: Avignon cellar incident, Prague hotel incident.*

**Operational Environment:**
- Rural, isolated, limited civilian population
- Dense forest provides approach cover
- Single road access — controlled by target
- Romanian law enforcement presence: minimal (nearest station 23km)
- Local population: reportedly avoids the area (source: regional folklore databases, cross-referenced with municipal records showing zero commercial activity within 5km radius for 40+ years)

**Assessment:**

This is a hardened facility maintained by a target with resources exceeding any non-state actor in the documented database. The underground construction, biological shipments, and anomalous thermal signatures suggest defensive capabilities that conventional intelligence frameworks cannot adequately categorize.

**Recommendation:**

A direct assault on the Carpathian Residence by a solo operator is operationally inadvisable.

Miriam stared at her own recommendation. Read it again. Read it a third time.

Then she highlighted the last paragraph and deleted it.

In its place, she typed:

**Revised Recommendation:**

Go anyway.

## V. THE NIGHT HER MOTHER DIED

She didn't sleep. She never slept well, but tonight the proximity of the target — the nearness of the thing she'd spent a decade preparing to confront — made sleep impossible.

Instead, she let herself remember.

---

*November. High desert. New Mexico.*

*The house smelled like cardamom and old paper. Mom kept her work files in the study — three filing cabinets, two safes, and a laptop with encryption that would have given GCHQ a headache. The study door was always locked. Miriam had learned not to ask about it.*

*But that night, the door was open.*

*Mom was at her desk. She wasn't working. She was sitting with her hands flat on the surface, staring at nothing, and Miriam — nineteen years old, home from her first term at LSE, still jet-lagged and irritable and not yet aware that this would be the last night she spent in a house that felt like home — had walked into that open door and found her mother crying.*

*Victoria Ashford did not cry. Victoria Ashford audited Fortune 500 companies. Victoria Ashford testified before parliamentary committees. Victoria Ashford wore Chanel suits and spoke in sentences that could strip paint from walls and had once made a Goldman Sachs managing director actually weep during a regulatory hearing.*

*Victoria Ashford did not cry.*

*But she was crying now.*

"*Mom? What's—"*

"*Sit down, habibti."*

*The Arabic endearment. Mom only used Arabic when she was scared or sad — the language of her grandmother, the language of prayers whispered in dark rooms, the language of a family that had survived the Inquisition and the Ottoman courts and the British Mandate and still kept the Shabbat candles burning every Friday at sundown.*

*Miriam sat.*

*Victoria opened a drawer. Inside was a flash drive — black plastic, unremarkable, the kind you'd buy at any electronics store. She placed it on the desk between them.*

"*If something happens to me, you take this. You run. You don't call the police. You don't go to the authorities. You don't trust anyone whose name I haven't given you. You go to James. Scotland. He's expecting you."*

"*Mom, you're scaring me."*

"*Good." Victoria's voice cracked and reformed, harder than before. "You should be scared. I should have been scared years ago." She pressed the flash drive into Miriam's palm and closed her daughter's fingers around it. "This contains everything I've found. Names. Accounts. Structures. Proof of things that — that you won't believe until you see them yourself. There are people in this world, habibti, who serve something very old and very evil, and I have spent eighteen months documenting their operations."*

"*What kind of people?"*

"*The kind who buy children."*

*The silence that followed was absolute.*

"*They wanted to buy me," Miriam said. Not a question.*

*Victoria's face confirmed everything.*

"*There's a man. I don't know his real name. He works through a corporate structure, layers of legal protection, but I traced him. He saw you at a company event when you were seven. He — decided you were—"*

*She couldn't finish the sentence.*

"*Mom."*

"*I stopped it. I got you out. I sent you to school in London. I built walls around you that they couldn't —" Victoria stopped. Drew a breath. Let it out. "But walls don't hold forever. They're coming. Not tonight. Maybe not this month. But soon. And when they come, I need to know that you can run."*

"*I'm not running."*

"*Yes, you are." Victoria's hand covered Miriam's. Warm. Strong. Steady now, the way it always was when the stakes were highest. "You are going to run, and you are going to survive, and you are going to take everything I've gathered and you are going to use it. Not yet. When you're ready. When you're strong enough."*

"*Strong enough for what?"*

"*To burn them down."*

*Three weeks later, they came.*

*Not the corporate agents Miriam had expected. Not lawyers or private investigators or men in suits with polite threats. Something else. Something that moved through the darkness with the fluid certainty of an apex predator in its native environment. Something that tore the front door off its hinges as casually as a person opens a book.*

*Three of them. Through the windows in the living room, Miriam could see them in the security lights. Two were — she didn't have words for what they were. Wrong. Fast. Hungry.*

*The third was different.*

*The third stood apart. Watching. His face was composed, almost serene. He wore a dark coat, well-tailored, and his hands were clasped behind his back in the posture of a man attending a function he found mildly tedious.*

*Victoria pushed Miriam toward the back hallway. "The tunnel. Go. NOW."*

"*Mom—"*

"*I said GO."*

*The tunnel was a storm cellar that Victoria had modified — a concrete passage that emerged through a drainage culvert two hundred meters from the house, screened by mesquite brush and invisible from any approach. Miriam had practiced the route. They'd drilled it. She knew the way.*

*She ran.*

*Halfway through the tunnel, she heard the screaming start.*

*She stopped. Every instinct screamed at her to go back. Every lesson her mother had taught her screamed at her to keep running.*

*She kept running.*

*She emerged into the desert night — cold, sharp, the stars so bright they looked like wounds in the sky — and turned back toward the house. Two hundred meters. She could see the windows. She could see the shapes moving inside.*

*Mom was fighting.*

*Not running. Not hiding. Fighting. The sounds coming from the house were — Victoria had a gun. A shotgun. Miriam heard it fire three times. Heard something shriek. Heard furniture breaking, glass shattering, the terrible percussive symphony of a woman who knew she was going to die and had decided to make it cost her killers everything she could take from them.*

*Then the third one — the one who watched, the one in the coat — walked through the front door.*

*The sounds stopped.*

*Not gradually. Not the way a fight winds down. They just — stopped.*

*Silence. Desert wind. Stars.*

*Then a voice. Distant but clear in the crystalline desert air. A man's voice, speaking with the unhurried cadence of someone who had infinite time and no particular reason to raise his volume:*

"*A pity, truly. She was magnificent."*

*Victoria's body hit the front porch.*

*Miriam didn't move. Didn't breathe. The mesquite brush hid her. The darkness hid her. She was invisible. She was nineteen years old and invisible and alone and watching her mother's body lying on the porch of the house that had smelled like cardamom and old paper.*

*The third one — Domnul, the Lord, the one she would spend the next decade of her life hunting — turned and looked directly at the desert.*

*Not at her. At the darkness in general. As if sensing the presence of something alive in the night but unable to pinpoint its location.*

*He stood there for perhaps ten seconds. Then he turned away, nodded to his companions, and walked back inside.*

*They left through the back. Miriam heard a vehicle. Headlights swept across the terrain and faded east.*

*She waited two hours before moving. By then, rigor mortis had begun to set in. By then, the November cold had made her mother's skin feel like marble.*

*She pressed her hand against the cold face. She said the prayer her grandmother had taught her — the Mourner's Kaddish, the sanctification of God's name in the presence of death, the most ancient and terrible prayer in the Jewish liturgicon: Yitgaddal v'yitqaddash sh'meh rabba.*

*Then she took the flash drive and walked into the desert.*

*She didn't cry.*

*She would never cry again.*

## VI. THE DECISION

Dawn came to Bucharest like an accusation — grey light through blackout curtains, the city waking to a Tuesday that had no idea what was heading into the mountains.

Miriam washed. Ate. Cold, mechanical, functional nutrition — protein bar, electrolytes, coffee strong enough to etch glass. She checked the weather forecast for the Carpathian range: clear skies, temperatures dropping to minus four at altitude, no precipitation expected. Good visibility. Bad for someone who preferred to work in the dark.

She set out her gear on the bed and took inventory.

**Weapons:**
- Glock 19 Gen 5, suppressed (20 rounds, 3 magazines — 60 total)
- Glock 43X (backup, ankle holster — 10 rounds, 2 magazines)
- Ka-Bar TDI knife (belt-mounted, right hip)
- Two incendiary devices (modified thermite — designed for total incineration of biological targets)

**Equipment:**
- Night vision (PVS-14 Gen III, monocular)
- Lock picks (custom, titanium — her own design)
- Multi-band frequency scanner (for electronic surveillance detection)
- Medical kit (tourniquet, haemostatic gauze, chest seal — she was realistic about her chances)
- Comms (satellite phone, encrypted — James was her only emergency contact)
- GPS tracker (breadcrumb — if she died, James would know where she fell)

**Clothing:**
- Dark tactical fatigues, layered for cold weather
- Lightweight body armor (Level IIIA — would stop a pistol round; would not stop a vampire)
- Boots she'd broken in over three years of fieldwork (you don't assault a fortress in new boots)

She looked at the spread of equipment. Adequate for killing humans. Adequate for killing ghouls, if she was lucky. Entirely inadequate for killing whatever *Domnul* actually was.

She didn't care.

Twelve years. Ten years of training. Three years of hunting. £11.4 million spent on becoming a weapon. And the target was sitting in a fortress seventy kilometres away, surrounded by creatures that shouldn't exist, protected by resources that would make a narco-state envious.

She was going anyway.

Not because she believed she would win. Not because she had a brilliant tactical plan. Not because the operational assessment — the one she'd deleted — was wrong.

She was going because Victoria Ashford had pressed a flash drive into her daughter's hand and told her to burn them down.

And Miriam always kept her promises.

She packed the gear. Checked each weapon one final time — function test, press check, magazine seated. She locked the safehouse, left the key in the dead drop she'd arranged with the landlady's niece, and walked to the underground car park where she kept the rental vehicle — a dark grey Dacia Duster, anonymous, adequate for mountain roads, rented under the Elise Bergmann legend.

She drove north.

Bucharest fell away behind her. The Carpathian foothills rose ahead. The road narrowed. The trees thickened. The light changed — from the diffuse grey of urban overcast to the sharp, angled illumination of mountain forest, shadows lying across the road like bars across a cage.

Her phone buzzed. A text from a number she didn't recognise. No words. Just coordinates.

The same coordinates as the Residence.

And a timestamp: *48 hours ago.*

Someone else knew where *Domnul* was. Someone else was heading for the same target.

Miriam's hands tightened on the steering wheel. She ran the implications. If the Synagogue had discovered the Residence's location, they'd send a cleanup team — sanitise the site, eliminate witnesses, relocate the asset. If a competitor had found it, they'd send an acquisition team — capture the vampire's resources, absorb his network, take his territory.

Either way, someone was about to kick the hornet's nest. And Miriam, who had spent a decade building toward this singular moment of reckoning, was about to lose her target to institutional politics she didn't fully understand.

She pressed the accelerator.

The Carpathians rose before her like teeth against the sky.

She wasn't done waiting because she'd chosen to stop.

She was done waiting because the clock had just started.

*Mom saved me so I could live free. Unprogrammed. Uncorrupted. Myself.*

*For twelve years, I've told myself that living well was the best revenge. That building my network, gathering my evidence, preparing my case — that was honouring her sacrifice.*

*But that's a lie.*

*The best revenge is revenge.*

*The best way to honour her is to kill the thing that killed her.*

*And I'm going to do it.*

*Alone if I have to.*

*Tonight.*
