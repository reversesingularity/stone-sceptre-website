# CHAPTER 6: GUNS. LOTS OF GUNS.

---

## SCENE 1: THE FORGE

**2027 — European Safehouse, Undisclosed Location**
**Three weeks after the Austin infiltration**

The relocation had taken four days.

After the biology lesson, after the weapon designs had solidified on paper and the crystal growth chambers were loaded and humming, Cian had made the call. Austin was too far from the target. Lord Vârcolac's fortress sat in the Carpathian Mountains — old Romania, deep wilderness — and running an assault from Texas meant trans-Atlantic logistics that added days of exposure. Days the vampire could use to fortify, to scatter, to run.

So Cian had done what he'd done a hundred times across twenty-six centuries: packed the essentials, sealed the Austin safehouse, and moved east. The European staging point was one of six he maintained on the continent — a converted farmhouse outside Graz, Austria, close enough to the Carpathians for a single night's ride on the Shadow Hound, far enough west to avoid Vârcolac's intelligence network. He'd owned the property since 1847, purchased under a name that no longer existed from a family that had long since forgotten the transaction.

Brennan had handled the heavy logistics from Austin — crystal growth chambers, chemical precursors, ammunition components — shipping them through the Josephite network's freight channels, which had the considerable advantage of never being inspected by customs agencies that didn't know they existed.

Three weeks of fabrication. Three weeks of eighteen-hour days, of crystal growth chambers humming at frequencies that made the fillings in his teeth vibrate, of metallurgical processes that combined medieval smithing techniques with twenty-first-century materials science and a healthy dose of what Raphael called "consecrated interference" and Cian called "cheating."

The Tintreach rounds had been the hardest. Growing gallium orthophosphate crystals around a seed of blessed metal was not, as it turned out, a process that responded well to impatience. The crystals grew at their own pace — approximately 0.3 millimeters per day in the hydrothermal autoclave — and no amount of glaring, swearing, or increasingly creative threats had accelerated the timeline.

Mo Chrá had found the whole thing hilarious. Her hum had acquired a distinctly *I told you so* quality around day four, when Cian had tried increasing the temperature gradient and shattered an entire growth batch.

"Don't say it," he'd muttered, sweeping crystalline fragments off the workbench.

The sword's silence had been louder than any comment.

But now — now the work was done. The crystals had grown. The compounds had been synthesized. The casings had been machined. And the workshop looked like the aftermath of a very organized explosion, every flat surface covered with components in various stages of assembly.

Cian stood at the center of it all, surveying three weeks of obsessive labor, and felt the particular satisfaction that only came from turning raw materials into instruments of precise, theologically justified violence.

"Alright," he said, cracking his knuckles. "Let's build some guns."

From her rack, Mo Chrá's hum carried the unmistakable quality of *finally*.

## SCENE 2: THE ARMORY TOUR

But first — inventory.

Cian moved through the armory with the practiced economy of a man who had organized this space a hundred times across a dozen safehouses, in six countries, over twenty-six centuries. Every weapon had a purpose. Every placement had a reason. And every piece told a story that would give a museum curator a coronary.

The historical section occupied the north wall — climate-controlled, humidity-regulated, lit with UV-filtered LEDs that preserved the metal without casting the kind of harsh shadows that made ancient bronze look cheap. These weren't display pieces. They were veterans.

A pair of leaf-bladed Bronze Age swords, the copper-tin alloy still showing the distinctive green patina of Aegean metalwork. He'd carried these during the Persian Wars — back when "monster hunting" meant tracking Nephilim remnants through the chaos of Thermopylae's aftermath while three hundred Spartans provided an unintentional distraction.

A Roman gladius, Mainz pattern, the Spanish steel still holding an edge that would shame most modern kitchen knives. First century. He'd taken it off a centurion who had been asking too many questions about the "unusually large" corpse Cian had been burning outside Capernaum.

A Viking seax with a blade like frozen lightning — pattern-welded steel, the layered folds visible even through the centuries. He'd carried this at Lindisfarne. Not his proudest century.

Japanese katanas — three of them, each representing a century of the art form. The oldest was Kamakura period, 1280s, acquired from a swordsmith who'd been forging weapons for the Mongol defense. The middle was Muromachi, 1467, given to him by a master fleeing the Ōnin War. The youngest was Edo period, a showpiece that a daimyō had pressed on him as thanks for dealing with an *oni* problem that had been eating his peasants.

Ottoman kilij, Safavid shamshir, Crusader arming sword with a blade cross still sharp enough to draw blood. An Indian tulwar with a hilt wrapped in silver wire. A Chinese jian that predated the Qin dynasty. A Scottish claymore — not the basket-hilted version Hollywood liked, but the true two-handed great sword, five feet of murderous intent that had cleared more than one battlefield of things that had no business walking on it.

Not all of these had been easy to come by. The Eastern blades in particular — the katanas, the jian, the tulwar — each represented territory that wasn't his. The Archons who governed those lands under the Deuteronomy 32 mandate were ferociously protective of their jurisdictional borders, and every incursion into foreign domain had required Michael or Raphael to negotiate passage with the local Prince beforehand. Weeks of celestial diplomacy exchanged for days of hunting. The cooperation was never free, never friendly, and never offered twice at the same price. In twenty-six centuries, Cian could count his extra-territorial operations on both hands, and every one of them had cost Michael something he never discussed.

These were his history. His autobiography, written in steel — and in debts settled in courts no mortal would ever see.

But the south wall — the south wall was where the *work* happened.

The modern section was organized not by era but by function. Rifles on the upper racks, handguns in the locked cabinets below, shotguns on the far end, specialty weapons in the reinforced cases along the bottom. Every piece cleaned, oiled, and maintained with the kind of obsessive attention that came from knowing that a stovepipe jam at the wrong moment didn't just mean embarrassment at the range — it meant death, or worse, having to watch something you'd been trying to kill walk away.

Cian ran his fingers along the racks, taking mental inventory.

"Going to need the primary build. The support platforms. And something for close-quarters work that isn't you," he said, glancing at Mo Chrá.

The sword's hum carried a distinct *why would you need anything else?*

"Because Lord Vârcolac's fortress has corridors, *a chuisle*. Narrow ones. And you're magnificent, but you can't ricochet around corners."

Mo Chrá's vibration conveyed that she considered this a design flaw in the architecture, not in herself.

## SCENE 3: THE PRIMARY — BÁS MÓR

Cian pulled the rifle case from the upper rack and laid it on the main workbench. The case was custom-built — CNC-machined aluminum, foam-lined, the exterior finished in the same matte charcoal as his exoskeleton. No markings, no manufacturer logos, nothing that would survive more than a cursory glance. Inside, the weapon rested in precisely cut channels, each component nested in its own depression.

The CheyTac M200 Intervention.

Or rather, what had *started* as a CheyTac M200 Intervention before Cian had spent the better part of three years systematically improving every component until the original manufacturer would have needed dental records to identify it.

"Bás Mór," he said quietly. *Great Death*.

He began the assembly with the reverence of ritual, hands moving with a fluidity that belied the mechanical precision of what he was doing. Twenty-six centuries of warfare had given him an intimate understanding of projectile weapons — from slings to crossbows to arquebuses to the precision instruments that modern arms engineers produced. He'd watched the evolution happen. He'd pushed it forward more than once.

**The action** was where the money lived. CheyTac's original M200 used a manually operated bolt action — reliable, accurate, but with a cycling time that assumed your target would politely hold still between shots. Cian had replaced the bolt with a custom unit machined from 4340 chrome-moly vanadium steel, nitrided to 70 Rockwell case hardness. The locking lugs — seven of them, not the standard two or three — engaged with a rotation of sixty degrees rather than the usual ninety, reducing lock time by a third. The bolt face had been trued to within 0.0002 inches of perpendicular to the bore axis.

He slid the bolt home, feeling the glass-smooth action that came from ten thousand cycles of hand-lapped fitting. No grit. No resistance. Just metal moving through metal with the kind of precision that made Swiss watchmakers envious.

**The barrel.** Twenty-nine inches of 416R stainless steel, cryogenically treated at minus three hundred degrees Fahrenheit to relieve residual stress in the crystalline structure, then hand-lapped with progressively finer compounds until the bore looked like polished chrome under magnification. The rifling was 1:13 twist, optimized for the 419-grain solid copper projectiles he'd be using — heavy for caliber, but the .408 CheyTac cartridge generated enough velocity to stabilize them at distance. The muzzle was threaded 3/4x24 for his suppressor.

He mounted the barrel to the receiver, torquing the barrel nut to exactly sixty-five foot-pounds — not sixty-four, not sixty-six, sixty-five — using a calibrated torque wrench that he'd had verified by the German national metrology institute because trusting Harbor Freight with a precision rifle was the ballistic equivalent of trusting a cat with a goldfish.

**The suppressor.** Thirty inches of Grade 5 titanium, fourteen baffles in a progressive-expansion configuration that reduced the report of a .408 CheyTac from "artillery piece" to "very angry door slam." The first four baffles were Inconel 718 — a nickel-chromium superalloy rated for continuous operation at 1,300 degrees Fahrenheit — because the initial blast pressure on a .408 round would liquefy conventional titanium baffles inside thirty rounds.

He threaded the suppressor onto the muzzle, checking alignment with a bore rod to ensure concentricity. A misaligned suppressor on a .408 didn't just reduce accuracy — it reduced suppressors. And sometimes the shooters attached to them.

**The stock.** Carbon fiber over an aluminum chassis, adjustable for length of pull, cheek height, and cant angle. The original M200's folding stock was designed for transport convenience. Cian's was designed for a man who had spent twenty-six centuries learning exactly how a rifle should sit against his shoulder. The butt pad was Sorbothane — the same polymer used in satellite launch vibration dampeners — because the felt recoil of a .408 CheyTac was approximately equivalent to being rear-ended by a resentful motorcycle.

**The trigger.** Two-stage Jewel, adjusted to a first-stage weight of twelve ounces and a second-stage break of six ounces. Clean. Crisp. The kind of trigger pull that felt like thinking about shooting and having the rifle agree that yes, now would be an excellent time.

"THE WEAPON PLEASETH THEE," Raphael observed.

"The weapon is a tool, Liaigh." Cian seated the trigger group and tightened the guard screws to thirty-two inch-pounds. "I don't get emotional about tools."

"THOU HAST NAMED IT."

"I name all my tools. It's organizational."

"THOU DIDST NOT NAME THY TOASTER."

"My toaster hasn't killed a Nephilim blood lord at 2,400 meters."

"...AN EQUITABLE DISTINCTION."

**The optics.** This was where Cian's money — considerable, after twenty-six centuries of compound interest — intersected with Brennan's technical wizardry. The scope body was a Nightforce ATACR 7-35x56, a seventy-ounce monument to American optical engineering that could resolve a playing card at a mile. But the internals had been comprehensively reworked.

The reticle was Tremor5 — a Christmas tree pattern of hashmarks and windage dots that allowed holdover corrections without dialing the turrets. The turrets themselves were thirty-four millimeter, positively locking, with a zero-stop mechanism and .25 MOA clicks that Cian could feel through shooting gloves.

But the party piece was the secondary overlay. Brennan had integrated a low-power thermal imaging sensor into the scope housing — FLIR Boson 640-core, twelve-micron pixel pitch — that superimposed thermal signatures onto the optical image in a ghostly amber wash. It wasn't as detailed as a dedicated thermal optic, but it didn't need to be. When your targets ran at ambient temperature and only generated thermal bloom during active regeneration, even a basic thermal overlay turned a sniper scope into a lie detector.

It told you whether what you'd just shot was actually *dying*, or merely pretending.

Cian mounted the scope in its Spuhr rings — one-piece aluminum, torqued to eighteen inch-pounds, lapped to the receiver rail with rouge compound before final installation. He ran a plumb line check, confirmed the reticle was level to within half a degree, and nodded.

"Range?"

"THOU DOST INTEND TO TEST THE WEAPON HERE?" There was something in Raphael's voice that might have been alarm.

"The basement range, Liaigh. Not the living room."

"THY CLARIFICATION IS APPRECIATED, IF SOMEWHAT OVERDUE."

## SCENE 4: THE AMMUNITION — TINTREACH ROUNDS

The rifle was an instrument. The ammunition was the music it would play.

Cian moved to the crystal growth station — a converted autoclave system that looked like someone had crossed a pressure cooker with a church organ. Eight cylindrical chambers, each holding a seed crystal suspended in a supersaturated solution of gallium orthophosphate, maintained at exactly 540 degrees Celsius and 15,000 PSI for three weeks straight.

He opened the first chamber with the delicacy of a man defusing a bomb, which was appropriate given that the contents were worth approximately four thousand dollars per crystal and had taken twenty-one days of uninterrupted growth to produce.

The crystal gleamed in the workshop light — a pale amber column, twelve millimeters in diameter, eighteen millimeters long, with the clean hexagonal geometry that indicated successful growth. He held it up, rotating it slowly. The internal structure was flawless — no twinning, no inclusions, no stress fractures from the thermal cycling.

And at the center, barely visible without magnification, the seed crystal: a speck of Mo Chrá's whetstone residue, consecrated metal that had been in physical contact with a Watcher-forged blade for twenty-six centuries.

He could feel it. Not with his fingers — with something deeper. A faint vibration, almost subliminal, like the echo of Mo Chrá's hum preserved in miniature. The crystal was alive with it.

Blessed material, grown into the heart of a piezoelectric generator.

"Beautiful," he murmured.

From her rack, Mo Chrá's hum carried something warm. These crystals were, in a sense, her children.

"Right. Assembly."

He set up the loading station — an RCBS Rock Chucker Supreme press bolted to a dedicated bench, with a Redding Competition Seating Die calibrated for the .408 CheyTac cartridge. The process was methodical, meditative, and required the kind of focus that left no room for extraneous thought.

**Step one: The case.** Lapua brass, .408 CheyTac, already sized, trimmed to 2.580 inches, neck-turned to 0.0145 wall thickness for uniform grip on the projectile. He'd weighed each case to within 0.1 grains, segregating them into lots by weight for consistency. These were the light-lot cases — 240.2 to 240.4 grains — which tended to produce marginally tighter extreme velocity spreads.

**Step two: The primer.** Federal 215M — large rifle magnum match, the "Gold Medal" variant that competitive benchrest shooters treated with the sort of reverence normally reserved for religious artifacts. He seated each primer with a hand priming tool, feeling for the distinct *click* that indicated the anvil was properly set. Too deep and the primer would be sluggish. Too shallow and the bolt wouldn't close. Cian's primers were seated 0.004 inches below flush. Every time. Without exception.

**Step three: The powder.** Hodgdon H50BMG — a ball powder designed specifically for the .50 BMG and its derivatives, with the burn rate characteristics to generate consistent velocities in the .408's cavernous case. He threw each charge on an RCBS ChargeMaster, verified it on an A&D FX-120i analytical balance, and trickled up or down to exactly 112.5 grains. The extreme spread on a ten-round lot was 0.2 grains. For context, a human hair weighed approximately 0.5 grains.

**Step four: The projectile.** This was where standard reloading ended and Cian's particular brand of applied theology began.

Each round comprised three nested components:

**Outer penetrator:** A turned-copper boat-tail projectile, 419 grains, with a secant ogive calculated to a G7 ballistic coefficient of 0.408. The copper was sourced from a Chilean mine whose product had unusually low sulfur content, which reduced jacket fouling in the bore. The base was hollow — not for expansion, but to house the crystal core. The cavity walls were 0.040 inches thick, reinforced with a steel insert ring to maintain structural integrity during launch acceleration.

**Dampening matrix:** A sleeve of viscoelastic polymer — essentially a custom-formulated silicone rubber — press-fitted into the projectile cavity before the crystal core was inserted. This was the engineering that turned the Tintreach concept from theory into function. During launch, the rifle's acceleration forces would compress the polymer rather than the crystal, absorbing the 30,000-G spike that would otherwise shatter the GaPO₄ before it left the barrel. On impact, the sudden deceleration compressed the crystal *through* the now-rigid polymer, generating the piezoelectric discharge.

The trick was the polymer's rate-dependent behavior. Slow loading — like the smooth push of a rifle's acceleration curve — allowed it to deform and absorb energy. Fast loading — like slamming into a Nephilim at 2,900 feet per second — caused it to glass-transition, becoming rigid and transmitting the impact force directly to the crystal.

Cian had tested fourteen polymer formulations before finding one that worked. The first thirteen were currently embedded in the backstop of his basement range, along with three destroyed barrels, two shattered scope lenses, and a chunk of workbench that he'd never adequately explained to Brennan.

**Crystal core:** The GaPO₄ cylinder, seated into the polymer matrix with a press fit of 0.001 inches interference. The crystal's orientation was critical — the piezoelectric axis had to align with the direction of impact, or the voltage generation would be attenuated. He'd designed a jig — machined from tool steel, keyed to the crystal's hexagonal geometry — that ensured consistent orientation to within two degrees.

He assembled the first round: crystal into polymer, polymer assembly into projectile cavity, steel retention ring crimped into place. The completed projectile looked entirely conventional from the outside. Boat-tail. Copper jacket. Slightly longer than standard, but nothing that would raise questions unless someone X-rayed it.

Cian weighed the completed round on the analytical balance. 857.3 grains. Within spec.

He loaded the first five rounds into a custom magazine — CNC-machined from 7075-T6 aluminum, single-stack, with a controlled-feed geometry that presented each round at exactly the same angle to the chamber mouth. Feed lip spacing was set at 0.415 inches — tight enough to prevent double-feeding, generous enough to avoid the dreaded nose-dive that sent bullets into the bottom of the feed ramp rather than the chamber.

Cian held up the loaded magazine. Five rounds. Each containing a blessed crystal capable of generating 47,000 volts on impact.

"THE LIGHTNING CONTAINETH WITHIN THE THUNDER," Raphael observed.

"That's almost poetic, Liaigh."

"THOU SHALT FIND THAT THE EMPYREAL HOST IS GIVEN TO OCCASIONAL POETRY."

"And modest, too."

"MODESTY IS A MORTAL VIRTUE. WE OPERATE UNDER NO SUCH CONSTRAINT."

Mo Chrá's hum somehow managed to convey agreement with Raphael's sentiment while simultaneously implying that the entire three-week crystal growth process could have been avoided if Cian had simply asked her to cut things more often. She was nothing if not consistent.

## SCENE 5: THE SUPPORT PLATFORMS

The CheyTac was for Lord Vârcolac. The precision instrument. The conversation opener.

But conversations sometimes got complicated. And when they did, Cian preferred to have several follow-up arguments ready to go.

**The battle rifle: Bás Beag.**

*Little Death.*

She'd started life as an FN SCAR 17S — a 7.62x51mm NATO battle rifle that the United States Special Operations Command had adopted because it combined the accuracy of a precision rifle with the reliability of a weapon that could be dragged through sand, mud, and the kind of environments where creatures that leaked acidic ichor tended to reside.

Cian had kept the operating system — FN's short-stroke gas piston design, where the piston contacted the bolt carrier for a brief, violent kiss before retracting, which kept the action cleaner than a traditional direct-impingement design. In sustained-fire scenarios against ghoul swarms, a direct-impingement rifle would be choking on carbon fouling by magazine three. The piston gun would still be cycling at magazine ten.

What he'd changed: everything else.

The barrel was sixteen inches — not the original's twenty — shortened for maneuverability in close quarters but fitted with a Surefire SOCOM762-RC2 suppressor that added another eight inches and dropped the decibel level from "permanent hearing damage" to "only temporary hearing damage." The barrel itself was chrome-lined and cold hammer-forged by FN's Herstal factory, then shipped to a small shop in Prescott, Arizona, where a retired Marine armorer who asked no questions and accepted payment in cash had lapped the bore and re-crowned the muzzle to 11-degree target specifications.

The trigger was a Geissele Super SCAR — a drop-in unit that replaced the SCAR's notoriously mediocre factory trigger with a clean 3.5-pound two-stage pull that broke like a glass rod. The difference between the factory trigger and the Geissele was the difference between steering with oven mitts and steering with racing gloves.

The optic was a Trijicon VCOG 1-8x28 — variable-power, ranging from a wide-field 1x for room clearing to 8x for engagements out to six hundred meters. The reticle was the segmented circle design, illuminated in red, with bullet-drop-compensation holdover marks calibrated to the Tintreach round's specific ballistic profile. Because when you were shooting custom piezoelectric ammunition at supernatural targets, using holdover marks calibrated for standard M80 ball was the kind of optimism that got people killed.

Twenty-round magazines. Cian had twelve of them, loaded and staged. That was 240 rounds of Tintreach. Enough to ruin approximately 240 individual moments of a Nephilim's day.

**The shotgun: An Béal Mór.**

*The Big Mouth.*

The Fostech Origin-12 was the answer to a question nobody in polite society had thought to ask: what if a shotgun could fire as fast as you could pull the trigger and feed from a drum magazine that held thirty rounds of twelve-gauge mayhem?

The Origin-12 operated on a gas-operated, rotating-bolt action that cycled faster than any pump gun and more reliably than most semi-automatic designs. Its party trick was the dual gas pistons — one on each side of the barrel — that provided symmetrical force on the bolt carrier, eliminating the carrier tilt that plagued single-piston designs and caused failures to feed when you needed them least.

Cian had left the internals mostly stock. The Origin-12 was, in his professional assessment, one of the few modern firearms that actually worked better than its marketing materials claimed. What he'd modified was the furniture — a Magpul SGA stock shortened by an inch, a Streamlight TLR-9 weapon light mounted at the twelve o'clock rail position, and an Aimpoint Micro T-2 red dot sight that provided the "point and click" simplicity required when your targets were closing at forty-three miles per hour and you had approximately three seconds to decide if you wanted to live.

The ammunition was where it got interesting.

Standard twelve-gauge shells — two and three-quarter inch, full power — loaded with Cian's custom flechette payload. Each shell contained twenty-three hardened steel darts, 1.75 inches long, weighing eight grains each. The flechettes were coated in colloidal silver and tumble-polished with blessed iron filings from a monastery forge in County Clare that had been in continuous operation since 1214.

The effect on ghoul tissue was approximately what you'd expect from twenty-three tiny silver-blessed javelins entering the body at 1,300 feet per second: catastrophic. Each dart created its own wound channel, its own zone of silver-disrupted necrotic binding, its own little contribution to the ghoul's sudden and irreversible career change from "terrifying undead predator" to "collapsing pile of formerly animated tissue."

Thirty shells per drum. Twenty-three flechettes per shell. That was 690 individual darts per magazine change.

Mo Chrá's hum conveyed something between *adequate* and *how many of those do you actually need when you have me?*

"As many as it takes," Cian replied without looking at her. "Your range is approximately three meters. Their closing speed gives me six seconds at fifty meters. I'd rather not depend entirely on your willingness to be thrown."

The sword's vibration acquired an offended quality. She did *not* like being thrown. She'd made this clear in 1453 during the siege of Constantinople, when Cian had hurled her through an Ottoman ghoul at forty paces and she'd spent the next three days refusing to shift forms in protest.

"I said I was sorry."

The hum suggested that "sorry" had an expiration date, and Cian's had run out somewhere around the sixteenth century.

**The sidearm: Tine Láimhe.**

*Hand Fire.*

A SIG Sauer P320-XTEN. 10mm Auto. Because when the rifle ran dry and the shotgun was empty and the sword was busy being dramatic on the other side of the room, a man needed something that fit in a holster and hit like a freight train.

The 10mm Auto cartridge was the Goldilocks round for supernatural work. Nine-millimeter was too light — adequate for human targets but insufficient to generate the tissue disruption needed against undead musculature. The .45 ACP was a gentleman's agreement in bullet form — wide, slow, and polite. The .50 AE from a Desert Eagle was an attention-getter, but the weapon itself was the size of a small child and about as concealable.

The 10mm split the difference. 200-grain hardcast lead at 1,250 feet per second generated 694 foot-pounds of muzzle energy — enough to disrupt ghoul motor function through sheer hydraulic shock, while remaining controllable enough for rapid follow-up shots. The P320 platform gave him a modular trigger group that he could swap between full-size and compact grip modules depending on whether he was wearing the exoskeleton's gauntlets or bare hands.

He'd fitted it with a Trijicon SRO red dot — the 5 MOA version, because handgun shooting at speed was not the time for precision dots that disappeared every time the slide cycled — and a Surefire X300U weapon light. The holster was a Safariland 6390RDS, Level I retention, mounted on the exoskeleton's right thigh plate with a custom MOLLE adapter.

The magazine held fifteen rounds. Cian carried four spares.

Seventy-five rounds of 10mm. His minimum comfort level for a sidearm.

"A MAN WHO CARRYETH SEVENTY-FIVE ROUNDS FOR A WEAPON OF LAST RESORT," Raphael observed, "DOTH HARBOR EITHER TREMENDOUS PESSIMISM OR COMMENDABLE FORESIGHT."

"The two are indistinguishable in my line of work."

## SCENE 6: THE FUARTÁN

The firearms were precision instruments. The Fuartán cryo-grenades were controlled tantrums.

Cian moved to the chemistry bench, where twelve cylindrical casings sat in a foam-lined tray like eggs in a carton — if the eggs were designed to flash-freeze everything within thirty meters and then keep it frozen for twenty seconds while the operator made editorial decisions about which frozen targets most urgently needed to stop existing.

Each casing was machined from 7075-T6 aluminum — the same aerospace alloy used in fighter jet structural components — anodized in matte black and exactly the diameter of a standard 40mm grenade. This was not coincidence. The Fuartán was designed to be fired from an M203 underbarrel launcher *or* thrown by hand, and achieving compatibility with both delivery methods had cost Cian two weeks of geometry calculations and one memorable afternoon when a prototype had detonated during the machining process and frozen his lathe chuck to minus eighty degrees Celsius.

The lathe had survived. Cian's coffee, which had been sitting on the adjacent bench, had become an ice sculpture of considerable artistic merit.

"THOU DOST CONTINUE THE TRADITION OF LEARNING THROUGH CATASTROPHE," Raphael had observed at the time.

"It's a *method*."

"IT IS A METHOD IN THE SAME SENSE THAT A HURRICANE IS A VENTILATION SYSTEM."

The internal architecture was triple-chambered:

**Primary chamber:** Eight grams of iron-thermite — ferric oxide and aluminum powder, mixed at a stoichiometric ratio of 8:3 by weight. Upon detonation, the thermite ignited at 2,500 degrees Celsius and provided the thermal energy to flash-vaporize the contents of the secondary chamber. A magnesium igniter strip — three millimeters wide, running the length of the chamber — served as the initiation train, fired by a standard electric detonator in the grenade's fuze assembly.

**Secondary chamber:** Twenty-two grams of solid carbon dioxide — dry ice — contained in a thin-walled stainless steel sleeve that maintained the CO₂ at atmospheric pressure until the thermite fired. Upon vaporization, the dry ice expanded to approximately 550 times its solid volume, generating an overpressure that ruptured the outer casing along pre-scored fracture lines and ejected the tertiary compound in a spherical dispersal pattern.

The physics were elegant in their brutality: the thermite's heat vaporized the CO₂, but the rapid expansion of the gas consumed that heat and *more*, pulling thermal energy from the surrounding environment through the Joule-Thomson effect. The result was a flash of superheated gas that immediately collapsed into a zone of intense cold — a thermal whiplash that dropped ambient temperature by sixty to eighty degrees Celsius within the thirty-meter dispersal radius.

**Tertiary compound:** A proprietary expanding polymer matrix — polyurethane base, loaded with microencapsulated ammonium nitrate crystals at 40% by weight. The polymer was ejected as an aerosol during the CO₂ expansion, adhering to every surface it contacted. Once deposited, the ammonium nitrate crystals dissolved in ambient moisture and initiated an endothermic reaction that continued absorbing heat for fifteen to twenty seconds, maintaining the cold zone well below the point where undead muscle tissue lost structural coherence.

Cian assembled the grenades with the careful precision of a man who understood that the difference between a weapon and a suicide device was approximately two millimeters of wall thickness and a proper understanding of thermodynamic equilibria.

He loaded four into an M320 grenade launcher module — single-shot, side-loading, attached to a Picatinny rail mount that would sit beneath the SCAR's barrel. The remaining eight went into a padded bandolier designed for manual deployment.

"Twelve Fuartáin," he said, surveying the completed inventory. "Twelve thirty-meter cold zones. If I place them correctly, I can lock down a four-hundred-meter perimeter."

"AND IF THOU DOST PLACE THEM INCORRECTLY?"

"Then I'll have a very cold fortress and a very annoyed angel."

"THY ASSESSMENT OF MY POTENTIAL RESPONSE IS WELL-CALIBRATED."

## SCENE 7: MO CHRÁ'S OPINION

The guns were built. The grenades were assembled. The ammunition was loaded, tested, and staged.

Which left one final piece of the arsenal to address.

Cian turned to the sword rack.

Mo Chrá hung in bastard-sword configuration, her blade gleaming with the dull blue-grey sheen of Cydonian ore, the impossible metal that had been forged by Enoch himself from materials that had been present at the Watcher council on Mount Hermon. Five thousand years of existence, and she still looked like she'd been finished yesterday. The acoustic resonance that maintained her molecular structure didn't care about human timescales.

"We need to talk about forms," Cian said.

The sword's hum dropped to a frequency that Cian had learned, over twenty-six centuries of intimate acquaintance, meant *I am already unhappy with the direction of this conversation*.

"The fortress will have tight corridors. Narrow stairwells. Low ceilings. A bastard sword won't clear them without catching masonry."

The hum didn't change. This was the stone wall of non-negotiation.

"I'm thinking gladius for the interior work. Twenty-inch blade, double-edge, broad enough for cutting power but short enough to work in confined—"

The hum became a *buzz*. An angry, affronted buzz that vibrated the rack screws and caused a nearby beaker to walk itself three inches across the bench.

"It's a Roman design! It conquered half the known world! There's nothing wrong with being a gladius!"

The buzz intensified. Mo Chrá's position on Roman weaponry had been firmly established in 48 BCE, when Cian had fought his way through a mob of Pompey's supporters outside the Senate house. She had been in gladius form for the occasion. She had *hated* it. Something about the proportions offended her at a fundamental level — "too fat, too short, no elegance" was the approximate translation of her harmonic complaint.

"Okay, not a gladius. Fine. Short sword? Irish style? Something like the claidheamh you were when we first met?"

The hum shifted — contemplative now, almost nostalgic. She was remembering. Six hundred years before Christ, when a young man named Cian had first picked up a blade that hummed with an intelligence older than civilization, and the blade had decided — with the instantaneous certainty that only an entity forged by the longest-lived man in biblical history could exhibit — that this particular mortal was *worthy*.

"THE BLADE CONSIDERETH THY PROPOSAL," Raphael supplied.

"She's being dramatic."

"SHE IS BEING *DELIBERATE*. THERE IS A DISTINCTION THAT THOU HAST FAILED TO APPRECIATE FOR TWENTY-SIX CENTURIES."

Mo Chrá's hum resolved into something decisive. The blade began to shift.

It was always hypnotic, watching her change form. The metal didn't melt or deform — it *rearranged*, the molecular structure flowing like liquid mercury under glass, the acoustic resonance within the Cydonian ore reorganizing the material at a speed that violated several laws of thermodynamics and at least one of Newton's suggestions about the conservation of matter.

The bastard sword compressed, the blade shortening, the cross-guard narrowing, the grip adapting. Twenty-six inches became twenty-two, then twenty, then settled at nineteen. The straight edges developed a slight curve — not enough to qualify as a full saber curve, but enough to optimize the geometry for draw cuts. The cross-guard flattened from a cruciform to a disc, Irish style, and the pommel resolved into a compact sphere of the same dull blue-grey metal.

She was beautiful. An Irish short sword from the late Iron Age, proportioned for close-quarters work with the kind of aesthetic that made modern bladesmiths weep into their forge aprons.

And she was *humming*. A warm, satisfied hum that said *this is what I should have been all along, why do you keep putting me in other shapes, you have no taste*.

"She's happy," Cian said.

"THE BLADE EXPRESSETH SATISFACTION WITH HER CURRENT CONFIGURATION. SHE ALSO WISHETH ME TO CONVEY—"

"That she could have done this three weeks ago and saved everyone the argument?"

"—THAT SHE ALLOWETH THEE TO BELIEVE THOU WERT IN CONTROL OF THE SELECTION PROCESS, OUT OF CONSIDERATION FOR THY FRAGILE MORTAL EGO."

"Generous."

Mo Chrá's hum radiated *I know*.

Cian lifted her from the rack, turning the blade in the light. The balance was superb — she'd shifted her mass distribution along with her geometry, placing the center of gravity exactly where he preferred it, two inches forward of the cross-guard. She knew his preferences better than he did.

He slid her into the dorsal sheath mount on the exoskeleton's back panel. The magnetic lock engaged with a soft *click*. She settled against his spine like she'd been born there — which, in a sense, she had.

"Right," he said. "Primary rifle, battle rifle, shotgun, sidearm, cryo-grenades, binary chlorine system, and one very opinionated sword."

He looked at the complete loadout, laid out on the workbench in precise order.

"THOU ART FORGETTING SOMETHING," Raphael said.

Cian frowned. He ran through the inventory mentally — ammunition, magazines, grenades, binary system canisters, weapon maintenance kit, first aid, comms equipment—

"Transportation," he said, realizing. "I need to get to the Carpathians."

"THOU DOST POSSESS A VEHICLE FOR SUCH PURPOSES."

"The *Ward Runner* is still in transit. Brennan's driving it over from Dublin — should arrive tomorrow." Cian moved to the garage bay door that connected the workshop to the adjacent building. "But I'm not taking the SUV to the target. Too conspicuous. The approach has to be silent."

He punched the access code — a twelve-digit sequence that changed daily, generated by an algorithm based on the date, the Schumann resonance measured at that safehouse's location, and the closing price of gold three days prior — and the reinforced door slid open.

The Shadow Hound sat in the bay, its matte-black frame absorbing the workshop light like a hole in reality. Two hundred and fifty kilograms of electric silence, designed to deliver one man and one sword to places that didn't want visitors.

The motorcycle's Vantablack finish matched the Stealth Variant exoskeleton — because when you were approaching a seven-century-old vampire's fortress at midnight, aesthetic coordination was the least of your concerns, but it didn't *hurt*. The dual electric motors were rated for 300 horsepower — silent, torque-heavy, capable of accelerating from zero to sixty in 2.8 seconds without producing a sound louder than a whispered prayer.

The Enochian micro-wards etched into the frame weren't decorative. They were the same ward pattern used on the exoskeleton — active suppression of spiritual detection, the supernatural equivalent of stealth coating. To any demon, ghoul, or supernaturally enhanced sentry, the Shadow Hound would register as a vague sense of *nothing* — the cognitive equivalent of trying to focus on a word that was on the tip of your tongue.

Cian ran through the pre-mission checklist. Motor response: nominal. Battery: full charge, 400-kilometer range. Holy oil seals on the tires: intact. Sonic emitter: functional. MOLLE attachment points for the rifle cases: secure.

"THE SHADOW HOUND IS PREPARED," Raphael confirmed.

"Then so am I."

The garage door opened. The night waited.

"Let's go hunting."
