# CHAPTER 4: THE DATA TRAIL

## I. PREPARATION

**2027 — Austin, Texas**
**McNeeve Industries Safehouse, Sub-Level 2**
**0147 Hours**

The suit stood in its alcove like a shadow waiting to be worn.

Cian circled it slowly, running pre-flight checks the way a pilot circles an aircraft. Except this aircraft was designed to make him invisible to everything from thermal imaging to the naked eye, and its flight plan involved walking through walls of corporate security like they didn't exist.

"Stealth configuration," he said aloud. "Full spectrum suppression."

The Vantablack coating drank the light—99.965% of every photon that touched it, absorbed into a void so absolute that the suit didn't look like an object at all. It looked like a hole in reality. The internalized heat dampeners were already cycling, drawing body heat into ceramic reservoirs that would release it slowly over the next six hours, keeping his thermal signature indistinguishable from ambient temperature.

Mo Chrá hummed from the workbench—a low, suspicious vibration.

"Yes, I know you hate stealth ops."

The hum acquired a distinctly *petulant* quality.

"You'll be small. You'll be quiet. You'll stay sheathed unless absolutely necessary."

Mo Chrá's vibration shifted to something that, in a creature capable of facial expressions, would have been an eye-roll of epic proportions.

"*A chuisle*, this isn't a combat mission. It's intelligence gathering. The humans in that building are *innocent*. They process transactions for a company they think is legitimate. They have families. Children."

The sword's hum softened—slightly.

"Ephesians 6:12. We wrestle not against flesh and blood."

A reluctant harmonic. Acknowledgment without enthusiasm.

"Thank you for your *overwhelming* support."

Mo Chrá's vibration acquired a warm, amused quality. *You're welcome.*

Cian began the gear-up sequence.

First, the base layer—silver-thread mesh woven into tactical compression fabric. It sat against his skin like a second epidermis, the metal content high enough to cause a siren's pheromones to burn off before they could reach his lungs, low enough that he could still move like water through a pipe. The consecrated fibers had been blessed by a Coptic priest in Alexandria, an Orthodox metropolitan in Moscow, and a Lakota medicine woman in South Dakota. Belt and suspenders. Redundancy. Twenty-six centuries of experience taught you that supernatural threats didn't respect denominational boundaries.

Second, the interface layer. Bio-monitors that tracked heart rate, respiration, blood oxygen, stress hormones. Micro-pumps that could deliver stimulants or sedatives directly into his bloodstream if the situation demanded. Neural haptic relays that would translate the suit's sensor data into subtle pressure patterns across his skin—a second language of tactical awareness that left his conscious mind free for the threats sensors couldn't detect.

Third, the armor itself.

The Vantablack plates locked into place with the soft precision of components machined to micron tolerances. Each piece carried micro-etched Enochian wards—invisible at anything beyond arm's length, but potent enough to deflect minor demonic influence and corruption attempts. The plating was light—carbon-fiber composite reinforced with a ceramic matrix—but it would stop anything short of armor-piercing rounds. And even those would need to be very precisely placed.

The helmet came last.

Wide-angle respirator face shield, sealed against chemical and biological agents. The amber HUD initialized with a cascade of data: ambient temperature, humidity, barometric pressure, electromagnetic interference levels. The spiritual signature overlay activated—a proprietary addition that existed in no military handbook anywhere in the world, but had saved Cian's life more times than he could count.

**HUD: SPIRITUAL SCAN — AMBIENT NEGATIVE. NO DEMONIC PRESENCE DETECTED. NO NEPHILIM SIGNATURES. NO POSSESSION MARKERS.**

The safehouse basement registered as spiritually clean. Good. That meant his destination's readings would be accurate.

Finally, the sword.

Mo Chrá's hum shifted to something almost *pleading* as Cian approached.

"Dagger form."

A vibration of absolute *betrayal*.

"I said dagger form."

The sword resisted. Mo Chrá could be persuaded, but she wasn't a pushover. Twenty-six centuries of partnership had established a negotiation dynamic that would have made labor union representatives weep with envy.

"Fine. *Tanto*. You can be a Japanese-style dagger. More elegant than a letter opener. Acceptable?"

The hum was reluctant, but the blade began to shimmer. Metal flowing like mercury, reshaping itself from the bastard sword's comfortable length to the compact eight-inch blade of a tantō. The same mass, compressed into a smaller form—which made it denser, heavier relative to its size, and extraordinarily effective as a thrown weapon if the situation degraded that badly.

Cian lifted the transformed blade and slid it into the dorsal sheath. The magnetic lock engaged with a soft click. Mo Chrá's hum settled into resigned acceptance, muffled somewhat by the sheath's acoustic dampening.

"You survived the Nika Riots as a stiletto. You tracked Nephilim through the court of Kublai Khan as an eating knife. You can handle one night as a tantō."

No response. The sword was sulking.

"THOU DOST HANDLE HER WELL."

Cian didn't jump—but only because twenty-six centuries had burned that reflex out of him.

"Liaigh. Intel briefing."

"THE TARGET FACILITY IS A NEXUS WITHIN THE SYNAGOGUE'S FINANCIAL NETWORK. SEVENTEEN FLOORS ABOVE GROUND, THREE BELOW. THE SERVERS THOU DOST SEEK ARE LOCATED ON SUB-LEVEL TWO—BEHIND A FARADAY CAGE, AIR-GAPPED FROM ALL EXTERNAL NETWORKS."

"Human security?"

"TWENTY-THREE PERSONNEL ON THE NIGHT SHIFT. FOUR ROVING PATROLS, FIFTEEN-MINUTE ROTATION. SEVEN STATIONARY POSTS. TWELVE ADMINISTRATIVE STAFF WORKING LATE, CONCENTRATED ON FLOORS NINE THROUGH TWELVE."

"Supernatural presence?"

A pause. An empyreal pause, which meant Liaigh was consulting something beyond Cian's perception.

"ABSENT. THE FACILITY BEARETH NO DEMONIC WARDS, NO NEPHILIM BLOODLINE CARRIERS AMONG THE CURRENT STAFF, NO SPIRITUAL CONTAMINATION OF ANY KIND. IT IS, BY ALL MEASURES, AN ORDINARY CORPORATE FACILITY."

"That's what makes it dangerous."

"ELUCIDATE."

"The Harlot's oldest trick. Use human infrastructure, human personnel, human systems. No supernatural fingerprints. Nothing for people like me to detect. The evil is in the *data*, not the building."

"THY ANALYSIS IS ACCURATE."

"That's why I have to do this the hard way." Cian checked the dorsal sheath one final time. "Physical infiltration. Air-gapped systems. No remote access, no digital intrusion, no satellites or drones. Just me, in that building, copying those drives."

"THE RISK IS CONSIDERABLE."

"The risk is *calculated*." Cian pulled up the facility schematics on his HUD—he'd spent three weeks memorizing them, but a final review never hurt. "Entry through the HVAC shaft on the roof. Down the maintenance ladder to Sub-Level 2. Bypass the biometrics with the hardware I built. Mirror the drives. Egress through the same route. Total time inside: fourteen minutes."

"AND IF THOU ART DETECTED?"

"Then I improvise."

"THAT PHRASE HATH PRECEDED CONSIDERABLE DESTRUCTION ACROSS THE SPAN OF THY TENURE."

"Improvisation is a skill. I'm very skilled."

"THOU ART ALSO VERY CONFIDENT."

"Confidence and competence. They complement each other."

From the dorsal sheath, Mo Chrá offered a vibration that somehow conveyed *here we go again*.

---

*He remembered the first time he'd worn armor for infiltration.*

*Constantinople. 532 CE. The Nika Riots burning around him, smoke rising from the Hippodrome, the city tearing itself apart over chariot racing factions and centuries of accumulated grievance.*

*The armor had been bronze and leather, borrowed from a dead Varangian guardsman. The mission had been similar—infiltrate the imperial palace, locate three shapeshifters who were using the chaos to position themselves near the Emperor, eliminate them before they could assassinate Justinian and plunge the Byzantine Empire into civil war.*

*He'd been younger then. More impulsive. Less patient.*

*Mo Chrá had been the length of his forearm, disguised as a guardsman's sidearm. She'd hated being small even then. The hum of complaint had nearly given him away twice—once in the servants' corridor, once in the antechamber outside the Empress's quarters.*

*But he'd succeeded. Three shapeshifters, dead in their assumed forms, their bodies dissolving into the ichor they truly were. Justinian had lived. The Empire had endured.*

*And Cian had learned a lesson he'd never forgotten: the most dangerous missions weren't the ones where everything went wrong. They were the ones where everything went *right*—until the single moment when they didn't.*

---

Fourteen centuries later, the armor was carbon fiber and Vantablack.

The infiltration techniques were satellite-mapped and sensor-assisted.

Mo Chrá was the size of a letter opener.

Some things changed.

The mission never did.

## II. THE APPROACH

**FinSecure Holdings Corporate Campus**
**15847 Research Boulevard, Austin, TX**
**0217 Hours**

The corporate campus sprawled across forty acres of manicured Texas landscape—three buildings arranged around a central courtyard, connected by covered walkways and surrounded by the kind of thoughtful landscaping that said *we spent more on ornamental shrubbery than most companies spend on employee salaries*.

FinSecure Holdings. On paper: a financial technology company specializing in secure transaction processing for high-net-worth clients. Annual revenue: $2.3 billion. Employees: 847. Clients: undisclosed, but the SEC filings suggested connections to banks on four continents.

In reality: a node in a network that had been moving money for the Synagogue of Satan since the Temple fell.

The Harlot's fingerprints were everywhere if you knew how to look. The corporate structure was a maze of shell companies and holding entities that traced back through Luxembourg, the Caymans, and Singapore to a trust established in 1923—which itself drew on assets from a partnership formed in 1847, which succeeded a concern founded in 1689, which emerged from financial instruments drafted during the final years of the Medici banking empire.

Four centuries of continuous operation. Four centuries of moving wealth for entities that never appeared in any public record. Four centuries of enabling operations that left bodies in their wake.

The Synagogue played the long game. And FinSecure was one of its oldest surviving pieces on the board.

Cian moved through the darkness like smoke through an open window.

The Stealth Variant's thermal dampeners had brought his signature down to background levels—indistinguishable from the ambient temperature of the Texas night. The Vantablack coating absorbed what little starlight reached him, turning his silhouette into an absence rather than a presence. The low-profile plating hugged his body without the angular bulk that attracted attention in peripheral vision.

He was carrying minimal equipment—nothing that would rattle, nothing that would catch the light, nothing that would leave traces. The intrusion hardware was built into the suit's left forearm: a compact device that could interface with any security system built in the last forty years. The data capture unit was at his lower back: solid-state drives with enough capacity to mirror an entire corporate server infrastructure.

And Mo Chrá, of course. Small. Quiet. *Sulking*.

The HUD painted the world in tactical geometry.

**CAMERA ALPHA-7: CONE OF VISION DISPLAYED**
**PATROL UNIT BRAVO-2: CURRENT POSITION, ESTIMATED ROUTE, TIME TO INTERSECTION**
**THERMAL ANOMALY: HVAC EXHAUST VENT, NORTHWEST CORNER, BUILDING A**

The approach route he'd mapped three weeks ago still held. A blind spot between Alpha-7 and Alpha-9, created by an architectural overhang that the security consultants had apparently failed to notice. Eighteen seconds of complete invisibility, if he moved at exactly the right pace.

Seventeen.

Sixteen.

*Fifteen—*

A security guard stepped out of the side entrance, directly into Cian's path.

Time slowed.

The guard was young—mid-twenties, probably fresh out of community college, working nights while he figured out what to do with his life. He was holding a cigarette that he hadn't lit yet and patting his pockets for a lighter he'd apparently forgotten.

He was also standing exactly where Cian needed to pass in the next three seconds or lose the window between patrol rotations.

The HUD screamed warnings:

**CONTACT IMMINENT**
**EVASION OPTIONS: LIMITED**
**RECOMMENDED ACTION: ABORT/REPOSITION**

Cian didn't abort.

He dropped.

Not dramatically—there was no time for drama. Just a controlled collapse, body flowing from upright motion to prone concealment in a movement so practiced it was nearly automatic. The Vantablack coating pressed against the ornamental shrubbery, and Cian became part of the landscaping.

The guard lit his cigarette. Took a drag. Exhaled.

Cian was three feet away.

The man's boot was eighteen inches from Cian's head.

He could hear the guard's breathing. Smell the cheap tobacco. See the wedding ring on his left hand—a reminder that this was a person, not an obstacle, someone with a life that had nothing to do with the ancient evil his employer served.

The guard finished his cigarette. Stubbed it out on the building's wall. Flicked the butt into the shrubbery.

It landed on Cian's back.

The ember was still hot.

The Vantablack coating didn't care—it had been designed to resist temperatures far higher than a cigarette cherry. But Cian felt the heat through the neural haptic interface, and his body wanted to twitch in response.

He didn't twitch.

Twenty-six centuries of discipline kept him perfectly, absolutely still.

The guard yawned, scratched his hip, and walked back inside.

Cian counted to ten. Then he rose from the shrubbery like an oil slick gaining sentience, brushed the cigarette butt away with surgical precision, and resumed his approach.

From the dorsal sheath, Mo Chrá's vibration carried a distinct quality of *I told you infiltration was a bad idea*.

"Not now," Cian sub-vocalized. The throat mic in his suit picked up the whisper and transmitted it to his own ears only—no external sound at all.

Mo Chrá's hum shifted to something that, in a human, would have been stifled laughter.

---

The roof access was exactly where the schematics said it would be.

HVAC maintenance shaft—a vertical tube three feet in diameter, designed to allow technicians access to the air handling equipment without disrupting operations. It was secured by a grated cover locked with a standard commercial padlock.

Cian had it open in four seconds.

The lock-picking tools built into his gloves were products of centuries of refinement. He'd started with bronze picks in ancient Persia, graduated to iron in medieval Europe, adapted to precision steel during the Industrial Revolution, and now used tungsten-carbide implements with feedback sensors that told him exactly when each pin aligned. Breaking into places had been part of his job description since before most modern nations existed.

This lock wasn't even a challenge.

He slid through the shaft's opening headfirst, bracing against the sides to control his descent. The internal surfaces were aluminum—smooth, quiet, thermally neutral. His suit's ambient temperature matched it perfectly. To any sensor watching, the shaft contained exactly what it was supposed to contain: air.

Twenty feet down. Fifty feet. One hundred.

The maintenance ladder was exactly where the blueprints indicated.

Sub-Level 2.

**HUD: PROXIMITY SCAN — THREE PERSONNEL WITHIN 50 METERS**
**PATROL ROUTE BRAVO-4: CURRENTLY FLOOR ABOVE**
**SERVER ROOM ACCESS: 47 METERS NORTHEAST**

Cian emerged from the shaft into a maintenance corridor—the kind of space that existed in every commercial building but that nobody ever thought about. Painted concrete walls. Exposed conduit. The hum of HVAC equipment providing continuous ambient sound that covered any incidental noise he might make.

He moved.

## III. THE BREACH

**FinSecure Holdings, Sub-Level 2**
**0231 Hours**

The server room was behind a door that didn't exist.

That was literally true. The floor plan filed with the Austin Building Department showed this corridor terminating in a janitorial closet. The actual corridor continued another sixty feet, through a security checkpoint that no city inspector had ever seen.

Cian stood at the edge of visibility—just beyond the checkpoint's camera range—and studied the obstacle.

**HUD: ANALYZING SECURITY INFRASTRUCTURE**

Biometric scanner: fingerprint plus retinal. Voice recognition system with phrase-key input. RFID proximity detector. Pressure sensors in the floor. Camera with facial recognition capability.

Five layers of security.

Any three of which would have been sufficient for a legitimate corporate installation.

All five combined with hardware that was *slightly* too advanced, *slightly* too integrated, *slightly* too... old.

Cian recognized the underlying architecture. It wasn't modern at all. Strip away the digital interfaces and you found a design philosophy that originated in the vaults of Venetian banking houses, refined through the private security of East India Company installations, evolved through the clandestine infrastructure of nineteeth-century European finance.

The Synagogue built security the way they built everything else—by layering centuries of accumulated knowledge into systems that looked modern but thought *ancient*.

He was going to enjoy this.

"THOU HAST THAT EXPRESSION UPON THY COUNTENANCE," Liaigh observed.

"What expression?"

"THE ONE THAT PRECEDETH ACTIVITIES THOU SHALT LATER DESCRIBE AS 'IMPROVISATION.'"

"I prefer to call it adaptive operational methodology."

"TITLES DO NOT ALTER SUBSTANCE."

"Semantics do, actually. That's what makes them fun."

Cian extended the intrusion hardware from his left forearm. A thin cable with a contact probe at the end—designed to interface with security systems at the hardware level, bypassing software protections entirely.

Step one: the camera.

The probe touched the conduit feeding the security camera. Cian's HUD displayed the system architecture—standard IP-based surveillance, feeding to monitors in the security office three floors up.

**INJECTING LOOP: 60-SECOND CAPTURE**
**CURRENT FOOTAGE REPLACING LIVE FEED**
**SECURITY OFFICE MONITORING TEAM: UNAWARE**

The camera now showed an empty corridor. An empty corridor it would continue to show for as long as Cian needed.

Step two: the pressure sensors.

These were trickier. They monitored weight distribution across the floor, looking for signatures that didn't match authorized personnel. Step wrong, and alarms would trigger before he reached the door.

But they were also connected to the same conduit network as the camera.

**INJECTING CALIBRATION OFFSET**
**PRESSURE BASELINE: ADJUSTED**
**FLOOR NOW READING: NORMAL AMBIENT**

The sensors would register nothing. A ghost could walk this corridor and leave no trace.

Step three: the biometric gauntlet.

Fingerprint. Retinal. Voice. Three biological markers that Cian couldn't fake—not with any technology he was carrying.

But the system didn't *require* those markers. It required *signals* that matched authorized templates. And signals could be replicated.

Cian had spent considerable resources acquiring the biometric profiles of FinSecure's senior security personnel. Not their actual fingers or eyes—that would be crude, and more importantly, it would be *noticed*. Just the digital representations of how their biometric data was stored and transmitted.

**LOADING CAPTURED PROFILE: DR. MARTIN WELLS, CISO**
**TRANSMITTING FINGERPRINT SIGNATURE**
**TRANSMITTING RETINAL PATTERN**
**GENERATING VOICE SYNTHESIS: "WINTER SUNRISE PROTOCOL"**

A speaker in his suit's collar emitted the passphrase in a perfect reproduction of Dr. Wells's voice—a 78-year-old cybersecurity expert who was currently asleep in his Westlake home, dreaming of a retirement he would never quite reach.

The door clicked.

**ACCESS GRANTED**

Cian stepped through.

---

The server room was cold.

That was expected—the machines generated enormous heat, and they required constant cooling to operate. What wasn't expected was the aesthetic.

The room looked *old*.

Not functionally old—the servers themselves were cutting-edge, blade installations that represented the absolute state of the art in computational infrastructure. But the architecture surrounding them carried a different DNA entirely.

The floor was marble. White and gray, polished to a mirror finish. The walls were lined with mahogany paneling—warm wood that had no business in a server facility. The lighting came from brass fixtures that looked like they belonged in a Victorian gentleman's club.

In the center of the room, surrounded by humming servers, someone had placed a desk.

It was an antique. Nineteenth century. The kind of desk a Rothschild might have used to sign documents that moved empires.

Cian understood immediately.

This wasn't just a server room. It was a *statement*. A declaration of continuity. A reminder that the organization using these servers had been moving money and power since long before electricity existed, and would continue doing so long after the current technological paradigm was forgotten.

The Synagogue liked its symbols.

He ignored the desk. The servers were what mattered.

The air-gapped system was exactly where his intelligence indicated—a separate rack, physically isolated from the primary infrastructure, connected to nothing but its own power supply. No network cables. No wireless capability. The only way to access its data was to physically plug in.

Cian crossed the marble floor, his suit's soft-soled boots making no sound against the stone. The HUD displayed the estimated time for the data transfer:

**ESTIMATED DRIVE CAPACITY: 4.7 TB**
**TRANSFER SPEED: HARDWARE-LIMITED**
**ESTIMATED COMPLETION TIME: 4 MINUTES 23 SECONDS**

Four and a half minutes.

He could hold position for four and a half minutes.

The data capture unit extended from his lower back—a compact interface device with connection cables rated for every major data format. It found the server's maintenance port, made connection, and began copying.

**TRANSFER IN PROGRESS: 0.3%**

Cian settled into stillness.

---

Waiting was the hardest part of any operation.

The sword-bond kept him physically capable of perpetual motion—no fatigue, no wavering attention, no biological need for rest. But the mind still wandered. Still drifted. Still found patterns in silence that reminded him of missions past.

**TRANSFER IN PROGRESS: 7.2%**

The servers hummed. Climate control systems cycled. Somewhere in the building, pipes carried water and conduit carried data and circuits carried decisions that would affect lives on four continents.

And Cian mac Morna stood invisible in the center of it all, an ancient predator in a modern jungle, waiting for a download bar to complete.

---

*They had been half right.*

*The berserkers that made landfall weren't ordinary raiders. Something rode behind their eyes—entities that whispered tactics into their ears, that fed on the bloodlust, that used the violence as a gateway into the material world.*

*Cian had killed fourteen of them before the monks finished their morning prayers. Mo Chrá had sung with particular fury that day—she despised entities that corrupted warriors, turned courage into cruelty, twisted the battle-joy into something dark.*

*The monastery had burned anyway. Some things couldn't be prevented—only mitigated.*

*But the possessed berserkers had died, and the entities that rode them had been cast back to whatever pit had vomited them forth, and the pattern of demonic infiltration through Norse raider culture had been disrupted for another generation.*

*Small victories. They were all he ever got.*

**TRANSFER IN PROGRESS: 23.8%**

*He'd killed all three in a single night—disguised as wounded soldier, as Turkish defender, as a Franklin knight who'd gotten separated from his unit. Three different covers to reach three different targets.*

*And in a clearing in the Carpathian forest, a blood cult had gathered around a bonfire, preparing to sacrifice three prisoners to powers they didn't understand.*

*Cian had tracked them for three weeks. Crossed half of plague-ravaged Europe. Killed six of the cult's outriders before reaching the clearing.*

*He'd saved the family. Killed every cultist who stood between him and the prisoners.*

*Lord Vârcolac.*

*Seven centuries ago.*

*Cian had been watching from the crowd—not for reformers or Catholics, but for the entities that always gathered when history's currents shifted.*

*He'd seen Naamah herself. She'd been wearing a merchant's wife's face—middle-aged, respectable, unremarkable. Standing near the back of the crowd, watching Luther speak with an expression of *amusement*.

*Their eyes had met across the square.*

*She'd smiled.*

*One cut.*

The capture unit chirped softly in his ear.

Four minutes and twenty-three seconds, exactly as estimated.

Cian disconnected. The cable retracted. He turned toward the exit.

And Mo Chrá *screamed*.

## IV. THE DISCOVERY

The sound wasn't audible—not to anyone without the sword-bond's intimate connection. But to Cian, it was an alarm klaxon, a warning siren, a cry of *DANGER* that bypassed conscious thought and went straight to the survival instincts.

*Not danger in front of you. Danger in what you're missing.*

He froze. Hand on the server rack. Heart rate elevated despite twenty-six centuries of practice at staying calm.

"What is it?"

Mo Chrá's hum modulated—complex, layered, communicating in frequencies below human language. The meaning was clear:

*Recognition. This place. Something here that I've encountered before.*

"Something supernatural? The HUD isn't detecting—"

*Not supernatural. Memory. Acoustic memory. The same resonance pattern I felt—*

And then Cian understood.

The sword wasn't detecting a threat. She was detecting *history*. The residue of something that had passed through this place—someone, or something, that Mo Chrá had encountered before.

The harmonic she was producing was the same one she'd made in the Carpathians.

Seven centuries ago.

Following the trail.

Cian abandoned his egress plan, drawn by the sword's insistent humming toward the far end of the server room. Past the antique desk. Past the rows of humming servers. To a door that the floor plans hadn't shown—paneled in the same mahogany as the walls, almost invisible unless you were looking for it.

The handle turned easily.

Beyond was a small office—or what might politely be called an office. In reality, it was closer to a shrine.

Leather chairs. A second desk—smaller, more intimate, arranged facing a wall covered in photographs. Crystal frames. Expensive lighting. The kind of space where important people reviewed important information in perfect privacy.

And on the wall, directly facing the desk, a gallery of surveillance photographs.

Cian's blood went cold.

*Emma McNeeve. Age 8. Playing in her backyard.*

*Emma McNeeve. Age 6. At a birthday party.*

*Emma McNeeve. Age 10. Walking to school.*

*Sarah McNeeve—Emma's mother—at the grocery store. At the gym. Picking Emma up from soccer practice.*

*Brennan McNeeve. At the McNeeve Industries campus. At his daughter's dance recital. Standing on the porch of his family home.*

Dozens of photographs. Years of surveillance. All focused on a single family.

Mo Chrá's hum had dropped to something low and *dangerous*. The sword remembered.

Beneath the photographs, on the desk, a folder.

Not digital. Paper. Physical records—the kind that couldn't be hacked, couldn't be remotely accessed, couldn't be traced through any electronic system.

Old habits for old evil.

The label read: **PROJECT NIAMH**.

Cian opened it.

---

The first page was a ledger. Transactions dating back centuries—payments made in commodities rather than currency. Gold. Land. Favors. The kind of wealth that transcended national borders and temporal boundaries.

All flowing toward a single purpose: *acquisition of bloodline assets*.

The second page was a genealogy. A family tree extending back thirty generations—from the present day, through Irish immigrants in the nineteenth century, through English landholders in the sixteenth, through Norman knights in the eleventh, through Saxon farmers in the ninth, all the way to a root that Cian recognized immediately.

*Niamh.*

The sister he'd loved. The sister he'd lost. The sister whose descendants he'd been protecting—in secret, from the shadows, without their knowledge or consent—for twenty-six centuries.

The genealogy was annotated with dates. Acquisitions. Attempts. Failures.

*1347 CE: Carpathian ritual interrupted. Target rescued. Lord Vârcolac: STATUS PENDING.*

*1588 CE: Spanish convoy intercepted. Target eliminated. Bloodline continues through secondary branch.*

*1789 CE: Revolutionary purge. Three targets eliminated. Bloodline continues through émigré population.*

*1942 CE: Axis occupation. Target protected by unknown operative. Vatican intervention suspected.*

*2024 CE: Bloodline concentrated in single nuclear family. Acquisition priority: MAXIMUM.*

And at the bottom of the page, in a different hand—older ink, more elaborate script:

*The debt is unpaid. The blood price is owed. What was denied in Tara shall be claimed in full.*

Signed: **Lord Vârcolac**

---

The third page was an intelligence summary.

Target: Emma McNeeve, daughter of Sarah and Brennan McNeeve.

Location: Austin, Texas.

Acquisition method: *Shedim contract (fulfilled). Summoning ritual (completed). Attack on family residence (successful). TARGET STATUS: Protected by unknown operative.*

**0248 Hours**

The exit was supposed to be simple.

It *had* been simple.

Until the guard with the suspicious bladder decided this exact moment was the perfect time for a bathroom break.

Cian was halfway up the maintenance shaft when he heard it: footsteps in the corridor below. Not the steady rhythm of a patrol—irregular, hurried, the gait of a man who'd had too much coffee and not enough foresight.

The guard stopped directly beneath the shaft opening.

There was a pop. A hiss. The unmistakable sound of someone urinating into a corner while muttering excuses to himself.

"...told maintenance three weeks ago, three weeks, and do they fix the bathroom sink? Do they? No, they're too busy with their 'priority work orders' and..."

The muttering continued. The stream continued.

Cian was suspended in the maintenance shaft, fifteen feet above the guard's head, holding himself in place through pure muscle tension and the grace of God.

Mo Chrá's vibration carried a quality of barely-contained hysterical laughter.

The guard finished. Zipped. Sighed.

And then—*then*—the man tilted his head back and looked *directly up the shaft*.

Cian stopped breathing.

The Vantablack coating was doing its job—he knew that rationally. At this distance, in this lighting, he should be indistinguishable from the shaft's dark interior. The thermal dampeners were still functional. There was no sound, no scent, nothing for the guard's senses to detect.

But the man was *looking right at him*.

Two heartbeats. Three. Four.

"Huh," the guard said. "Could've sworn I saw..."

He shook his head. Muttered something about too much caffeine. Walked away.

Cian waited another sixty seconds before resuming his ascent.

Mo Chrá's vibration had escalated from barely-contained laughter to what could only be described as *losing it entirely*.

"One word," Cian murmured under his breath. "You say one word about this to anyone—Mo Chrá, Liaigh, anyone—and I swear by the eternal throne I will have you reshaped into cutlery."

Mo Chrá's hum dropped to a low, innocent frequency. *Who, me? I would never.*

"You absolutely would."

The sword's vibration shifted to something warm and amused. *Yes. Yes, I would.*

---

The roof access was clear. The campus was dark. The night had maintained its integrity.

Cian paused at the edge of the building, looking out across the forty acres of manicured landscaping, the three connected buildings, the covered walkways, the security checkpoints.

Seventeen floors above ground. Three below. Twenty-three night shift personnel. Seven security stations.

And he'd walked through all of it like smoke through an open window.

Behind him, FinSecure Holdings continued its ordinary operations. The employees would arrive at 8 AM. They would drink coffee. They would process transactions for a network whose roots extended four thousand years into the past.

They would never know they worked for the Synagogue of Satan.

And they would never know that a man older than their civilization had just walked through their building like a ghost, copying their secrets, reading their files, touching nothing and leaving no trace.

Cian vaulted from the roof, caught the ornamental tree that had been positioned *just so* for exactly this purpose (the landscaper three weeks ago had been very cooperative about the "windbreak" recommendations from the anonymous consultant), and descended to ground level without breaking stride.

The darkness swallowed him.

The mission was complete.
