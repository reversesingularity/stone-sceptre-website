## Dramatis Personae

*Characters who appear or are named in Book One, listed in order of first appearance. Roles reflect what the reader knows by the final page.*

### THE PROTAGONISTS

**Cian mac Morna** — *The Guardian, The Undying Hunter*
A Celtic knight of the Craobh Ruadh (Red Branch), born circa 611 BCE, commissioned in 586 BCE by the prophet Jeremiah and kept alive for 2,636 years by Methuselah's Sword. The last of the bloodline of Machir ben Manasseh — the Israelite giant-slayer — he has spent two and a half millennia hunting the scattered offspring of the fallen Watchers. Book One opens when the sword delivers the Cydonia Revelation to him in Austin, Texas, and ends with him departing for Antarctica at the head of a five-person team. His prophesied role as Guardian of the Two Witnesses is seeded in private — Cian himself does not yet know it.

**Mo Chrá** — *The Sword, "Pulse of My Heart"*
Methuselah's Sword, forged by the patriarch Enoch from obsidian and iron ore retrieved from the Watcher council complex on Mars. A sentient acoustic weapon communicating in tonal patterns — pitch as directional data, key signature as emotional state, volume as urgency. Inert during Cian's sleeping hours; opinionated during his waking ones. Carries acoustic memory of events she witnessed as raw ore at the Cydonian council. Named by Cian in 532 CE during the Nika Riots in Constantinople. A character in her own right, not merely an artefact.

**Liaigh** — *"The Healer"; Cian's Guardian Angel*
Communicates through the Empyreal Register: archaic, formal, capitalised, and frequently exasperating to a man who has been arguing with the same voice for twenty-six centuries. His true identity is never confirmed in Book One. Observant readers will note that Mo Chrá calls him something different in private — and that no being of merely ordinary angelic rank could have contained what she and Liaigh together contained in Chapter Thirteen.

**Brennan McNeeve** *(Brennan mac Niamh)* — *The Engineer, Cian's Nephew*
CEO of McNeeve Space Systems and, as revealed in Chapter Three, Cian's blood nephew — a living descendant of Niamh, the five-year-old sister Cian rescued in 586 BCE. The man who sent five rovers to Mars and then destroyed his own company to keep the data out of the wrong hands. His Asperger's-spectrum precision and aerospace genius make him the team's architect. By the final chapter, he has given up everything built across twelve years. He considers it worth it.

**Miriam Elizabeth Ashford** *(née Miriam bat Elisheva)* — *The Other Hunter*
British-Israeli intelligence operative, GCHQ-trained, twenty years a fugitive from her mother's killers. She arrives in Chapter Seven hunting the same Vârcolac network Cian is hunting, for different reasons. She delivers the killing blow at Dunluce Castle in Chapter Ten and commits to the Antarctica mission in Chapter Sixteen. A demon of the order of Asmodeus carries an unbreakable claim on her — she does not know this, and neither does anyone else in the team. Her full backstory is withheld for Books Two through Five.

**James Madigan** — *The Operator*
SAS veteran, forty years of intelligence tradecraft, the man who raised Miriam after her mother's murder. Extracted from Synagogue of Satan targeting in Chapter Eleven. Quiet, practical, and possessed of the specific calm that belongs to people who have seen worse than this and survived it. Joins the Antarctica team as its senior tactical mind.

**Marcus Reeves** — *The Acoustic Specialist*
Brennan's former Navy sonar specialist and Emma's godfather. Twenty years of submarine acoustic analysis. The only person in the McNeeve team who recognised the return signal from Antarctica as deliberate transmission rather than geological noise. He commits to the team in Chapter Fifteen for the simplest of reasons: a thing looked at his godchildren as though they were property, and he will not sit down while the people responsible are still in the world.

**Sarah McNeeve** — *The Lawfare Specialist*
Brennan's wife. Corporate lawyer by training and practice — the kind who understands how money moves through jurisdictions that don't speak to each other, how to build or dismantle legal structures across multiple sovereignties, and how a five-thousand-year-old network uses financial architecture to own the institutions that are supposed to stop it. Her natural domain is the war that looks like paperwork. While Miriam briefs the Antarctica team in Chapter Sixteen, Sarah is already mapping the Synagogue's financial exposure across eleven countries. She will not leave Emma and Liam for kinetic operations, and she is not asked to. The battle she is entering requires a different kind of nerve — and her role will expand across every subsequent book as the lawfare front becomes the campaign the Synagogue cannot outrun.

**Emma McNeeve** — *Brennan's daughter, approximately eight years old.* Her encounter with a shedim in Chapter Two is the event that forces Cian to move from strategic patience to operational urgency.

**Liam McNeeve** — *Brennan's son, approximately five years old.* Emma's brother; present during the same attack.

### THE MCNEEVE SPACE SYSTEMS TEAM
*(Introduced in Chapter Fifteen; committed to the broader mission by the chapter's end)*

**Dr. Siorse Mercer** — Flight dynamics and orbital mechanics; Welsh-American; two MIT doctorates; calculated Titan-1's Mars transfer orbit "by hand on a napkin" and was accurate to seven decimal places. The team's mother-figure.

**Chris Guilder** — Software architecture; twenty-nine years old; German-American from El Paso; designed the autonomous AI systems that allowed five rovers on Mars to make independent decisions across a seventeen-light-minute communications gap. Turned down Google three times. During the anti-singularity event, threw himself across Siorse Mercer to shield her with his body. Did not speak for two days afterwards. Commits to the broader mission when Brennan makes the offer.

**Dr. Jian-Wei "JW" Chen** — Materials and structures; Taiwanese-American; seventeen patents; the man responsible for every alloy and thermal protection system on the Titan programme. During the anti-singularity, the only person in the observation gallery besides Marcus who did not scream.

**Adrienne Prescott** — Communications and deep-space network liaison; British-American; former ESA engineer; spent four straight hours trying to re-establish contact with the five Mars rovers after they went dark, cycling every known frequency and protocol, refusing to accept silence as a final answer until the physics left her no choice.

**Hal Okonkwo** — Robotics and mechanical systems; Nigerian-American; discovered during the post-event assessment that every single fastener in the facility had loosened by exactly three-quarters of a turn. Spent two days re-torquing every bolt in the building. Did not complain. Not once.

**Dr. Anya Petrov** — Data systems and cybersecurity; Russian-American; recruited from the NSA after she casually demonstrated a zero-day exploit in their own conference presentation software. Manages the air-gapped systems, encrypted backups, and offshore data mirrors that ensured the Cydonia data survived McNeeve Space Systems' destruction.

### THE ANTAGONISTS

**Lord Vârcolac** — *The Debt Collector, The Ward-Maker*
A turned vampire of Sumerian origin, approximately five thousand years old, created by the last of the antediluvian blood-drinkers described in 1 Enoch 7:5. His original human name has been lost. In 586 BCE he was contracted to imprison Methuselah's Sword using Niamh mac Morna's blood as the ritual anchor. Cian's rescue of his sister left the contract incomplete — and in the supernatural economy of blood magic, an undelivered sacrifice accrues interest. Vârcolac has been collecting that interest against Niamh's descendants for twenty-six centuries, calculating that eliminating the entire bloodline will finally balance the ledger. It is not hatred. It is accounting. He also murdered Victoria Ashford, Miriam's mother, on contract for the Synagogue of Satan — a fact he does not know will be discovered, and that Cian does not yet know. He is decapitated by Miriam Elizabeth Ashford at Dunluce Castle, County Antrim, using a Saxon sword, in Chapter Ten. **Dead by the end of Act Two.**

**Naamah bat Lamech** — *The Queen Moves*
Born of Cain's line, daughter of Lamech ben Methushael, sister of Tubal-Cain. She survived the Flood through the Siren Transformation — the Watcher wives were not drowned but changed into something between human and demon, no longer dwelling on the land, no longer subject to its judgments. For five thousand years she has been remembered, imperfectly, in every culture that produced a goddess of beauty, war, and the liminal: Ishtar, Inanna, Astarte, Isis, Aphrodite, Hecate. In Chapter Sixteen she is aboard her yacht *Ashtoreth* in the Mediterranean, hearing Shemyaza's voice in the Oath playback for the first time in five millennia, and deploying seven of the Thirteen Houses toward Antarctica. She is the series' endgame antagonist. She is not fought in Book One.

**Azazel** — *He Who Is Inviting Them*
A fallen Watcher's Nephilim son — the False Prophet of Revelation — imprisoned in the pit of Dudael beneath the Antarctic ice by the Archangel Raphael at the time of the Flood. Known in Book One only through Mo Chrá's acoustic data and Marcus Reeves' sonar analysis of the return signal: two words in ancient Hebrew, transmitted instantaneously across a planetary acoustic network. *Bo elai.* Come to me. Present only as a signal. Every other detail withheld for Book Three.

**Dragomir Petrescu** — A mid-level financial operative within Vârcolac's Synagogue network in Bucharest; Miriam tracks and eliminates him in Chapter Seven. A chapter villain, not a series villain.

**The Shedim** — Disembodied Nephilim spirits, capable of inhabiting willing or weakened human vessels; the recurring low-end supernatural threat and the creature-type against which Cian's operational methodology is most clearly established. First encountered in Chapter Two.

**The New Breed Ghouls** — Vârcolac's enhanced undead programme. Unlike traditional animated corpses, the New Breed can sprint at thirty-five kilometres per hour, use pack tactics and flanking manoeuvres, climb vertical surfaces, and communicate via pheromone signals. The product of seventy years of selective reanimation research. The primary massed-combat threat of Acts Two and Three.

# APPENDIX D
## A Note on Sources

*This is a work of biblical apocalyptic fiction. The following is an honest account of where the series' lore originates, and where the author's imagination has extended beyond the documentary record.*

---

**Treated as historically accurate within the narrative world of this series:**

*The Book of Enoch (1 Enoch)* — a pseudepigraphical text referenced explicitly in the canonical epistle of Jude (Jude 14–15) and preserved in its most complete form in the Ge'ez canon of the Ethiopian Orthodox Church. The names of the twenty Watcher chiefs, the nature of their sin and their punishments, the spirit-state of their Nephilim offspring, and the binding of Azazel in a desert prison — all derive from 1 Enoch, Chapters One through Thirty-Six. Where 1 Enoch is clear, the series follows it. Where it is ambiguous, the series makes a defensible choice and stays with it.

*The Book of Giants (4Q530–532)* — fragments recovered from Qumran Cave Four, part of the Dead Sea Scrolls collection. The giant brothers Ohya and Hahya — their names, their prophetic dreams, the references to their violence — are drawn from these fragments. The identification of Ohya as Apollyon of Revelation 9:11 ("whose name in Hebrew is Abaddon, and in Greek, Apollyon") follows from that textual evidence, not from imagination.

*The Book of Jubilees* — regarded as canonical by the Ethiopian Orthodox Church and the Beta Israel community; consulted for the cosmological framework and the principle that angels were created on the first day of Creation (Jubilees 2:2).

*The Epistle of Jude* — canonical New Testament; the Greek word *oiketerion* in Jude 1:6 is the Septuagint rendering of the Hebrew *ma'on* (dwelling, habitation). The series traces that three-language chain — Enochian oath, Hebrew rendering, Greek translation, English loss of resonance — as a character beat in Chapter One.

*Ezekiel 28 and the Hebrew rekullah (merchandise, trafficking)* — the basis for the series' portrayal of Satan as a Trader rather than merely a Rebel; a being who knows what entities desire and offers to fulfill it in exchange for compliance. The methodology is consistent across every biblical account of his activity.

---

**Consulted but not treated as canonical:**

*Zohar, Yalkut Shimoni, Bereishit Rabbah* — Kabbalistic and midrashic traditions on Naamah; helpful for establishing the range of Jewish interpretive tradition around Genesis 4:22's otherwise unexplained naming of Lamech's daughter. The series follows the seductress-of-Watchers tradition over the alternatives.

*Josephus, Antiquities of the Jews* — for the post-Flood world and the transmission of antediluvian knowledge.

---

**Invented for this series and clearly so:**

The Acoustic Paradigm — the principle that all Watcher technology operates through sound rather than electromagnetism — is the author's extrapolation from the scriptural observation that God spoke creation into existence (Genesis 1; John 1:1–3; Job 38:4–7). It is internally consistent and scripturally grounded but goes beyond anything in the source texts.

Mo Chrá as a sentient acoustic weapon is the author's invention, though the sword's connection to Enoch (who the text says "walked with God and was not") and its origin in materials connected to the Watcher council is an imaginative extension of the lore, not a claim drawn from any text.

The Thirteen Houses of the Synagogue of Satan; the specific identity of the Beast, False Prophet, and Whore of Babylon as Ohya, Azazel, and Naamah respectively; the geography of Cydonia on Mars as a Watcher installation — all are the author's interpretive synthesis, offered as fiction, not doctrine.

The series operates from a conviction that the Book of Enoch is *historically factual within the narrative world* — that is, the author believes 1 Enoch preserves genuine memory of genuine events, filtered through human transmission and therefore imperfect. Readers who disagree are still welcome here. The story works whether one reads it as theology or as the finest kind of speculative fiction.

One boundary the series does not cross: the core gospel. Christ's resurrection was bodily and historical. Salvation is by faith in Him alone. The supernatural entities in these pages — however powerful, however ancient — are created beings operating within limits they did not set. The outcome of the story was decided at Calvary. This series is about the final chapters of a war that is already won.

# APPENDIX E
## Glossary of Principal Terms

*Series-specific vocabulary introduced in Book One. Some terms are drawn from scripture and ancient languages; others are the author's invention. Both are noted.*

---

**Acoustic Paradigm** — *(Author's invention, scripturally grounded)* The principle, central to this series, that all Watcher technology operates through sound and acoustic vibration rather than electromagnetic radiation. Derived from the observation that God *spoke* creation into existence (Genesis 1; John 1:1). The creation frequency — the foundational harmonic at which God spoke reality into being — is embedded in Cydonian ore and is what Mo Chrá has been producing for twenty-six centuries. Human technology, which is electromagnetic in nature, cannot read Watcher inscriptions because they were never designed to be read. They were designed to be *heard.*

**Anti-singularity** — An acoustic event triggered in Chapter Thirteen when Mo Chrá activates all twenty Watcher oaths simultaneously. The propagation of the creation frequency in an uncontrolled environment causes localised reality to return toward its pre-differentiated state — matter approaching the condition it was in before God spoke it into form. Contained by Mo Chrá (visibly) and by a second, hidden intervention (not fully explained in Book One).

**Apkallu** — The Babylonian "fish-men sages," remembered in Sumerian iconography as hybrid fish-men emerging from the waters to teach civilisation to humanity. Within the series: the disembodied spirits of drowned Nephilim, appearing in this form to post-Flood priestly cultures and transmitting antediluvian knowledge into the new world. The sudden appearance of organised Sumerian civilisation — writing, mathematics, astronomy, law — without apparent developmental prehistory is the historical evidence cited for their activity.

**Blood debt** — In the supernatural economy of dark magic, a promised sacrifice not delivered creates a binding obligation. The failed sacrifice of Niamh mac Morna in 586 BCE left Lord Vârcolac owed a debt that compounds across centuries. His vendetta against Niamh's descendants is not personal hatred; it is collection activity. The debt required 247 deaths across 2,500 years before Miriam Ashford paid it in full at Dunluce Castle with a Saxon sword.

**CREMATOR rounds** — .50 BMG ammunition with a thermate-metal incendiary core producing 2,500°C sustained burn on target. The only conventional firearm solution for Lord Vârcolac, whose regenerative biology requires complete physical destruction rather than wounding.

**Craobh Ruadh** — *Irish: Red Branch.* The warrior fellowship of Ulster to which Cian mac Morna belonged before his 586 BCE commission. His equivalent of a regiment or a sworn brotherhood. He names his current network after it.

**The Empyreal Register** — *(Series term)* The speech mode of celestial beings: archaic, formal, capitalised in the text, drawing on the KJV-era grammar that best approximates the weight of the language of heaven. Raphael speaks only in the Empyreal Register. Mo Chrá communicates without words, but her tonal patterns carry the equivalent emotional authority.

**The Empyreal Register** *(database)* — The celestial database in which every action of every angelic or demonic entity is recorded in real time. Accessible to Cian through physical contact with Mo Chrá. The primary source of intelligence for twenty-six centuries of hunting.

**Josephite Network** — Cian's legitimate civilian support infrastructure: Josephite is the series' term for the descendants of the Israelite tribe of Joseph through his sons Manasseh and Ephraim, broadly identified with the English-speaking nations. The network provides transport, documentation, communications, and safe houses.

**Liaigh** — *Irish: Healer, Physician.* Cian's name for his guardian angel. Not the angel's name. The angel has never confirmed or denied it.

**Ma'on** — *Hebrew: Habitation, Dwelling.* The celestial form the Watchers possessed before their descent. When they spoke the oath "I FORSAKE MY MA'ON" at Mount Hermon, the speaking of it was causative — the words enacted the stripping of their celestial nature. The Greek Septuagint rendering is *oiketerion*, used by Jude in his epistle. English translations flatten both words to "habitation," losing everything the original carries.

**Mo Chrá** — *Irish: Pulse of My Heart (lit. My Torment).* Cian's name for Methuselah's Sword. Not a description of the sword's effect on him but an affectionate/exasperated term of address — the Irish equivalent of calling someone "you devil." Both meanings are simultaneously true. She accepts neither as a compliment.

**MORTIS-7** — Cian's primary chemical weapon system for crowd-scale supernatural threat neutralisation. The compound disrupts the biological and spiritual coherence of Nephilim-derived entities (shedim, ghouls, lower-level supernatural presences) through aerosolised delivery.

**New Breed Ghouls** — Lord Vârcolac's enhanced undead programme, initiated in 1950 and still active at the time of Book One. Phase I–IV development produced animated dead capable of full-speed sprinting (35 km/h), wall and ceiling traversal, tactical pack coordination, and pheromone-based communication. Estimated current force: 800–1,200 units at peak.

**Oiketerion** — *Greek (Jude 1:6).* The celestial habitation-body the Watchers abandoned. See also: *Ma'on.*

**Shedim** — *Hebrew: Demons.* Within the series: the disembodied spirits of drowned first-generation Nephilim, requiring human vessels to manifest in the physical world. Capable of possessing willing or spiritually vulnerable individuals. The lowest and most common supernatural threat Cian encounters; the enemy that attacks Brennan's family in Chapter Two.

**The Thirteen Houses** — The Synagogue of Satan's operational structure, named for the thirteen principal bloodlines that form Naamah's inner council. Seven Houses are deployed toward Antarctica by Chapter Sixteen. The full structure of all thirteen is documented internally and will be revealed across subsequent volumes.

**Tamid Hours** — Not introduced in Book One. Retained for reference only.

**Tintreach** — *Irish: Lightning.* Cian's custom crystal-growth ammunition. The crystals are grown in acoustic bath chambers over three-week cycles at the Austrian safehouse and provide enhanced stopping power against Nephilim-derived biology. Named for the visible discharge effect on target.

# A NOTE ON CIAN MAC MORNA

*From the Author*

Cian mac Morna did not begin here.

He was born in a different story — a story set entirely in 586 BCE, following a twenty-eight-year-old Irish knight as he received a prophecy from Jeremiah at Tara, undertook a quest into giant country alone, found a sword no one had held in five centuries, and brought his sister home. That story is called **The Stone and the Sceptre Chronicles**, and it is complete in its own right.

I wrote that book first. It is the origin myth: the young man before the centuries, before the exoskeleton and the Tintreach rounds and the dry wit that only comes from watching history repeat itself thirty or forty times. That story contains Jeremiah and Tea Tephi and the Morrigan, and it ends at the moment this series picks up — not in plot terms, but in character terms. The moment Cian mac Morna became the man he would spend the next twenty-six centuries being.

If you have read what he is in this volume and want to know what he was before any of it — before Mo Chrá knew his name, before Liaigh had occasion to call him insufferable, before he had buried three wives and stopped counting his kills — *The Stone and the Sceptre Chronicles* is where that story lives.

It is not required reading for this series. But it is the prequel that exists if you want it.
