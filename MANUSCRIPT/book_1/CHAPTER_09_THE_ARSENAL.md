# CHAPTER 9: THE ARSENAL

---

**European Safehouse, Outside Graz, Austria**
**72-Hour Sanctioned Incursion — Hour 9**

---

## I. THE RIDE

The argument lasted the entire four-hour drive.

Cian had expected this. What he hadn't expected was that Miriam Ashford — five-foot-ten of Sephardic fury packed into tactical fatigues and righteous indignation — would prove to be the single most relentless verbal combatant he'd faced in twenty-six centuries of existence, and he'd once argued with the prophet Elijah about the proper way to season lamb.

The Shadow Hound led. The rented Dacia Duster followed. At every fuel stop, rest area, and traffic light between the Carpathian foothills and the Austrian border, the argument resumed with the enthusiasm of a tennis match between two people who had replaced the ball with a live grenade.

They'd switched to a single vehicle at the Hungarian border — a rest stop outside Győr, where Cian had abandoned the Shadow Hound in a pre-arranged Josephite dead drop and climbed into the Duster's passenger seat, because explaining the motorcycle's Enochian ward systems to a woman who was still calling him a "cosplayer" seemed premature.

The Ward Runner — the armored SUV that Brennan had been driving from Dublin — would meet them at the safehouse. Until then, they had four hours of confined space and unresolved grievances.

Miriam drove. She drove the way she argued — fast, precise, and with complete disregard for anyone else's comfort.

"You had *no idea* I was in that fortress," she said, taking a mountain curve at a speed that would have concerned a rally driver. "You planned your assault without any intelligence about a second operator in the area. That's sloppy."

"I knew you were there."

"You did *not*."

"I watched you park three kilometres east of the fortress at 2247 local time. You left the vehicle, approached on foot through the eastern tree line, and entered the keep through a ground-level servants' entrance that was last used in approximately 1834."

Miriam was quiet for two seconds. This was the longest silence she'd produced since the keep.

"Then why didn't you abort?"

"Because I assumed you were a civilian. A lost tourist. An extremely stupid hiker with a death wish. I thought you'd take one look at the ghoul-infested ruins and leave."

"I *cleared* six ghouls on the way in."

Cian looked at her. "You what?"

"Six. Ground floor, east wing corridor. Two in the servants' passage, four in the kitchens." She downshifted for a hairpin turn. "They were slow. Uncoordinated. The ones on the main level were much worse."

"The ones on the main level were being *controlled*. The east wing was peripheral — Vârcolac probably forgot they were there." Cian processed the information. Six ghouls. Alone. With a nine-millimetre and a combat knife. "How?"

"Contact shots to the brain stem. The knife was for the ones that got too close." She said it the way other people said *I picked up milk on the way home*. "The brain stem disrupts their motor control even if it doesn't kill them. Gives you time for follow-up shots."

"That's... actually sound methodology."

"Don't sound so surprised."

"I'm not surprised. I'm *appalled* that someone with your level of training attacked a four-thousand-year-old vampire with a nine-millimetre Glock."

"What was I supposed to use? A *catapult*?"

"Silver ammunition. Acid-compound rounds. Consecrated jacketed hollowpoints. *Anything* with anti-supernatural properties. You brought standard ball ammunition to fight an apex predator that shrugs off conventional weapons. That's not hunting — that's *commentary*."

"I don't have *access* to supernatural ammunition! I didn't even know supernatural ammunition was a *thing* until tonight! I thought I was hunting a — a — an extremely old, extremely strong *human* with a blood-drinking fetish."

"He's a vampire."

"I don't believe in vampires."

"You watched him lift you off the ground with one hand, move faster than your eyes could track, and shrug off three nine-millimetre rounds without flinching. What *exactly* would you call that?"

Miriam's knuckles whitened on the steering wheel.

"A man with exceptional physical characteristics and possible pharmaceutical enhancement."

From the back seat, Mo Chrá produced a sound that Cian had never heard from her before.

It was a snort.

The sword — five thousand years old, forged by the antediluvian scribe Enoch from Cydonian metamaterial, carrier of acoustic frequencies that predated human civilization — *snorted*.

Miriam's eyes flicked to the rearview mirror.

"Your sword is laughing at me."

"She finds your commitment to rationalism in the face of evidence... endearing."

"My *commitment to—*" Miriam swerved around a truck with the kind of lane change that suggested the truck had personally offended her. "I'm a Cambridge-educated archaeologist with a master's in ancient Near Eastern studies. I don't believe in *magic swords*."

Mo Chrá's snort became a sustained vibration that Cian could only describe as *giggling*.

"She's never done that before," he said, genuinely surprised.

"Done what?"

"Giggled. In twenty-six centuries, she has never *once* giggled. You've accomplished something historically unprecedented."

"Wonderful. I'll put it on my CV. 'Made an ancient weapon system laugh.' That'll play well at academic conferences."

"PERHAPS," Raphael said — and Cian heard the carefully modulated neutrality that meant the angel was trying very hard not to join Mo Chrá's amusement — "THE DISCUSSION MIGHT BENEFIT FROM A TEMPORARY CESSATION IN HOSTILITIES."

"She started it," Cian said.

"*He* started it," Miriam said simultaneously.

Mo Chrá's vibration achieved new and previously unexplored frequencies of delight.

## II. THE ARGUMENT

They arrived at the safehouse at 0300 local time.

The property didn't look like a fortress. It looked like what it had been before Cian bought it in 1847 — a converted farmhouse nestled into the Austrian countryside south of Graz, white-washed walls, clay tile roof, the kind of unremarkable rural architecture that the eye slid over without engaging. The nearest neighbour was four kilometres away. The nearest town was eleven. The road leading to it was unpaved for the last six hundred metres and discouraged casual visitors with the passive-aggressive efficiency of a surface designed to destroy suspension systems.

The outer appearance was camouflage. The reality was beneath it.

Cian entered the security code — a sixteen-digit alphanumeric that changed every twelve hours — and led Miriam through the farmhouse's ground floor, past the kitchen that hadn't been updated since 1963 and the sitting room with furniture that could generously be described as "historical," down a staircase concealed behind a bookshelf that moved on hydraulic rams, and into the operational level.

The operational level was forty feet underground, accessed through a blast door rated for overpressures up to 15 PSI, and contained everything Cian needed to wage a one-man war against entities that had been terrifying humanity since the Bronze Age.

The argument resumed immediately.

"You said you've been hunting Vârcolac for seven centuries," Miriam said, following him down the concrete corridor. "Seven *centuries*. And you haven't caught him?"

"He's good at running."

"He's good at running because you're *bad at catching*."

"I've killed over three thousand creatures in twenty-six centuries of active operations. My conversion rate is—"

"Clearly not one hundred percent, or we wouldn't be having this conversation."

"—approximately ninety-eight-point-seven percent, which is a record that any statistical framework would consider—"

"Ninety-eight-point-seven. And the one-point-three percent that got away include the *specific vampire* that murdered my mother."

"THAT IS," Raphael observed to no one in particular, "A STATISTICALLY VALID CRITICISM."

"You're not helping, Liaigh."

"I WAS NOT ATTEMPTING TO HELP. I WAS OBSERVING."

Miriam stopped walking.

"Who is that? Who keeps *talking*? That voice — it's been making comments since the fortress. It's not coming from the sword. It's coming from—" She looked at the breastplate, at the helmet clipped to Cian's belt, at the gauntlets. "—from your *armour*?"

"It's complicated."

"Un-complicate it."

Cian sighed. The sigh of a man who had been maintaining operational security for two and a half millennia and was watching it unravel in real time because of a woman who asked questions the way artillery asked structural questions of load-bearing walls.

"I call him Liaigh — it means 'healer' in Irish. He won't tell me his real name. He's been refusing for twenty-six centuries." Cian paused. "He's an angel. Some kind of guardian. Bound to a relic in my armour's power cell. He provides tactical intelligence, spiritual sensing, and unsolicited commentary."

Miriam stared at him.

"An angel."

"Yes."

"A *guardian angel*. Who won't tell you his name."

"He considers it classified information. I've asked approximately four thousand times."

"And he lives in your *chest plate*."

"THE CHARACTERISATION IS IMPRECISE," Raphael said, "BUT NOT SUFFICIENTLY INCORRECT TO WARRANT CORRECTION AT THIS JUNCTURE."

Miriam looked at the ceiling. Then at the floor. Then at Cian. Then at Mo Chrá, who was humming with the quiet satisfaction of someone watching a play they'd already seen and knew had a good second act.

"I need a drink," Miriam said.

"Kitchen's upstairs."

"Something *strong*."

"There's a bottle of Redbreast 21 in the cabinet above the sink. It's—"

"Irish whiskey. Of course it's Irish whiskey. Why would a twenty-six-hundred-year-old man with a magic sword and a pet angel drink anything *civilised*."

"Redbreast 21 is the *definition* of civilised. It was aged in sherry casks for—"

"I'm going upstairs. Don't follow me. I need seventeen minutes of not being in a conversation with a lunatic."

She turned and walked back toward the staircase.

"THE WHISKEY IS EXCELLENT," Raphael said helpfully. "TELL HER TO USE THE CRYSTAL GLASSES. THE STONEWARE MUGS DO IT AN INJUSTICE."

"Liaigh."

"YES?"

"Stop helping."

"I AM AN ANGEL OF THE MOST HIGH. 'HELPING' IS DEFINITIONALLY WHAT I DO."

Mo Chrá's hum conveyed that she sided entirely with Raphael and that Cian was handling this badly.

## III. THE ARSENAL

Seventeen minutes.

Cian used them to secure the exoskeleton on its armature, run diagnostics on the damaged back plate, reload magazines, and have a brief, intense conversation with Mo Chrá and Raphael about operational security that went approximately as well as every other conversation he'd had tonight.

"She's a civilian."

"SHE KILLED SIX GHOULS WITH A NINE-MILLIMETRE HANDGUN AND A COMBAT KNIFE."

"A civilian with exceptional training and terrible judgement."

Mo Chrá hummed — the hum that meant *you had terrible judgement once, and I chose you anyway*.

"That was different."

"HOW?"

"I was chosen by a prophet of the Most High. She chose *herself*."

"AND THOU DOST FIND THIS... INFERIOR?"

Cian didn't answer. The honest answer was complicated, and complicated answers at 0300 after a combat operation that had expended over a hundred and fifty rounds of ammunition and one full deployment of chemical weapons was the kind of emotional gymnastics he didn't have the balance for.

"SHE HEARETH THINGS," Raphael said quietly. "THE SWORD'S FREQUENCIES. MY VOICE. MORTAL EARS SHOULD NOT PERCEIVE EITHER. NOT WITHOUT—"

"I know."

"IT MEANETH SOMETHING, CIAN."

"I *know*, Liaigh."

Silence. The angel didn't push it. Raphael had the patience of an entity that measured time in geological epochs. He could wait.

Eighteen minutes after departing, Miriam came back downstairs.

She'd poured two glasses of Redbreast. She handed one to Cian without comment. She'd used the crystal glasses.

"Alright," she said. Her voice was calmer. Not calm — she was too angry for calm — but the whiskey and the seventeen minutes of solitude had converted the boiling fury into something more controlled. More dangerous. "I'm going to ask questions. You're going to answer them. If I think you're lying, I'm leaving. If your sword laughs at me again, I'm leaving. If your pet angel makes one more *helpful* comment—"

"You're leaving. Understood."

"I wasn't finished."

"Sorry."

"If *any* of what you've told me turns out to be true — the vampires, the angels, the ancient war, *any* of it — then you and I are going to have a much longer conversation about why a man with your supposed resources let my mother die without lifting a finger."

The air in the room changed.

Mo Chrá's hum dropped to a frequency so low it was felt rather than heard — a subsonic tremor that vibrated through the concrete floor and made the whiskey glasses ring faintly. It was not a threatening hum. It was the sound of a blade that recognized pain when it encountered it. The hum of ancient compassion.

Cian met her eyes. Dark eyes. Victoria's eyes.

"That's fair," he said.

"It's not fair. It's the *minimum*."

"Then we start with the minimum. Ask your questions."

She took a breath. Opened her mouth—

And looked past him.

Her eyes moved beyond Cian's shoulder, through the open blast door at the end of the corridor, into the room beyond — a room she hadn't noticed on the way in because she'd been too busy yelling. A room that was now visible because Cian had left the lights on when he'd secured the exoskeleton.

The armoury.

Miriam Ashford stopped talking.

Her mouth closed.

Then opened.

Then closed again.

Then opened significantly wider than the first time.

She walked past Cian without a word. Through the blast door. Into the armoury.

And stood there.

---

The armoury occupied a space roughly the size of a tennis court, climate-controlled, humidity-regulated, lit with UV-filtered LEDs that turned every blade into a statement and every firearm into a position paper. The north wall — the historical section — held weapons spanning three thousand years of human conflict, mounted on custom brackets with the meticulous care of a man who understood that these weren't display pieces. They were survivors. Veterans. Each one a chapter in an autobiography written in steel, bronze, and blood.

The south wall held the modern section. Rifles, handguns, shotguns, speciality weapons, the custom platforms built for hunting things that didn't show up in conventional threat assessments. Beneath them, locked cases containing ammunition types that the Geneva Convention hadn't specifically prohibited only because the Geneva Convention hadn't imagined they were possible.

The east wall was edge weapons. Swords, axes, spears, polearms — a timeline of humanity's commitment to the proposition that the best way to resolve a disagreement was to put sharp metal through the other party.

The west wall was armour.

Miriam stood in the centre of it all and behaved as if someone had hit her with a stun grenade.

Her eyes — dark, intelligent eyes that had spent twelve years cataloguing evidence and cross-referencing data and maintaining the kind of analytical discipline that allowed a person to hunt monsters without believing in monsters — those eyes were doing something new.

They were *twitching*.

Not uniformly. Each eye appeared to be working independently, like a chameleon trying to process two museum wings simultaneously. The left eye had locked onto the north wall and was tracking along the historical blades with the desperate hunger of a woman who had studied ancient Near Eastern metalwork at Cambridge and was seeing specimens that would have made the British Museum's curators commit crimes of passion. The right eye had discovered the Japanese katanas and was attempting to resolve the impossible — three blades representing three centuries of Japanese swordsmithing, any one of which would have been the centrepiece of any collection on Earth, hanging side by side on a wall in a farmhouse basement in Austria.

Her mouth had achieved a configuration that Cian had previously only seen on the faces of people witnessing solar eclipses or the second coming of a particularly good dessert.

The Redbreast sat forgotten in her hand, tilted at a thirty-degree angle that was losing whiskey to gravity at a rate of about two drops per second. She didn't notice.

"THE WHISKEY," Raphael observed. "SHE IS SPILLING THE WHISKEY."

"I can see that."

"THE REDBREAST 21."

"Yes, Liaigh."

"TWENTY-ONE YEARS IN SHERRY CASKS. THOU SHOULDST PERHAPS—"

"I'm letting her have her moment."

Mo Chrá's hum had evolved into something Cian had never heard — a bright, rippling frequency that oscillated between warmth and mischief, like a cat watching a bird discover a room full of bird feeders.

Miriam took three steps toward the north wall. Her left hand rose, fingers extended but not touching — the instinctive hover of a person trained in artefact handling, wanting to make contact but knowing that the oils on human skin could damage historical metal.

"That's a Luristan bronze sword," she whispered. "Third millennium BCE. The tang construction — that's *lost wax casting*. There are maybe twelve of these in existence."

Cian said nothing.

"And that—" Her eyes tracked right. The right eye had stopped twitching and joined its partner in synchronized stupefaction. "That's an Aegean leaf blade. Bronze Age. The patina — the specific green — that's Aegean copper-tin alloy. This belongs in a museum. This belongs in *every* museum."

"It belonged to a man who tried to kill me outside Thermopylae," Cian said. "He donated it. Involuntarily."

Miriam didn't hear him. She'd moved on. Her feet carried her along the north wall with the slow, reverent shuffle of a pilgrim approaching a shrine, each step revealing a new impossibility.

"Roman gladius. Mainz pattern. First century — no. Earlier. This is *Republican* period. The fuller channel is — oh, God, the edge geometry—" She turned to the next bracket. "Viking seax. Pattern-welded. The layering is visible in the — the fold count, that's at least two hundred layers. This is master-level smithing. This is—"

She reached the Japanese section.

She stopped.

The silence that followed was the kind of silence that existed in cathedrals — large, architectural, loaded with significance.

Three katanas. Kamakura. Muromachi. Edo.

Miriam's hands were shaking. Not from fear. Not from cold. From the specific neurological response of a person who had dedicated their intellectual life to the study of historical artifacts and was standing in front of three specimens that collectively represented the apex of an art form, hanging in a basement like laundry.

"May I?" she said, her voice approximately two octaves higher than it had been during the argument.

"Third from the left. The Muromachi."

Miriam lifted the katana from its bracket with the two-handed reverence of a woman handling a newborn made of crystal and nuclear material. She rotated it. The blade caught the UV-filtered light and threw a line of illumination across the ceiling — the *hamon*, the temper line, visible as a ghostly wave pattern along the edge where the differential hardening process had created a boundary between the hard cutting edge and the softer spine.

"The *nie* structure," she breathed. "The grain pattern — this isn't just Muromachi period. This is *Masamune school*. The Sōshū tradition. Fourteenth or fifteenth century." Her voice dropped to a register that Cian normally associated with prayer. "The way the *nioi* clusters at the *ashi*... this could be Masamune *himself*. Or a direct student. Sadamune, maybe. Or—"

"It's a student of Kaneuji," Cian said. "Kaneuji studied under Masamune. The swordsmith who gave it to me in 1467 said it was forged around 1340, during Kaneuji's middle period when he'd relocated from Mino to Kamakura."

Miriam looked at him.

The argument was gone. The fury was gone. The controlled, professional hostility of a woman who had spent twelve years building walls around her emotions and weaponizing the anger that lived behind them — gone. Replaced by something raw and unguarded and utterly genuine.

"You were *there*," she said.

"I was there."

"You — Ōnin War. 1467. You were in *Kyoto* during—" She stopped herself. Her brain was running multiple processes simultaneously and some of them were crashing. "The Kamakura blade. 1280s. You said you acquired it from a swordsmith forging weapons for the Mongol defence. The *Mongol invasions of Japan*?"

"Kublai Khan's second attempt. 1281. The *kamikaze* — the divine wind that destroyed his fleet. I was there on a sanctioned incursion. The local Archon had granted Michael a seventy-two-hour operational window to deal with a specific Nephilim-derived entity that was using the invasion chaos as cover for a different agenda."

"And the Edo blade?"

"A gift from a daimyō in 1623. He had an *oni* problem. I solved it."

"An *oni*. A Japanese demon."

"Not a demon. A Nephilim remnant. Fourth-generation. Approximately nine feet tall. Very angry. Very hard to kill." Cian paused. "The daimyō was grateful."

Miriam replaced the Muromachi katana on its bracket with hands that were steady again — the professional taking over, the artefact handler overriding the overwhelmed human. She turned to face Cian, and her expression had achieved a new configuration that he couldn't quite read.

It was the face of a woman whose entire worldview had just suffered a structural failure and was engaged in emergency reconstruction.

"THE WHISKEY," Raphael reminded.

Miriam looked down at the glass in her hand. The tilt had increased to forty-five degrees. Approximately a third of the Redbreast had migrated to the armory floor.

She corrected the angle. Drank the remaining two-thirds in a single swallow.

"I owe you a drink," she said.

"You owe me approximately seventy millilitres of a twenty-one-year-old single pot still whiskey that retails for two hundred and forty euros per bottle." Cian paused. "I'll put it on Vârcolac's tab."

Mo Chrá's hum rippled with the sword's particular brand of amusement — a sound that managed to convey *I like this one* without using any words at all.

"Your sword is doing it again," Miriam said.

"She's laughing."

"At me?"

"*With* you. There's a difference. She's... she doesn't laugh very often. Not sincerely. The giggling in the car was unprecedented. This—" He listened to the hum. "This is different. She's not laughing at what you said. She's laughing because she's *happy*."

"Your sword is *happy*."

"She has moods."

"Your sword has *moods*."

"Complex ones. She's been alive for five thousand years. She's had time to develop nuance."

Mo Chrá's hum shifted to something warm and definitive — the acoustic equivalent of a nod. *Yes. I have moods. They are excellent moods. You are welcome.*

Miriam looked at the sword. Then at the katanas. Then at the gladius. Then at the leaf blade. Then at the firearms on the south wall. Then back at Cian.

"How much of this collection did you *personally acquire*?"

"All of it."

"Over..."

"Twenty-six centuries. Give or take."

"You're the most obsessive weapons hoarder in human history."

"I prefer 'curator.'"

"You prefer *curator*?" She gestured at the south wall — seven rifles, twelve handguns, three shotguns, ammunition in types and quantities that would have given a law enforcement agency cardiac arrest. "A curator puts things in *cases*. With *labels*. And *opening hours*. This is — this is a *dragon's hoard*. This is what happens when someone with combat PTSD and unlimited funding discovers eBay."

"I don't use eBay."

"What *do* you use?"

"A network of discreet arms dealers, antiquarian specialists, and a man in Zurich named Heinz who sources decommissioned military equipment and asks no questions. Also, occasionally, battlefield scavenging. And a small number of items that were donated by grateful clients who were no longer being eaten by the things I'd killed."

Miriam opened her mouth. Then closed it. Then opened it again, thought better of whatever she'd been about to say, and closed it once more.

"FOUR TIMES," Raphael said quietly. "SHE HATH ATTEMPTED TO SPEAK AND CHOSEN SILENCE FOUR TIMES. A PERSONAL RECORD, I SUSPECT."

"Liaigh."

"I AM MERELY COUNTING. IT IS A HOBBY."

"Angels don't have *hobbies*."

"THOU HAST KNOWN ME FOR TWENTY-SIX CENTURIES. IN THAT TIME, I HAVE DEVELOPED SEVERAL."

"Such as?"

"COUNTING THE NUMBER OF TIMES MORTAL WOMEN RENDER THEE SPEECHLESS. PREVIOUSLY, MY TALLY WAS ONE. THE PROPHETESS HULDAH, 621 BCE. SHE CALLED THEE A 'WELL-INTENTIONED CATASTROPHE.'"

Mo Chrá's hum achieved a frequency that was definitely laughter.

Miriam was looking between Cian and apparently empty air with the expression of someone watching half a tennis match.

"He's doing it again. The voice. Can you tell him to—"

"He says you've tried to speak and chosen silence four times, which is a personal record."

"Tell your angel that I have killed things tonight and I am not in a mood to be *counted*."

"SHE IS MAGNIFICENT," Raphael said, with the tone of someone revising an initial assessment significantly upward.

"He says you're magnificent."

"Tell him I'm *armed*."

"She says—"

"I HEARD HER. INFORM HER THAT ARMAMENT IS, IN MY EXPERIENCE, ONE OF THE MORE RELIABLE INDICATORS OF MAGNIFICENCE."

Mo Chrá's hum hit a crescendo and broke into something that could only be described as full-throated, bell-clear, harmonic *laughter* — not the snort from the car, not the giggling from the corridor, but actual laughter. A sound that five thousand years of existence had never produced until this specific moment, when a Cambridge-educated archaeologist with bloody hands and broken pride stood in an armoury full of impossible history and threatened an angel with a Glock that still had half a magazine.

Cian stared at the sword.

"Did she just—"

"I BELIEVE THE SWORD JUST LAUGHED IN FULL HARMONIC," Raphael confirmed, and his voice carried something Cian hadn't heard in twenty-six centuries of partnership.

Wonder.

## IV. THE PROOF

They moved to the war room — a space adjacent to the armoury that served as Cian's operations center. Maps on the walls. Screens on the desk. A table large enough to seat twelve that had been manufactured in 1847 and modified with cable routing channels that the original carpenter would have found baffling.

Miriam sat across from Cian. The Redbreast bottle between them. Refills poured. Crystal glasses. She'd stopped yelling. She hadn't stopped being angry — the anger was there beneath the surface like lava beneath basalt, visible in the set of her jaw and the way her fingers never quite relaxed from the loose curl of someone ready to draw a weapon — but the anger had been joined by something stronger.

Curiosity.

"Start from the beginning," she said. "Not your beginning. *The* beginning."

Cian leaned back. Collected his thoughts. Found the thread.

"Before the Flood," he said. "Two hundred angels — the Watchers — descended to Mount Hermon. Northern Israel. Syrian border today. They came because they wanted human women. They'd watched from heaven. They'd desired. And they fell."

"Genesis six."

He looked at her with renewed respect. "You know the text?"

"My grandmother was a Torah scholar. My mother insisted I study the scriptures even while she was teaching me to hack encrypted databases." Miriam's voice carried the echo of someone quoting from memory. "'*The sons of God saw the daughters of men, that they were fair; and they took them wives of all which they chose.*' Genesis 6:2."

"The sons of God. *Bene Elohim*. The Watchers. Two hundred of them, led by Shemyaza. They landed on Hermon. They took human wives. And their children were—"

"The Nephilim. Giants. I know the Sunday school version."

"The Sunday school version leaves out the important parts. The Nephilim weren't just big. They were *hybrid* — angelic intelligence in bodies that were stronger, faster, and more durable than anything the human genome could produce. Some were six feet tall. Some were thirty-six. The warriors, the kings, the 'mighty men of renown' that Genesis describes — they were the ruling class of the antediluvian world."

"And the Watchers taught humanity forbidden knowledge."

"Azazel taught metalwork and weapons. Shemyaza taught enchantment and herbalism. Others taught astronomy, astrology, the working of precious stones. Every domain of knowledge that the Watchers possessed — knowledge they'd gained as celestial beings — they gave to women they wanted to impress and a world that wasn't ready for it."

Miriam's eyes narrowed. "The Oiketerion."

Cian set down his glass. "Where did you learn that word?"

"Book of Jude. Verse six. '*The angels which kept not their first estate.*' The Greek word is *oiketerion* — it means 'habitation.' Their celestial bodies. The Watchers left their celestial bodies." She leaned forward. "But your angel said something earlier — in the car. Something about the sword's frequencies and mortal ears. If the Watchers gave up their celestial bodies, they gave up the abilities that came with those bodies."

"Yes."

"Which means they could *teach* the knowledge — because they remembered how it worked — but they couldn't *demonstrate* it. They'd lost the innate ability. They had to build technology to replicate what they could no longer do naturally."

Cian stared at her.

In twenty-six centuries, he had explained the Oiketerion principle to perhaps forty people. Most had required hours of exposition. Miriam had arrived at it in thirty seconds through textual analysis and logical deduction.

"That's exactly right," he said.

"The technology. The 'supernatural' elements — the wards, the weapons, the things I've seen tonight. They're not magic. They're *engineering*. Built on principles that the Watchers understood because they'd been created with those abilities, and that humanity could replicate because the Watchers taught us the theory."

"The Acoustic Paradigm," Cian said. "*All* Watcher technology operates on sound. Vibration. Frequency. Genesis 1 — God *spoke* creation into existence. The Watchers were present. They heard the frequencies. They lost the ability to generate them when they shed their celestial forms, but they remembered them. Everything they built — including the ore that Mo Chrá is made of — resonates with those creation frequencies."

Mo Chrá hummed. The hum was confirmation. Agreement. *This is what I am. This is why I am.*

"Your sword is made from Watcher material?"

"Cydonian ore. Metamaterial with acoustic memory. Enoch forged Mo Chrá from it before the Flood — Enoch who walked with God, who was shown the frequencies of creation and given the knowledge to work them into metal. She contains harmonics that the Watchers themselves lost when they fell. That's why she cuts through their wards. She speaks a language they forgot."

Miriam was quiet for a long moment. The whiskey sat untouched. Her mind was working — Cian could see it behind her eyes, the rapid processing of a woman who had spent twelve years building an intelligence network and was now discovering that her intelligence had been operating on incomplete data.

"Tell me about the Flood," she said. "What happened to the Nephilim."

"They drowned. All of them. God sent the Flood specifically to destroy the hybrid bloodline — the corruption of the human genome that the Watcher-human breeding programme had introduced. Noah's family was saved because their bloodline was *tamim* — complete, uncorrupted, genetically clean."

"But the spirits—"

"The Nephilim's bodies died. Their spirits didn't. They're not human spirits — they don't pass on to Sheol. They're hybrid entities. Terrestrial. Bound to the earth. After the Flood, they existed as incorporeal influences. *Demons*, in the common parlance. But a specific subset of them — the priestly caste, the scholars — found a way to interact with the post-Flood world."

"The Apkallu," Miriam said.

The word hung in the room like smoke.

"You know about the Apkallu?"

"Fish-men who emerged from the sea to teach humanity. Seven sages. The leader was Oannes. Every Assyriologist knows the legend." Her voice had changed — the anger receding further, replaced by the electric excitement of an academic encountering evidence for a theory she'd never dared to voice. "But the standard interpretation is mythological. Founding myths. Origin stories that explain how Sumerian civilization appeared so suddenly without a developmental period."

"The Sumerian Problem."

"*Exactly* the Sumerian Problem. No gradual progression. No primitive-to-sophisticated transition. They appear in the archaeological record with fully-formed writing, mathematics, astronomy, medicine, law codes. As if—" She stopped.

"As if someone handed them a civilisation."

"Someone did."

"The dead Nephilim."

"Not just any Nephilim. The priests. The scholars. The ones who'd been educated by their Watcher fathers in the sciences of heaven — astronomy, mathematics, the working of the earth. After the Flood, their spirits survived as incorporeal entities. They couldn't pick up tools. Couldn't write. But they could *appear*. Could *teach*. Could whisper into the ears of people who were willing to listen."

"And the fish imagery?"

"Not what they are. What they *were*. How they died. The Flood drowned them. The fish-man image is a death mask — the Nephilim wearing the manner of their destruction like a brand. They've been teaching anyone who'll listen for four thousand years."

Miriam stood. Paced. The armoury's weapons watched her from the walls like a silent jury.

"There's a link," she said. "A transmission chain. Watchers taught the Nephilim. Nephilim drowned — spirits became the Apkallu. Apkallu taught the Sumerians. But someone had to bridge the gap between the antediluvian world and the post-Flood world. Someone had to bring the physical knowledge — the inscriptions, the texts, the *data* — across the Flood."

"Cainan."

"Who?"

Cian moved to a locked cabinet in the war room's corner. He entered a combination — mechanical, not digital, a dial lock that had been manufactured in 1911 and never updated because mechanical systems didn't produce electromagnetic signatures that could be detected remotely. The cabinet opened to reveal a shelf of documents preserved in archival-quality acid-free sleeves.

He withdrew a folio. Pages brown with extreme age, the script predating Latin by a comfortable margin.

"Cainan was the great-grandson of Noah. You won't find him in most Hebrew Bibles — the Masoretic text excised him. But he appears in the Greek Septuagint. Luke 3:36. The Book of Jubilees."

"What did he do?"

"He was learning to write from his father, Arpachshad, when he found something. Pre-Flood inscriptions. Watcher teachings carved into bedrock — stone that had survived the deluge because the Watchers designed it to survive. They inscribed their knowledge into materials that could withstand the judgment they knew was coming."

Miriam's breath caught. "Designed to survive. They *knew* the Flood was coming and they made sure their teaching would endure."

"Cainan transcribed everything. Kept it hidden from Noah, who would have destroyed it. And that transcription became the foundation of—"

"Sumer," Miriam whispered. "Babylon. The mystery traditions. The occult renaissance. *All of it*."

"All of it. The knowledge was supposed to drown. The Watchers ensured it didn't. Between Cainan's transcriptions and the Apkallu's direct teaching, the post-Flood world received a complete curriculum in secrets that God had judged too dangerous for humanity to possess."

"And the network I've been tracking — the Synagogue of Satan, the financial structures, the shell companies, the trafficking operations—"

"Four thousand years old. Give or take. The Harlot system. It's gone by many names — Babel, Babylon, the mystery schools, the Templars, the banking houses, the East India companies. More recently: the Club of Rome. The World Economic Forum. The branding changes every few centuries. The bloodlines don't."

Miriam sat down slowly. Not the controlled sit of a woman choosing a tactical position. The gravity-assisted sit of a woman whose legs had made an executive decision about load-bearing.

"And Vârcolac," she said.

"Made during the fall of Sumer. One of the first generation of vampires — created through Naamah's *pharmakeia* rituals. Naamah was the wife of Shemyaza — chief of the two hundred Watchers who descended at Hermon. She bore him Nephilim children, and through that union she learned things no human woman was meant to know. After the Flood, her spirit survived. She became something else. The grandmother of vampire creation. Every turned vampire traces its curse back to her."

"And Vârcolac serves the Harlot system."

"Has for four thousand years. He's not a leader. He's a debt collector. An enforcer. And right now, he believes your blood — and my sister Niamh's bloodline — will purchase him favour with powers older than Babylon."

Miriam's jaw set. The same expression she'd worn when she'd raised the Glock at a four-thousand-year-old vampire and pulled the trigger three times because someone had to and she was the only one in the room.

"Then we find him," she said. "Together. Before your seventy-two-hour clock runs out."

Cian shook his head. "The clock already ran. The Carpathian operation was the sanctioned incursion. We're back in Austrian territory — Michael's jurisdiction. The clock is irrelevant here."

"Then we have time."

"We have time. But Vârcolac doesn't. He's exposed. His fortress is compromised. His ghoul army is destroyed — fifty-four kills tonight—"

"*Sixty*. You're forgetting my six."

Mo Chrá hummed. Approval.

"THE BLADE COUNTETH HER KILLS," Raphael said. "SHE WISHETH ME TO NOTE THAT SIX GHOULS WITH A NINE-MILLIMETRE AND A KNIFE IS, BY ANY REASONABLE STANDARD, HEROIC."

Cian almost smiled. Almost.

"Sixty kills," he conceded. "His network is exposed. His wards are broken. He'll run — it's what he does. But he can't run far. Not anymore. I've disrupted his financial infrastructure. You've mapped his corporate network. Between your intelligence and my—"

"Arsenal of insanely obsessive weaponry."

"—*operational capabilities*, we can track him. Corner him. And this time, we end it."

Miriam lifted her glass. "To finding him."

Cian lifted his. "To ending him."

They drank.

Mo Chrá hummed — warm, bright, anticipatory. The hum of a blade that had been waiting five thousand years for this specific moment and hadn't known she was waiting until it arrived.

"THOU KNOWEST WHAT SHE IS," Raphael said privately, the words pitched below Miriam's hearing.

*No. Tell me.*

"I CANNOT. NOT YET." A pause — weighted, deliberate, full of things unsaid. "BUT THOU WILT UNDERSTAND. IN TIME."

*You always say that.*

"AND I AM ALWAYS CORRECT."

Cian looked at Miriam across the table. She was already on her feet again, drawn back to the armoury by the gravitational pull of three thousand years of curated violence. He could hear her muttering — something about the Crusader arming sword's edge geometry and the impossibility of a blade cross maintaining a cutting edge across nine centuries of presumed use.

She was touching the Indian tulwar now. The silver wire on the hilt. Turning it in the light with the careful, cataloguing attention of a woman who had spent her adolescence in museum conservation labs and could not physically prevent herself from analyzing metallurgy when metallurgy was present.

*Who trained you?* he thought. *Who turned a traumatised nineteen-year-old into someone who could kill six ghouls in a dark corridor and then stand in a room full of impossible things and notice the edge geometry on a Crusader sword?*

He didn't ask. Not yet.

There would be time for questions.

Right now, there was a vampire to hunt.

And for the first time in seven hundred years, Cian mac Morna wasn't hunting alone.

## V. THE INVENTORY

They spent the next three hours in the armoury.

What began as Miriam's archaeological rapture evolved into something more practical — an operational inventory that combined Cian's encyclopedic knowledge of his own collection with Miriam's forensic analytical skills. She approached the weapons with the same methodology she'd applied to the Synagogue's financial networks: systematically, exhaustively, and with a level of detail that would have made an auditor nervous.

"This Safavid shamshir," she said, lifting the curved Persian blade from its bracket. "The watermark on the steel — that's *wootz*. Crucible steel. This is genuine Damascus, not the modern pattern-welded imitation. Seventeenth century. *Maybe* sixteenth."

"Sixteenth. 1528. Isfahan."

"And this one—" She'd moved to the Ottoman kilij. "This hilt construction. The *karabela*-style pommel. This is officer grade. Janissary?"

"Court weaponsmith. Made for a man named Kasim who had the misfortune of being assigned to guard a prison that contained something I needed to release."

"You *released* something from an Ottoman prison?"

"A Josephite brother who'd been arrested for exorcism. The Ottomans didn't appreciate unlicensed theological operations in their territory." Cian paused. "In fairness, the exorcism *had* set fire to a mosque. An accident, but the Janissaries didn't see it that way."

Miriam set down the kilij with the careful deliberation of someone who was simultaneously fascinated and reassessing whether the man she was talking to might be genuinely insane.

"And that?" She pointed at the claymore on the far wall. "Scottish, obviously. But the blade length—"

"Five feet two inches. It's a true great sword, not a basket hilt. I carried it at Bannockburn."

"Bannockburn was in 1314."

"Yes."

"Which side?"

Cian was quiet for a beat.

"THE QUESTION," Raphael observed, "IS POLITICALLY SENSITIVE."

"I was a neutral observer."

"A neutral observer who brought a five-foot sword."

"One encounters things on the edges of battlefields. Things that use the chaos of human conflict as cover for activities that have nothing to do with Scottish independence."

"Things."

"A particular Nephilim-derived entity that had been using the war as a recruitment opportunity. It was animating the battle dead. I was invited — discreetly — by certain parties within the Scottish church to address the situation."

"And the claymore?"

"A gift from a grateful blacksmith whose village I'd cleared of reanimated corpses."

Miriam looked at him for a long moment.

"You have an answer for everything."

"Twenty-six centuries of practice."

"That's not reassuring."

"It's not meant to be. It's meant to be *accurate*."

She turned to the south wall — the modern section — and her demeanor shifted. The archaeological wonder was still there, but it was overlaid by something harder. More immediate. More *tactical*.

"Alright," she said, her voice switching from museum mode to mission mode. "If we're going after Vârcolac again, I need an upgrade. I'm not assaulting another fortress with a Glock and a steak knife."

"It wasn't a steak knife."

"It was a *Ka-Bar TDI*. Against a creature that shrugged off standard nine-millimetre. It might as well have been a steak knife." She crossed her arms. "What do you have that works?"

Cian considered the question. Then he considered the woman asking it — her build, her training, her operational preferences. She moved like someone with Krav Maga fundamentals. Her shooting was competent but not exceptional — combat effective, not competition standard. She favoured close-range work. She'd chosen brain-stem contact shots because she understood that precision at intimate range was more reliable than volume at distance.

"It depends," he said. "Can you shoot a rifle?"

"I qualified on the L85A3 during a training programme I'm not going to talk about."

"British military."

"I said I'm not going to talk about it."

"The L85 is five-five-six. Light recoil. I don't work in five-five-six — insufficient terminal performance against supernatural targets. Minimum calibre is seven-six-two for Tintreach ammunition, and the recoil profile on the SCAR—"

"Let me try it."

He handed her Bás Beag.

She handled it well. Not perfectly — the SCAR 17S was a heavier weapon than the L85, and the balance point was different with the M320 grenade launcher mounted underneath — but she found the grip, checked the chamber, tested the trigger pull, and shouldered the rifle in a sequence that demonstrated training.

"Heavy," she said.

"It fires rounds that generate forty-seven thousand volts on impact. Heavy is the cost of effectiveness."

"I can work with it." She lowered the rifle. "Handgun?"

Cian unlocked one of the secondary cabinets. Inside: eight handguns in various configurations, each one maintained in the state of perpetual readiness that characterised everything in the armoury.

"SIG P226 Legion. Nine-millimetre, but loaded with custom silver-jacketed hollowpoints. Not as effective as the Tintreach rounds, but significantly more potent than standard ball against undead tissue." He paused. "Or — if you prefer something with more authority — the FN Five-seveN. Twenty-round magazine, 5.7x28mm. The rounds are small but the velocity is exceptional and the penetration against ghoul bone armour is—"

"The P226." No hesitation. She knew what she wanted. "I've fired one before. The DA/SA trigger is familiar." She took the weapon, press-checked it, verified the magazine, and dry-fired twice — smooth trigger pulls, her stance adapting to the weapon's balance. "What about melee?"

"I thought you didn't believe in magic swords."

"I believe in *sharp things that kill monsters*. The theological origin is negotiable."

Mo Chrá's hum brightened considerably.

"THE BLADE OFFERETH HER APPROVAL OF THIS PRAGMATISM," Raphael translated.

Cian moved to the edge weapons section. He considered the collection. The gladius was too short for someone of Miriam's height and reach. The seax was a specialist weapon. The claymore was absurd.

His hand stopped on a blade near the end of the east wall.

It was a Saxon long sword. Twelfth century. Double-edged, thirty-two inches of hand-forged carbon steel with a wheel pommel and a simple cross-guard. Nothing exotic. Nothing decorated. A working weapon — built for killing, maintained for killing, still sharp enough after nine centuries that Cian tested the edge monthly and always drew blood.

He lifted it from the bracket and held it out.

Miriam took it. Tested the balance — two-handed grip, the pommel seated against the heel of her right hand. She made a slow cut — diagonal, left to right, the kind of basic strike that built muscle memory for faster work.

The blade moved well. She moved well with it.

"How does it feel?" Cian asked.

"Like it was made for this."

"It was. Everything in this room was made for this." He met her eyes. "Including, apparently, you."

The air between them held for a moment. Not charged — *weighted*. The specific gravity of two people recognizing something that hadn't been present until they were in the same room.

Then Mo Chrá hummed — warm, approving, and slightly smug — and the moment broke.

"Your sword is editorialising again," Miriam said.

"She does that."

"Can you tell her to stop?"

"I've been trying for twenty-six centuries. She doesn't take direction well."

"MUCH LIKE HER BEARER," Raphael added.

Miriam almost smiled. The *almost* was the key — the anger still there, the grief still there, the twelve years of weaponized solitude still there behind the expression. But the *almost* existed. And it was new.

She hefted the Saxon sword. Settled it across her shoulders. Turned back to the operations table.

"Alright," she said. "Show me everything you know about Vârcolac's escape routes. Financial networks, property records, allied entities, historical bolt holes. Everything."

"That could take a while."

"Good. I've been tracking this monster for three years with incomplete intelligence. I'd like to see what my picture looks like when someone fills in the last seven hundred years."

She sat down. Put the sword on the table. Opened her laptop — the one she'd retrieved from the Dacia Duster before they'd left the Carpathians — and looked at Cian with the particular expression of a woman who had just discovered that her war had allies and couldn't decide whether to be relieved or terrified.

"And Cian?"

"Yes?"

"After this is done — after we've killed the thing that murdered my mother—" Her voice was steady. Not calm. Steady. "—you're going to sit down and tell me exactly why you — with your twenty-six centuries, your angel, your magic sword, and your *insane* weapons collection — weren't there the night she died."

The silence that followed was the kind that existed between a question and the answer that would change everything.

"I will," Cian said. "I owe you that."

"You owe me more than that. But it's a start."

Mo Chrá hummed. Low. Warm. The hum that existed between grief and hope, between the end of one thing and the uncertain beginning of another.

"WE SHOULD BEGIN." Raphael's voice was gentle now — the tactical commentator replaced by something older and more compassionate. "VÂRCOLAC WILL NOT WAIT FOR US TO FINISH OUR INTRODUCTIONS."

"Where do we start?" Miriam asked.

Cian pulled a map onto the operations table. Eastern Europe. The Carpathian corridor. Red circles marking Vârcolac's known properties, financial nodes, alliance networks.

"He's running," Cian said. "The question is *where*."

He didn't know yet.

But with Miriam's financial intelligence layered over his centuries of operational data, they were about to find out.

Together.
