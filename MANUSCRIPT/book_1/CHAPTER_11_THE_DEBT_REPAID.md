# CHAPTER 11: THE DEBT REPAID

---

## I. THE WEIGHT

The Austrian safehouse had acquired the particular silence of aftermath.

Thirty-six hours since Dunluce. Thirty-six hours since thermate reduced four thousand years of predation to ash and carbon in a sea cave beneath the Antrim coast. Thirty-six hours since Miriam Elizabeth Ashford — the architect of their intelligence operation, the woman who had tracked Vârcolac through a decade of financial forensics — had stood over the vampire's burning corpse and watched her mother's murderer cease to exist.

Victory should have felt like something.

It didn't.

Cian sat at the operations table in the converted wine cellar that served as their tactical centre. The holographic displays showed nothing but standby patterns — Brennan's systems maintaining passive surveillance across the Synagogue's communication networks, but no active operations. No threats flagged. No targets acquired.

Just silence. And Miriam's accusation, hanging in the air like smoke that wouldn't clear.

*"So after this is done — after we've killed the thing that took her from me — you're going to sit down at this table and tell me exactly why a twenty-six-hundred-year-old immortal warrior with an angel, a magic sword, and the most insane weapons collection in human history — operating within his own jurisdiction, fighting his own war — wasn't there the night a Jewish woman died fighting his enemy."*

The thing was done. Vârcolac was dead. And now the debt was due.

Miriam sat across from him, her posture unchanged from the hundred hours of planning that had preceded the assault. Back straight. Hands folded. Eyes that gave nothing away — the same eyes that had watched her L67 chemical rounds dissolve ghoul champions in the castle depths, the same eyes that had seen her first human kill in the corridors above, the same eyes that had reflected thermate fire as Vârcolac burned.

She wasn't crying. Miriam Ashford didn't cry. She hadn't cried in twelve years, not since a November night in New Mexico when everything she'd loved had been torn apart by creatures that shouldn't exist.

"You said you owed me an explanation," she said. Her voice was flat. Professional. The voice of a woman conducting a debriefing, not a daughter demanding answers. "The operation is complete. The target is neutralised. I'd like that explanation now."

Mo Chrá's hum had been muted since their return — the sword's usual commentary replaced by something that felt almost like mourning. The blade had been present for the accusation in the armoury. She remembered guilt when she heard it. And she was not offering editorial assistance for what came next.

Cian looked at the woman across the table and felt the full weight of twenty-six centuries of failure pressing down on his chest.

"Your mother—" he began.

"Victoria Mizrachi Ashford. Born in Jerusalem, 1973. Emigrated to London in 1991. Married David Ashford, 1994. Intelligence analyst, HMRC — officially. Financial investigator for an unnamed three-letter agency — actually. She spent fifteen years mapping the Harlot system's banking infrastructure. She was killed on November 14th, 2014, in a safehouse in New Mexico, by the creature we destroyed two nights ago."

"I know who she was."

"Then you should know who she *wasn't*." Miriam's jaw tightened. "She wasn't a supernatural combatant. She wasn't flagged in any of your networks. She wasn't connected to your Josephite contacts or your angelic intelligence feeds or whatever other resources you've been using for the last two and a half millennia. She was a Jewish woman with a laptop and a conviction that the financial trail would lead somewhere that mattered. And she was *right* — it led to the same enemy you've been fighting since Nebuchadnezzar's armies burned Jerusalem to the ground."

The accusation was precise. Surgical. The intelligence analyst's methodology applied to grief.

"THE WOMAN HATH A VALID INQUIRY," Liaigh said quietly. For once, the angel's voice carried no tactical edge. No counting. Just the weight of acknowledgment.

"She does." Cian's voice was rough. "And she deserves an answer."

"Then give me one."

He opened his mouth—

And the operations table lit up.

## II. THE ALERT

The holographic display shifted from standby to active threat — red icons cascading across the tactical projection like blood spreading across water. Brennan's face appeared in a video feed window, his expression carrying the particular tension of a man who had found something he wished he hadn't.

"We have a problem."

"Define 'problem.'" Cian was already on his feet, the explanation to Miriam set aside with the reflexive efficiency of a man who had been operating on interrupted timetables for twenty-six centuries.

"Signals intelligence from the Synagogue's communication infrastructure. The same networks we compromised during the Vârcolac operation." Brennan's fingers moved across his keyboard, pulling data feeds into the display. "Kill order. Not supernatural assets — human contractors. Private military. A team is assembling in Edinburgh."

The map shifted to Scotland. Edinburgh highlighted. A line extending north into the Highlands.

"Target?"

"Retired SAS operative, currently residing in a converted stone farmhouse in the Scottish Highlands. Name on file is James Madigan." Brennan paused. "The connection is flagged as 'Ashford investigation — tertiary contact, threat assessment pending.'"

Miriam's face went white.

Not pale — *white*. The colour draining from her cheeks in a way that Cian had never seen, not even in the caves of Dunluce when she'd been surrounded by ghouls and covered in calcium hydroxide dust.

"James," she whispered. And then, louder, sharper: "*James.*"

"Define the connection." Cian was already running operational calculus — Edinburgh to Highlands, contractor assembly time, terrain, access routes.

"He—" Miriam stopped. Started again. "He's the man who received me after my mother died. The man who trained me. He's—"

*The man who turned a traumatised nineteen-year-old into someone who could kill six ghouls in a dark corridor*. The question Cian had asked himself in the armoury, watching Miriam's hands move over his weapons with the precision of a seasoned operator. The question he'd set aside for later.

"He's retired SAS," Miriam said, her voice steadying as operational discipline reasserted itself over the terror beneath. "Twenty years in the Regiment. He took me in when I had nothing. He spent a year teaching me everything he knew."

"THE SYNAGOGUE HATH TRACED THE CHAIN," Liaigh observed. "The Bucharest operation stirred the water. Thy convergence with Cian's Carpathian assault created waves. Someone hath connected the links back to the man who sheltered the daughter."

"Timeline." Cian's voice was flat. Focused. The voice of a man who had coordinated rescues across twenty-six centuries of human warfare.

Brennan pulled up the intelligence feed. "The team started assembling six hours ago. PMC out of Edinburgh — former military contractors, professional extraction specialists. They're moving north by vehicle convoy. Estimated arrival at the target location..." He checked the data. "Four to six hours from now."

"Four to six hours," Miriam repeated. The words were hollow.

"CAN WE REACH HIM?" Liaigh asked.

"Charter flight from Vienna to Inverness — three hours if Brennan can arrange it." Cian was already moving toward the equipment lockers. "Ground transport from Inverness to the Highlands — another hour, depending on road conditions. That gives us—"

"A window," Miriam finished. "A narrow one."

A window that would close the moment six professional operators reached a stone farmhouse in the Scottish Highlands and did to James Madigan what Vârcolac had done to Victoria Ashford.

Cian stopped. Turned to face Miriam.

The accusation was still there — still hanging in the air, still waiting for an answer he didn't have. The explanation she deserved. The reckoning he owed her.

But right now, none of that mattered.

"A man who protected a child is about to die because my war touched his world," Cian said. His voice was quiet. Certain. The voice of a man who had made decisions exactly like this one more times than he could count and had never once hesitated when the parameters were this clear. "That's enough."

"Enough for what?"

"Enough to move."

He reached into the equipment locker and pulled out two fighting knives — a modern Winkler WK II in Micarta and canvas, and something older. Much older. Sheffield steel, the blade eight inches long, the balance point at the guard. The pommel was wrapped in wire that had turned dark with age — eight hundred years of handling, eight hundred years of being carried close to a warrior's body.

"Where is he?" Cian asked.

"Scotland. The Highlands. A farmhouse near—"

"I know the Highlands." Cian sheathed both knives — the modern blade on his left hip, the ancient one on his right. "Every stone of them."

Miriam stared at him. The analyst's mind was working behind her eyes — correlating, calculating, building the framework that would make sense of this.

"Of course you do," she said quietly. "You're Irish. You probably walked those mountains before the Scots built their first hill forts."

"I did." Cian turned to the video feed. "Brennan. Charter. Now. Fastest aircraft you can arrange. I don't care what it costs."

"Already on it." Brennan's hands were flying across his keyboard. "I have a Josephite contact at Vienna International who owes me three favours. Wheels up in forty-five minutes."

"THE MORTIS SUIT?" Liaigh asked.

"No." Cian shook his head. "This isn't a supernatural operation. These are human contractors — mortal, however corrupt. Mo Chrá stays sheathed. The suit stays here."

"And what do *I* do?" Miriam's voice was sharp. Controlled. The voice of a woman who had just watched her entire emotional framework get tabled for an operational emergency and was not remotely prepared to be left behind.

Cian met her eyes.

"You come with me," he said. "This is your family. You have the right to be there."

Something shifted in Miriam's expression — not gratitude, not relief, but acknowledgment. The recognition that Cian mac Morna, whatever his failures had been, understood this particular calculus without needing it explained.

"Then let's move," she said. "We're wasting time."

## III. THE RACE

The Cessna Citation X screamed through the winter darkness at .92 Mach — the fastest civilian aircraft Brennan's contacts could arrange on forty-five minutes' notice. Below them, the Alps gave way to the North Sea, and Cian watched the darkness through the window with eyes that had seen this world from a thousand different perspectives and never stopped being amazed by how quickly humanity had learned to traverse it.

Twenty-six centuries ago, this journey would have taken weeks. Now it took three hours.

Some things got better.

"You're quiet." Miriam sat across from him in the leather cabin, her hands folded in her lap with the precise stillness of a woman who was using every ounce of her training to keep the fear contained. "Usually, Mo Chrá doesn't stop talking. Neither do you."

The sword rested against Cian's thigh, sheathed and silent. Her hum was muted — present, but restrained. The blade understood what this flight meant. What was waiting at the end of it.

"Mo Chrá is thinking," Cian said. "So am I."

"About what?"

About the look on Miriam's face when she'd heard James's name. About the way her voice had broken on that single syllable — just once, just for an instant, before operational discipline reasserted itself. About the question he'd asked himself in the armoury: *Who trained you?*

Now he knew.

"Your mother entrusted you to him," Cian said. "After."

It wasn't a question. Miriam's jaw tightened anyway.

"She didn't *entrust* me. She was dead." The words were flat. Clinical. The words of a woman describing events that had happened to someone else, in some other life. "I showed up on James's doorstep with a backpack, a flash drive, and eyes that had seen things no nineteen-year-old should see. He took me in. He didn't ask questions. He just... took me in."

"Why him?"

"He served with a man who owed my mother a debt. Something from her intelligence days — she never told me the details. When everything fell apart, he was the only contact I had who wasn't connected to any official network." Miriam's hands tightened in her lap. "He spent a year teaching me to shoot. To fight. To survive. He gave me the foundation that kept me alive for the next decade."

*Who turned a traumatised nineteen-year-old into someone who could kill six ghouls in a dark corridor*.

The question had been answered. And the answer was currently in a farmhouse in the Scottish Highlands with six professional operators closing in.

"Ní fhéadfaidh mé ligean dó bás fháil."

The words came out before Cian could stop them — Irish, the old form, the language he'd learned before Latin existed. He hadn't spoken it aloud to anyone in years. Decades. The mask of "Sean Morna" covered everything, including the tongue that had been carved into his soul.

But the mask was slipping. Under pressure. Under time. Under the weight of what was waiting at the end of this flight.

Mo Chrá's hum shifted — rising, brightening. The blade responded to the language like a plant responding to sunlight. Warmth spread through the sheath, through the harness, through Cian's chest.

Miriam stared at him.

"What language is that?"

"Irish. The old form. Before the English came and put their boots on our throats."

Something in his voice had changed. Miriam could hear it — the flattened mid-Atlantic accent of "Sean Morna" peeling away, revealing something older beneath. Harder. The cadence of a man who had learned to speak in a world where Latin was the language of conquerors and Irish was the language of the free.

Mo Chrá's hum became something Miriam had never heard before. Not the sardonic warmth of combat commentary. Not the calculating edge of tactical assessment. Something almost... joyful.

"Your sword is making a new sound."

"She recognises the tongue she was first spoken to in." Cian's hand rested on Mo Chrá's hilt — an unconscious gesture, the touch of a man communing with a blade that had been his only true constant for five thousand years. "She likes it when I remember who I am."

"And who is that?"

The question hung in the cabin, suspended between them like a bridge that hadn't been crossed in twenty-six centuries.

"Cian mac Morna." His voice was quiet. "A Gael. A warrior of the Craobh Ruadh. The boy who watched his family's *ráth* burn when King Aodhan's ally Morrigan the Shadow-weaver came for them."

The words settled between them. Miriam heard the shape of something familiar in his loss — a family destroyed by a supernatural predator sent by a human master. The same architecture of grief that had defined her own life since New Mexico.

Not Sean Morna the consultant. Not the cover identity that had served him through a hundred different aliases across a hundred different centuries. The man underneath. The one that Mo Chrá remembered.

Mo Chrá's hum achieved a frequency that conveyed, with absolute clarity: *There you are. I was wondering when you would stop pretending.*

"Your sword is editorialising again."

"She does that." But Cian's voice was softer now. The tension in his shoulders had eased slightly — not relaxed, never relaxed, not with what was waiting at the end of this flight, but something closer to settled. "She's been waiting for me to stop hiding."

Miriam studied him across the cabin — the man she'd known for days, the operative she'd entrusted with her mother's vengeance, the ancient warrior whose failures she'd catalogued with surgical precision in the armoury of his Austrian safehouse.

"You're terrified," she said quietly.

Cian met her eyes. "Yes."

"For James?"

"For you." He held her gaze. "A man you love is in danger because of a war I've been fighting for twenty-six centuries. If we don't reach him in time—"

"We'll reach him."

"If we *don't*—"

"Then I will have lost the only father I have left." Miriam's voice was steady. Absolutely, impossibly steady. The voice of a woman who had learned, long ago, to speak through fear without letting it show. "And I will spend the rest of my life wondering if I could have moved faster, planned better, been *there* instead of arguing about jurisdictions and blood debts in an Austrian wine cellar."

The silence stretched between them.

"Ní ligfidh mé é sin tarlú," Cian said softly. *I will not let that happen.*

Mo Chrá's hum was warm and fierce and absolutely certain.

"I don't know what that means," Miriam said. "But I'm choosing to believe it."

## IV. THE FARMHOUSE

James Madigan had spent forty years learning to read his environment.

Twenty of those years had been in the Special Air Service — the world's first special forces unit, the template from which every other Tier 1 operator had been derived. He'd served in places Her Majesty's Government still wouldn't acknowledge, done things that existed only in classified after-action reports buried in vaults beneath Whitehall, and killed men in conditions that ranged from the jungles of Sierra Leone to the mountains of Afghanistan.

The other twenty years had been spent in the Scottish Highlands, in a converted stone farmhouse that he'd purchased with his pension and retrofitted with the systematic paranoia of a man who knew exactly how the world's most dangerous people operated.

Because he'd been one of them.

At sixty-three, his knees ached on cold mornings — a parachute landing in Helmand in 2009 that had never quite healed right. His left shoulder carried the ghost of a bullet graze from an operation in Basra that officially never happened. His reflexes weren't what they'd been at thirty-five, when he could put three rounds through a playing card at fifteen meters in under two seconds.

But his eyes still worked. His brain still worked. And his sense of *wrongness* — the instinct that had saved his life more times than he could count — was screaming.

The surveillance team had been good. Professional. The kind of good that came from former military training and corporate funding. They'd set up on the ridge three kilometers east, using the natural terrain to mask their observation post. They'd rotated positions to prevent pattern establishment. They'd communicated on encrypted frequencies that wouldn't show up on civilian scanners.

But James Madigan wasn't using civilian scanners.

The equipment in his basement — purchased over two decades from contacts who asked no questions and accepted only cash — had been watching them since they'd arrived six hours ago. Infrared signatures on the ridge. RF emissions from their comms. Vehicle movements on the access road.

Six operatives. Professional kit. Assault rifles, breaching charges, the choreographed methodology of a team that had done this before.

Coming for him.

James sat in his kitchen, his Browning Hi-Power on the table in front of him, and considered his options.

Running was out. They'd have the roads covered. Helicopters on standby. The Highlands were vast, but he was sixty-three with bad knees and they were six with the resources of someone who could hire former Tier 1 operators for wet work.

Calling for help was out. His contacts in the Regiment were scattered across three continents, and by the time anyone could mobilise, he'd be dead and burned in his own farmhouse.

Which left fighting.

James Madigan smiled — a cold, thin expression that would have been familiar to anyone who'd served with him in the sandboxes of the Middle East.

He was outnumbered and outgunned and about to die.

But he could make it expensive.

He rose from the table, joints protesting, and moved to the reinforced door of his basement armoury. Twenty years of collected equipment. Weapons from four continents. Enough ammunition to hold a small siege.

The farmhouse was his fortress. He'd designed it that way — sight lines cleared through what looked like "natural" landscaping but was actually interlocking fields of fire. Reinforced doors on every entrance. Prepared fighting positions in every room that mattered.

If they wanted James Madigan, they were going to have to work for him.

---

The breach came at 0347 local time.

Flashbangs through the windows — three simultaneous detonations designed to disorient and disable. The front door buckled under breaching charges, wood and metal splintering inward as the first two operators flowed through with the choreographed precision of men who had practiced exactly this manoeuvre a hundred times.

They were good.

James was better.

The first round from his L85A3 took the point man in the shoulder — centre mass would have been the clean shot, but James was firing from a prepared position behind his kitchen island and the angle was wrong. The second round corrected, catching the stumbling operator in the side of his ballistic vest and knocking him sideways into his teammate.

Professional chaos. James had seen it before. The moment when a plan met resistance and operators had to adapt.

The second shooter cleared around his fallen comrade, rifle up, scanning. James was already moving — out of the kitchen, through the hallway, using the geography he'd memorised in his sleep to stay one step ahead of their entry pattern.

A third operative breached through the side door. James met him with two rounds that punched through his vest at the seam beneath his arm — one of the few places where ballistic protection failed. The man dropped.

Three down. Three to go.

But James was bleeding now. The first exchange hadn't been clean — a round had caught him in the left shoulder, punching through the upper trapezius in a through-and-through that sent hot blood streaming down his arm. His grip on the rifle was weakening. His vision was beginning to narrow at the edges.

*Sixty-three*, he reminded himself. *You're sixty-three, you stupid old bastard. Should have retired to Spain.*

The fourth and fifth operators came together, moving in a coordinated assault that pushed James back through his own house. He dropped the rifle — the shoulder couldn't sustain the weight anymore — and drew his Browning Hi-Power. The pistol felt familiar in his hand. He'd carried one just like it since 1983.

Two more shots. One hit — centre mass this time, the operator dropping — and one miss as James's weakening arm betrayed him.

Four down. Two left.

But he was out of time.

The team leader — the sixth operative, the one who'd hung back directing the assault — materialised behind him. James spun, tried to bring the Browning up, and felt the rifle butt connect with his wounded shoulder.

The world went white with pain.

He was on his knees before he registered falling. The stone floor of his own kitchen pressing against his legs. The smell of cordite and blood and the particular copper taste of a body running out of options.

The team leader stood behind him. The barrel of a suppressed pistol pressed against the back of James's head.

Execution position.

*Well*, James thought with the peculiar calm of a man who had accepted his own death a long time ago, *you made it expensive. That's something.*

He didn't close his eyes. James Madigan had never closed his eyes.

---

Miriam froze in the doorway.

She had arrived with Cian moments before — the rental car abandoned at the end of the farm road, the two of them approaching on foot through the darkness. They'd heard the gunfire. Smelled the cordite. Seen the tactical flashlights moving through the windows of the stone farmhouse she'd called home for a year of her life.

And now she stood in the ruined doorway and watched James Madigan — the only father she had left — kneeling on his own kitchen floor with a gun to his head.

*This is New Mexico*, her brain screamed. *This is November 14th, 2014. This is my mother on the porch.*

Her vision tunnelled. Her body locked. Ten years of training evaporated in an instant, replaced by the nineteen-year-old girl who had watched everything she loved get torn apart.

She couldn't move.

She couldn't *breathe*.

## V. TWENTY-SIX CENTURIES

Cian was fifteen feet away when he processed the geometry.

The team leader had James at execution position — kneeling, barrel to the back of the head. Miriam was frozen in the doorway, her body locked in the paralysis of recognition. The angle precluded a firearm solution — James was in the line, and the margin of error at fifteen feet with a moving target was unacceptable.

The operative was a professional. He'd seen Cian enter. His finger was on the trigger.

One and a half seconds. Maybe less.

Mo Chrá hummed against Cian's back — urgent, eager, ready. But the blade stayed sheathed. These were humans. Mortal, however corrupt. Ephesians 6:12 applied in spirit if not in letter. Mo Chrá was for supernatural threats. For creatures that had abdicated their humanity in service to entities that predated the Flood.

These men were killers. But they were *human* killers.

And Cian mac Morna had been killing human killers for two thousand years before the First Crusade.

The knife from Acre was in his hand before conscious thought caught up — Sheffield steel, eight inches, balanced by a smith named Thomas who had served the Knights Hospitaller in a fortress that had traded hands more times than any man could count. The blade had no supernatural properties. No celestial frequency. No acoustic resonance with the technologies of the Watchers.

Just steel. Weight. Balance. And eight hundred years of muscle memory.

Cian threw.

The distance was fifteen feet. The target was a man's throat — not the largest target, not the easiest, but the only one that would guarantee an instant stop. The operative's finger was on the trigger. Any hit that didn't kill immediately would result in a reflexive pull. James would die.

Eight hundred and thirty-four years of practice.

The knife took the team leader in the throat.

He dropped before his nervous system could register the damage — the blade severing the carotid and the trachea simultaneously, the body collapsing sideways as the pistol clattered to the stone floor unfired.

James Madigan, kneeling in his own blood on the kitchen floor of his Scottish farmhouse, watched his executioner fall and felt the wind of the throw pass his ear.

He turned.

A man stood in the doorway behind Miriam. Average height. Average build. The kind of face that could disappear in any crowd on any continent. He held no weapons in his hands — the throwing knife was currently embedded in a dying man's throat — but his posture carried the weight of violence so complete that James's lizard brain registered it immediately.

*Dangerous. More dangerous than anything you've ever faced. More dangerous than you were at your peak.*

And then James saw the eyes.

Old. Ancient. Eyes that had seen civilisations rise and fall and hadn't blinked.

"Who the bloody hell are you?" James said.

The man stepped into the kitchen—

And spoke.

Not the flattened mid-Atlantic accent that Miriam had described in her communications. Something else. Something older. Something that James's grandmother would have recognised.

Irish.

The old form. The cadence of a language that had been spoken on this island before the English, before the Normans, before anyone had drawn the lines that divided Ireland from Scotland from Wales from what would become Great Britain.

"Get up," the man said in English. His accent was wrong for modern Ireland — too archaic, too precise. "You're bleeding."

"I noticed." James pushed himself to his feet, ignoring the fire in his shoulder and the spots dancing at the edges of his vision. "The girl behind you—"

"Miriam." The man's voice softened. Not much, but enough. "She brought me here."

*Miriam*.

James looked past the stranger to the doorway, where his adopted daughter stood frozen in the posture of a woman who had just relived her worst nightmare and was still processing the fact that this time, the ending was different.

"Miriam," he said. His voice was rough. "Lass."

She unfroze.

The transition was instantaneous — the paralysis shattering like glass as the sound of his voice reached her. She crossed the kitchen in four steps, dropping to her knees beside him, her hands finding the wound in his shoulder with the trained efficiency he'd taught her a decade ago.

"Through and through," she said. Her voice was steady. Her hands weren't. "Upper trapezius. You're going to need—"

"I know what I need." James put his right hand on her shoulder — the good arm, the one that wasn't leaking blood onto his own kitchen floor. "I'm alive, lass. I'm alive."

The stranger was still watching. Still assessing. His eyes moved across the room — cataloguing the dead operatives, the spent brass, the geometry of the firefight — with the efficiency of a man who had walked through a thousand battlefields.

"Four down before we arrived," he said. "Two more from my team. Liaigh confirms no additional hostiles in the immediate area."

*Liaigh confirms.*

James's tactical mind flagged the phrase. Filed it for later. There were more immediate concerns.

"You took that last one with a throwing knife at fifteen feet," he said. "While he had me at execution position."

"Yes."

"That's not a shot anyone makes. That's not a throw anyone *throws*."

"Most people don't have eight hundred years to practice."

James stared at him.

The strange man stared back.

Something passed between them — a recognition that went deeper than words. The bone-deep acknowledgment of one warrior assessing another and realising that the categories they'd been using no longer applied.

"Right," James said finally. "I think I need a drink. And then I think you need to tell me a very long story."

## VI. THE DEBT REPAID

The whiskey was Redbreast 21 — James's emergency bottle, kept in a cabinet that had survived the firefight through sheer luck and good positioning. He poured three glasses with his right hand while Miriam field-dressed his shoulder with the systematic efficiency of a woman who was using the task to keep herself from falling apart.

"Twenty-one-year single pot still," Cian observed. "A Limerick man's choice."

"Born and raised." James's eyes narrowed. "How did you know I was from Limerick?"

"Your accent. When you're not controlling it. The vowels." Cian accepted the glass. "Also, only a Limerick man would keep Redbreast as his emergency whiskey. A Dublin man would keep Jameson. A Cork man would keep Paddy's. A Limerick man knows better."

James studied him. The tactical assessment that had dominated the last hour was giving way to something else — curiosity, mixed with the particular wariness of a man who had served in rooms where the impossible was a daily occurrence and had learned to keep his mind open.

"You're Irish."

"I was Irish before there was an Ireland."

"That's the second time you've said something like that." James took a long pull of his whiskey. "The knife throw. The way you moved through my kitchen — I saw the bodies as Miriam was patching me up. Professional operators, SAS-tier, taken apart like training dummies. The accent that shouldn't exist anymore."

"Do you want the long version or the short version?"

"I've got a bullet hole in my shoulder and a houseful of dead bodies. I think we've got time for the long version."

Cian looked at Miriam. She was still working on the field dressing — gauze, compression, the careful motions of a woman who was absolutely not going to meet his eyes until she was ready.

"Before I tell him," Cian said quietly, "I owe *you* something. The explanation I was about to give when the alert came."

Miriam's hands stilled.

"You said you'd tell me why you weren't there." Her voice was flat. Controlled. The voice of a woman who had been carrying this question for twelve years and had finally found someone who could answer it. "Why a twenty-six-hundred-year-old immortal warrior with an angel and a magic sword — operating within his own jurisdiction, fighting his own war — let my mother die."

James's eyes widened slightly at *twenty-six hundred years* and *angel* and *magic sword*, but he had the discipline to keep his mouth shut. The Regiment had taught him many things. One of them was knowing when to listen.

"I didn't know about her." Cian's voice was quiet. Steady. "Victoria Ashford née Mizrachi — your mother — was fighting the same war I'd been fighting for twenty-six centuries. The Harlot system. The Synagogue of Satan. The financial infrastructure that connects the ancient evil to its modern manifestations. She was good enough to map what I'd been fighting for millennia and disciplined enough to do it without being caught for fifteen years."

"And you didn't know."

"I didn't know because she was invisible to my operational model." Cian set down his whiskey. His hands were steady, but his voice carried weight. "I fight the supernatural layer. The vampires. The Nephilim remnants. The entities that carry spiritual signatures detectable by angelic intelligence. Your mother was fighting the *human* layer. The shell companies. The banking networks. The paperwork that made the supernatural predation possible."

"Same enemy," Miriam said. "Different fronts."

"Opposite ends of the same system. She was attacking from below — following the money upward, toward the apex predators she couldn't name. I was attacking from above — hunting the apex predators downward, toward the human infrastructure they relied on. We were fighting the same war from opposite directions." His voice roughened. "And neither of us knew the other existed."

James was watching with the focused stillness of a man receiving an operational briefing about matters he hadn't known existed an hour ago.

"When the system decided she was a threat," Cian continued, "they didn't send bankers to stop her. They didn't send lawyers or accountants or the usual human mechanisms of suppression. They sent Vârcolac. A four-thousand-year-old vampire who had been serving the Harlot since before the first Sumerian city-state. A supernatural solution to a human problem."

"And by the time the supernatural asset entered the picture—"

"There was no warning. No lead time. No intelligence that would have crossed my desk. "I CATALOGUED THE SIGNATURE. DID NOT INVESTIGATE FURTHER. THE STANDARD PROTOCOL FOR ISOLATED PREDATION EVENTS IN URBAN AREAS — ONE HUMAN CASUALTY IN A CITY OF NINE MILLION. ROUTINE. BELOW OPERATIONAL THRESHOLD." The angel's voice was heavy with something that might have been shame, if angels could feel shame. "I DID NOT CROSS-REFERENCE THE LOCATION AGAINST ACTIVE INTELLIGENCE INVESTIGATIONS. DID NOT FLAG THE SIGNATURE AS SPECIFICALLY VÂRCOLAC'S. DID NOT RECOGNISE THAT THE VICTIM MIGHT BE... SIGNIFICANT."

What the entire operational architecture of twenty-six centuries failed to account for."

Something shifted in Miriam's expression. Not forgiveness — forgiveness was too simple a word for what was happening behind her eyes. Something more fundamental. A tectonic recalibration of the rage that had been defining her existence for twelve years.

"I couldn't save your mother," Cian said. "I will die before I let it happen again."

"That's the exoneration," she said. "Not in words. In action."

Mo Chrá hummed — warm and fierce and absolutely certain.

James Madigan looked between them — the ancient warrior and the woman who had been his adopted daughter for a decade — and made a decision.

"Right," he said. "I think you'd better tell me the rest. The long version. All of it."

## VII. THE OLD DOG

The long version took four hours.

James listened with the focused stillness of a man receiving the most consequential operational briefing of his career. He asked no questions until the first telling was complete — the soldier's discipline, the operator's patience. *Give me the full picture first. Then I'll pick it apart.*

When Cian finished — the Watchers, the Nephilim, the Acoustic Paradigm, the Oiketerion, the Commission of 586 BCE, the twenty-six centuries of hunting monsters while the world changed around him — James poured another round of whiskey and considered.

"How many of them are there?"

"Nephilim remnants? Thousands, scattered across six continents. Most are minor entities — corrupted bloodlines, diluted heritage, the descendants of creatures that mated with humanity over four millennia. The major threats — the apex predators, the ones with genuine power — number in the dozens."

"Who's in command?"

"The Harlot. Mystery Babylon. The spiritual entity that coordinates the global network through the Synagogue of Satan and its modern manifestations — the WEF, the Club of Rome, the international governance structures that carry their theological fingerprint."

"What are their capabilities?"

"Everything humanity has. Financial networks worth trillions. Political influence across every major government. Media control sufficient to shape public perception at will. And that's just the human layer." Cian paused. "The supernatural layer adds capabilities that most people couldn't process. Acoustic technology derived from pre-Flood sciences. Nephilim-derived entities with physical abilities beyond human limits. Access to knowledge that predates the Sumerian civilisation by millennia."

"What assets do we have?"

"Me. Mo Chrá. Liaigh. Brennan's technical capabilities. Miriam's intelligence network. Josephite contacts scattered across the diaspora — limited operational utility, but useful for sanctuary and logistics. And now—" Cian met James's eyes. "Possibly you."

James's lips quirked — not quite a smile, but the acknowledgment of a recruitment pitch when he heard one.

"And what's the mission?"

"The short version? Delay the Beast system's agenda until the appointed time. Buy humanity time to choose."

"And the long version?"

"The long version would take another four hours and require theological context that you may or may not be prepared to accept."

James considered this.

He was sixty-three years old. His knees hurt on cold mornings. His shoulder was currently held together by field dressing and whiskey and the particular stubborn refusal to die that had characterised his entire career. He had killed four professional operators in his kitchen tonight, watched two more die to a man who moved like no human being he'd ever seen, and just received a briefing that rewrote everything he thought he knew about the nature of reality.

He should be terrified.

He wasn't.

Twenty years in the SAS had taught James Madigan many things. One of them was that the world was larger—and stranger—and more dangerous—than any civilian could imagine. Another was that when someone offered you a mission that mattered, you took it.

"I spent twenty years protecting Miriam from something I couldn't name," he said. "Now you're telling me you've been fighting it for twenty-six centuries." He drained the last of his whiskey. "I'm sixty-three. Bad knees. Dodgy shoulder from a parachute landing in Helmand — and now apparently a bullet hole as well. I'm not what I was."

"Neither am I," Cian said.

James looked at him — really looked, with the assessment eyes that had evaluated soldiers across three decades of special operations. The ancient eyes. The weight in the shoulders. The particular fatigue of a man who had been fighting too long and couldn't stop.

"You're tired."

"I'm exhausted."

"Then you need someone watching your back who knows what they're doing." He set his empty glass on the table. "I'm in. What do you need?"

---

The answer came over the next several hours, as dawn broke over the Scottish Highlands and James Madigan began to understand exactly what he had volunteered for.

He brought capabilities the team hadn't known they were missing.

Modern special operations planning — the doctrine, the methodology, the ISR frameworks that had evolved over decades of asymmetric warfare while Cian had been learning new martial arts and new weapons and new ways to kill things that shouldn't exist. James understood combined arms. He understood battlespace management. He understood the particular mathematics of operating in a world where everyone had satellites and drones and communications networks that would have seemed like magic to anyone who'd fought before 1990.

NATO-adjacent networks — former Regiment contacts across the Five Eyes, retired operators scattered from Hereford to Coronado who asked no questions and delivered results. Men who had been where James had been and done what James had done and who, if he called, would *answer*.

Counter-intelligence expertise — the systematic paranoia of a man who had spent his career in the shadows, who understood surveillance detection and operational security and the thousand small ways that a team could be compromised if they didn't pay attention.

Institutional access — the ability to operate in military and government spaces where Cian's cover identity couldn't reach, where a former SAS sergeant with a spotless record could walk through doors that would slam shut on an unknown consultant with ancient eyes.

And perhaps most importantly: perspective. The everyman's perspective. The mortal man's reaction to angels and demons, grounding the supernatural in human experience.

"Brennan," James said, studying the video feed that Miriam had finally established with their remote technical support. "The one who runs your communications."

"The one who designed the alert system that brought us here tonight."

"He's a civilian."

"He's a contractor. Tech specialist. Drone engineer."

"He's a *civilian*." James's voice carried the particular edge of a man who had trained soldiers for decades and knew the difference between someone who could operate a computer and someone who could operate in combat. "Has he ever been shot at?"

"Not yet."

"Has he ever shot anyone?"

A pause. Cian consulted his memory — all the operations, all the missions, all the moments where Brennan had been on the other end of a communication feed providing technical support while Cian and Mo Chrá did the actual fighting.

"No."

"Then he's a liability." James leaned back in his chair, wincing as the movement pulled at his shoulder. "I saw what you did tonight. Six professional operators, taken apart in thirty seconds. That's not normal. That's not *human* — and yes, I'm aware now that it isn't supposed to be. But Miriam and I can't do what you do. We're mortal. We need backup that bleeds."

"You're suggesting—"

"I'm suggesting that if you're building a team, you build a *team*. Combat-capable assets on the ground who can handle the human threats while you and your magic sword deal with the supernatural ones." His eyes were sharp. Focused. The eyes of a man who had commanded operations across three decades and understood exactly what he was offering. "That button-smasher of yours needs to see a gym. And someone needs to teach him which end of a weapon the bullets come out of."

Miriam's laugh was unexpected — a sharp sound that broke the tension in the room and made both men look at her.

"James teaching Brennan to fight," she said. "I would pay money to see that."

"You won't have to pay. You'll be helping." James turned back to Cian. "She's good. I trained her. But there's gaps in her methodology that need filling, and she's been operating solo for too long. A team needs team training."

"YOU MAKE EXCELLENT POINTS," Liaigh observed.

"Who's that?"

"The angel."

"Right. Yes. The angel." James rubbed his eyes with his good hand. "I'm going to need a lot more whiskey to process the angel."

## VIII. THE VETERAN'S FRAME

Three days later, James Madigan stood in the Austrian safehouse and considered murder.

Not against Cian — the ancient warrior had proven himself in the farmhouse, and besides, killing a man with a magic sword and an angel in his chest seemed like a poor strategic choice. Not against Miriam — she was the reason he was here, the reason he had a bullet hole in his shoulder and a dead kill team in his kitchen and a new understanding of reality that was still settling into place.

Against the exoskeleton that Brennan was trying to fit him for.

"It's the Mark VII MORTIS platform," Brennan was explaining through the video feed, his image projected onto the tactical display while one of Cian's Josephite contacts — a mechanical engineer named Dara who apparently built armour as a *hobby* — made adjustments to the frame. "The same core system we used in the Dunluce operation, adapted for your specifications."

"My specifications include *not dying in this contraption*."

"The standard MORTIS weighs fifty-three kilos," Brennan continued, ignoring the complaint with the particular focus of a man who had been called a button-smasher by an SAS sergeant and was now determined to prove his worth through technical excellence. "We've stripped the heavy assault components and rebuilt around your operational profile. Power-assist primary — hydraulic knee braces, shoulder stabilisation, the load-bearing infrastructure that will compensate for your injury profile."

"You're approaching the weapons placements wrong."

Brennan blinked. "I'm sorry?"

"The dorsal rifle mount." James pointed at the schematic rotating in the holographic display. "That's a single-shooter's solution. One operator, working alone, needs a weapon he can reach over his shoulder. A *team* working together needs covering-fire geometry. If I'm providing support while the magic sword goes after the supernatural threats, I need weapons I can bring to bear without having to reach behind my back."

Cian watched from the corner of the room, Mo Chrá's hum containing an undercurrent of what might have been *told you so*.

"What would you suggest?" Brennan asked.

"Forearm mounts. Quick-deploy SMGs on articulated brackets — I can bring them up while my hands are doing something else. Secondary mount on the hip for heavier loadout. And—" James paused. "You've got an angel in your chest."

Cian raised an eyebrow. "Yes."

"He feeds you tactical information. Enemy positions, threat assessments, all of it directly into your brain."

"HEY," Liaigh said. "I DO MORE THAN THAT."

"Noted. But I don't have an angel." James turned back to the display. "I need eyes in the sky. Something small enough to deploy from the suit, autonomous enough to do its own navigation, with a video feed to a HUD in the helmet."

"A micro-UAS," Brennan said, his eyes brightening with the particular enthusiasm of an engineer who had just been given an interesting problem. "Launched from a forearm mount, gimballed camera, return-to-base capability..."

"Now you're thinking." James's tone softened fractionally — the smallest acknowledgment that the button-smasher might have some value after all. "IFF system. If there's more than one of us on the field, I need to know who's friendly at a glance. NATO-standard comms as backup — your angelic frequencies are impressive, but I can't receive them, and any system with a single point of failure is a system that's going to get someone killed."

"The hardpoints can be adapted," Dara offered. He was a quiet man — heavyset, with the hands of someone who worked with metal for a living — and he spoke with the particular Irish lilt of a Galway native. "The MORTIS chassis is modular. What he's describing isn't incompatible with the core architecture."

"Then let's make it compatible." James turned to the physical frame — the skeleton of carbon fiber and hydraulic actuators that Dara was adjusting. "Quick-release panels. SAS doctrine says you can't take kit into the water that you can't shed in three seconds. If I need to swim — or climb, or crawl through a space that this thing won't fit — I need modular components I can dump without drowning myself."

Brennan was taking notes furiously. "The power system—"

"Distributed. Not centralised. One battery pack takes a round, I want the rest of the suit to keep functioning. Redundancy in everything — hydraulics, communications, sensors. The fancy features are nice, but I've spent forty years operating with kit that breaks in the field, and I'm going to assume this will too."

"THE OLD DOG HATH TEETH," Liaigh observed.

"Damn right I do."

Miriam watched from the doorway, her arms crossed, something approaching a smile on her face. She had seen James in training mode before — the particular intensity that came over him when he was teaching, the refusal to accept anything less than excellence. She had been on the receiving end of that intensity for a year of her life.

Now it was Brennan's turn.

"EW and SIGINT capability," James continued. "My background is signals intelligence — I spent three years attached to 14 Int in Northern Ireland before I made the Regiment. If we're operating against an enemy with communications networks worth intercepting, I want to be able to intercept them."

"We can integrate with Brennan's existing SIGINT infrastructure—"

"No. Not integrate. *Independent*. I want local collection capability that doesn't rely on someone on the other end of a video feed. If comms go down, if Brennan takes a coffee break, if the satellite gets knocked out by whatever supernatural nonsense decides to target us next — I want to be able to collect and process intelligence on my own."

Dara was making adjustments to the frame as James spoke — the quick, confident movements of a man who understood what was being asked and was already solving the engineering problems.

"The weight budget—" Brennan began.

"Is a guideline, not a limit." James pointed at the hydraulic knee assemblies. "Those are rated for how much load assist?"

"Three hundred pounds."

"Then I can carry more kit than you specced. The whole point of the power assist is that I'm not carrying the weight — the *suit* is. Build in fifty percent more capability than you think I need, and I'll figure out what to fill it with."

Cian was smiling now — a small expression, barely visible, but present. He had watched warriors evaluate equipment for twenty-six centuries. He knew the difference between someone who understood their tools and someone who just used what they were given.

James Madigan understood his tools.

"Ward layer," James said. "Whatever protective nonsense you put on Miriam's suit — the stuff that made the ghouls not want to bite her — I want the same. Better, if you can manage it."

"The ward system requires—"

"I don't care what it requires. I care that it works." He fixed Brennan with a look that had reduced SAS recruits to puddles over three decades. "You're the engineer. *Engineer* it."

Brennan, to his credit, didn't flinch. "I'll talk to Dara about the options."

"Good. Now—" James looked at the frame. At Dara. At Brennan's nervous face in the display. "Let's try this thing on."

---

The fitting took two hours.

Adjustment after adjustment. Calibration after calibration. The particular fussiness of a system designed for one body type being adapted for another.

But when it was done — when the last servo had been tuned and the last panel had been locked into place — James Madigan stood in the centre of the Austrian safehouse's tactical room and moved.

And stopped.

And moved again.

His knees didn't hurt.

For the first time since a parachute landing in Helmand Province in 2009 — fifteen years of chronic pain, fifteen years of morning stiffness and evening swelling and the particular grinding sensation of cartilage that had been worn past the point of repair — his knees didn't *hurt*.

The hydraulic assist took the load. The articulated joints moved in perfect synchronisation with his own movements. The weight of the frame — thirty-eight kilos, stripped of the heavy assault components he didn't need — was distributed across his body in a way that let him stand straighter than he had in years.

He took a step. Another. Felt the suit respond to his movements with the seamless precision of technology that had been designed by someone who understood that warriors were not civilians and could not be treated as such.

He laughed.

The sound startled everyone in the room — Miriam, who had never heard James laugh like that; Dara, who was watching his work with the quiet satisfaction of a craftsman seeing his creation tested; Brennan, who had tensed in anticipation of criticism and was now processing something entirely different.

Even Cian looked surprised.

"What is it?" Miriam asked.

"It doesn't hurt." James walked across the room — smooth, easy strides that carried him from one end to the other without the hesitation, the careful weight distribution, the unconscious compensation he had been living with for fifteen years. "My knees. The suit takes the load. It doesn't *hurt*."

Mo Chrá's hum achieved a frequency that conveyed something very close to satisfaction.

"THE VETERAN HATH FOUND HIS FRAME," Liaigh observed.

James turned to face Cian — the ancient warrior, the impossible man, the Irishman who had been alive before Ireland existed. The man who had saved his life in a farmhouse in Scotland and offered him a place in a war older than civilisation.

"I'm in," James said. His voice was different now. Not the calculation of a man weighing options, but the commitment of a man who had made his choice. "Whatever you need. Wherever you need it. I'm in."

Cian nodded once. "Welcome to the team."

"There's conditions."

"Name them."

"One: Miriam's safety is my priority. Whatever the mission requirements, whatever the tactical situation, I'm watching her back first."

"Understood."

"Two: That button-smasher—" James nodded toward Brennan's image in the display. "—learns to fight. I don't care if he never fires a shot in anger. He learns to handle himself, or he doesn't deploy."

Brennan looked like he wanted to protest. The look on James's face made him reconsider.

"THREE WOULD BE AN AWKWARD NOTE TO END ON," Liaigh observed.

"Three." James met Cian's eyes. "Whatever the endgame is — whatever the actual conclusion of this war you've been fighting for twenty-six centuries — I want to know. Not the tactical situation. The *theology*. The stakes. What we're actually fighting for."

Cian was quiet for a moment.

"The stakes are everything," he said finally. "The theology is... complex. But the short version is this: we're buying time. Time for people to choose. Time for the truth to spread before the lies become unchallengeable. Time for the few who can still be saved to make the decision that matters."

"And when do we stop buying time?"

"We don't. That's not our call." Cian's voice was soft now. Steady. The voice of a man who had made his peace with an assignment that would never end — not until the assignment ended him. "We fight until we can't fight anymore. Or until the Commander tells us to stand down."

"The Commander."

"HIGHER AUTHORITY," Liaigh said quietly. "MUCH HIGHER."

James absorbed this.

Forty years of operating in rooms where impossible things happened. Twenty years of protecting a girl from enemies he couldn't name. One night of watching an ancient warrior move through professional killers like they were training dummies.

And now: a war against entities that predated civilisation, conducted by a team that included an immortal with a magic sword, an angel in a breastplate, a woman who had turned grief into weaponry, and a button-smasher who apparently built exoskeletons for a living.

It was, James reflected, exactly the kind of insane assignment that had made him fall in love with the Regiment in the first place.

"Right," he said. "Where do we start?"

Cian looked at Miriam. At James. At the displays showing Brennan's nervous face and Dara's quiet satisfaction.

"We start," he said, "with Mars."
