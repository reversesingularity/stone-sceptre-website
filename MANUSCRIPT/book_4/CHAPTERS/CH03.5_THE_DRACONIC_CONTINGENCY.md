## MOVEMENT I — THE APEX SHIFT

The HCV exoskeleton components occupied most of the safehouse floor the next morning, arranged in the specific order that told Cian what he needed to know without making him say it aloud. Right pauldron assembly: unsalvageable — the structural failure had propagated to the power coupling root, and rebuilding it would require fabrication resources and a three-week lead time that neither of them had. Left lateral plate: fractured through at the load-bearing joint, stress-fractured rather than shattered, which meant the rest of the composite had absorbed more than it was rated for before that one finally gave. Power system: at eleven percent and dropping in its resting state, which meant a thermoelectric fault in the distribution matrix, which meant the suit would need a full chassis strip to diagnose.

He assessed all of this in the time it took him to drink the first cup of tea. Then he set the cup down and arrived at a state he had been accumulating toward across the previous seven hours.

It was not a decision. It was not a calculation he ran and reported back to himself with a conclusion. It was something older — the cognitive fossil surfacing from whatever stratum it lived in when it was not needed, the layer that had been laid down across 2,636 years of wars that did not have names in any surviving record. The layer that knew, without requiring analysis, when a tactical situation had changed so fundamentally that the previous operational framework was no longer a guide but a liability.

He assumed command of the 1,260-day siege.

Not with a statement. Not with a speech. He was not a man who made speeches about the things that were true — you either knew them or you didn't, and explaining them to people who didn't was a category of effort that the 1,260-day timeline did not have room for. He simply occupied the role the way the bedrock of Jerusalem occupied the ground beneath the city: absolutely, without announcement, load-bearing.

Miriam was at the communications board running a frequency analysis of the waveform she had pulled from the HCV telemetry. She had been there since 0600 and had built a spectral decomposition of the 17.2 Hz dread broadcast that was, technically speaking, the most thorough characterisation of a Watcher-derived EW system that any human analyst had ever produced. She was performing competence and she knew she was performing it and she was doing it anyway, because the GCHQ-trained part of her brain had decided that competence was the correct response to what had happened to her on Plugat HaKotel and it was not entirely wrong.

Cian looked at the spectral decomposition. Then he looked at her.

"How long to replicate the effect on an untrained civilian?" he asked.

She blinked. "What?"

"The physiological override. If you deployed the waveform against an unprotected target, someone with no foreknowledge of the mechanism, how long before complete motor control failure?"

She calculated. "Against an untrained civilian? Four to six seconds at that amplitude. The autonomic cascade runs faster without voluntary interference."

"And your training reduced that to what?"

A beat. "I maintained motor control for approximately twenty seconds before losing leg function."

"And you maintained cognitive function throughout."

"Yes."

"Your GCHQ training," Cian said, "was designed for the threat environment that existed before 2027. Human adversaries, human EW systems, human acoustic warfare doctrine. The counter-surveillance protocols you learned are irrelevant against an entity that does not need line of sight and does not use radio frequencies. The anomaly-detection framework you were trained on has no category for a cloaking architecture that operates through satellite position pings at 17.2 Hz. The physical conditioning you built — four hours a day, every day, excellent by human standards — does not affect the mammalian stress-response system at the cellular level."

He was not angry. He was not cruel. He was simply telling her what was true, with the clinical precision of a man who had watched people die from the gap between what they trained for and what they faced, and had decided long ago that the kindest thing was accuracy.

"Everything you learned at GCHQ was built for human adversaries," he said. "We are not fighting human adversaries."

Miriam sat with that. She was good at sitting with things that were true — it was one of the things he had noted in the first week, the capacity to receive accurate information and let it revise the model rather than defend the model. She was GCHQ-trained, which meant she had been trained to revise.

"All right," she said.

"The three changes are these." He moved to the board and pulled up a tactical overlay of their last contact. "First: counter-surveillance protocols from this point forward are acoustic, not visual. You are looking for phase shifts, not shadows. The entity does not cast a visual signature. It casts a frequency distortion at the boundary of its cloaking field. You were standing two metres from it before it revealed itself. At two metres, you were inside the detection threshold. You need to be outside forty metres with a passive acoustic sensor running before any approach to an objective."

She was already noting it.

"Second: the physiological override. It is not psychological. You cannot overcome it with will. The GCHQ-trained part of you was fully operational the entire time — I could see it in your face. Your body was not responding to your mind. This means training to resist it is the wrong category of response. The correct response is pre-deployment architectural protection. We will address that."

"And third," she said.

"Third." He looked at the HCV components on the floor. "You need to understand what we are fighting. The entity that paralysed you last night has been operational for a period of time that does not fit in any human category for 'long time.' It has been doing this since before every institution that trained you existed. Your training was built on the assumption that the adversary is at roughly the same level of accumulated experience as the defender. It is not. We are not. What I bring to this from 2,636 years is the only experiential database that is relevant, and you need to be able to interface with it in real time, not process it in retrospect."

He paused.

"The previous operational framing," he said, "was two professionals working together. The revised operational framing is a command structure. I give the call. You execute it. Questions after, never during. This is not a reflection of capability. It is a reflection of what the threat requires."

Miriam looked at the table for a long moment.

"All right," she said again. And then, without any visible transition, with the same level tone: "What's the first problem?"

He picked up the spectral decomposition she had built and looked at it properly.

"The body-commitment posture," he said. "The infrasonic shockwave. It had a tell. 0.15 seconds before each propulsive dash, the chest expanded into the rear stance — weight shifted, posture locked. I didn't know what it meant the first time. Now I do." He set the display down. "We are going to find out if that 0.15-second window is consistent across multiple instances. If it is, there is a 0.3-second firing window after the shockwave lands and before the next dash cycle completes." He looked at her. "0.3 seconds is not much."

Mo Chrá, dormant in her scabbard against the wall, gave a single low note.

"No," Cian agreed. "It's enough."

## MOVEMENT II — THE SIMULATION AND THE DUAL-WIELD DOCTRINE

Brennan's voice came in over the encrypted relay at 1100, and the first thing he said was: "The Jerusalem resupply chain is solid. I've got four active couriers and a dead-drop rotation that works. The second thing I need to tell you is that I have seventeen active Josephite safe-houses to manage across five continents and I cannot divert for a week."

"I'm not asking for a week," Cian said. "I'm asking for Guilder and Mercer."

A beat. "What are you building?"

"A simulator."

The silence on the line had the specific texture of Brennan McNeeve considering whether something was possible, which was a different texture from him considering whether something was advisable. "Give me four hours," he said, and the line dropped.

Chris Guilder came online from the McNeeve Space Systems Auckland facility at 1500. Dr. Siorse Mercer joined from the flight dynamics lab forty minutes later, and Cian knew within two minutes of hearing her work through the problem that she was the right person for it: she spoke in kinematic vectors the way a musician spoke in intervals, natively, without translation, and she moved from the sensor telemetry Cian fed her to the first predictive model in under twenty minutes.

"I'm going to need the full contact dataset," she said. "All three attack instances. If the tell is consistent, I'll see it in the closing-speed signature."

He fed her the HCV data. She was quiet for nine minutes.

"Three instances," she said. "0.15 seconds. Look—" and she put the overlay on the shared display, three waveforms aligned, the infrasonic spike preceding the propulsive dash in three separate exchanges with a variance of less than four milliseconds. "It's not just consistent. It's *mechanical*. The same pre-dash pneumatic load sequence every single time. Like a bow being drawn." She paused. "Has it ever needed to change this pattern?"

"No," Cian said. "In four thousand years, it has not needed to change this pattern."

"Then it hasn't," she said simply. The flat factual statement of a flight dynamics specialist who understood that bodies optimised by long operation toward their own efficiency. "Your firing window is 0.3 seconds from the moment the shockwave lands. The dash cycle completes at 0.3 and the next loading sequence begins. That's your window."

Chris Guilder had been running the haptic simulator architecture in parallel — a real-time physics model built on Scáthgheal's HCV sensor telemetry, the closing-speed data, the kinetic mass readings from each exchange. "I can give you force feedback in the bracer and the grip," he said. "Not perfect, but calibrated to the encounter data. You'll feel something close to the actual load."

"Build it," Cian said.

The simulator went live at 1830. Cian ran the first iteration cold, without preparation, purely to see what the data felt like in practice rather than theory. The haptic feedback was not perfect — it was approximately 70% of the actual kinetic load, Guilder had been honest about that — but it was enough. The 0.15-second tell landed in the haptic shoulder system as a specific compression event, distinct from the other signals in the combat sequence. Distinct enough.

He ran it again. And again.

Brennan came back on the relay at 2100, monitoring the telemetry from Stewart Island where the global Josephite logistical network ran through servers that Cian had insisted on placing in the most geopolitically boring location in the Southern Hemisphere, which Brennan had agreed was New Zealand's single greatest strategic asset. "You're running it differently each time," Brennan observed.

"I'm finding the variance."

"Is there any?"

"Three instances of 4-millisecond variance. Below perceptible in real-time combat."

"So no variance."

"Effectively none."

"That seems like it should be harder to exploit than it sounds."

"It is," Cian said. "The 0.3-second window is the window for a single round, aimed, from a weapon I don't yet have, at a target moving through the back half of a propulsive dash at approximately forty kilometres per hour in a direction determined by the tactical geometry at the moment of launch. From a distance of fifteen to twenty metres, which is the minimum safe distance for the creation harmonic to operate at, which means the round needs to cover that distance in 0.3 seconds." He paused. "Point-five-hundred Smith and Wesson Magnum achieves that at this range."

Brennan was quiet for a moment. "Where are you going to hold a .500 Mag while you're running Mo Chrá in the other hand?"

"Mo Chrá docks," Cian said. "The adaptive sheath. I've been thinking about it. If the sheath can flow to the scabbard, it can flow to the forearm. Bracer form, left forearm. Mo Chrá anchored and active, generating the creation harmonic field to maintain the cascade against the target — that continues. The right hand takes *An Ord* and fires in the window."

The silence on the relay had a different texture now. The texture of a man who had spent a significant portion of his professional career making things work in the field and was now processing whether this particular thing would actually work in the field.

"JW Chen," Brennan said finally. "She did the adaptive sheath integration in Book 2. She'll know whether the sheath can sustain a bracer configuration under combat load."

"Get her."

He ran three hundred more iterations across the next eighteen hours. By the end of the first hundred, the 0.15-second tell was automatic — his body had learned it as a signal the way a submariner learned the specific frequency of a torpedo acquisition lock. By the end of the second hundred, he could fire a simulated round in the window consistently at a success rate above seventy percent. By the end of the third hundred, it was above ninety.

"That's not much," Brennan said, watching the telemetry from Stewart Island.

"I have fought in smaller windows for 2,636 years," Cian said.

## MOVEMENT III — AN ORD

The frame arrived in a diplomatic pouch routed through the Josephite network's Istanbul relay, which had taken Hal Okonkwo four days to machine to the specifications Cian had transmitted. It was heavier than a production .500 S&W Magnum frame should have been, because Okonkwo had solved the recoil problem not by lightening the frame but by increasing its mass at the grip and barrel junction in a configuration that distributed the felt recoil across a larger contact area.

"The standard frame," Okonkwo said from his workshop in Lagos, where the background sound was a lathe that had not stopped running in the four days since Cian's specifications arrived, "is not built for what you are going to do with it. The recoil signature from a .500 Mag round at full load is approximately 75 foot-pounds. In a standard production frame, that is manageable for a trained shooter. In a combat configuration where the primary weapon is in the other hand and the platform is not stable—" He did not finish the sentence. He picked up the modified frame and held it in front of the camera. "This is different. The mass distribution compensates at the receiver. You will feel it in the forearm, not the wrist."

Cian turned it over in his hand. The weight was correct. The balance was not perfect — nothing with a barrel that long could balance perfectly — but it was honest. It told him the truth about what it was.

"Twelve rounds," Okonkwo said. "That is what the integration will carry. The Tintreach Mk III casing diameter does not permit a conventional magazine configuration. Twelve is the architectural limit."

"I know."

"Cian." Okonkwo had been in this war since before it had a name. He had machined half the bespoke hardware that had come out of the Vârcolac campaign. He had not called Cian by name in three years of working together. "I want to be certain you understand that twelve is not resupplable."

"I understand."

"The piezoelectric crystal cores are single-manufacture. The crystal growth process takes eight weeks minimum. We cannot accelerate it. We have twelve finished rounds. When those twelve are gone, there are no more."

"I understand."

Okonkwo was quiet for a moment. "All right," he said.

Marcus Reeves came online from his router address in Austin, and his voice had the specific quality that Cian had come to associate with a certain category of intelligence: the intelligence of a man who had spent decades listening to the ocean and had learned to think in frequency signatures. He did not approach *An Ord*'s tuning problem from the direction a gunsmith would have approached it. He did not have a gunsmith's vocabulary for it. What he had was the vocabulary of a sonar operator who had hunted Soviet submarines by the acoustic signature of their propeller cavitation at forty miles, and he applied it to the muzzle blast problem the way he had once applied it to a blade's worth of water in the North Atlantic.

"The Tintreach Mk III has a piezoelectric core that resonates at a specific frequency when it impacts the Cydonian metamaterial lattice," he said. "The muzzle blast from a .500 Mag is, by default, a broadband acoustic event — a lot of energy across a wide frequency range. Most of that energy is wasted relative to the payload's resonance profile. What we want is for the muzzle blast to be tuned to complement the crystal's resonance frequency rather than create acoustic interference with it."

"Can you do that?" Cian asked.

"I did it once before. The anti-Mo Chrá ward formula was encoded in the acoustic resonance pattern of Voivode Dragan's hilt. Most people would have heard it as metal under stress. I heard it as data." A pause. "Same principle. I'm not optimising the blast for decibel level. I'm optimising it for frequency signature."

He worked for six hours with the round specifications and the frame geometry and the barrel length Okonkwo had machined, running acoustic models against the Cydonian metamaterial frequency data that Enoch had extracted from the Empyreal Register's pre-Flood archive records. Cian watched the models run and said nothing. This was not work he could accelerate by contributing to it. This was the work of a very specific kind of mind operating in its area of complete competence.

"There," Reeves said at the end of the sixth hour. He put the tuned barrel specification on the shared display. "The muzzle crown geometry and the porting pattern. When Hal machines this, the blast at the muzzle will have a frequency signature that constructively interferes with the Mk III crystal resonance at impact. You're not losing energy — you're converting broadband into targeted."

Okonkwo took the specifications and said nothing for forty seconds. Then: "Three days."

Cian looked at the frame in his hand. Twelve rounds. No resupply. Ever.

The mathematics of the engagement against Scáthgheal: he needed 8 to 12 seconds of sustained Mo Chrá cascade to reach critical mass in the field. In those 8 to 12 seconds, Scáthgheal would fire the infrasonic shockwave once, possibly twice. Each shockwave created 0.3 seconds of vulnerability in Scáthgheal's dash cycle. Each *An Ord* round, if it struck and the piezoelectric payload functioned against the Cydonian lattice, would degrade the metamaterial reinforcement incrementally — how much per round was unknown, but the target was reduced structural cohesion, which would reduce the time to cascade critical mass, which would reduce the number of shockwave cycles Cian had to survive to reach the endpoint.

Twelve rounds. For 1,260 days.

He could afford to fire approximately one round per hundred days of ministry, averaged. Engagements with Scáthgheal would not average. They would cluster.

"Twelve prayers," Brennan said from Stewart Island.

Cian put the frame down on the table. He did not say anything. He had accepted the arithmetic before Okonkwo had told him the number, because the arithmetic of what they were doing — the specific, bounded, ferociously constrained arithmetic of operating with no supply chain, no institutional support, no reinforcements, and a 1,260-day hard clock — was the arithmetic he had signed for when he accepted the commission, and accepting it cleanly was the only posture from which anything useful could be done.

He began preparing the next set of simulator parameters.

## MOVEMENT IV — THE ADAPTIVE BRACER AND THE MYSTERY

The patch came from Dr. JW Chen's terminal in Austin at 0347 on the fourth morning, and it was twenty-three lines of adaptive sheath control code with annotations in a font so small it required enlarging twice to read. The annotations explained, in the precise engineering language of a woman who had spent two years of her professional life making Cydonian composite behave in ways it had not been designed to behave, why the bracer configuration was structurally feasible and what the load tolerances were and where the failure modes lived.

Cian read the annotations. Then he read them again. Then he issued the command.

Mo Chrá's adaptive sheath liquefied.

It was not a slow process — not the gradual settling of a material remembering a different form. It was immediate, purposeful, a transition that had the quality of something that had been waiting to be asked. The Cydonian composite that had held the sword's edge for nineteen hundred years in scabbard configuration flowed up through the grip channel, past the guard, and down Cian's left forearm in a cascade of interlocking segments, each one locking into the next with the specific mechanical precision of a material that had been machined under conditions no extant human technology could replicate. It covered the forearm from wrist to elbow, the segments conforming to the musculature beneath with a fit that was not quite comfortable and was not trying to be comfortable — it was trying to be functional, and functional it was.

The Cydonian composite hummed.

Faintly, at a frequency just below the threshold of conscious hearing — not Mo Chrá's 432.7 Hz operating frequency, something lower, more structural, the baseline resonance of the material itself in a configuration where it was in continuous contact with the sword's pommel through the grip that now rested against the underside of his forearm. The same material, two different forms, recognising each other. The passive trickle-charge property that Enoch had documented in the Antarctic expedition records, the property by which Cydonian composite in proximity to Mo Chrá's active field received a fractional increment of creation harmonic — this was that property, running quietly in the background, permanent now.

"The contact geometry is stable," Chen said from Austin. "The sheath is drawing from the same power system as the HCV bracer slot. If the HCV power system fails, the bracer goes passive — it will hold structural form but the trickle-charge property stops. It's not independent."

"Understood."

"And the weight distribution?" Her voice had the particular quality of an engineer who wanted the field data and was being politely persistent about it.

"Correct," Cian said. He raised his forearm, ran through the range of motion he needed for Mo Chrá's combat arcs in bracer configuration, and found nothing that limited what he required.

Chen was quiet for a moment. "All right," she said. "I'm glad it works."

He was looking at the bracer, considering the next step, when he became aware that Elijah was in the room.

He had not heard the prophet enter. He was not certain Elijah had used the door. What he noticed was a change in the room's register — not the acoustic register, which was what he would have noticed first under normal circumstances. The deeper register. The quality of the air. The sense that the space had been altered by the presence of something that was not operating at the level of physics.

Elijah was standing beside him. He had not announced himself. He did not announce himself. He looked at the bracer for a moment and then raised his hands and placed them on the Cydonian composite surface, and the room changed.

Cian had been in the presence of Elijah's prophetic authority before. He had been present when the prophet had spoken in the Empyreal register, when the uncreated fire had moved through a room and left scorch marks in the air. This was not that. This was quieter than that and heavier than that and it filled the room the way the presence of deep ocean fills a submersible — you did not hear it, you felt the pressure of it at every structural joint.

The fire was not bright.

It was *present*. The uncreated fire — not created energy, not the combustion of matter releasing light, but the raw luminous authority of something that had never been part of the created order and was operating through it now — arrived at the surface of the Cydonian composite and touched it.

The composite changed.

Something in its structure settled. Or locked. Or resolved. The hum shifted — altered, incrementally, in a direction that had no name in any acoustic vocabulary Cian had access to. He was standing with his forearm in the prophet's hands watching a material that was made from Martian ore mined before the Flood respond to the touch of uncreated fire, and he did not understand what was happening to it, and he did not ask.

He was the Shield of the Prophets. His function, in this moment, was to receive.

Elijah lowered his hands. He looked at Cian for a moment — the look of a man who has done a thing and knows what he has done and has no intention of explaining it — and then he stepped back and the room's register changed again, slowly, back toward the ordinary.

Cian looked at the bracer.

The Cydonian composite looked back at him. The hum was still there. It was not the same hum it had been four minutes ago.

He did not ask what had changed. He would find out when he found out, and he would find out in the field, which was where he found out everything that mattered, and that was correct.

He picked up *An Ord* from the table. He dry-fitted the grip against his right palm. The balance was honest and the weight was correct and there was nothing more to assess until Okonkwo's machined barrel arrived.

Twelve rounds. A bracer carrying an uncreated secret. A sword that had been named by a dying man on a street in Constantinople while the city burned.

He began running the next simulator iteration.

## MOVEMENT V — THE AEGIS VISOR AND THE CODA

Miriam did not wait to be assigned the problem.

She pulled the Ch3 acoustic telemetry at 0900 on the morning after the bracer consecration, and she had the waveform decomposition open on one screen and the Layer Zero orbital infrastructure maps — pulled from Band Two intercepts over the previous two weeks, a slow patient accumulation that she had been building since the first night in Jerusalem — on the other screen, and she was working the problem with the specific quality of focus that Cian had observed in her and had noted as one of the few faculties that transferred directly from the GCHQ environment to this one: the capacity to sit with an unsolved problem for as long as the problem required, without forcing a conclusion.

She called Dr. Anya Petrov first.

Petrov was cybersecurity, three years with the Josephite network, and she had the useful quality of approaching every problem with the assumption that it had a solution and the further useful quality of being right about that more often than probability justified. She was based in a safehouse in Bern and she answered on the second call attempt, which meant she had been awake.

"The cloaking architecture," Miriam said without preamble. "Walk me through how it works from the satellite layer."

Petrov pulled up her own copy of the intercept data. "It's not a passive system," she said after four minutes of analysis. "The cloaking field requires periodic positional recalibration. The entity's position geometry relative to the satellite constellation changes as she moves. To maintain the cloaking field's integrity, she needs to periodically ping the Layer Zero satellite grid for updated positional data. Without those pings, the field drifts and the cloaking loses coherence."

"How periodic?"

"Every three to five seconds, based on the packet signature. Short pings, low power, difficult to detect unless you know what you're looking for."

"And if you know what you're looking for," Miriam said.

Petrov understood where she was going. "If I write a SIGINT virus that catches those ping packets and maps their origin point," she said, "I can triangulate the entity's position in real time. Paint a targeting overlay on whatever HUD is running the virus. Red box at the entity's position, updated every three to five seconds."

"The detection window."

"The ping lasts about thirty milliseconds. If you're scanning in the right frequency band, you'll see it. The virus would need to be deployed close enough to the entity to receive the local ping traffic — you can't do it from a general network intercept, the signal degrades with range."

"How close?"

Petrov ran numbers. "Under thirty metres. Ideally under twenty."

Miriam was quiet for a moment. Twenty metres from an entity that deployed a physiological paralysis broadcast across a four-hundred-metre radius. Twenty metres, invisible, detected by her own sensor architecture long before Miriam could see her.

She called Adrienne Prescott.

Prescott was deep-space communications, which meant she thought about signal propagation at distances that made Earth-based EW feel pedestrian, and she had been in the network since the Antarctic campaign where her work on interplanetary relay had contributed to the Titan fleet coordination. She came online from Wellington and did not say hello because she and Miriam had passed through the period of saying hello six months ago.

"The dread broadcast," Miriam said. "17.2 Hz. I need a counter."

Prescott had already reviewed the spectral decomposition Miriam had sent. "Active noise cancellation," she said. "It's standard acoustic engineering. The broadcast has a specific waveform signature. If I write an ANC algorithm that runs at the HUD audio layer, the moment the algorithm detects the 17.2 Hz signature in the input, it generates a phase-inverted counter-wave at the same frequency and amplitude. The two waves cancel."

"At the audio layer," Miriam said.

"At the HUD audio layer specifically. The physiological mechanism requires the acoustic wave to reach the inner ear and couple with the vestibular system. If the cancellation happens at the ear before the wave couples — yes, the physiological override should fail to complete. You'd feel the onset of the effect and then it would stop."

"Should."

"It's standard physics. Destructive interference at the correct frequency. The uncertainty is in the implementation — my ANC algorithm needs to be fast enough that the cancellation fires before the vestibular coupling occurs. The coupling takes approximately 80 milliseconds. I need the counter-wave to fire in under 60."

"Build it," Miriam said.

She returned to the problem of deployment and she returned to the same conclusion she had reached twice before and not yet said aloud because saying it aloud meant accepting what it implied about who she was in this specific war.

The SIGINT virus needed to be injected within twenty metres of the entity. At twenty metres, the entity's sensor architecture — the Layer Zero orbital grid, the chimeric olfactory integration running through whatever human host she was occupying, the acoustic cloaking system's own environmental scanning — should detect any normal operator long before they reached injection range.

Should detect.

Miriam had been a sensor artefact on the Layer Zero orbital grid since the first night in Jerusalem. She had run the band analysis herself. Her biometric profile in the Band Two frequency range generated an anomaly the grid's classification algorithm flagged as interference rather than a biological signature. Not degraded signal. Not partial detection. Absence. As if the part of her that should have been visible in that frequency band was not there.

Enoch had explained it, once, in the oblique way he explained things that were theological first and operational second. Her spirit-signature was unfallen. The Layer Zero detection architecture had been designed by Azazel's engineering team to map the frequency range of the corrupted human spiritual acoustic profile — Band Two's baseline assumption was that every detected human being would carry the specific resonance pattern of humanity post-Flood, post-Fall in its fully expressed form. Miriam did not carry that pattern. She carried something older and quieter, an acoustic shadow that had no footprint in the band Azazel's sensors were tuned to see.

She was invisible to the system.

Not because she had engineered her way past it. Not because she had a better jammer or a more sophisticated cloaking field. Because of what she was, in the acoustic architecture of whatever she was, in the deepest frequency of however she was constituted, in a way she had not chosen and could not manufacture and did not entirely understand.

The technology required grace to deploy it. The engineering worked only because of who she was.

She pulled up the injection protocol and began writing the deployment procedure.

The implementation took four days. Petrov built the SIGINT virus and tested it against simulated Layer Zero packet signatures provided by the network's intercept archive. Prescott tuned the ANC algorithm through forty-two iterations before the cancellation timing came in under sixty milliseconds consistently across the test waveform. Miriam built the integration layer that ran both systems through the HCV's HUD, the red targeting box and the dread-cancellation firing as a single unified response to the entity's presence.

Brennan came on the relay on the afternoon of the fourth day. He had been monitoring the work sessions in the background between logistics calls — he had the specific quality of a man who managed multiple simultaneous threads by paying fractional but genuine attention to all of them — and he had been quiet about it.

"The deployment requirement," he said. "She has to get within twenty metres."

"Yes," Miriam said.

"Against an entity that has a four-hundred-metre paralysis broadcast and a cloaking system."

"The cloaking system won't see her," Cian said from the other side of the room, where he had been running *An Ord* dry-fire drills through the bracer-simultaneous-firing sequence. "The Layer Zero sensors have no category for her acoustic profile. She has no footprint in Band Two."

Brennan was quiet for a long moment. "So the thing that almost got her killed is what makes her the only person who can deploy it."

"Yes."

"That's—" He stopped himself. Then: "The Draconic Contingency."

The room was quiet.

Mo Chrá gave a single, clear, 432.7 Hz note from her scabbard against the wall. Not a warning. Not an alert. The pure creation harmonic, unsolicited, undirected, present.

The room did not answer it.

*1,258 days remaining.*
