The Prophetic Sequence Is the Plot

Most apocalyptic thrillers use Revelation as a loose inspiration. The Nephilim Chronicles uses it as the clock. Every event in the series is calibrated against the prophetic timeline — the seals, the trumpets, the witnesses, the beast. The enemy roster is not invented: Ohya is the Beast of Revelation 13, the Nephilim son of Shemyaza and Naamah. Azazel is the False Prophet — a Nephilim son of Gadreel, bound in Dudael since before the Flood. The Dragon is Satan. The Whore of Babylon is Naamah, who survived the Flood and has been preparing for the endgame across fifty centuries.

This is eschatological fiction where the identities, the sequence, and the stakes are derived from primary sources — 1 Enoch, Jubilees, the Dead Sea Scrolls, and the text of Revelation itself. If you've wanted apocalyptic fiction that respects the precision of what the prophetic books actually say, this is that series.
