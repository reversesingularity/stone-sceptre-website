The 1,260 Days Begin

Azazel is free. The Edenic Mandate executed. The Two Witnesses have walked out of what was preserved for them and into a world that has no existing framework for their arrival.

Elijah. Enoch. The two who did not die. Held — not hidden, not forgotten, but preserved — for exactly this moment in Jerusalem's history. The 1,260 days that Revelation measured in advance. The testimony that every seal, every trumpet, and every bowl has been building toward. They do not require belief. They do not ask permission. They have already been on record for millennia.

Cian mac Morna is their Guardian. His commission, assigned in 586 BCE on the banks of the Euphrates, has been building to this. Liaigh — depleted from the Dudael operation, his voice increasingly thin — remains the Watcher liaison. Mo Chrá sings in a register she has not used since before the Flood. The Josephites are permanently attached. Something in their operational doctrine always suspected it would come to this.

On the other side of the equation: Ohya — Apollyon, the Beast — has a face the world finds plausible and a solution to every crisis the Two Witnesses are causing. The crisis it experiences as worse than the one it inherited. Azazel moves through structures built for him across twelve centuries of preparation. The Dragon coordinates from behind the architecture of everything.

The Jerusalem Indictment is not a verdict. It is a declaration. Heaven does not request that the nations respond. It requires that they hear.

THE JERUSALEM INDICTMENT is the fourth volume in The Nephilim Chronicles — a five-book biblical apocalyptic thriller drawing on 1 Enoch, the Book of Jubilees, and the prophecies of Revelation. For readers of Frank Peretti, Joel C. Rosenberg, and anyone who has read Revelation 11 and felt the weight of the appointed hour.

About the Author

Kerman Gild brings together extensive research in biblical prophecy, ancient Jewish apocalyptic literature, and Celtic history to craft The Nephilim Chronicles. Drawing from primary sources including the Book of 1 Enoch, the Book of Jubilees, the Dead Sea Scrolls, and detailed biblical chronology, the author weaves together theological precision and narrative compulsion.

The Jerusalem Indictment continues the series' investigation of the prophetic sequence through the lens of Revelation's witness testimony — now extended into the contested streets of a Jerusalem at the centre of the world's final crisis. The Two Witnesses, the Beast, and the Guardian who has been waiting twenty-six centuries for this commission are all drawn from careful study of Second Temple Judaism, early Christian sources, and the eschatological tradition surrounding Revelation 11.

Kerman Gild is also the author of The Stone and the Sceptre Chronicles, a companion series tracing the covenant promises from 586 BCE through the establishment of Celtic kingdoms in the western lands.
