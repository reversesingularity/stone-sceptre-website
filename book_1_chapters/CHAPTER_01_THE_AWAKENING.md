# CHAPTER 1: THE AWAKENING
## 2020 — Austin, Texas

---

Cian mac Morna woke gasping, his hand already closing around the hilt of Methuselah's Sword before his eyes had fully opened.

The blade was *singing*.

Not the subtle hum he had grown accustomed to over two and a half millennia—the background vibration that marked the sword's presence in his mind, constant as a heartbeat and just as ignorable. This was different. This was a frequency he had never heard before, a resonance that seemed to bypass his ears entirely and speak directly to his bones.

He sat up in the darkness of his safehouse bedroom, the sheets pooling around his waist, and stared at the weapon mounted on the wall opposite his bed. Even in the dim glow of the security monitors, he could see it: the blade was *glowing*. A faint luminescence traced the edge, silver-white and steady, as if the sword had swallowed a star and was slowly digesting it.

"What the hell," Cian said, and his voice came out rough with sleep and something else—something that felt uncomfortably like fear.

The sword had never done this. Not once in twenty-six centuries.

And he would know. He had studied the blade obsessively for half a millennium — subjected it to every test his expanding scientific knowledge allowed. X-ray crystallography in the 1920s. Electron microscopy in the 1950s. Spectroscopic analysis that would have made his colleagues at MIT weep with envy. The metallurgical structure defied everything he understood about atomic arrangement. The iron-carbon lattice existed in a configuration that shouldn't be stable at room temperature — shouldn't be stable at *any* temperature. And yet.

He threw off the covers and crossed the room in three strides, reaching for the hilt. The moment his fingers touched the leather wrapping—worn smooth by Methuselah's hands before his own, and before that by the hands of Enoch himself—the images slammed into him again.

*Mars. Red stone. Darkness older than Earth.*

*Two hundred voices raised in oath.*

*A woman's smile. Calculation in her eyes. No fear.*

*"You are mine."*

Cian jerked his hand back as if the hilt had burned him.

"LIAIGH."

His voice cracked on the name. He hadn't called for his guardian like that—with genuine alarm—in centuries. The last time had been during the cholera outbreak in London, 1854, when he'd watched a child die in his arms and felt something inside him finally break.

**"I AM HERE."**

The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, as it always did. No visible manifestation. No convenient apparition for Cian to glare at. Just that eternal presence, hovering at the edge of perception like heat shimmer over summer pavement.

"What is happening to me?"

**"THE SWORD HATH CHOSEN TO SPEAK."**

"Speak?" Cian's voice cracked with frustration. "That was not just a *vision*." He forced his breathing to steady, drawing on discipline older than most nations. "I've had visions. I've had thousands of visions. That was something else. That was—" He stopped, searching for words. "That was a *memory*."

**"PERHAPS."**

The single word hung in the air between them. Cian felt his jaw tighten.

"Whose memory? What did I see?"

**"WHEN THE TIME IS RIPE, THOU SHALT KNOW."**

"That's not an answer."

**"IT IS THE ONLY ANSWER I AM PERMITTED TO GIVE."**

Cian looked at the blade again. It was still glowing, still singing that impossible frequency. Twenty-six centuries. He had carried this weapon for twenty-six centuries, and he had always known it held secrets—Methuselah had wielded it for 969 years, and the ancient patriarch had surely experienced things beyond mortal comprehension. But this...

He stared at the sword, his mind racing. The images had been so vivid. Not a vision of the future, not a prophetic warning—a *record*. As if the blade itself had been there.

His materials engineer's mind began assembling the pieces. He had spent centuries analyzing this blade — had discovered that its crystalline structure contained what he'd eventually termed "metallic memory polymers" when he'd published the theory under a pseudonym in 1987. The shape-memory alloys that powered modern aerospace actuators? He'd reverse-engineered the principles from Mo Chrá's shifting properties. The self-healing steel alloys that Brennan's company was now pioneering? Cian had understood the concept three hundred years ago, watching the sword repair microscopic fractures after battle.

But this was different. The sword wasn't just *remembering* stress patterns in its crystalline lattice. It was remembering *events*. Storing experiential data in atomic structure like a geological record compressed into metal.

**"HAST THOU EVER NOTICED,"** Liaigh murmured, **"THAT THE BLADE DOTH PREEN WHENEVER THOU GIVEST HER THY SCHOLARLY ATTENTION? SHE LOVETH BEING STUDIED."**

Cian ignored him. "The sword," he said slowly, realization dawning. "The metal itself. The isotope ratios never matched terrestrial sources. I assumed contamination from the forging process — some unknown flux or quenching agent. But that was never the answer, was it? The ore itself. Where did Enoch find it?"

Liaigh said nothing.

"You're not going to help me, are you?"

**"THE LORD HIMSELF SHALL REVEAL IT TO THEE, IN HIS TIME. YET... THY REASONING DOTH NOT ERR."**

Cian ran a hand through his hair—black as it had been at twenty-eight, unmarked by the millennia that should have aged him to dust. The vision replayed in his mind: the red stone of Cydonia, the Watchers' council, the woman's calculating smile. And something else—a gate. A pathway of light connecting two worlds.

*Jacob's Ladder*, he thought. The Planar Gate. The passage the Watchers had used to descend from their heavenly station to the summit of Hermon.

And when they descended, they would have brought traces of Cydonia with them. Dust on their garments. Fragments of stone. Loose debris from the red planet, scattered across the landing site like seeds.

The anomalous isotope ratios suddenly made sense. He had identified trace signatures in the blade's composition that matched no known terrestrial deposit — iron-57 abundances that were off by three standard deviations, chromium isotopes that suggested formation under conditions impossible on Earth. He had spent two centuries assuming the data was corrupted. Now he understood: the data was perfect. His baseline assumptions were wrong.

The metal wasn't *from* Earth.

"Stone has memory," Cian murmured, half to himself. "Even the rocks cry out to testify."

He looked at the blade with new eyes. Enoch had been a scribe, a prophet, a man who walked with the Almighty. He would have known. He would have understood that the materials from Cydonia—obsidian and iron ore carried across the void on angels' feet—held within them an imprint of everything they had witnessed.

"Enoch retrieved the debris from Hermon," Cian said, the pieces falling together with the terrible clarity of a theorem finally proven. "The rocks the Watchers brought through the Gate. He extracted the ore — recognized the extraterrestrial signature, understood that material which witnessed the conspiracy would carry testimony. He forged this blade from metal that was *there*. On Mars. At the council."

Liaigh remained silent.

"Am I wrong?"

**"I CANNOT SAY."**

"Cannot? Or will not?"

**"BOTH, CHILD OF MORNA."**

Cian's jaw tightened. Twenty-six centuries of service, of hunting, of bleeding and killing and bleeding again—and still his guardian kept secrets from him.

"What am I supposed to do with this? Why is the sword showing me this *now*?"

**"PERHAPS THE SWORD KNOWETH THINGS THAT I DO NOT."**

---

The safehouse was a nondescript single-story ranch house in the Austin suburbs, the kind of property that real estate agents described as "having potential" and everyone else described as "forgettable." Cian had chosen it precisely for its forgettability. No one looked twice at a young tech consultant living alone in a neighborhood full of families and retirees. No one wondered why he kept unusual hours or occasionally left for weeks at a time.

The security was something else entirely.

Cian moved through his morning routine on autopilot while his mind churned. Weapons check: the throwing knives in their concealed sheaths, the backup pistol in the nightstand, the katana hidden in the false wall of the closet, the crossbow in the attic. Perimeter check: the motion sensors, the pressure plates, the cameras covering every approach. News scan: scrolling through six different feeds—mainstream, alternative, dark web—looking for patterns that might indicate Nephilim activity.

*Stone has memory.*

The words echoed in his mind as he stood in his kitchen, drinking coffee that he didn't taste. He had built three careers on the principles he'd extracted from Mo Chrá's impossible metallurgy. The shape-memory alloys. The self-healing microstructures. The piezoelectric resonance effects that he'd published as "Morna's Principle" in 1962, attributing the discovery to a fictitious great-grandfather. Every major materials science breakthrough of the twentieth century had its roots in his sword — he'd simply had to invent the theoretical frameworks to explain what he already knew empirically.

But this? This was beyond metallurgy. If he was right—if the sword's metal had originated on Mars, transported through the Planar Gate by the descending Watchers—then the blade had been silently carrying a record of the Watchers' conspiracy for five thousand years. The crystalline structure wasn't just holding phase-transition memory. It was holding *experiential* memory. Data encoded at a level of atomic precision that made quantum computing look like an abacus.

Waiting. For what?

*For someone to listen*, he thought. *For the right moment to speak.*

But why now? What had changed?

The Two Witnesses. The phrase surfaced unbidden from his years of study. Enoch and Elijah, the two prophets who had been taken to heaven without dying, preserved across millennia for their role in the end times. He had heard the prophecies. He had read Revelation. He understood the theology.

Was this connected? Was something about to begin?

"LIAIGH."

**"AYE."**

"The Two Witnesses. Enoch and Elijah. Are they coming back?"

**"PERHAPS."**

"When?"

**"WHEN THE FATHER WILLS IT. THAT HOUR IS NOT FOR ME TO KNOW."**

Cian set his coffee cup down with more force than necessary—too much force. The mug tipped, and scalding liquid sloshed over the rim and onto his hand.

"*Agh*—!" He jerked back, shaking his hand, and the mug completed its betrayal by rolling off the counter and shattering on the floor. Coffee spread across the tile like a crime scene.

Cian stared at his reddening hand. Then, very deliberately, he adopted the most pathetic voice in his arsenal—the voice of a petulant child who had stubbed his toe and wanted the whole world to know about it.

"Owieee." He held up his hand toward the ceiling, lower lip pushed out, eyes wide and glistening with theatrical tragedy. "Liaigh. *Liaigh.* My hand hurts. It hurts *so much*. I think I'm dying. Peeeease heal me. Pretty peeeeeease. I'll be good. I'll be *so* good. I'll say my prayers and everything."

Silence.

Absolute, thunderous silence.

"I said *please*," Cian added, wiggling his burned fingers pathetically. "Twice. With the 'ee' sounds and everything. That has to count for something."

**"...THOU ART TWO THOUSAND SIX HUNDRED AND THIRTY-ONE YEARS OLD."**

"And my hand *hurts*. I'm practically an infant in angel years. Have some compassion."

**"THOU HAST SURVIVED GIANT AXES. DRAGON FIRE. THE BLACK PLAGUE. AND THOU DOST WHIMPER OVER *COFFEE*?"**

"It was *really* hot coffee. Scalding. Molten. Practically volcanic. I might never recover."

**"I HEAL THEE DAILY FROM WOUNDS THAT WOULD SLAY MORTAL MEN. NEVER ONCE HAST THOU THANKED ME."**

"I'm thanking you now. In advance. *Pleeeease*, Liaigh. Pretty please with honey on top."

Another pause. Cian could *feel* the exasperation radiating from the invisible presence—a sensation he had learned to recognize over millennia, like the pressure drop before a storm.

Then, with what could only be described as an audible sigh, warmth flooded through his hand. The redness faded. The pain vanished.

"Thank you," Cian said, in his normal voice.

**"THOU ART INSUFFERABLE."**

"And yet you keep showing up."

**"I AM BOUND BY DIVINE MANDATE."**

"Keep telling yourself that."

He grabbed a towel and began cleaning up the coffee, a small smile tugging at the corner of his mouth. Twenty-six centuries, and he could still make his guardian sigh. It was the little victories.

"Now," he said, tossing the towel aside. "The vision. The woman. Who was she?"

**"THE SWORD SHOWED THEE WHAT IT WILLED. THE MEANING IS FOR THEE TO DISCERN."**

"That's not helpful."

**"I AM NOT THY ORACLE, CHILD OF MORNA."**

Cian felt frustration coiling in his chest. "You know something. You always know something."

**"THERE ARE THINGS I KNOW. THERE ARE THINGS I SUSPECT. AND THERE ARE THINGS THAT ONLY THE FATHER KNOWETH—NOT THE SON, NOR THE ANGELS IN HEAVEN. I CANNOT SPEAK WHAT HAS NOT BEEN REVEALED TO ME."**

The sword sang louder, and Cian felt the name rise in his mind like a brand pressed against raw flesh.

*Naamah.*

He saw her face again—the calculation, the predatory smile, the absolute absence of fear as she faced a being who could have unmade her with a thought. He saw her standing among the kneeling women, the only one who had not bowed.

*"You are mine."*

"Naamah," Cian said aloud. The name felt wrong on his tongue, like speaking a curse. "That was her name. In the vision."

Liaigh said nothing.

"The Flood should have killed her. Everyone who wasn't on the Ark—"

**"THE FLOOD DESTROYED ALL WHO DWELT UPON THE LAND."**

Cian waited for more. Nothing came.

"That's it? That's all you're going to give me?"

**"IT IS ALL I *CAN* GIVE THEE."**

He stared at the ceiling, processing. *All who dwelt upon the land.* Liaigh's emphasis had been deliberate. The waters had covered the earth—but what about beneath it? What about the deep places, the caverns, the realms where the Nephilim had supposedly built their kingdoms in the antediluvian world?

"She went underground," Cian said slowly. "Before the waters came. She found a way to survive."

Liaigh remained silent, but the silence felt different now. Affirming.

Cian closed his eyes.

He had spent twenty-six centuries hunting the descendants of the Nephilim. He had killed more of them than he could count—bloodline carriers, possessed vessels, demon-ridden cultists who served the old powers. He had thought he understood the enemy. He had thought he was winning, slowly, generation by generation, reducing their numbers, limiting their influence.

But if his theory was correct—if the woman from the vision had survived the Flood, if she had endured for five thousand years in the deep places of the earth—then everything Cian had accomplished was nothing more than pruning branches while the root grew stronger.

"Twenty-six centuries," he said, and his voice was very quiet. "I've been fighting for twenty-six centuries. And I'm only now starting to see the shape of what I'm fighting against."

**"THOU HAST NOT BEEN ALONE."**

"You know what I mean."

**"AYE. I KNOW."**

Silence stretched between them—the comfortable silence of beings who had shared too much to need constant words. Twenty-six centuries of bickering, of arguments, of Cian's rage and Liaigh's infinite patience. Twenty-six centuries of the angel patching his wounds, guiding his steps, keeping him alive when by all rights he should have died a thousand times over.

Twenty-six centuries, and Cian still didn't know his guardian's true name.

"Why now?" Cian asked finally. "Why is the sword showing me this now, after all this time?"

**"PERHAPS BECAUSE THE HOUR DRAWETH NIGH."**

"The hour of what?"

**"THAT, I CANNOT SAY. WHEN THE LORD WILLS IT, THOU SHALT KNOW."**

Cian felt his jaw tighten. More riddles. More half-answers. But beneath his frustration, something else stirred—a cold certainty forming in his gut. The Witnesses. The woman. The sword's sudden awakening. The pieces were there, even if Liaigh wouldn't—or couldn't—assemble them for him.

*Something is coming*, he thought. *And somehow, I'm meant to be part of it.*

He turned away from the window and looked at the sword again. It had stopped glowing, but the song continued—quieter now, a background hum that seemed almost expectant. Waiting.

For what?

---

The rest of that first day passed in a blur.

Cian went through the motions of his cover life — a conference call with Lockheed about a materials testing issue, a stack of performance reviews that needed signatures, the mundane bureaucracy of maintaining a thirty-four-year career at the world's most secretive aerospace company. He smiled at colleagues. He answered emails. He ate a lunch he didn't taste.

All the while, the name burned.

*Naamah.*

By evening, he had retreated to the safehouse's basement — a space that officially didn't exist, hidden behind a false wall in what appeared to be an ordinary storage room. Three thousand square feet of reinforced concrete, climate-controlled and electromagnetically shielded, filled with the accumulated resources of twenty-six centuries of hunting.

Weapons covered one wall. Swords from every era — Roman gladii, Viking seaxes, Japanese katanas, modern combat blades. Crossbows. Longbows. Compound bows. Firearms ranging from Revolutionary War muskets to prototype directed-energy weapons he'd appropriated from Skunk Works. Each one maintained with the obsessive care of a warrior who understood that the difference between life and death was often measured in microns of edge alignment.

The opposite wall held his research library. Shelves reaching to the ceiling, packed with texts in languages that scholars no longer spoke. Sumerian cuneiform tablets he'd acquired in the 1920s. Akkadian cylinder seals. Fragments of the Book of Enoch in their original Hebrew, purchased from a monastery that no longer existed. Grimoires and demonologies and church records stretching back to the Council of Nicaea.

And in the center of the room, surrounded by computer monitors and analysis equipment, sat his workstation.

Cian pulled up the vision's details on the largest screen, transcribing every fragment he could remember with the precision of a man who had trained his memory over millennia. The red stone. The chamber. The two hundred Watchers in their celestial garments. And Shemyaza's voice, speaking of a woman he had observed from afar — calculating, predatory, *unafraid*.

*You are mine.*

"LIAIGH."

**"AYE."**

"Tell me what you know about the antediluvian cults. Everything."

**"THOU HAST STUDIED THOSE TEXTS FOR CENTURIES."**

"I want to hear it from you. The parts that weren't written down."

A long pause. Then:

**"THE WATCHERS WERE NOT THE ONLY BEINGS WHO COVETED HUMANITY. BEFORE THEIR DESCENT, BEFORE HERMON, THERE WAS ONE WHO HAD ALREADY... TRAFFICKED."**

"Trafficked?"

**"THE ADVERSARY. HELEL. THE FALLEN ONE. HE WAS ALONE IN HIS REBELLION IN THOSE DAYS — THE THIRD PART OF THE HOST WOULD NOT FALL UNTIL MUCH LATER. BUT HE WAS A MERCHANT OF SOULS EVEN THEN. HE KNEW WHAT BEINGS DESIRED, AND HE OFFERED TO FULFILL THOSE DESIRES... FOR A PRICE."**

Cian felt his blood chill. Satan — the original rebel — had been alone in those antediluvian days. No army of fallen angels to command. Just himself and his whispers. But that had been enough. He had trafficked with Eve in the Garden. Why not traffic with her descendants?

"You're saying he made a deal with the Cainites. Before the Watchers descended."

**"I AM SAYING THAT SATAN KNEW WHAT LAMECH DESIRED — POWER, WEALTH, PRESTIGE AMONG THE SONS OF ADAM. AND HE OFFERED TO FULFILL THAT DESIRE. 'I CAN GIVE THEE ALL OF THIS,' THE ADVERSARY WHISPERED, 'IF THOU WILT OFFER THY DAUGHTERS TO ENTICE THE WATCHERS DOWN FROM THEIR STATIONS.' LAMECH ACCEPTED THE TRADE. TUBAL-CAIN FORGED WEAPONS OF WAR. AND NAAMAH..."**

"What about Naamah?"

**"NAAMAH DANCED. SHE WORSHIPPED. SHE PRAYED TO THE CAPTAIN OF THE WATCHERS, KNOWING SHE WAS BEING OBSERVED. HER FATHER INSTRUCTED HER TO DO THIS — TO ENTICE SHEMYAZA DOWN. BUT SHE WAS NO PASSIVE PAWN. SHE HAD PLANS OF HER OWN."**

Cian turned back to his monitors. The name stared back at him from a dozen sources — fragments of text he had collected over centuries without understanding their significance.

*Naamah, sister of Tubal-Cain...*

*The Zohar speaks of Naamah as a demon who seduces men...*

*In Talmudic tradition, Naamah was one of the angels of prostitution...*

*Some sources identify Naamah as the mother of Asmodeus...*

He had read these passages before. He had assumed they referred to demons who had *taken* the name of the antediluvian woman — parasites wearing a stolen identity, as demons so often did. He had never considered that the woman herself might have survived.

*How?*

The question consumed him.

---

The next three weeks established a pattern that would devour the next three years.

Days: maintaining the Skunk Works cover, attending meetings, solving materials problems that no one else could solve, pretending to be Sean Morna — calm, competent, unremarkable.

Nights: hunting. The shedim nests in Houston had gone quiet after his last raid, but there were always more. Corrupted bloodlines in New Orleans. A cult in Memphis that had been sacrificing homeless men to something they called "the Mother." A sex trafficking ring in El Paso that bore all the hallmarks of Naamah's network — the branded victims, the ritualized abuse, the careful documentation of bloodlines.

Dawn: research. Digging through databases, cross-referencing sources, building connection maps that sprawled across entire walls. Who was Naamah? Where had she hidden when the waters rose? What had she *become* in five thousand years of darkness?

The sword gave him nothing more.

He tried everything. Meditation while holding the blade, attempting to replicate the trance state of that first vision. Blood offerings — his own, drawn with Mo Chrá's edge and dripped onto the gleaming metal. Sleep deprivation, on the theory that altered consciousness might open pathways. Fasting. Prayer. Rage.

The blade remained silent.

**"THE SWORD SHOWED THEE WHAT IT WILLED,"** Liaigh said, after Cian's fourth consecutive night without sleep. **"THOU CANST NOT FORCE REVELATION."**

"Then what am I supposed to *do*?" Cian's voice cracked with exhaustion. "I have a name. One name. And no way to find her."

**"PERHAPS FINDING HER IS NOT THY TASK. PERHAPS UNDERSTANDING HER IS."**

"Understanding what? That she's old? That she's patient? That she's been building her network for five thousand years while I've been — what — pruning branches?"

Liaigh said nothing.

Cian hurled the closest object at the wall — a clay tablet from the third millennium BCE, worth approximately two million dollars at auction. It shattered into a dozen pieces, cuneiform fragments scattering across the floor.

He stared at the destruction for a long moment.

"I'm sorry," he said finally. "That was... that was uncalled for."

**"THOU ART TIRED."**

"I'm *frustrated*."

**"THEY ARE THE SAME, FOR THEE."**

Cian knelt and began gathering the fragments. The tablet had recorded a merchant's inventory — grain shipments from Ur to some city that no longer existed. Irrelevant to his search. But still. Twenty-six centuries of preservation, destroyed in a moment of weakness.

"Shemyaza spoke of her at the council," he said, fitting pieces together with hands that didn't shake. "Before the Watchers descended. Before they took wives. He said he had *beheld* her. *Observed* her. From their station on... wherever they were. She wasn't there — but he was watching her."

**"THE WATCHERS OBSERVED HUMANITY FOR A CENTURY BEFORE THEIR DESCENT. THEY SAW EVERYTHING."**

"And she knew she was being watched. She was *performing* for them. For him specifically." Cian's mind raced. "But how did she know? How could a human woman know that celestial beings were observing her from the heavens?"

Silence.

**"LAMECH TOLD HER. THE ADVERSARY TOLD LAMECH. IT WAS PART OF THE TRADE."**

Cian set down the tablet fragments and looked up at the ceiling — the direction he always looked when addressing his invisible guardian, as if Liaigh occupied some space above rather than simply *everywhere*.

"Satan's trafficking. His *rekullah*." The Hebrew word rose unbidden from centuries of study. "He's always been a merchant. A dealer in desires. He offered Eve knowledge. He offered Lamech power. And he offered the Watchers..."

**"WHAT THEY HAD DESIRED FOR A HUNDRED YEARS. WOMEN. OFFSPRING. THE EXPERIENCE OF FATHERHOOD."**

"And his price?"

**"THEIR FALL. HIS FIRST ARMY."**

Cian felt something cold settle in his chest. The logic was terrible and perfect. Satan had been alone in his rebellion since before the Flood — the third of the heavenly host wouldn't fall until much later, at the war with Michael. But he had needed allies. An army. And so he had *trafficked*.

But there was something else. Something Cian's centuries of Torah study should have shown him earlier.

"Why Lamech first?" he asked aloud. "Why not approach Naamah directly?"

**"DOST THOU NOT KNOW THE LAW?"**

Numbers 30. The passage rose in his memory with crystalline clarity. *If a woman vow a vow unto the LORD, and bind herself by a bond, being in her father's house... and her father hear her vow, and hold his peace at her: then all her vows shall stand. But if her father disallow her in the day that he heareth; not any of her vows shall stand.*

"Her father could have voided any oath she made," Cian breathed. "Any covenant with Satan — Lamech could have nullified it on the day he heard of it. 'And the LORD shall forgive her.'"

**"AYE."**

"So Satan went to Lamech first. Made him *complicit*. Made him *instruct* his own daughter to entice the Watchers. By the time Naamah made any oaths of her own—"

**"LAMECH COULD NOT DISALLOW WHAT HE HIMSELF HAD SANCTIONED."**

Cian's mind raced backward, seeing the pattern with terrible clarity. "It's the same thing he did in Eden. Eve ate the fruit. She brought it to Adam. But Adam wasn't deceived — *Timothy says so explicitly*. He knew exactly what he was doing. And if he had refused — if he had exercised his authority as her husband and *disallowed* her vow—"

**"'THE LORD SHALL FORGIVE HER.'"**

"But he didn't refuse. He ate *with* her. He sanctioned her transaction instead of voiding it. And that's why Romans says sin entered by *one man*, not by Eve." Cian stood abruptly, pacing. "This is his MO. This is how he operates. He doesn't just traffic — he *compromises the authority structure* that could void the transaction. He closes the legal loopholes before anyone can use them."

Liaigh's silence was affirmation.

*Immortality*, he thought. *That's what Naamah wanted. What she's always wanted. Not the corrupted half-life of the Nephilim bloodlines. Not the parasitic existence of a shedim riding a human host. True immortality. The kind that survives Floods.*

And if Satan had been trafficking with the entire Cainite line — offering each of them what they desired in exchange for his designs — then Naamah would have had access to knowledge that even the Watchers didn't possess. The Adversary had witnessed everything from the first day of creation. He knew things no one else knew.

"She knew," Cian said slowly. "She knew the Flood was coming. She had time to prepare."

**"THE WATCHERS KNEW. THE NEPHILIM KNEW. MANY OF THEM TRIED TO BUILD SHELTERS, REFUGES BENEATH THE EARTH. ALL FAILED."**

"But she didn't fail."

**"NO."**

"Because she was *smarter*? More prepared? Or because—" The thought made his stomach turn. "Because she had *help*. From something more cunning, more powerful than the Watchers. Something that knew how to survive divine judgment."

Liaigh's silence was answer enough.

---

Three months after the vision, Cian's Lockheed cover began to crack.

He'd been missing meetings. Delegating work he should have done himself. His performance reviews had shifted from "exemplary" to "concerning." Director Dunsmore had called him into the office twice — gentle conversations about work-life balance and the importance of maintaining focus.

Cian had nodded through both meetings, promised to do better, and returned to his real work the moment he was alone.

The connection maps now covered three walls of his basement. Red strings linking photographs and documents, forming a web of relationships that spanned millennia. Every cult he'd destroyed. Every trafficking ring he'd dismantled. Every Nephilim bloodline he'd severed. All of them connected. All of them feeding into the same dark center.

*Naamah.*

She had been there, behind everything. Not always directing — she was too smart for that — but *facilitating*. Enabling. Watching as her network grew stronger while Cian exhausted himself fighting symptoms.

"I've been a fool," he said to the empty room. "Twenty-six centuries of hunting, and I never once stopped to ask *why* they kept coming back."

**"THOU WERT NOT A FOOL. THOU WERT OCCUPIED."**

"Occupied with the wrong things."

**"THERE IS NO 'WRONG' IN OPPOSING EVIL. EVERY NEST THOU HAST DESTROYED, EVERY VICTIM THOU HAST SAVED, EVERY BLOODLINE THOU HAST SEVERED — THESE WERE NOT WASTED."**

"They weren't *enough*."

**"NO. THEY WERE NOT. BUT THEY WERE NECESSARY."**

Cian stared at the connection map, his eyes tracing the red strings to their central convergence point. A blank space. A question mark. The empty center where Naamah should be.

"I need to find her."

**"HOW?"**

"I don't know. But I can't keep doing this. Chasing symptoms while the disease spreads. There has to be a way to—"

His phone buzzed. A text from Director Dunsmore:

*Mandatory meeting tomorrow 9AM. We need to discuss your future with the company.*

Cian looked at the message for a long moment. Then he set the phone aside and returned to his maps.

"Liaigh."

**"AYE."**

"How long do humans typically live? In this era?"

**"SEVENTY-EIGHT YEARS ON AVERAGE. LONGER WITH MODERN MEDICINE."**

"And how long have I been at Lockheed?"

**"THIRTY-FOUR YEARS."**

"Which means Sean Morna is starting to look a bit *too* preserved for a man who should be approaching retirement."

**"THOU HAST ALWAYS KNOWN THIS IDENTITY HAD A LIFESPAN."**

"I thought I'd have more time." Cian ran a hand through his hair — still black, still unmarked by age, still the hair of a twenty-eight-year-old man despite the millennia stacked behind his eyes. "Another decade, at least. Enough to set up the next identity, the next cover, the next carefully constructed lie."

**"AND NOW?"**

Cian looked at the connection map. At the blank space where Naamah should be. At the question that had consumed him for three months and showed no signs of releasing its grip.

"Now I'm starting to think that maybe I've been hiding in the wrong place."

---

Six months after the vision, he stopped sleeping.

Not entirely — even Liaigh couldn't heal the cognitive damage of true sleep deprivation — but close. Two hours a night, sometimes three. Just enough to maintain function. The rest of the time, he worked.

The research had expanded beyond his basement. He traveled — short trips, weekends, using accumulated vacation time that HR kept reminding him he needed to take. London, to access the British Museum's restricted archives. Rome, for the Vatican's sealed collections. Jerusalem, where certain antiquarians remembered his previous identities and were willing to look the other way.

He was looking for patterns.

The cult networks he'd fought for centuries — the temple prostitution rings, the child sacrifice cults, the trafficking operations — they were all connected. But the connections were old. *Ancient*. The same organizational structure, the same ritualized branding, the same careful documentation of bloodlines. It was as if someone had designed a template five thousand years ago and simply... kept using it.

*Because she did*, Cian thought. *She built the system before the Flood and rebuilt it after. The same architecture. The same methods. The same patient, methodical corruption.*

The scale was staggering.

He had thought he was fighting scattered remnants — the degenerate descendants of antediluvian evil, clinging to power through corrupted bloodlines and demonic possession. He had thought the Flood had destroyed the old powers and left only shadows behind.

He was wrong.

The Flood had destroyed the Nephilim giants. It had destroyed the hybrid bloodlines, the corrupted flesh, the physical manifestations of angelic-human transgression. But it had not destroyed the *architect*. And in the millennia since, Naamah had rebuilt everything.

*Not just rebuilt*, he realized. *Improved. Refined. Made more subtle.*

The ancient cults had been obvious — temples of sacred prostitution, public sacrifices, open worship of forbidden powers. Modern evil was different. It hid behind corporations and governments and respectable institutions. It wore suits and spoke in press releases. It had learned to work *within* the system rather than against it.

And it was winning.

Cian looked at his maps, at the red strings connecting organizations and individuals across centuries, and he saw the truth that had eluded him for so long:

*This isn't a war. It's a harvest. And she's been farming humanity for five thousand years.*

---

The vision came again on a Tuesday.

Nine months after the first time, without warning, without the sword's singing to announce it. One moment Cian was reviewing financial records from a suspected trafficking front; the next, he was *there*.

Mars. The red chamber. The council of Watchers.

But this time, he saw more.

He saw Shemyaza — beautiful, terrible, blazing with stolen light — rising to address the assembly. He saw the two hundred swearing their oath, binding themselves to a course that would reshape human history. He saw the woman watching from the shadows, her smile a calculation, her eyes ancient beyond human measure.

And he saw the moment when Shemyaza's gaze found her in the crowd.

*"You should not be here,"* the Watcher chief said.

*"And yet I am."* Her voice was honey and venom, silk wrapped around steel. *"The passage opens for those who know how to ask."*

*"What do you want?"*

*"What I have always wanted."* She stepped forward, into the light, and Cian saw her face clearly for the first time — beautiful, ageless, *wrong* in ways that had nothing to do with her features and everything to do with what looked out through her eyes. *"Partnership."*

The vision shattered.

Cian found himself on his hands and knees, gasping for breath, the sword clutched in his grip so tightly that his knuckles had gone white. The blade was singing again — that impossible frequency, that bone-deep resonance that bypassed his ears entirely.

"Partnership," he said aloud. "She offered them *partnership*."

**"SO IT WOULD SEEM."**

"They were angels. Celestial beings. What could a human woman offer them?"

**"KNOWLEDGE, PERHAPS. THE ADVERSARY HAD TRAFFICKED WITH HER FATHER'S HOUSE FOR GENERATIONS. SHE HAD WALKED FAR DOWN THE PATH OF FORBIDDEN LEARNING. HE HAD TAUGHT HER THINGS THAT EVEN THE WATCHERS DID NOT KNOW."**

"And in exchange?"

**"PROTECTION, PERHAPS. THE WATCHERS KNEW THEIR TRANSGRESSION WOULD BRING JUDGMENT. PERHAPS SHE OFFERED THEM A WAY TO... PRESERVE SOMETHING. TO ENSURE THAT THEIR WORK WOULD CONTINUE EVEN AFTER THEY WERE IMPRISONED."**

Cian thought of the networks. The cults. The careful preservation of bloodlines across millennia. The patient, methodical rebuilding after every setback.

"She's not just running their network," he said slowly. "She's their *successor*. Their agent. Their way of continuing the work even after they were bound."

**"IT IS... POSSIBLE."**

"And the oath sigil on Mars. The mark where they sealed their damnation. That's where—"

He stopped.

The implications crashed over him like a wave.

"That's where they swore the oath. The Watchers. The council chamber where Armaros bound them all with his enchantment. And she was *there*. She saw it happen. And she's—"

**"CIAN."**

"She's *waiting* for them. Five thousand years of building, of preparing, of corrupting humanity's institutions. And when the time comes—"

**"CIAN."**

"—when the Abyss opens, when the binding breaks—"

**"ENOUGH."**

Liaigh's voice carried a weight Cian had rarely heard — the full authority of an archangel commanding silence. He stopped speaking, but his mind continued racing.

The Watchers were imprisoned in the Abyss. That much was clear from Scripture and tradition. The Book of Enoch spoke of them being bound in darkness until the Day of Judgment — not on Mars, but in spiritual chains beneath the earth. The Cydonia chamber wasn't their prison. It was their *crime scene*. The place where they sealed their own damnation.

But the sigil was still there. The chamber still existed. And if Brennan's rovers had found it...

What if the oath sigil on Mars wasn't just a marker — wasn't just a monument to past transgression — but a key to something? What if the Watchers had left something behind in that chamber? Technology. Knowledge. Perhaps even a gate to the Abyss where they now languished?

And what if Naamah had spent five thousand years preparing for the day when someone would find it?

"When?" Cian asked quietly. "When does the Abyss open? When are they released?"

**"I DO NOT KNOW THE HOUR."**

"But they *will* be released. Someday."

**"ALL BINDING HATH AN END. EVEN THE CHAINS OF TARTARUS WERE FORGED FOR A SEASON, NOT FOR ETERNITY."**

Cian looked at the sword in his hands. The blade that had shown him Mars. The metal that remembered the council of the Watchers.

"Why did you show me this?" he asked the blade. "Why now, after twenty-six centuries?"

The sword, predictably, offered no response.

But in the silence that followed, Cian felt something shift. A subtle change in the weight of reality, as if some cosmic clock had ticked forward to a new hour.

Something was coming.

Something that had been waiting for five thousand years.

And somehow, impossibly, he was meant to be part of it.

---

One year after the vision, Cian faced the truth.

He was obsessed.

The research had consumed everything. His Lockheed cover was hanging by a thread — Director Dunsmore had shifted from concerned mentor to suspicious overseer, and HR was building a file that would eventually result in "early retirement" or worse. His hunting had become erratic, driven by emotion rather than strategy. He hadn't spoken to Brennan in months, despite the younger man's increasingly worried messages.

*Hey Cousin Sean, haven't heard from you in a while. Everything okay?*

*Sean, I'm getting concerned. Please call me.*

*Look, I know you value your privacy, but we're family. If something's wrong...*

The messages sat unanswered on his phone, digital evidence of relationships he was letting wither.

And still the obsession burned.

*Naamah.*

He had found fragments of her trail across the millennia. A priestess in Sumer who had established the first temple prostitution network. A queen in Babylon who had advised Nebuchadnezzar. A noblewoman in Rome who had survived four emperors and three purges. A merchant's wife in Venice who had bankrolled the Fourth Crusade. A salon hostess in Paris who had whispered in Napoleon's ear.

Never the same face. Never the same name. But always the same pattern — a woman in the shadows, guiding events, accumulating power, building networks that outlasted empires.

She had been there for everything. Every atrocity. Every corruption. Every moment when humanity had the chance to choose light and instead chose darkness.

And she was still out there. Still working. Still building toward something.

*Partnership*, the vision had shown him. *She offered the Watchers partnership.*

What had they given her in return?

Cian didn't know. But he was going to find out.

Even if it killed him.

Even if it took another twenty-six centuries.

Even if—

His phone rang.

The caller ID read: **McNeeve Space Systems**.

Cian stared at the screen for a long moment. Then, finally, he answered.

"Cousin Sean!" Brennan's voice was bright with forced cheer, but underneath it Cian could hear worry. Genuine worry. "I've been trying to reach you for weeks. Is everything okay?"

"Everything's fine," Cian lied. "Just... busy. Work has been—"

"Sean." Brennan's voice dropped, becoming serious. "I talked to Director Dunsmore. He told me you've been distracted. That you're not yourself."

Cian closed his eyes.

"You're family," Brennan continued. "I don't know what's going on, but I want to help. Whatever it is. Even if you can't tell me everything. *Especially* if you can't tell me everything."

The offer hung in the air between them — genuine, unconditional, *human* in a way that Cian had almost forgotten.

"There might be something," Cian said slowly. "Something I'm working on. Something big."

"Then let me help."

Cian looked at the connection maps. At the red strings. At the blank space where Naamah's face should be.

He thought about the vision. About Mars. About a binding that would someday break and release something that humanity was utterly unprepared to face.

He thought about family.

"Not yet," he said finally. "But... soon. I think. When the time is right."

"I'll hold you to that." Brennan's voice warmed. "And Sean? Whatever this is — whatever's eating at you — you don't have to carry it alone. We MacNeeves stick together. Always have."

*We McNeeves*, Cian thought. *If only you knew how long that line stretches back. How much blood and sacrifice has gone into keeping it alive.*

"Thank you, Brennan. I mean that."

"I know you do. Now get some sleep. You sound like you haven't slept in months."

Cian almost laughed.

*If only you knew.*

He ended the call and set the phone aside. The maps stared back at him. The sword lay silent on its stand. The name continued to burn in his mind.

*Naamah.*

Somewhere in the darkness of history, she was waiting. Planning. Preparing for whatever came next.

And Cian mac Morna — two thousand six hundred and thirty-one years old, tired beyond words, consumed by an obsession he couldn't escape — was the only one who knew her name.

It wasn't enough.

It was nowhere near enough.

But it was a start.

And somewhere in the back of his mind, beneath the exhaustion and the frustration and the endless burning need to *understand*, something else stirred. Something that felt uncomfortably like hope.

*The sword showed me Mars for a reason. It gave me her name for a reason. And whatever's coming — whatever she's been building toward for five thousand years — I'm going to be there to stop it.*

*Or die trying.*

*Again.*

He picked up the sword and carried it to the weapons wall. Mounted it in its place of honor. Stood for a moment, looking at the blade that had been his companion for twenty-six centuries.

"Thank you," he said quietly. "For showing me."

The sword, as always, offered no reply.

But in the silence that followed, Cian could have sworn he felt something like acknowledgment. Like a promise made and understood.

*When the time is right*, the silence seemed to say. *When the hour draweth nigh. Then thou shalt see more.*

It wasn't much.

But for now, it was enough.

He turned away from the weapons wall and looked at the clock. 4:47 AM. Another night without real sleep. Another day of pretending to be someone he wasn't.

But for the first time in a year, the exhaustion didn't feel quite so crushing.

*Something's coming*, he thought. *And somehow, I'm meant to be part of it.*

The name burned on.

*Naamah.*

*Naamah.*

*Naamah.*
