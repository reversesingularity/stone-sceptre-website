# CHAPTER 2: THE HUNTER

---

## I. THE NEST

**2022 — Juárez, Mexico**

The intelligence had come through a contact in Matamoros — a priest who'd noticed the wrong kind of congregation gathering in the warehouse district. Trafficking cover, Father Mendoza had said, but the ritual markers suggested something older. Symbols that shouldn't exist outside museum archives. Chanting that made the locals cross themselves and walk faster.

Cian had driven through the night. Two years into his obsession with Naamah, he'd learned to recognize her network's fingerprints. This had them.

The warehouse smelled of rot and incense and something older than both.

Cian crouched in the shadows of the loading dock, counting bodies through a gap in the corrugated steel. Eleven. No—twelve. The twelfth was barely visible behind the altar, swaying in that boneless way that marked something *other*.

Ghouls. Eleven of them, at least—corpses reanimated by dark ritual, their flesh gray and waxy, their movements carrying the jerky wrongness of meat that had forgotten how to be alive. The twelfth figure moved differently. More fluidly. More *aware*. Not a ghoul, then. Something else.

One exit he could see, probably two more he couldn't. Industrial shelving created lanes and chokepoints. Flickering fluorescents cast stuttering shadows across concrete stained with things he didn't want to identify.

Mo Chrá hummed against his spine—a low, eager vibration that he felt in his teeth.

*Not yet,* he thought.

The humming intensified. Irritated.

*I said not yet.*

The blade's vibration shifted to something that could only be described as *sulking*.

Twenty-six centuries, and she still had the patience of a terrier spotting a rabbit. Liaigh had warned him about that, the day the sword-bond was forged. *She is eager, young one. Too eager. You must be her wisdom, for she has none of her own. Only hunger.*

Great. An immortal sword with impulse control issues. Just what every eternal warrior needed.

He scanned the warehouse again, building the tactical map in his mind. The ghouls were gathered around the altar—some kind of summoning ritual, from the geometry of the candles and the subsonic chanting that made his fillings ache. The undead weren't alert. Weren't expecting interruption. Whatever animating force drove them was focused on the ritual, on whatever door they were trying to open.

Eleven seconds to cross the open ground to the first shelving unit. Three seconds of exposure in the fluorescent light. Then shadow again, and the geometry would favor him.

Mo Chrá's hum shifted—questioning now. *Well?*

*Thirty seconds. Let them commit to the ritual.*

The humming became something almost verbal in its frustration. If a sword could tap its foot impatiently, this one was doing it.

Cian counted heartbeats. Watched the ritual build toward its crescendo. The chanting grew louder, the corpse-bodies beginning to convulse in synchronization, their attention pulled inward toward whatever door they were trying to open.

*Now.*

Mo Chrá sang.

Not the low hum of anticipation—a full-throated keen of joy that would have been audible across the warehouse if Cian hadn't already been moving. He cleared the loading dock in a single vault, crossed the killing ground in nine seconds instead of eleven, and was among the shelving units before the first ghoul turned its dead head.

Too late.

The blade took the first one through the throat—and kept going. No hesitation needed for ghouls. No innocent host to preserve. Mo Chrá sheared through dead muscle and rotted spine, and the ghoul collapsed in two pieces. One down, eleven to go.

Mo Chrá was *singing* now—a complex melody that Cian had learned to read over millennia. High notes meant threats from the left. Low notes meant movement behind. That particular trill meant *duck, you idiot*.

He ducked.

A length of pipe whistled through the space his skull had occupied. Cian pivoted, hamstrung the attacker—the ghoul didn't slow, pain meant nothing to the dead—so he reversed his grip and took its head clean off. The body kept moving for three more steps before whatever dark magic animated it finally noticed the absence of a skull.

Two down. Ten to go.

The remaining ghouls were scrambling now—the ritual abandoned, the circle broken, their corpse-bodies lurching toward him with the unnatural speed of things that felt no pain, knew no fear, and would keep coming until physically destroyed.

Cian didn't give them seconds.

He moved through them like a scythe through wheat—twenty-six centuries of muscle memory guiding every strike, every parry, every economy of motion. Mo Chrá sang her joy, her bloodlust, her absolute *delight* at finally being allowed to do what she was made for.

A ghoul came at him with a sacrificial knife, gray fingers locked around the hilt with rigor mortis strength. Cian shattered its elbow, spun the stumbling corpse into two others, and bisected all three at the waist with a single horizontal sweep. Mo Chrá sang with joy—no restraint, no mercy, just clean destruction.

Five down. Seven to go.

Mo Chrá's pitch changed—*behind you, two of them, one high one low*—and Cian dropped into a crouch that let the high attacker sail over his head while he drove the blade up through the low one's jaw and out the crown of its skull. He caught the airborne ghoul by the ankle as it passed, redirected its momentum into a concrete pillar, and watched the dead thing splatter against the stone like rotten fruit.

Seven down. Five to go.

The remaining ghouls were spreading out now—some vestige of tactical programming emerging through the necromantic haze. They were trying to flank him, to use the shelving units as cover, to buy time for reinforcements or escape.

Mo Chrá's song shifted to something almost *conversational*. A series of notes that Cian had learned to interpret over centuries:

*The one behind the blue drums is reaching for something. The two on your left are trying to circle. The one by the altar is running. The one in the corner...*

The sword's hum dropped to something low and dangerous.

*...is not like the others.*

Cian's eyes found the corner. Found the host standing motionless while its companions scrambled and died. Found the smile on its borrowed face—a smile that didn't belong to any shedim he'd ever encountered.

It was *watching* him. Studying him. *Evaluating* him.

Later. Deal with it later.

He took the two flankers first—a feint left, a spin right, Mo Chrá singing directions he followed without conscious thought. The first ghoul lost both arms at the elbow before realizing it had been hit; the second he split from collarbone to hip in a single downward stroke. Then the one behind the blue drums, who had produced a shotgun from somewhere and was trying to bring dead fingers to bear on the trigger. Cian closed the distance before the barrel aligned and removed the ghoul's head from its shoulders—then, for good measure, kicked the still-standing corpse into a shelf of industrial solvents.

Ten down. Two to go.

The runner had almost reached the back exit—a door Cian hadn't seen, hidden behind stacked pallets. Mo Chrá's song took on an almost *offended* quality. *You're letting it escape.*

He drew the knife from his boot and threw in a single motion. The blade took the fleeing ghoul through the base of its skull, severing the spine, and the corpse collapsed like a puppet with cut strings.

Eleven down.

One to go.

Cian turned to face the corner.

The twelfth figure hadn't moved. It stood in the same spot, wearing the same smile, watching him with eyes that glittered with something far older than the ghouls he'd been destroying. This was no reanimated corpse—this was a living host, possessed by something that made ordinary shedim look like gnats.

"Impressive." The voice was wrong—layered, as if multiple speakers were trying to use the same throat. "Twenty-three seconds. Eleven ghouls. We had heard you were skilled, but hearing and seeing are different things."

Mo Chrá's hum dropped to something Cian had rarely heard—a low, warning drone that vibrated in frequencies below human hearing.

*Danger. Real danger. Not like the others.*

"You've been tracking me." It wasn't a question.

"We have been *observing* you. For quite some time. The Guardian. The Sword-Bearer. The one who has spent twenty-six centuries pruning branches while the root grows strong." The smile widened. "She knows you, Cian mac Morna. She has *always* known you."

*She.*

Naamah.

Cian's grip tightened on Mo Chrá's hilt. The blade's song had shifted again—still warning, but with an undercurrent of something that might have been *jealousy*.

Twenty-six centuries of partnership, and the sword still got possessive when someone else's name was mentioned.

*Not now,* he thought.

Mo Chrá's hum acquired a distinct *hmph* quality.

"Deliver a message," the possessed host said. "Tell whoever sent you that I don't take messages."

"You misunderstand. The message is not for you." The host raised one hand, and Cian saw the detonator a fraction of a second before the thumb moved. "The message is what happens *after* you survive this."

Mo Chrá *shrieked*—the high, piercing alarm that meant *MOVE NOW*—and Cian was already diving when the warehouse turned to fire.

---

He came to in the rubble, ears ringing, Mo Chrá's hum the only steady thing in a world of smoke and settling concrete.

*Still alive?* the blade seemed to ask. The tone was almost disappointed, as if she'd been hoping for a more dramatic resurrection.

"Still alive," Cian muttered, pulling himself from the debris. His left arm was broken—he felt the bones grinding as he moved—but it was already beginning to knit. The sword-bond was good for something.

The warehouse was gone. Scattered across a blast radius that spoke of professional demolition charges, not the improvised explosives of a desperate cult. Whatever that thing had been—whatever had been wearing that living host while its ghoul soldiers provided cover—it had *planned* this. Had used eleven walking corpses as bait, as a *test*, just to see how he fought.

*She knows you,* it had said. *She has always known you.*

Naamah.

Mo Chrá's hum settled into something almost *smug*. As if to say: *I've known you longer. I've known you better. I was here first.*

Cian looked down at the blade, still clutched in his unbroken hand, gleaming despite the soot and blood and dust.

"You're ridiculous," he told her.

The sword's hum acquired a warm, contented quality. Compliments accepted.

He staggered to his feet, broken arm cradled against his chest, and began the long walk to the extraction point. Behind him, the warehouse burned. Ahead of him, the questions multiplied.

She knew him. She'd been watching him. And she'd just sent a message written in fire and demon-blood:

*I see you. I've always seen you. And when I'm ready, I'll come for you.*

Cian mac Morna walked into the darkness, his sword singing softly against his spine.

Twenty-six centuries, and she was the only woman who'd never left him.

*That's because I can't leave,* Mo Chrá's hum seemed to suggest. *I'm a sword.*

"And yet you have more opinions than any woman I've ever met."

The blade's vibration shifted to something that was definitely laughter.

They understood each other.

## II. THE OBSESSION

**2023 — Austin, Texas**

Three years.

Three years since the sword had sung a frequency Cian had never heard. Three years since the name *Naamah* had burned itself into the back of his mind like a brand that refused to heal. Three years of hunting by night, researching by dawn, and maintaining the ragged remnants of a cover identity that should have been retired long ago.

Sean Morna's career at Lockheed Martin had ended in early 2022 — "voluntary early retirement," the HR paperwork said, though everyone involved knew the truth. Too many missed meetings. Too many unexplained absences. Too many colleagues whispering about the man who used to be their best materials engineer and had become a ghost haunting his own office.

Director Dunsmore had been kind about it. Understanding, even. "You've given us thirty-six years, Sean. Whatever you're dealing with — health issue, family problem, whatever it is — take the time you need. The door's always open if you want to come back."

Cian had shaken his hand, cleaned out his desk, and never looked back.

The Lockheed exit package had been generous. The retirement account Sean Morna had accumulated over three decades was more than sufficient for a quiet life. But Cian didn't need money — he had centuries of accumulated wealth hidden in accounts scattered across the globe — and he certainly didn't want a quiet life.

What he wanted was *her*.

The basement had become his world. Connection maps covered every wall now, red strings linking photographs and documents across millennia of human history. He'd traveled to fourteen countries in the past three years, following traces of her network, interviewing sources who remembered fragments of the old stories. He'd burned through seventeen false identities, three safe houses, and more ammunition than he cared to count.

The shedim nests kept appearing. The trafficking networks kept recruiting. The cult cells kept gathering in dark places to perform their ancient rituals. And behind all of it, invisible and patient and ancient beyond measure, *she* kept building.

*Naamah.*

He knew her name now. He knew her history — or at least, as much of it as survived in scattered texts and corrupted oral traditions. He knew she had been the bait, the lure, the woman whose beauty had drawn Shemyaza down from the stars. He knew her father Lamech had traded her to the Watchers in exchange for forbidden knowledge. He knew she had survived the Flood through means that even Liaigh couldn't explain. He knew she had spent five thousand years constructing a network that spanned the globe.

What he didn't know was where she was. How to find her. How to *stop* her.

The sword remained silent.

He'd tried everything — meditation, sleep deprivation, prayer. He'd held vigil over the blade for days at a time, waiting for another vision, another fragment of memory, another piece of the puzzle. Nothing. Mo Chrá had shown him what it wanted to show him, and now it sat on his weapons wall like a beautiful, infuriating monument to his own helplessness.

**"THOU ART BECOMING WHAT THOU HUNTEST."**

Liaigh's voice, cutting through the silence of another sleepless night. Cian looked up from the ancient Akkadian text he'd been translating — a merchant's account from the second millennium BCE that mentioned a "Lady of the Deep Places" — and addressed the empty air.

"What?"

**"CONSUMED. SINGULAR. UNABLE TO SEE ANYTHING BEYOND THE OBJECT OF THY OBSESSION."**

"I see plenty."

**"THOU HAST NOT SPOKEN TO THY DESCENDANT IN NINE MONTHS."**

Cian's jaw tightened. Brennan had called. Had called repeatedly, in fact — worried messages about family and health and *Please, Sean, just let me know you're alive*. Cian had responded to a few of them. Brief texts. *Busy with a project. Will call soon. Don't worry.*

He hadn't called.

"Brennan doesn't need me hovering over him. He's doing fine."

**"HIS COMPANY HATH ADVANCED SIGNIFICANTLY. THE MARS PROGRAM IS ACCELERATING. THE TITAN ROVERS ARE PREPARING FOR THEIR FIRST ORBITAL LAUNCH."**

"Good for him."

**"HE DISCOVERED SOMETHING IN THE CYDONIA DATA. TWO YEARS AGO. HE HATH BEEN TRYING TO REACH THEE EVER SINCE."**

Cian's hand stopped mid-page. He turned slowly, staring at the ceiling.

"What did you just say?"

**"THE OATH SIGIL. THE MARK OF THEIR BINDING. THY DESCENDANT FOUND IT IN THE ORBITAL IMAGERY — THE VERY PLACE WHERE THE TWO HUNDRED SEALED THEIR DAMNATION. HE HATH BEEN SEEKING THY COUNSEL."**

"And you're telling me this *now*? After two years?"

**"THOU WERT NOT READY TO HEAR IT. THOU WERT LOST IN THY OBSESSION."**

"I'm not—" Cian stopped himself. Took a breath. Considered the state of the basement around him — the connection maps that had consumed every available surface, the empty coffee cups and whiskey bottles, the weeks-old takeout containers, the general atmosphere of a man who had stopped caring about anything except the hunt.

"Point taken," he said quietly.

**"THE LORD WORKETH IN MYSTERIOUS WAYS. THY DESCENDANT FOUND THE SIGIL BECAUSE IT WAS MEANT TO BE FOUND. PERHAPS IT IS TIME FOR THEE TO STOP CHASING SHADOWS AND START FOLLOWING THE LIGHT."**

Cian looked at the phone on his desk. Brennan's last message was from six months ago: *Sean, I don't know what's happening, but I'm worried about you. McNeeve Space Systems is doing incredible things. We're going to Mars. Real Mars, not just probes. If you ever want to be part of it... the offer still stands. Family first. Always.*

He'd read the message. Hadn't responded.

Three years of obsession, and what did he have to show for it? A name. A history. Thousands of dead ends and unanswered questions. The certainty that Naamah existed, that she was working toward something terrible, and that he was absolutely no closer to stopping her than he'd been the night the sword first sang.

But Brennan had found the sigil.

Brennan was going to Mars.

And suddenly, pieces that had refused to connect for three years began sliding into place.

"The Watchers swore their oath somewhere," Cian said slowly. "The sword showed me the council on Cydonia. The sigil marks where they bound themselves — where Armaros sealed their damnation. If Brennan's company is planning a mission to that region—"

**"THEN PERHAPS THE FATHER HATH PROVIDED A PATH."**

"Through my family's aerospace company."

**"THROUGH THY DESCENDANT'S DREAM. WHICH THOU HAST BEEN TOO CONSUMED TO NOTICE."**

Cian picked up his phone. Stared at Brennan's message. Then scrolled up to see the string of increasingly worried texts stretching back months, years — a pattern of concern that he'd been too obsessed to appreciate.

"I've been an idiot."

**"THOU HAST BEEN WOUNDED. THERE IS A DIFFERENCE."**

"Is there?"

**"THE VISION STRUCK DEEP. IT SHOWED THEE AN ENEMY OLDER THAN THY COMPREHENSION. IT IS... UNDERSTANDABLE... THAT THOU WOULDST LOSE THYSELF IN THE HUNT."**

"That's remarkably sympathetic."

**"I HAVE HAD THREE THOUSAND YEARS TO STUDY THEE. I KNOW THY PATTERNS."**

Cian almost smiled. Almost.

He didn't dial. Not yet.

Instead, he reached for the sword.

## III. THE VIGIL

**That Evening — Austin Suburbs**

Cian had been watching Brennan's house for three hours when he finally admitted to himself what he was doing.

The McNeeve residence sat at the end of a quiet cul-de-sac in the Westlake Hills — a sprawling craftsman-style home that spoke of success without shouting it. Two cars in the driveway: Brennan's Tesla and the Volvo that belonged to his wife, Sarah. Through the kitchen windows, Cian could see movement. A family preparing dinner. Normal. Safe. *Unaware*.

Mo Chrá hummed softly against his spine — not the combat frequency, but something lower. Questioning.

*Why are we here?*

"I don't know," Cian murmured. "Liaigh told me to stop chasing shadows. This felt like... following the light."

The sword's hum shifted to something that might have been skepticism.

He'd been doing this more often lately. Parking down the street. Watching from the shadows. Not just longing — though there was that too. But he knew what Niamh's escape had cost them. Debts to beings that lived for millennia didn't expire. They waited. They watched. And eventually, they collected.

Twenty-six centuries of solitude, and here was a family that carried his sister's blood. A family he'd kept at arm's length because closeness meant loss, and loss meant—

The kitchen lights flickered.

Cian sat up straighter.

Mo Chrá's hum changed. Sharpened.

Through the window, he saw Sarah McNeeve pause mid-motion, a serving spoon frozen in her hand. She was looking at something. Someone. The angle was wrong — Cian couldn't see what had drawn her attention.

But he could feel it.

The air pressure shifted. That subtle *wrongness* that preceded possession — the displacement of a human soul by something that didn't belong. He'd felt it ten thousand times across twenty-six centuries, and it never got easier to sense.

Something was in that house.

Mo Chrá *shrieked*.

Cian was out of the car before conscious thought caught up. The sword cleared its sheath as he crossed the lawn, and the blade was already singing its joy — high, eager notes that spoke of *finally, finally, after all this waiting*.

He hit the front door at full speed.

---

## IV. THE WAR FINDS YOU

The living room was chaos.

Sarah McNeeve had the children behind her — a girl of about eight and a boy perhaps five — pressed into the corner by the fireplace. Her face was pale but her stance was solid. *Protective*. Not running. *Defending*.

Brennan was on the floor, blood streaming from a gash on his forehead, trying to rise. Between him and his family stood something wearing a delivery driver's uniform.

The uniform was wrong. The posture was wrong. The *eyes* were wrong — flat and dark and ancient, tracking Emma McNeeve with the focused hunger of a predator that had found exactly what it was looking for.

"The bloodline," the thing said, its voice layered with harmonics that shouldn't exist in a human throat. "The blood that was *promised*. The blood that *escaped*." Its borrowed face twisted into something like hunger. "We have been owed for *so* long—"

Mo Chrá's song cut through the creature's words like a blade through silk.

The possessed host turned. Saw Cian. And *smiled*.

"Guardian. We had hoped you would come. She will be pleased—"

Cian didn't let it finish.

Twenty-six centuries of combat distilled into a single, efficient motion. Mo Chrá swept low, hamstringing the vessel without severing — preserve the host, disable the threat — and as the creature collapsed, Cian's free hand caught its throat.

"Who sent you?"

The thing laughed through borrowed lips. "The Lady sees all. The Lady waits. The Lady—"

"I've heard that speech." Cian's grip tightened. "Try again."

"You cannot stop what is coming, Sword-Bearer. The bloodlines are being gathered. The vessel is being prepared. The time of the Beast approaches, and your little family—"

Cian's fingers found the precise point where possessed flesh met corrupted spirit.

The extraction was brutal — always was. The shedim spirit shrieked as Mo Chrá's edge pressed against the host's throat, the blade's ancient power forcing the demon to *choose*: flee or be banished to the Abyss. Most chose flight. This one was no exception.

The dark presence tore free in a rush of cold air and sulfurous stench, spiraling toward the ceiling before dissipating into shadows. The host — a middle-aged man, probably an actual delivery driver grabbed off the street — collapsed unconscious but breathing. Innocent. Used. *Violated*.

Cian lowered him gently to the floor.

Then turned to face the family.

Sarah was still shielding the children, but her expression had shifted from terror to something more complex. Brennan had made it to his feet, swaying slightly, blood painting half his face in crimson.

The little girl — Emma — was staring at Mo Chrá with wide eyes.

"That sword," she whispered. "It was *singing*."

"Emma, don't—" Sarah started.

"It's okay." Cian sheathed the blade. The singing stopped, replaced by a low, satisfied hum. "She's right. The sword does sing. It's been singing for a very long time."

Silence.

Then Brennan spoke, his voice rough: "Sean. What the fuck was that?"

"That," Cian said, "was something I've been protecting you from for fifteen years."

"Protecting—" Brennan's jaw tightened. "That thing knew my *daughter's name*. It was looking at her like—"

"Like she was what it came for. Because she was."

Sarah's arms tightened around both children. "Explain. *Now*."

Cian looked at the family he'd watched from shadows. The family he'd kept at distance because closeness meant loss. The family that had just been attacked because of blood that ran back twenty-six centuries to a girl the giants had stolen, raised as a slave, and kept pure for virgin sacrifice — a blood ritual meant to strengthen the prison wards that kept an ancient weapon trapped. Wards that had been weakening for millennia. Wards that finally gave way the very night Cian came to claim what lay within — and found his sister being dragged to the altar as he escaped with the blade.

Niamh's blood had been *promised* to them. And she had escaped.

Now, twenty-six centuries later, they were collecting the debt. Emma's blood carried the same properties that had made Niamh valuable — the same purity they needed, passed down through eighty generations. And beyond the practical use, there was *revenge*. The sacrifice that was stolen. The ritual that failed. The bloodline that got away.

The sword that Emma had heard singing.

Liaigh's voice, soft in his mind: **"THE TIME FOR SECRETS IS PAST, CHILD OF MORNA."**

He reached into his jacket and withdrew a sealed cylinder — brass, tarnished with age, inscribed with symbols that predated every alphabet the modern world remembered. He'd carried it for two thousand years, waiting for the moment when it would be needed.

That moment was now.

"There's something you need to read," he said. "Something written a very long time ago. By someone who knew this day would come."

"Written by who?" Brennan demanded.

Cian met his eyes. "By your ancestor. By my sister. By the woman whose blood runs in Emma's veins and whose enemies have been hunting your family since before Rome was built."

He offered the cylinder.

"Her name was Niamh. And this is her letter to you."

## V. THE INHERITANCE

Brennan's hands shook as he broke the seal.

The cylinder opened to reveal a scroll — not papyrus or parchment, but something older. Preserved by means that had nothing to do with modern chemistry. The ink was faded but legible, written in a script that Brennan couldn't read.

"It's Old Irish," Cian said quietly. "But there's a translation. She wrote both — she knew her children's children would eventually forget the old tongue."

Brennan unrolled the scroll until he found Latin characters. Sarah moved to his shoulder, reading with him. The children sat on the couch, Emma still watching the sword on Cian's back, Liam pressed against his sister in confused silence.

The letter was not long. But it was enough.

> *To my children's children, unto the last generation—*
>
> *If you are reading this, my brother has found you. Trust him. He has carried our burden alone for too long.*
>
> *I was born Niamh mac Morna, daughter of Irish nobility. When I was five years old, creatures out of nightmare stole me from my home and murdered my parents. They kept me alive for seventeen years, raising me unsullied as a virgin for sacrifice — a blood ritual meant to strengthen the prison wards that kept an ancient weapon trapped. But the wards were weakening. And on the very night they brought me to the altar, my brother Cian — sent by the prophet Jeremiah himself on a quest he was told he must complete alone or fail — broke through the failing wards and claimed the blade. He found me being dragged to the sacrifice as he escaped. The sword that was meant to remain imprisoned became the instrument of my rescue.*
>
> *But we carry more than royal blood. We carry a target.*
>
> *The Watchers fell before the Flood. Their children, the Nephilim, were destroyed — but their spirits persist. The demons you call legends, the monsters you dismiss as myth — they are real. They remember us. They hunt us. They have hunted us since before writing existed, and they will hunt you until the end of all things.*
>
> *My brother Cian has spent his life — his impossibly long life — standing between our blood and their hunger. He has done this alone because he believes loneliness is protection. He is wrong.*
>
> *I am dying as I write this. The sickness has taken hold, and even Liaigh cannot heal what God has ordained. But I die knowing my children will survive. Knowing their children will survive. Knowing that someday, when the shadows grow too dark and the enemy grows too bold, my brother will emerge from his solitude and share his burden with our blood.*
>
> *That day is now.*
>
> *Help him. Fight with him. Do not let him carry this alone.*
>
> *We are hunted because we are dangerous. Our blood carries something they fear — something they have feared since the days when angels walked the earth. The sword that rescued me sings to those of my blood. If your children hear its voice, do not be afraid. It recognizes its own.*
>
> *The war is coming. The final war, the one the prophets wrote of and the angels whisper about in the halls of heaven.*
>
> *Stand with my brother. He will need you more than he knows.*
>
> *Your ancestor in blood and faith,*
> *Niamh mac Morna*
> *Wife to the House of David*
> *Written in the forty-third year of my life*
> *Twenty-six centuries before you read these words*

Sarah finished reading first. She looked up at Cian with eyes that held no skepticism — not anymore.

"You're her brother."

"I am."

"You're twenty-six hundred years old."

"I am."

"And that thing—" she nodded toward the unconscious delivery driver, "—came for my daughter because of blood that runs back to ancient Israel."

"Yes."

Brennan set down the letter. His hands had stopped shaking. When he looked at Cian, his eyes held something new.

Not fear. Not confusion.

*Fury*.

"You've been watching us," he said. "Protecting us. For fifteen years."

"Longer than that. I've watched every generation of your family since Niamh died. From the shadows. Always from the shadows."

"Why? Why not tell us? Why not—"

"Because knowledge paints a target." Cian gestured toward the unconscious host. "That thing found you because the war is escalating. Because something is coming — something bigger than anything I've faced. In the old days, they couldn't track bloodlines this precisely. Now they can. Now they're *looking*."

"Looking for what?"

"I don't know yet. But I know it involves your company. It involves Mars. And it involves coordinates that the ancients carved into stone five thousand years before you were born."

Brennan's expression shifted. "The Cydonia data. The sigil."

"You found the mark of the Watchers. The place where they swore their damnation. And beneath it—"

"The heat signature." Brennan's voice was barely a whisper. "Something active. Something *running*."

"Something they left behind. Something they may be trying to reach." Cian looked at each of them in turn — Sarah, Brennan, the children who carried blood that had been hunted since before the Flood.

"You need to understand something," he said quietly. "Your family exists because of a Hebrew prophet. Jeremiah — the one from the Bible. He told me to quest for that sword. Told me I had to go alone or I would fail. I didn't know why at the time. Didn't know Niamh was even alive." His voice roughened. "The wards keeping the blade imprisoned were weakening. If I'd arrived a day later, they would have sacrificed her to strengthen them again. If I'd arrived a day earlier, the wards would have killed me. The timing wasn't luck. It was *providence*."

He let that sink in.

"A prophet who walked with kings and saw Jerusalem fall — *he* is the reason your bloodline survived. Every generation of your family, every child born in twenty-six centuries, exists because Jeremiah knew something was going to happen that night. And he made sure I was there."

"We need to move. Now. I have a safehouse. Once you're secure, I'll explain everything — the Watchers, the Nephilim, the war, all of it. But right now, we have maybe six hours before more of them come."

"More of—" Sarah started.

"That one was a scout. It will have reported back before I extracted it. They know I'm here now. They know I'm protecting you. The next wave won't be a single possessed host. It'll be a team."

He expected argument. Resistance. The natural human instinct to deny, to rationalize, to insist that *this can't be happening*.

Instead, Sarah McNeeve — aerospace engineer, mother of two, woman who had just watched her husband bleed and her daughter be hunted — stood up and said:

"Kids, get your shoes. We're going with Uncle Sean."

Emma looked at her mother. "Is the singing sword coming too?"

Mo Chrá hummed warmly against Cian's spine. *I like this one.*

"The sword is definitely coming," Cian said.

Brennan crossed to him. Stopped within arm's reach. His face was still bloody, his shirt was torn, and his eyes held the same determination Cian had seen in Niamh's face twenty-six centuries ago.

"You've been carrying this alone," Brennan said. "Since before Rome. Since before Christ. Alone."

"Yes."

"That ends tonight."

Cian blinked. "Brennan—"

"That *thing* came for my daughter. For Emma. It came into my *house*." Brennan's voice was quiet, but it carried the weight of something ancient awakening. "You think I'm going to hide while you fight this war? You think I'm going to let you protect my family from the shadows while we pretend everything is normal?"

"It's not safe—"

"*Nothing* is safe. That's what Niamh's letter said. We've been hunted since before writing existed. Hiding didn't protect us. Distance didn't protect us. The only thing that protected us tonight was *you*, walking through that door with a sword that sings."

He stepped closer. Close enough that Cian could see the blood still trickling from his temple. Close enough to see the fury beneath the calm.

"I'm not asking for your protection. I'm *joining* your war. My wife is an aerospace engineer. I run the company that's going to put humans on Mars. And now I find out my family has been soldiers in an invisible war for twenty-six centuries?"

Brennan's jaw tightened.

"You've been fighting alone long enough, *Uncle*. The McNeeves are in. All of us. And you're going to tell us everything — starting with what the hell is actually on Cydonia."

Cian looked at his descendant. At Sarah, who was already gathering the children with the efficiency of a woman who'd decided that demons were just another problem to solve. At Emma, who was still watching Mo Chrá with fascination rather than fear.

**"THOU ART NOT ALONE,"** Liaigh murmured. **"THOU HAST NEVER BEEN ALONE. BUT NOW... NOW THOU KNOWEST IT."**

"The safehouse first," Cian said. "Then I'll tell you everything."

"Everything?"

Cian thought of the Cydonia data. The heat signature. The Mars mission that was about to become the most important expedition in human history.

"Everything."

Brennan nodded once. Then turned to help his wife with the children.

And Cian mac Morna, who had walked the earth alone for twenty-six centuries, felt something he hadn't felt since Father Michael died in his arms during the Famine.

He felt *hope*.

Not fragile hope. Not desperate hope.

*Fierce* hope. The hope of a warrior who finally had an army.

### The Attack Sequence Payoff

The home attack accomplishes what a peaceful revelation couldn't:

1. **Brennan's zeal is earned** — his daughter was targeted
2. **Sarah becomes a player** — she witnessed everything
3. **The children are in** — they've seen the sword sing
4. **Cian's isolation ends** — he's forced to accept help

### Niamh's Letter — Thematic Weight

The letter transforms the revelation from "Cian telling his story" to "Niamh speaking across millennia." This:

- Validates Cian's mission through family voice
- Gives Brennan inherited purpose, not just acquired knowledge
- Creates emotional resonance that pure exposition couldn't achieve
- Establishes the bloodline's importance to the Enemy
